\chapter*{Preface: The Tide‑Clerk’s Ledger}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface}

\noindent
{\Large
\textit{I was born on the mudflats, which is to say I was born with salt in my blood and a deficit in my soul. My mother kept the beacon at Deadman’s Point. My father was a reef – that is to say, he took more ships than he saved. I learned to read the tide before I learned my letters, and I learned that the sea does not forgive arithmetic errors.}
}

But arithmetic is only one kind of account. A promise carved in stone, a red thread tied at a crossroads, a cup left empty for a guest who never arrives – these are also contracts, though no ledger records them. The Amaranthine runs on three ledgers: the Admiralty’s tariff books, the Syndicate’s invisible‑ink manifests, and the sea’s own memory – the one carved in wreckage and coral.

Some call it debt. I call it \emph{what remains when the tide goes out}.

This book is not a travel guide. It is a collection of thresholds: bridges that remember every toll, fog‑bells that count your breaths, stones that warm when you speak a true name. Use the cards to generate scenes. Let the fiction lead. When you are lost, ask: \emph{What does this place owe? And to whom?}

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textit{— A Tide‑Clerk of no fixed abode, whose abacus has nine beads and who has never balanced a single account}

\section*{How to Use This Book}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{How to Use This Book}

This book is a toolkit for the Game Master. Each regional chapter contains:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A short atmosphere paragraph and a local quote.
  \item An \emph{almanac} – a quick Q\&A about daily life in that region.
  \item A full set of card‑driven generator tables (\spadesuit\ places, \heartsuit\ people, \clubsuit\ complications, \diamondsuit\ rewards). To use them, draw cards and consult the appropriate rank.
\end{itemize}

For the full card system, see \textbf{The Cartomancer’s Compass} (next chapter). For mechanical rules (attributes, skills, actions, magic, summoning), refer to the \textit{Fate’s Edge System Reference Document}.

\section*{The Ledger of the Deck}

The world does not speak in words. It speaks in suits, ranks, and the spaces between.
Every bridge, every bribe, every broken oath can be laid out on a table of felt and
dealt from a worn deck. This is not superstition. It is method.

The regional generators in this book \— from the fog-choked levees of the
Mistlands (\autoref{chap:mistlands}) to the walking cities of Ngomebe
(\autoref{chap:ngomebe}) \— all speak the same language: the language of the
fifty-two-card deck. Learn to read it, and you can generate a campaign, a
crossroads, or a crisis in the time it takes to shuffle.

This chapter is your key. It explains how to interpret any card drawn from any
regional generator, and how to weave those interpretations into the fiction at
your table. Consider it the threshold between the abstract mechanics of the draw
and the living, breathing world of Fate’s Edge.

\section*{The Four Suits: A Universal Grammar}

Every card in every generator carries the same four suit-meanings. They are the
alphabet of the world’s ledger.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\spadesuit}{Spades}: Place \— The Stage}
\label{sec:spades-place}

Where does the scene happen? \spadesuit\ is the threshold, the landmark, the ground
beneath the action. It answers: \emph{Where are we?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (10–A)}: A major landmark \— a fortress, a capital, a
    haunted ruin. The place has a name that echoes in local songs.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6–9)}: A functional location \— a ford, a market, a
    shrine. The place has a purpose and a keeper.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2–5)}: A liminal or neglected place \— a crossroads, a
    collapsed bridge, a smuggler’s cove. The place is defined by what it lacks.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, \spadesuit\ entries are always places. See, for example,
the bridges and canals of Silkstrand (\autoref{sec:silkstrand-places}) or the
cairns and passes of Ubral (\autoref{sec:ubral-places}).

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\heartsuit}{Hearts}: Actor \— The Face}
\label{sec:hearts-actor}

Who drives the scene? \heartsuit\ is the person, the faction, the voice that
speaks. It answers: \emph{Who is here?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (J–A)}: An elite, a ruler, a power-broker. The actor has
    authority and reach beyond the immediate scene.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6–10)}: A professional, a functionary, a skilled
    operator. The actor has a trade and a price.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2–5)}: A commoner, an outcast, a victim. The actor
    needs something or has been failed by someone.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, \heartsuit\ entries are always people or factions. See the
merchants and spies of the Brass Coast (\autoref{sec:brasscoast-people}) or the
spirit-wardens of the Mistlands (\autoref{sec:mistlands-people}).

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\clubsuit}{Clubs}: Pressure \— The Complication}
\label{sec:clubs-pressure}

What pushes back? \clubsuit\ is the storm, the debt, the knife in the dark. It
answers: \emph{What goes wrong?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (10–A)}: A catastrophe, a betrayal, an invasion. The
    complication changes the shape of the journey.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6–9)}: A serious obstacle \— a feud, a quarantine, a
    broken bridge. The complication costs time, resources, or reputation.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2–5)}: A minor hazard \— a sudden squall, a lost
    token, a raised toll. The complication is a nuisance, not a disaster.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, \clubsuit\ entries are always complications or threats.
See the river floods and witness-cord cuts of Ayokha
(\autoref{sec:ayokha-complications}) or the market riots of Silkstrand
(\autoref{sec:silkstrand-complications}).

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{\diamondsuit}{Diamonds}: Leverage \— The Reward}
\label{sec:diamonds-leverage}

What opens the door? \diamondsuit\ is the token, the writ, the secret that changes
Position. It answers: \emph{What do you gain?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (J–A)}: A major asset \— a noble title, a keystone
    charter, a general pardon. The reward reshapes the party’s standing.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6–10)}: A significant tool \— a pass-stone, a witness
    cord, a lighthouse charter. The reward solves a specific class of problem.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2–5)}: A minor advantage \— a bridge token, a guest-cup,
    a rumour. The reward grants a single use of improved Position.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, \diamondsuit\ entries are always rewards or leverage. See
the keystone charters of Ngomebe (\autoref{sec:ngomebe-rewards}) or the
water-share tablets of Fhara (\autoref{sec:fhara-rewards}).

\section*{Rank and Severity: The Weight of the Card}
\label{sec:rank-severity}

The rank of a card \— from Ace (high) to 2 (low) \— sets the scale of its suit’s
meaning. Use this ladder:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Rank} & \textbf{Narrative Weight} \\
\hline
Ace & Legendary, campaign-altering, mythic \\
King/Queen/Jack & Major, faction-level, lasting consequences \\
10–7 & Significant, scene-defining, costly \\
6–4 & Moderate, manageable, a real cost \\
3–2 & Minor, a complication but not a crisis \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

When a generator instructs you to set a timer by the highest rank, use this
conversion:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{2–5}: 4-segment timer (short, local problem)
    \item \textbf{6–10}: 6-segment timer (standard, journey-length)
    \item \textbf{J, Q, K}: 8-segment timer (extended, regional problem)
    \item \textbf{A}: 10-segment timer (epic, campaign-length)
\end{itemize}

\section*{Card Faces: The Court as Character}
\label{sec:court-characters}

Face cards (Jack, Queen, King) are not merely high ranks. They are \emph{persons}
with agency, personality, and often a name.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Jack}: An agent, a functionary, a messenger. The Jack acts on
    someone else’s authority. They are competent but replaceable.
    \item \textbf{Queen}: A power in their own right. The Queen has influence,
    resources, and a network. They are not easily replaced.
    \item \textbf{King}: The ultimate authority in their domain. The King is the
    source of law, the peak of the hierarchy. To challenge a King is to challenge
    the order itself.
\end{itemize}

When a face card appears in a \heartsuit\ (actor) draw, give the NPC a name, a
motive, and a quirk. When it appears in a \spadesuit\ (place) or \clubsuit\
(pressure) draw, treat it as a \emph{personified} force: a King of Clubs might be
a feud that has lasted generations; a Queen of Spades might be a bridge that
remembers every toll.

\section*{Aces and the Ninth: The Hollow’s Attention}
\label{sec:aces-ninth}

An Ace is not merely the highest rank. It is a \emph{threshold}. When an Ace
appears in any draw, the Hollow takes notice. The GM gains 1 extra Story Beat
(beyond any generated by 1s) to spend on a supernatural complication: a ghostly
procession, a bell that rings without a clapper, a shadow that walks
independently.

In regions where the Ninth Taboo is strong (see \autoref{sec:theona-mechanics},
\autoref{sec:aelinnel-mechanics}, \autoref{sec:ways-between-features}), an Ace may
also trigger a specific regional omen. For example:

\begin{itemize}
    \item In Theona (\autoref{chap:theona}), an Ace of \spadesuit\ might add a
    “missing step” to a bridge.
    \item In the Ways Between (\autoref{chap:ways-between}), an Ace of \heartsuit\
    might cause a reflection to speak.
    \item In the Mistlands (\autoref{chap:mistlands}), an Ace of \clubsuit\ might
    cause a bell-line to fail.
\end{itemize}

The GM should always read an Ace as a signal to escalate the uncanny.

\section*{By the Numbers: Procedure for Any Generator}
\label{sec:generator-procedure}

When you sit down to generate a scene, a journey leg, or a settlement, follow this
procedure:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Name the destination}. Which region? Which route? Which authority
    controls the gate?
    \item \textbf{Shuffle and draw}. Draw cards face-up, one for each suit you
    need. Most generators require all four suits; some (like travel legs) may use
    only three.
    \item \textbf{Read the \spadesuit}. This is your place. Describe it first. Set
    the stage.
    \item \textbf{Read the \heartsuit}. This is your actor. Introduce them. What do
    they want? Why are they here?
    \item \textbf{Read the \clubsuit}. This is your pressure. Complicate the scene.
    What just went wrong, or what is about to?
    \item \textbf{Read the \diamondsuit}. This is your leverage. What can the
    players gain if they succeed? Be specific.
    \item \textbf{Set the timer}. Use the highest rank among the four cards to
    determine the size of the scene’s primary timer (see \autoref{sec:rank-severity}).
    \item \textbf{Interpret faces and Aces}. Give names to face cards. Apply
    regional Ace effects.
    \item \textbf{Play to find out}. Use the generated elements as prompts, not
    scripts. The cards are seeds, not fences.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Regional Flavours: How Suits Shift Meaning}
\label{sec:regional-flavours}

While the core suit meanings remain constant, each region inflects them with its
own texture. A \spadesuit\ in Ecktoria (\autoref{chap:ecktoria}) is a forum or a
coin-house; a \spadesuit\ in the Valewood (\autoref{chap:valewood}) is a phasing
ruin or a star-road. A \heartsuit\ in the Black Banners
(\autoref{chap:black-banners}) is a mercenary captain or a condotta broker; a
\heartsuit\ in Ykrul (\autoref{chap:ykrul}) is a khatun or a bone-singer.

Use the regional chapters as your lexicon. When you draw a card, consult the
relevant table (e.g., \autoref{sec:acasia-places} for Acasia, \autoref{sec:oshiira-people}
for Oshiira). The table will give you a specific entry keyed to the rank and suit.
Do not invent from scratch unless the table runs out \— the tables are designed to
evoke the specific flavour of the region.

\subsection*{Example: A Draw in Acasia}
\label{sec:example-acasia}

You are running a journey through the Broken Marches (\autoref{chap:acasia}). You
draw:

\begin{center}
\spadesuit\ 7, \quad \heartsuit\ \text{J}, \quad \clubsuit\ 4, \quad \diamondsuit\ \text{Q}
\end{center}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{\spadesuit\ 7}: You consult \autoref{sec:acasia-places}. A 7 of
    Spades is “Hill-Motte \— Fresh palisade; ditch scattered with caltrops.”
    \item \textbf{\heartsuit\ J}: You consult \autoref{sec:acasia-people}. A Jack of
    Hearts is “Bride with No Dowry but a Claim — Daggers in the wedding chest.”
    \item \textbf{\clubsuit\ 4}: You consult \autoref{sec:acasia-complications}. A
    4 of Clubs is “Wedding Turns Ambush — You are caught between two bloodlines.”
    \item \textbf{\diamondsuit\ Q}: You consult \autoref{sec:acasia-rewards}. A Queen
    of Diamonds is “Marriage Proxy — From the Margravine; bind a hillking to your cause.”
    \item \textbf{Highest rank}: Queen (rank equivalent to King/J/Q at 8). The scene’s
    primary timer is 8 segments: \textit{The Feud Escalates [8]}.
    \item \textbf{Interpretation}: The party arrives at a hill-fort (Hill-Motte) on
    the eve of a wedding. The bride (Bride with No Dowry) is caught between two
    feuding bloodlines. The wedding turns to violence (Wedding Turns Ambush). If
    the party can survive the ambush and broker peace, the Margravine will grant
    them a Marriage Proxy — a powerful tool to bind a hill-king to their cause.
    The 8-segment timer tracks how quickly the feud spirals into open war.
\end{enumerate}

This is the generator at work: four cards, one scene, immediate fiction.

\section*{The Unwritten Suit: When a Draw is Incomplete}
\label{sec:incomplete-draw}

Sometimes, a generator does not require all four suits. A travel leg, for example,
may only need \spadesuit, \heartsuit, \clubsuit\ (see
\autoref{sec:globaltravel-procedure}). A social encounter might only need
\heartsuit\ and \clubsuit. When a suit is missing, treat its absence as a
question:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No \spadesuit}: The place is ambiguous, shifting, or hidden. The
    scene occurs in a liminal space — a crossroads, a fog-bank, a threshold.
    \item \textbf{No \heartsuit}: The actor is absent, silent, or dead. The
    pressure and place act without a face. This can be more frightening than a
    named NPC.
    \item \textbf{No \clubsuit}: The scene is calm, peaceful, or mercifully
    straightforward. This is rare in Fate’s Edge. Treasure it.
    \item \textbf{No \diamondsuit}: The reward is not a token but an outcome. The
    players gain Position, information, or a cleared timer rather than a physical
    asset.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Interpreting Any Draw, Any Region}}
\label{sec:any-draw}

When you need a prompt in a hurry — a random encounter, a rumour at a tavern, a
discovery in a ruin — draw a single card. Use this quick-reference:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
\spadesuit & A place or landmark. What is its name? What happened here? \\
\heartsuit & A person or faction. What do they want? What do they fear? \\
\clubsuit & A complication or threat. Who or what is the obstacle? \\
\diamondsuit & A reward, token, or secret. What can be gained? \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The rank tells you the scale. A low rank is local and personal; a high rank is
regional and political. A face card demands a name; an Ace demands the Hollow’s
attention.

This single-card method is ideal for improvisation. It gives you one clear element
to build around. Let the fiction answer the rest.

\section*{Final Guidance: The Cards Are Oracles, Not Scripts}
\label{sec:cards-oracles}

The generators in this book are tools, not tyrants. If a draw gives you an entry
that contradicts established fiction, or if you simply prefer a different
interpretation, overrule it. The deck is a prompt, not a prison.

But trust it, too. The most memorable sessions often come from the card you did
not expect. The Ace that arrives at the wrong moment. The Jack who speaks a name
no one wanted to hear. The diamond that glows with a secret debt.

Shuffle. Draw. Interpret. And let the road unfold.

\vspace{1em}
\textit{“The ledger is never closed. It is only passed to the next keeper.”}
\hfill — Dusana of the Raven Road

  \end{itemize}
  The Hollow is the interest on the Omens’ oldest debt. The players are never meant to pay it off – only to decide whose name goes on the next line of the ledger.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section*{Travel Modes of the Amaranthine}

\subsection*{Sea Legs}
The Amaranthine Sea connects Kahfagia, Ecktoria, Acasia, and the northern coasts. Controlling authority is either the origin port’s navy or a neutral convoy league. Passage requires a \textbf{Lantern Code} (Kahfagia) or a \textbf{Convoy Letter} stamped with the Admiral’s seal. Without papers, you sail at your own risk – the reefs are hungry.

\subsection*{Mountain Passes \& Underways}
Aeler holds and the Mistlands are linked by the \textbf{Spine Underway}, a great tunnel beneath the peaks. Travel requires an \textbf{Underway Pass} and a breath‑counter – count to eight, never nine. The Mistlands’ bell‑lines are navigable only by those carrying \textbf{ward‑salt}.

\subsection*{River Legs}
The Belworth (Vhasia/Viterra) and the Dolmis (eastern coast) are arteries of trade and conflict. Crossing requires a \textbf{Safe‑Conduct} recognised on both banks. The Theona taboos (no ninth step, no ninth cup) apply on river bridges.

\subsection*{Steppe Legs}
The Violet Steppe (Ykrul) and the eastern grasslands (Kuvani) use remount stations and grazing compacts. A \textbf{Hostage String} or a \textbf{Story Toll} buys passage. Never ride between two wolves at dusk – that gap belongs to the Hollow.

\section*{Gateways to Other Expansions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Southern Realms (Oshiira, Ashaan, Ngomebe)}: From Kahfagia, sail the \textbf{Narrow of Sorrows} to the Brass Coast. From Ecktoria, follow the Imperial Road south to the Sahel.
  \item \textbf{Way of Silk (Fhara, Sidhi, Pereshi, Kuvani, Tulkani)}: From Ykrul, the \textbf{Eastern Steppe Road} leads to Kuvani remount stations. From Thepyrgos, caravans cross the Himadri passes to Pereshi highlands.
  \item \textbf{Eastern Realms (Sihai, Nihori, Ayokha)}: From the Linns, sail the Aberderrin Sea to Nihori. From Acasia, follow the old Imperial Road east to Sihai.
  \item \textbf{Dhahara (Brass Coast, Nine Kingdoms, Himadri)}: From Thepyrgos, the \textbf{Black River} leads south to the Brass Coast. From Aeler, the Himadri passes connect directly to the iron mines.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Kahfagia}
\emph{The Flag of Storms.}

\noindent
Kahfagia is a thalassocracy of lantern‑light and rust, where every beacon is a promise and every reef a debt collector. The Admiralty rules from its mirror‑towers, but the true power flows through oligarchic families, privateers, and the silent threads of the Lethai‑ar. Here, a well‑placed lantern code can start a war – or end one.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“The commodores don’t want you to know: the lanterns are afraid of the dark too. I’ve seen a Mirror‑Keeper weep when her reflection showed her a daughter she’d sold for a pilot’s license.”} \\
— Captain Mira ``the Lucky'', Sidhi exile

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Salt fish porridge – dried cod soaked overnight, boiled with hardtack and a splash of lamp oil. Every Kahfagian child learns to taste the salt and know which coast their grandmother came from.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count ships by their lanterns. Three lanterns means a Kahfagian merchant – safe. Four means an Oshiiran grain barge – neutral. One, low in the water, means a privateer. Run.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their hands. Dock‑workers have rope‑burned palms. Merchants have ink‑stained fingers. Privateers have no fingernails – they bite them off in storms.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} False manifests. Every third ship carries cargo that doesn’t appear on its papers. The Admiralty knows. The Admiralty takes a cut. The Admiralty calls it a “tariff surcharge.”

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Mirror‑Keepers are not born – they are made. And the making requires a death. A sister, a daughter, a lover. Someone who drowned so the keeper could see through the fog.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} The tide takes them. Kahfagia has no cemeteries above ground. The drowned are weighted with chain and dropped in the deep channel. Their families light candles in paper boats and float them out at dusk.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer your bell. Every Kahfagian carries a small brass bell – the sound of their name. Offering it means “I give you my voice.” Refusing to return it is a declaration of blood feud.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Beacon Lighting (midwinter). Every lighthouse keeper lights a new lamp from the Admiral’s flame. If your beacon catches first, your year will be lucky. If it sputters, you will owe a death to the sea.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Admiral’s lantern code has a ninth signal – never used, never spoken, burned into every keeper’s memory. It means \textit{“Kill the witnesses. Sink the evidence. The sea will hide the rest.”}
\end{almanac}

\chapter{Ecktoria}

\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Hardtack and camp stew – the stew is whatever was caught, killed, or traded for before dawn. The cook is called “The Alchemist.” No one asks their real name.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their colours. Every company has its own banner – lantern, thorn, bell, fishbone, broken scale. A stranger with no colours has no company. A stranger with no company is a deserter. A deserter is a corpse.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Desertion – but desertion is a negotiation. A soldier who wants to leave will sell their gear, their secrets, and their silence. The price is always “one more season.”

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Black Colonel is not a person. The title is held by a wax seal in a lead‑lined box. The box is carried by a mute lieutenant. The lieutenant signs documents with the Colonel’s thumb‑print. The thumb‑print is a forgery. The forger is the lieutenant. The lieutenant has no thumbs.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the pyre. Camp dead are burned in a bonfire of broken spears. The quartermaster charges a fee from the dead’s final pay. The final pay is never enough.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You stand a watch. The wronged party chooses the hour – midnight, dawn, the dead of winter. If you survive, you are forgiven. If you do not, the debt is paid.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} Payday – but payday is a negotiation. The captain promises payment on the first of the month. The quartermaster promises when the supply train arrives. The soldiers promise loyalty until the payment comes. The payment never comes. The loyalty never ends.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Banner‑Captain’s contract is written in disappearing ink. The ink was supplied by a Sidhi forger paid in promises. The promises were not kept. The forger is now a camp follower. The forger knows where the contract is hidden.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{The Linns}
\emph{Skerries \& Storm‑Oaths.}

\noindent
North of the Aberderrin Sea, where fjords split the coast and skerries break the tide, dwell the Linn. They are traders and raiders in equal breath, their longships crowding the whale‑road and river mouths. They swear oaths to storm and sea – not just words, but leverage with a supernatural clause. They pay in stories, not silver, because a tale well‑told is worth more than a chest of gold.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never name a drowned person aloud while underway. The sea collects names, and if it hears you speaking one it doesn’t recognise, it may reach up to correct the ledger.”} \\
— Astrid, Net‑Wife

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Seal blubber and flatbread – the blubber is chewy, the bread is dense, and both are stored in the same barrel. The barrel is shared with the crew. The crew shares everything. Family is obligation.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To tie a knot. Linn children are given a length of rope at birth. By age five, they can tie the eight sea‑knots. The ninth knot is for emergencies – and for funerals. The ninth knot is for when the sea asks for a life.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their breath. Linn sailors chew kelp to sweeten their breath. A stranger whose breath smells of salt has been at sea. A stranger whose breath smells of copper has spoken a false oath. The sea will answer.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Oath‑breaking – but oath‑breaking is a storm. The oath‑breaker’s ship will founder. Their children will be born with webbed fingers. The sea is patient. The sea does not forget.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Sea‑Queen is not a queen. She is a figurehead – literally. Her body is carved from the keel of a ship that sank in 612 AR. Her voice is a recording. The jarls pretend. The pretending keeps the peace.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the waves. Bodies are placed in boats and set adrift, weighted with stones carved with their name. If the boat sinks before the horizon, the dead is accepted. If it returns to shore, the dead is cursed. The beaches are littered with their bones.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a storm‑oath. Stand on a cliff during a gale and swear your apology to the wind. If the wind carries your words, you are forgiven. If it throws you into the sea, you are not.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Launching of the Longships (spring thaw). The ships are dragged from their winter shelters and blessed with blood and mead. The ships are named after ancestors. The ancestors are listening. The ancestors approve.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Volva of the Mist is a fraud. She cannot see the future. She reads the weather – pressure, wind, the taste of salt on the air. The sailors know. The sailors do not care. A weather‑reader is more useful than a seer.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{Ubral}
\emph{The Stone Between Spears.}

\noindent
Ubral is a land of upland mists, stone cairns, and clan oaths held tighter than iron. Caught between the Aelerian mountains and the lowland courts of Viterra, its hills are scarred by old raids and crowned by watch‑fires. Law is written not in charters but in cattle, wergild, and songs. A guest’s word can buy more than a sword, but one broken vow can spark a feud lasting generations.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never refuse a cup from a hearth‑aunt. Hospitality is a contract sealed in salt and bread. Refusal is an insult that blood must answer.”} \\
— Hearth‑Aunt Brigid

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Oatcakes and skyr – the oats are grown in highland clearings, the skyr is cultured in stone crocks buried in the peat. The peat remembers the old kings. The old kings are buried beneath it.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count cattle. Ubral children are taught the eight breeds of the hills. The ninth breed has no colour – it belongs to the cairn‑folk. The dead do not pay taxes.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their guest‑token. A token given by their last host. A warm token means recently welcomed. A cold token means travelling long. No token means a spy. Spies are given a rope.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Cattle raiding – but raiding is a sport. A stolen cow is a stolen child. The return is a negotiation. The negotiation is a feud. The feud is a song.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Lady of Tor is not the ruler. The cairns rule. The cairns vote at each full moon. Their votes are carried by the wind. The wind speaks to the hearth‑aunts. The clan obeys.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the cairns. Their bones are added to the cairns. The cairns grow taller each year. The oldest cairn has no name. It remembers the first clan.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a wergild – payment in cattle. The cattle are driven to the cairn. If they graze peacefully, you are forgiven. If they scatter, you are not. If they refuse to enter the cairn’s shadow, your debt is blood.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Gathering of the Clans (autumn). The clans meet at the Great Cairn. The cattle are counted. The songs are sung. The feuds are settled. The dead are named. The hills remember.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Great Cairn is a prison – the tomb of a Viterran king who tried to conquer Ubral and failed. He was buried alive. His ghost votes with the cairns. He votes for war. The cairns have not yet agreed.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{Silkstrand}
\emph{City of Bridges \& Dyewater.}

\noindent
Silkstrand is a city strung across canals and arched bridges, where every crossing is a negotiation and every current carries coin. The silk looms never sleep, the Exchange never closes, and the Archivolt’s notaries seal fates with a drop of wax. The canals are not just water – they are memory. Every bribe, every broken promise, every cargo never declared – the canals remember.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never name a rival while crossing a bridge at night. The water remembers, and the curse you send may come back wrapped around your own throat.”} \\
— Bobbin‑Runner, who ties a knot for every secret she carries

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Rice porridge with dried fish – the rice is from Oshiiran barges, the fish from Zakov fleets. Both are smuggled. Both are delicious. Both are paid for in favours.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count bridges. The eight bridges of the city: Merchant’s, Weaver’s, Dyer’s, Cooper’s, Pilot’s, Beggar’s, Saint’s, and the ninth – the bridge only there at low tide. The ninth bridge leads to the Sunken Quarter, where debts go to drown.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their ink. Every Silkstrand merchant has a seal‑ink stain on their right thumb. Fresh ink means new to the trade. Faded ink means retired. No ink means not a merchant. Not a merchant is not a person.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Contract fraud – but fraud is a negotiation. Contracts are written in disappearing ink. The notaries sell the antidote. The antidote is the fee.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Matron is not the ruler. The canals rule. The canals set the prices. The canals decide who drowns. The canals are the city’s memory. The memory is the Matron’s.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the canals. Bodies are wrapped in silk and sunk in the deep channels. The channels are the city’s ledger. The ledger is never balanced.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a bridge – a toll paid to the Bridge‑Lords. The toll is calculated by the wronged party’s estimate of loss. The silver is melted and poured into the canal. The canal remembers the weight.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Dyeing of the Waters (spring). The dyers empty their vats into the canals. The water turns red, blue, green, gold – the year’s fashions. The Matron’s favourite colour is the one that sells best. It is also the colour she dyed her own hair.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The canals were dug by a Sidhi engineer paid in promises. The promises were broken. The engineer cursed the city. Every ninth drop of water is a tear. She has been crying for nine centuries.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{Midh‑Akhaz}
\emph{The Violet Gate.}

\noindent
Midh‑Akhaz is not a city. It is a wound that learned to trade. No walls, no gates – just rings of felt tents, latrine trenches, and the smell of horse sweat, cedar smoke, and the faint copper tang of blood money. The grass grows tall enough to hide a body. Ecktorian emissaries call it “the Brink.” Caravan guards call it home. Thieves call it paradise.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never refuse the guest‑cup. The first pour is courtesy. The second is negotiation. The third is a blood‑price unspoken. The fourth means you’re already dead – you just haven’t stopped breathing.”} \\
— Horse‑Braid Apprentice

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Curd cheese and flatbread – the cheese is from Ykrul herds, the bread baked in dung‑fire embers. The poor dip it in salt; the rich dip it in honey stolen from a border hive.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count horses. The eight gaits of a healthy mount. The ninth gait – the death‑sprint – is never taught. It is discovered when the rider’s life depends on it.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their braids. Ykrul warriors braid with clan colours. Vilikari traders wear oiled braids with silver beads. A stranger whose braids are loose has just fought. A stranger with no braids is a slave or a ghost. Neither stays long.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Grass‑right forgery. The Oasis Compact issues branded sticks for grazing rights. A clever carver can duplicate the brand. The penalty is exile into the tall grass at dusk. The grass has no court of appeals.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Khatun is not the only ruler. The Silver Stall stable master knows where every stolen horse sleeps. The Dust Market auctioneer knows the price of every sin. The Iron Well keeper knows the name of everyone who will die before the next grass moon. They meet once a season. The grass records it.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the grass. Bodies are left beyond the caravan rings. The grass grows tall and green where a body falls. A patch of deep violet means the dead were not properly mourned. Locals avoid those patches.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a blood‑price on the Coin‑Weigh Tribunal’s scales. If the scale tips in your favour, you are forgiven. If it balances, you owe a story – a true story that makes the wronged party weep. If it does not, you owe a finger.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Grass Moon (autumn equinox). The violet seed‑heads glow under moonlight. The tribes ride in a great circle, singing the names of the dead. At midnight, the Khatun pours water onto the earth. If it steams, the Hollow is walking. No one sleeps.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Oasis Compact’s original seal was stolen by a Vilikari factor who wanted to control water rights. The factor still lives in the Foreign Quarter under a false name. The seal is hidden in a hollow horse skull buried under the Grass Ring. The skull belonged to the Khatun’s favourite stallion. The stallion did not die of old age.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{The Ways Between}
\emph{Spiritways \& Veilways.}

\noindent
The Ways Between do not measure miles. They measure choices. A wrong turn might lead to yesterday. A right turn might lead to a version of yourself who made a different decision – and is still alive to regret it. The mist does not hide landmarks. It hides destinations.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never answer a question asked by a reflection. The answer will bind you to the path you did not choose. But not answering is also a choice. And the road is very, very patient.”} \\
— The Wayfinder

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Nothing. There is no breakfast in the Ways Between. No morning, no hunger, only the walking. The walking is sustenance.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To forget. Children born in the Ways Between do not learn. They remember the path they took to be born – eight turns, then the ninth turn is the birth. The birth is a choice. The child does not know they chose.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their direction. A stranger walking toward you may be coming from your future. A stranger walking away may be going to your past. A stranger walking beside you may be yourself from a timeline where you did not turn left. Do not ask.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Stealing – but stealing is a paradox. A thief steals a memory from a version of you that no longer exists. The memory never belonged to you. You cannot miss it. You will miss it anyway.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Ways Between are not between anything. They are the only thing that exists. The solid ground you remember – cities, roads, hearths – is the dream. The Ways are the waking. The waking is the walking. The walking never ends.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} Deeper. The Ways have no surface, no bottom. The dead walk deeper. The deeper they walk, the less they remember. When they remember nothing, they become the path. The path is the walking. The walking is the dead.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You cannot. Apologies require memory. Memory requires a self. The Ways erase both. The only apology is to stop walking. Stopping is not an option.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} There are no festivals. There is only the moment between heartbeats when you realise you have been walking for nine years and you do not remember why. That moment is the festival. Celebrate it by taking the next step.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Wayfinder is not a person. It is the road itself, dreaming of a traveller who will finally reach the end. The end does not exist. The road knows this. The road dreams anyway.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{The Wilds}
\emph{Roads, Ruins, and Weather.}

\noindent
Between towns and treaties lies the patience of the land. Old roads remember armies; cairns remember names no book keeps. Law thins to trail‑marks and favours, and the weather keeps its own counsel. Travel is a negotiation with the land itself, and the land always remembers a slight. The hollows have long memories. They are not cruel. They are not kind. They are patient.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never sleep in a hollow that has soot on the ceiling without adding your own to the fire. The land knows the difference between a guest and a thief. It prefers the corpse. Less arguing.”} \\
— Moira of Stonehaven, ranger

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Whatever they found – berries, roots, a rabbit snared at dawn. The wilds provide. The wilds also take. The taking is breakfast for something else.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To read the sky. Wilds children are taught the eight winds by taste. The ninth wind has no direction – it is the land’s own breath. The land’s breath is a warning.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their pack. Heavy pack means travelling long. Light pack means just started. No pack means a fool or a ghost. The wilds eat fools. Ghosts eat the wilds.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Forgetting – but forgetting is a survival strategy. The wilds are full of things that want to be forgotten. A traveller who forgets them may pass safely. A traveller who remembers must pay a toll – a story. The memory is the trap.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The wilds are not empty. They are full of watchers – spirits, beasts, old things with no names. They do not interfere. They wait. They remember every footprint.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} Into the land. The dead are left where they fell. The land absorbs them. The land’s memory grows longer. The dead become the land’s memory. The memory is the wilds.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You leave an offering – a coin, a button, a lock of hair. If the offering is gone by morning, you are forgiven. If it remains, you are not. If it has been moved, the land is testing you. The test is the walk. The walk is the apology.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Turning of the Leaves (autumn). The wilds change colour – red is anger, gold is warning, brown is patience, green is forgetfulness. This year the leaves are red. The land is angry. Do not ask why.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The wilds have a heart. A stone buried beneath the oldest oak. The stone is warm and beating. If it stops, the wilds die. Nothing left to remember. Nothing left to wait.
\end{almanac}

\chapter{Dungeons}
\emph{Living Infrastructure.}

\noindent
Beneath the surface, ancient places breathe with mechanical lungs and dream electric dreams. The walls remember every footfall; the air carries whispers of forgotten conversations. Dungeons are not ruins – they are systems. Ventilation shafts that breathe, water channels that pulse, doors that open without a hand. Some were built; others grew. Like fungus. Like a story told too many times.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textit{“Never ignore a door that opens when you’re not looking. The dungeon is offering you a choice. Refuse, and the next door may not open at all.”} \\
— The Dungeon’s Architect

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Fungus bread and cave water – the fungus grows on the walls, the water drips from the ceiling. Both taste of stone. Both are all there is.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count footsteps. The eight sounds of the deep: Drip, Creak, Groan, Whisper, Scrape, Echo, Silence, and the ninth – the dungeon’s own heartbeat. The heartbeat is a door opening. The door is always behind you.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Their shadow. Dungeon shadows are long, even in bright light. A shadow too short means they have been here too long. A shadow that moves independently means they have been touched by the deep. No shadow means they are not a stranger – they are the dungeon’s guest. The dungeon never lets guests leave.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Map‑making – but map‑making is heresy. The dungeon changes its corridors when it feels watched. A map that is accurate at dawn is a lie by dusk. The punishment is to walk the lie until it becomes true.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The dungeon is not a place. It is a creature that dreamed itself into stone. The corridors are its veins. The chambers are its organs. The prisoners are its thoughts. The thoughts are not its own.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} Into the walls. Bones are ground into mortar and used to patch the cracks. The cracks always reappear. The dead are never finished being used.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a memory – a true one, witnessed by the dungeon itself. The memory is added to the walls. The walls whisper it back when you sleep. The apology never ends.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Turning of the Keys (whenever the dungeon decides). Locks change, prisoners move, corridors rearrange. There is no date, no warning, no end. It is happening now. You did not notice. That is the point.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The dungeon’s architect is still alive, sealed in the lowest chamber. The dungeon does not want to be alone. The architect is its only friend. The architect has not spoken in nine centuries. The dungeon does not need words.
\end{almanac}
