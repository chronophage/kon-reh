\chapter*{Preface: Three Voices}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface}

\noindent
This book is not a travel guide. It is a collection of thresholds: bridges that remember every toll, fog-bells that count your breaths, stones that warm when you speak a true name. Three travelers have walked these roads before you. They have agreed to lend their voices to the pages that follow. Listen to each; they will disagree, and that is the point.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{Lady Isolde of the Scar, Fifth of Her Name}
\newline
\textit{``The sun shattered at Lence. The pieces are still falling. Which shadow will catch you?''}

I am Isolde, and I have worn a gorget to three tournaments, buried two brothers, and learned that chivalry is not a code -- it is a weight. You carry it or it crushes you. There is no third option. My family has held the valley north of Rouen since before the Fenwoods burned Lence. In the high season we joust; in the low season we scheme. The Belworth River is our border and our wound. I speak for Vhasia, for Viterra, for the hedge-law courts and the fractured sun. When I tell you a bridge will hold, I have counted its stones. When I warn you a lord cannot be trusted, I have seen the crack in his seal.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{The Bravo of Silkstrand (no other name, no other need)}
\newline
\textit{``Every bridge is a promise. Every canal carries a secret. But the best secrets are the ones you share over wine. And the best wine comes from the east, where the sun rises over a different sea.''}

I am nobody -- or I was, once. The canals of Silkstrand do not care about your bloodline. They care about your stroke with an oar and your aim with a dagger. I started as a dock-rat, became a duelist, and now I am something else: a witness to the city that never sleeps. The Exchange closes at midnight, but the bargains never end. I have won purses and lost friends, and I still smile. Why? Because the sun rises over the laguna every morning, and there is always another bridge to cross. I speak for Silkstrand, for the Brass Coast, for the Sidhi freedmen who taught me that freedom is a story you tell yourself until it becomes true. The eastern Dolmis taught me that some stories are too old to be told. They have to be witnessed.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{The Edgewalker (Aeler, name forgotten by design)}
\newline
\textit{``The mountain breathes. The stone remembers. I am the crack in both. And the east is the oldest crack of all.''}

I am an Aeler Edgewalker. You call us mountain-dwellers who measure breath in silver bells and seal our agreements in keystone. How quaint. We don't just measure breath -- we know the load each lung can bear. When a Tulkani chieftain swears an oath on his firstborn? That's not honor -- it's a keystone I've already wedged into three different seals. The first crack was his. The second was mine. When a Veiled Sister binds a demon? That's not terror -- it's a fault line I've traced in the bedrock. The first tremor was hers. The second is coming. You see the surface as ``full of problems.'' I see the weight in the stone, the strain in the beam. Problems are not obstacles -- they are the pressure that shapes the mountain. I speak for the Aeler, for the Mistlands, for the deep vaults where the Hollow is not a myth but a flaw in the foundation. And now, for the eastern Dolmis, where the pressure is oldest, the stone is thinnest, and the Hollow walks openly.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent
The three of us have walked these roads. We have paid our tolls -- in blood, in silver, in silence. Use the cards to find your own. When you are lost, ask: \emph{What does this place witness? And what does it ask in return?}

Then listen. The road is patient. The Hollow is patient. We are not. But we have left the lamp lit for you.

\section*{The Four Suits: A Universal Grammar}

Every card in every generator carries the same four suit-meanings:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\spadesuit Spades:} Place -- the setting or landmark
  \item \textbf{\heartsuit Hearts:} Actor -- the people or factions present
  \item \textbf{\clubsuit Clubs:} Pressure -- the complications or threats
  \item \textbf{\diamondsuit Diamonds:} Leverage -- the rewards or advantages
\end{itemize}

The rank of each card determines its scale; face cards and aces add character and omen.

\section*{Rank and Severity: The Weight of the Card}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}

\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
\textbf{Timer Conversion:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item 2--5: 4-segment timer
  \item 6--10: 6-segment timer
  \item J, Q, K: 8-segment timer
  \item A: 10-segment timer
\end{itemize}

\section*{Aces and the Ninth: The Hollow's Attention}

When an Ace appears in any draw, the Hollow takes notice. The GM gains 1 extra Story Beat (beyond any generated by 1s) to spend on a supernatural complication.

In the Eastern Dolmis, an Ace may also trigger specific regional omens:
\begin{itemize}
  \item In \textbf{Aelaerem}: The ninth cup pours itself; the empty chair is occupied.
  \item In \textbf{Aelinnel}: The ninth step appears where none should be; the Green Gate yawns.
  \item In \textbf{Valewood}: The ninth leaf falls from a tree, pointing to a threshold that was not there a moment ago.
  \item In \textbf{Zakov}: The ninth ship to enter the harbor is claimed by the reef.
  \item In \textbf{Theona}: The ninth name spoken in a circle summons the Green Host.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Procedure for Any Generator}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Name the destination.}

  \item \textbf{Set the timer.} Use the highest rank.
  \item \textbf{Interpret faces and Aces.}
  \item \textbf{Play to find out.}
\end{enumerate}

% -------------------- Aelaerem --------------------
\chapter{Aelaerem}
\emph{Hearth \& Hollow.}

\noindent
\textit{``The hollow counts your steps. The thread remembers your name. In Amedell, the Aelaerem keep their bonds with bread, bells, and careful counting. Their magic is housekeeping writ large --- thresholds groomed, names jarred like jam, the Hollow fed with silence and the ninth cup left unpoured. When the Neighbors stir beneath the barrows, the folk answer with kettle songs and lantern law, and the fields behave because someone asked them to. Hospitality here is not kindness---it is a covenant.''} \\
--- Lady Isolde of the Scar

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Barley porridge with honey from the barrow-bees. The honey tastes of earth and old promises. The poor dip their bread in cider; the rich dip it in honey stolen from a winter church.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count to eight. The ninth count is for the Hollow. A child who counts to nine may find that the Hollow is counting back.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Whether they refuse the offered cup. In Aelaerem, a refused cup is a refused covenant. The Hollow remembers refusals.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Breaking guest-right. The penalty is not a fine; it is a name added to the Hollow's tally. The name is never forgotten.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The empty chair at every feast is not for the departed. It is for the Hollow. The Hollow rarely sits. When it does, the feast becomes a funeral.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the Hollow Field. The field is a mouth. It has not fed in a century. The dead are not buried; they are offered. The offering is a prayer.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You tie a red thread around your wrist and the wronged party's. The thread is cut when the apology is accepted. If it is never cut, you are bound together. The binding is called ``family.''

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Rehanging of the Threads (autumn). Every threshold's red thread is taken down, blessed, and rehung. The village is silent for one night. The Hollow listens. The Hollow waits. The Hollow does not knock.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Apple-Matron's first cup is always poured from a barrel that was never opened. The barrel is from the year the Hollow first walked. No one knows what is inside. No one asks.
\end{almanac}

% -------------------- Aelinnel --------------------
\chapter{Aelinnel}
\emph{Stone, Bough, and Bright Things.}

\noindent
\textit{``Count the steps, weigh the words, and never trust a single telling. The Aelinnel do not build cities. They grow them -- from granite and hawthorn, from tide-polished bone and the shadow of a laugh. Their halls thread through sea-cliffs and root-galleries, their roads are counted by antler-posts that move at night, and their doors open only when you have forgotten why you came. Here, promises have weight -- not the weight of ink on parchment, but the weight of a stone dropped into a still pool. The ripples go on forever.''} \\
--- The Bravo of Silkstrand

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Sea-oat porridge with a sprinkle of copper dust -- the dust is for luck, the porridge is for strength. The poor eat it dry; the rich add honey from the inland groves.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count in primes. Two, three, five, seven -- and then the silence. Nine is not a number. Nine is a suggestion.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Whether they carry iron. Iron is not forbidden, but it is a statement. The statement is always heard. The hearing is always remembered.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Insufficient courtesy. The penalty is a lesson. The lesson is always polite. The result is never.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Green Gate opens at neap tide. It expects exact change. It does not accept coin. It accepts explanations.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the barrow galleries. The dead are arranged in patterns. The patterns are a language. The living have forgotten how to read it.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer an oath-bead. If the bead warms, you are forgiven. If it cools, you are not. If it glows, the oath was misunderstood. The misunderstanding is your fault.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Counting of the Stairs (solstice). Every citizen climbs the tide-rift steps. They count aloud. They stop at eight. The ninth step is underwater. It still counts.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Stone Prince's cat is not a cat. It is a fae auditor. It has been auditing the Prince's promises for three centuries. It has found one error. It is waiting.
\end{almanac}

% -------------------- Valewood --------------------
\chapter{Valewood}
\emph{Empire Under Leaves.}

\noindent
\textit{``The forest remembers. Speak truth, step lightly, and never eat the fruit of a tree with a face. The Valewood is older than kingdoms, older than the maps that skirt its borders. Beneath its canopy lie the bones of a Lethai empire whose name is forgotten, its towers swallowed by vine and root, its star-roads cracked and phasing between worlds. The air hums with half-remembered songs of magic, and the ruins bleed their mysteries into root and stream. To walk the Valewood is to risk being claimed by it: as guest, prey, heir---or trespasser.''} \\
--- The Edgewalker

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Mushroom bread and dewwater -- the mushrooms are foraged from the roots of the Breathing City, the dew is collected from the Glassleaf Gallery at dawn. The bread tastes of stone. The water tastes of memory.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To listen to the trees. The trees do not speak in words. They speak in hums. A child who hears the wrong hum may find themselves walking a star-road that was not there a moment ago.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Whether they have a shadow. In the Valewood, a missing shadow means the forest has already claimed you. Your body just hasn't stopped moving yet.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Name-theft. The penalty is not death; it is a memory erased. The memory is never returned. The thief wears it as a charm.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Breathing City is not dead. It is sleeping. It has been sleeping for a thousand years. It is starting to stir.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} Into the roots. The empire's dead are still walking their patrols. They do not know the empire has fallen. They do not know they are dead. They have orders.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a truce-bough. The bough is green. Plant it in the ground, and the forest will not harm you -- or help you. The forest will pretend it didn't see you. The pretending is the apology.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Phasing of the Spire (whenever the spire decides). The Valeheart Spire aligns with a star that no longer exists. The alignment opens a door that should stay closed. The festival is not celebrated; it is survived.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Genesis Keeper was not a Lethai-thora. She was one of the first beast-kin. She sealed the vats because she could not bear to see her children used as weapons. She is still sealing them. She has been sealing them for a thousand years. She is almost finished.
\end{almanac}

% -------------------- Zakov --------------------
\chapter{Zakov}
\emph{Salt \& Serpent.}

\noindent
\textit{``The tide keeps a better tally than any scribe. The reef remembers every secret. Zakov is the pirate-city of the Dolmis coast, built on the drowned bones of an Astroegro port city that sank beneath the waves when that ancient empire fell. The old stone ruins --- shattered columns, flooded plazas, sealed vaults --- lie only a few fathoms down, and at low tide you can see their roofs breaking the surface like the spines of dead leviathans. The city's streets are built on the rubble, its piers driven into the wreckage, and its deepest cellars open into sunken chambers where the water is warm and still. The Astroegro are gone, but their secrets remain: crystalline shards that hum with a terrible patience, sealed doors that weep brine, and the slow, creeping corruption that the locals call the Reaping.''} \\
--- The Bravo of Silkstrand

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Salt fish and hardtack -- the fish is from the catch that no one asks about, the hardtack is from a ship that no longer has a name. Both are stolen. Both are delicious. Both are paid for in favors.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count the tide. Low tide is for business, high tide is for hiding, slack tide is for negotiation. A child who miscounts the tide may find themselves on the wrong side of a syndicate's ledger.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Whether they taste of brine. The Reaping leaves a taste in the mouth. A stranger who tastes of brine has been in Zakov too long. A stranger who tastes of crystal has been harvested.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Weight-evasion -- not paying what you owe. The penalty is not death; it is a name added to the reef. The reef collects names. The reef is always hungry.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Salt Prince's crown is not driftwood. It is a piece of the Reaping's prison. He has not taken it off in twenty years. He cannot. It has grown into his skull. He can feel the crystal thinking.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the reef. Bodies are weighted and dropped. The reef grows. The reef is patient. The reef remembers every weight.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You offer a weight-marker. The marker is a coin. The Syndicate will honor it -- once. Then they will want it back. The wanting is the apology.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Tide-Turning (whenever the tide decides). The city changes. Streets that were dry become canals. Doors that were sealed become entrances. The festival is not celebrated; it is navigated.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Kraken's Tongue is not an oracle. She is a living fragment of the Reaping's prison. The bell in her tower is not a bell; it is a lock. She tends it with her own blood. Her blood is crystalline. It glows in the dark. She is not keeping the Reaping in. She is keeping something else out.
\end{almanac}

% -------------------- Theona --------------------
\chapter{Theona}
\emph{Three Greens, No Ninth.}

\noindent
\textit{``The green is not a color. It is a covenant. Theona has three greens --- the green of the sea, the green of the fields, and the green of the Host that rides when the ninth name is spoken. The people count in eights, pour in eights, sing in eights. The ninth is the silence. The ninth is the gap. The ninth is where the Green Host waits.''} \\
--- Lady Isolde of the Scar

\vspace{1em}
\begin{almanac}
\textbf{What do people eat for breakfast?} Oatmeal with salt -- salt is the currency of the poor, honey is the currency of the hopeful, and both are measured in eights. The ninth bowl is never filled.

\textbf{What does a child learn before they can walk?} To count the names. Every child has a list of ancestors -- eight names, no more, no less. The ninth name is the one they will earn. Or the one the Host will take.

\textbf{What is the first thing you notice about a stranger?} Whether they count in eights. A stranger who counts to nine has not been taught. A stranger who knows to stop at eight has been warned. The warning is a gift. The gift is a debt.

\textbf{What is the most common crime?} Breaking the Ninth. The penalty is not death; it is a ride with the Green Host. The ride lasts until dawn. Some riders return. They do not speak of what they saw.

\textbf{What does no one talk about but everyone knows?} The Green Host is not a curse. It is a toll. The Host collects what is owed. The Host is patient. The Host is always riding.

\textbf{Where do people go when they die?} To the barrows. The dead are placed in cairns. The cairns are built in eights. The ninth stone is left unplaced. The stone is for the Host.

\textbf{How do you apologize for a serious wrong?} You count the names. You count them aloud -- eight names, one for each generation. If the names are true, the Host will not ride for you. If a name is false, the Host will know. The Host always knows.

\textbf{What is the most important festival?} The Counting of the Wells (spring). Every village counts its ancestors aloud. The dead listen. The dead approve. The dead do not count to nine. The dead are not stupid.

\textbf{What is the secret that could destroy a powerful person?} The Matron of Wells is not the ruler. The Green Host rules. The Host is not a spirit. It is a debt that has grown teeth. The Matron is not the host's keeper. She is its accountant. She is trying to balance the books. The books are written in blood. The blood is not hers. Yet.
\end{almanac}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere: The Host Rides},breakable]
Theona is the greenest of the Dolmis isles -- three islands linked by causeways and marsh, where the grass grows tall and the wells hold the memory of the dead. The people count in eights, pour in eights, and leave the ninth cup unfilled. The Green Host rides when the ninth name is spoken, when the ninth step is taken, when the ninth stone is placed. They are not spirits. They are the interest on every promise ever made in this land. They do not hate. They collect.

To the north, the Valewood's mist-shrouded coasts send driftwood and nightmares down the current; the Green Host is said to be an echo of the forest's oldest hungers. To the west, the Aelaerem halflings of Amedell tie red threads to keep the Hollow at bay -- a custom born from the same fear of the uncounted ninth. To the east, the sea stretches toward the Kuvani and the eastern Ykrul, who have their own counting songs.

Theona's magic is the magic of omission -- leaving the ninth cup unpoured, the ninth name unspoken, the ninth stone unplaced. Its witches are well-watchers, bride-peace weavers, and taboo-keepers. They do not summon the Green Host. They remind the Host that the debt is not yet due.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of peat and salt water. The way every well has a name. The silence when someone begins to count. The green that is not a color but a covenant.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never speak the ninth name. Never pour the ninth cup. Never take the ninth step. The Host is patient. The Host is always riding.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Quick Use Notes}
\label{sec:theona-quick-use}

\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Timer (2--5 $\rightarrow$ 4, 6--10 $\rightarrow$ 6, J/Q/K $\rightarrow$ 8, A $\rightarrow$ 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (blessings/names/tokens) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo green and silence motifs -- wells that remember, the Host's riders, the ninth name that is never spoken, the silence that is the Host's voice.
\item Track the \textbf{Host's Attention [8]} timer as a campaign-wide pressure. It advances when the Ninth is broken, when the Host rides, or when a name is spoken that should not be. When full, the Host rides for you.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Thematic SB Spend Table}
\label{sec:theona-sb}

\paragraph{Minor Complications (1 SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Exposure:} Your actions draw unwanted attention from well-watchers or Host-watchers.
\item \textbf{Noise:} Sounds of your actions alert nearby taboos or bride-peace weavers.
\item \textbf{Trace:} Evidence of your passage marks your route for the Host's spies or the well's memory.
\item \textbf{Delay:} A brief but meaningful setback costs you time or favorable green.
\item \textbf{Supply Strain:} Mark +1 segment on a relevant resource timer.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Moderate Setbacks (2 SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Alarm Raised:} \textbf{Taboo-Keeper or Matron of Wells} becomes aware and begins responding.
\item \textbf{Position Lost:} You lose advantageous ground/cover/stealth due to green shift or Host's attention.
\item \textbf{Foe Appears:} A \textbf{Host's rider, well-spirit, or taboo-breaker} arrives on scene.
\item \textbf{Gear Trouble:} A piece of equipment becomes Compromised/Neglected.
\item \textbf{Lock/Barrier:} A simple obstacle now requires a test to overcome.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Serious Trouble (3 SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Reinforcements:} Additional Host riders, well-spirits, or taboo-breakers arrive.
\item \textbf{Key Gear Breaks:} A crucial tool/weapon becomes temporarily unusable.
\item \textbf{Major Twist:} The situation fundamentally changes -- the Ninth is broken, the Host rides, the well runs dry.
\item \textbf{Rail Tick:} Advance a relevant campaign/front timer by 1 segment.
\item \textbf{Condition Applied:} Mark Fatigue 1/Harm 1/Condition appropriate to fiction.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Major Turns (4+ SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Trap Springs:} A prepared danger activates with full effect.
\item \textbf{Authority Arrival:} \textbf{Matron of Wells, Three-Isles King, or Lady Beneath} intervenes.
\item \textbf{Scene Shift:} The environment changes dramatically -- the Host rides, the wells flood, the green deepens.
\item \textbf{Patron Omen:} Divine/arcane forces take notice -- omen appears/blessing lost/curse manifests.
\item \textbf{Narrative Pivot:} The story takes an unexpected turn that reframes objectives.
\end{itemize}

\begin{chapter*}{Coda: A Random Encounter (Not Really) at the Green Gate Inn}  
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Coda: A Random Encounter (Not Really) at the Green Gate Inn}  

The night air was warm and salt-laden, and the Green Gate Inn smelled of peat smoke, brine, and the faint promise of bargains that had not yet been made. The fire crackled low, casting a wavering green glow over the handful of patrons who pretended not to be counting each other's breaths.

A soft creak announced the door's opening. In stepped a woman whose green-touched shadow marked her as a child of Theona. She brushed sea salt from her cloak, set her hand on the hilt of a well-worn blade, and surveyed the room with the practiced eye of someone who had counted the stones of every bridge she'd crossed.

``Evening,'' she said, voice low but carrying. ``I'm Isolde of the Scar. Does anyone know if the road east still has that... unsettling gap where the stones whisper in eights?''

A ripple of laughter rose from the corner where a lanky figure leaned back, a grin playing at the corners of his mouth. His cloak was patched with bright dyed patches from a dozen ports. He tipped an imaginary hat.

``Name's... well, I don't really need one, but you can call me Bravo. I've been tracing the canals of Zakov's Sunken Quarter---turns out the ninth tide only appears when the reef is hungry. You looking for a shortcut, or just a story to trade for a cup? The honey here is from barrow-bees. Tastes like memory and old promises. It's the only thing that keeps the Host from riding.''

From the shadows near the hearth emerged a third figure, tall and gaunt, whose breath seemed to fog the air even as the fire licked his boots. He carried no weapon, only a small brass bell that hung from his neck, silent for now. His eyes were the color of stone-wet after a storm. He spoke in a tone that made the crackling fire sound like a distant rumble.

``I've been walking the old tunnels beneath the Breathing City,'' he said, the name barely more than a whisper. ``The empire is still writing its last equation. The vats hum. The beast-kin do not speak of what they saw. And the Hollow is patient. It has always been patient. It will always be patient. The question is not whether it will collect. The question is when.''

The trio fell into an uneasy silence, each measuring the other not with suspicion but with the quiet respect of strangers who have walked too many roads to be surprised by another traveler's tale.

Isolde glanced at the bravo's dagger, then at the bell at the Edgewalker's throat. ``You both carry... reminders.''

Bravo chuckled, tapping the hilt of his blade. ``A reminder that I still owe someone a drink.''

The Edgewalker lifted the bell just enough for it to give a faint, reluctant chime. ``And a reminder that some debts are measured in breath, not coin. In the east, the Hollow measures everything.''

The innkeeper, a stout halfling with a red thread tied around her wrist, slid three mugs across the bar without a word. The ale was dark, frothy, and smelled faintly of honey and brine --- exactly the sort of thing that made a warrior think of home, a rogue think of a clever bargain, and a stone-walker think of the weight of a promise kept.

They clinked mugs, the sound a brief, bright note against the low hum of the tavern. No grand quest was sworn, no ancient prophecy whispered. They simply sat, drank, and let the night settle into the familiar rhythm of strangers who, for a moment, shared the same fire, the same stale bread, and the same unspoken understanding that the road ahead would be just as green, just as salt, just as old, and just as full of debts as the one they'd just left behind.

When the moon rose high enough to silver the green outside, each rose, tucked their respective token---sword, dagger, bell---back into their packs, and slipped back into the night. They did not exchange names again; they did not need to. The Green Gate Inn would remember them only as the three who came in, shared a drink, and left without breaking the Ninth---exactly the sort of uneventful, perfectly ordinary encounter that makes a tavern worth its name.

\end{chapter*}
