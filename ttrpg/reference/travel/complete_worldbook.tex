\chapter*{Preface: The Ledger Bleeds}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface: The Ledger Bleeds}
\label{chap:preface}

\noindent\textit{I am an Aeler Edgewalker. You call us mountain-dwellers who measure breath in silver bells and seal contracts in stone. How adorably naïve.}

We don't just measure breath—we own the lungs that draw it. When a Tulkani chieftain swears an oath on his firstborn's life? That's not honour—it's collateral I've already hypothecated three times over. When a Veiled Sister binds a demon to a corpse to prop up her corpse-throne? That's not terror—it's a futures contract on soul-debt, and I'm the broker who sold the short position before the ritual even began.

You see the surface as "full of problems." How quaint. Problems aren't obstacles—they're inventory.

This book isn't a tourist's guide. It's a hit list with footnotes. I've marked the places where a well-placed bell doesn't just open a pass—it starts a war I've already shorted the bond market on. Where a forged seal doesn't just move a mountain—it triggers a landslide I've bought mineral rights to before the first crack appeared. Where a single true story doesn't just buy a ship's loyalty—it buys the captain's silence while I sell his manifest to pirates.

You want to build journeys? Draw your cards. Set your clocks. Spend your Story Beats like they mean something. But remember: every Diamond you earn is a tooth knocked out of someone else's jaw. Every Debt you incur? I already bought it from your victim for half what you'll pay—and I'm collecting interest in your marrow before you finish reading this sentence.

The road isn't just a ledger. It's a butcher's block. Every gate isn't a clause—it's a throat waiting for the knife.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textit{— The Edgewalker}

\chapter{Kahfagia}
\emph{The Flag of Storms.}

\noindent
{\Large
\textit{I was born on the tide, which is to say I was born owing. My mother was a net-mender who lost her fingers to a reef-cutter's debt. My father was a bell—that is to say, he rang when the Admiralty pulled his string. I learned to read the lantern codes before I learned my letters, and I learned that every light is a lie waiting to be told.}

\vspace{0.5em}

The Admiralty runs this place like a pirate charter with a budget—and oh, how they love pretending it's legitimate. They control the beacons that lure ships onto reefs, the booms that shatter hulls for salvage rights, and the tariffs that fund their champagne baths. But here's the secret the commodores don't want you to know: the lanterns are afraid of the dark too.

\vspace{0.5em}

I've seen a Mirror-Keeper weep when her reflection showed her a daughter she'd sold for a pilot's license. I've watched a Privateer Captain kiss a silver coin before a raid—the same coin her mother kissed before she never came back. This is not a city of heroes. It is a city of people who learned that the sea is cheaper than mercy.

\vspace{0.5em}

If you want to move cargo through the straits without becoming fish food, get a convoy letter stamped with the Admiral's seal. Or get smart. Or get gone.

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\textit{— Captain Mira ``the Lucky,'' Sidhi exile, pirate by convenience}

Ecktoria is an Empire that never quite died—more like a corpse propped up by gilded scaffolding and the stubborn refusal of its undertakers to admit the job's done. \vspace{0.5em}

The statues watch. The fire judges. The marble remembers every name carved into its steps—and every name left out.

\vspace{0.5em}

Do not refuse a toast in a coin-house. The ledgers are the true judges, and a declined cup is a declined credit. You will not borrow again.

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\textit{— Marcus of the Red Ledger, Coin-House Factor, third generation}

\chapter{Acasia}
\emph{The Broken Marches.}

\noindent
{\Large
\textit{We do not speak of the Curse. We speak around it, under it, through the gaps where words used to fit. The Curse is not a thing. It is the space where a thing used to be.}

\vspace{0.5em}

I am a charcoal-burner. My family has worked these woods for three generations. My grandfather could name every tree between the Pale Causeway and the Wolfstairs Pass. My father could read a deer's track in packed clay. I can tell you where the villages used to be. The map in my head has more empty spaces than marks.

\vspace{0.5em}

Acasia was not always like this. Once, the granaries held grain through winter. Once, the roads carried more than ghosts and tax collectors. Then the trees started dying. Not all at once—slowly, branch by branch, forest by forest. The Blackwood turned grey, then brittle, then silent. The farmers said it was a curse. The duke said it was blight. The merchants said it was the tariffs. The priests said it was the will of gods who had stopped listening.

\vspace{0.5em}

Nobody said it was guilt. Nobody had to. Guilt is the smell of mildew on old parchment. Guilt is the way a village watches you pass without opening its gates. Guilt is the empty chair at the feast, the unmarked grave behind the chapel, the child who will not meet your eyes because their parents told them not to speak to strangers.

\vspace{0.5em}

The truth—the one we do not speak—is that we could have saved the Blackwood. We saw the first trees wither. We saw the sap run black. We did nothing. We told ourselves it would pass. We told ourselves the duke would fix it. We told ourselves the forest was not our responsibility. And then the forest died, and the silence that followed was our answer.

\vspace{0.5em}

The Curse of Acasia is not a spell. It is a memory. The land remembers what we did—and what we failed to do. Every broken milestone on the old Imperial Road is a forgotten promise. Every abandoned vineyard is a harvest we chose not to plant. Every bridge that leads back to the same shore is the road we refused to take.

\vspace{0.5em}

Never sign a contract on a bridge. The river is a witness, and it never forgets a signature. But more than that: the bridge knows when you are lying. Not to the other party. To yourself.

\vspace{0.5em}

We are the Curse now. We carry it in our silence, in our empty fields, in the way we turn away when someone asks where the children went. Acasia is not a broken province. It is a province that broke itself, one small refusal at a time, until there was nothing left to break but the people who stayed.

\vspace{0.5em}

And still we stay. Because leaving would mean admitting we were wrong. And that is the one thing guilt cannot bear.

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\textit{— Corvin, charcoal-burner of the Blackwood, who no longer names the trees because there are too few left to name}
}

\vspace{0.5em}

Never cross the Belworth without a charter recognized on both banks. Viterran law and Vhasian claim fight every crossing, and the river takes the losers.

Beneath the surface, ancient places breathe with mechanical lungs and dream electric dreams. Dungeons are not merely ruins but living systems—vascular networks of stone and shadow where every corridor hums with purpose and every chamber holds its own hungry logic. The walls remember every footfall, the air carries whispers of forgotten conversations, and the very architecture shifts like a sleeper turning in bed. The Edgewalker believes dungeons are "infrastructure." The Scholar-Prisoner believes they are "hungry." They are both correct. Infrastructure is always hungry. It just hides it better.

\vspace{0.5em}

The secret the Dungeon's Creator does not want you to know: he did not build the dungeon. The dungeon built itself around him. He is not the architect. He is the first prisoner. And the dungeon has been adding wings ever since.

\vspace{0.5em}

Never ignore a door that opens when you're not looking. The dungeon is offering you a choice. Refuse, and the next door may not open at all. Accept, and the door may lead to a room that was always there, waiting for someone to finally walk through. The room has been waiting for nine thousand years. It is not tired of waiting. It is patient. It is very, very patient.

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\textit{— The Dungeon's Creator, whose hands are cracked with mortar and who still moves rooms the way others move chess, and who has not left the Heart Chamber in forty years because the Heart Chamber will not let him}

\chapter*{Epilogue: The Ledger Closes}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Epilogue: The Ledger Closes}

\noindent
{\Large
I closed the book. The ink was dry.

\vspace{0.5em}

I had written the final entry months ago, in a damp counting-house on the Dhaharan coast, by candlelight. My hands did not shake—they were too good for that—but I set down my abacus very carefully. I had found something in the margins. A footnote I did not remember writing. A clause I did not remember adding. The ink was the same colour as my own blood. The handwriting was my own. The date was tomorrow.

\vspace{0.5em}

I have learned to trust the things I do not remember. They are usually the truest things I know.

\vspace{0.5em}

The hat—the one the Trow had given me, the one that was still warm—sat on the corner of the desk. I picked it up, turned it over in my hands, and for the first time in years, I could not think of a single angle. That should have frightened me. Instead, it felt like a door opening. Not the kind of door you walk through. The kind of door that walks through you.

\vspace{0.5em}

The road was south. I did not know who would be waiting. I went anyway.
}

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\textit{— The Edgewalker}

\vspace{1em}

\noindent\textbf{Postscript}

The road is always south. Or north. Or whichever direction you are not facing when you read this sentence. The road does not care about cardinal points. The road cares about movement. And I am still moving.

\vspace{0.5em}

When I dream, I dream of other faces. When I wander, I feel the weights I do not owe. And, from time to time, I find myself writing in a ledger with a hand I do not recognize as my own. The hat has never cooled.

\vspace{0.5em}

The ninth entry is always the hardest to write. Not because it is the last. Because it is the one you have to mean.

\vspace{0.5em}

\noindent\textit{— The Gray Wanderer (unsigned, undated, and probably still walking)}

\section{Core Travel Procedure}
For each leg of a journey, draw 3–4 cards using the decks for your destination and controlling authority.

\begin{itemize}
\item \SuitSpade{} from the destination deck: sets the scene (place).
\item \SuitHeart{} from the destination deck: introduces the local actor or faction.
\item \SuitClub{} from the Wilds (general hazards) or destination (if strongly policed): brings pressure.
\item \SuitDiamond{} from the authority that gates the route: papers, escorts, rights, or exceptions.
\end{itemize}

Set a travel clock by the highest rank:
\begin{itemize}
\item 2–5 $\Rightarrow$ 4 segments
\item 6–10 $\Rightarrow$ 6 segments
\item J/Q/K $\Rightarrow$ 8 segments
\item A $\Rightarrow$ 10 segments
\end{itemize}

On success, advance to the next leg; on failure, mark delay, debt, or diversion and resolve a consequence in the fiction.

\section{Mode Rules}

\subsection{Sea Legs}
(Amaranthine, Dolmis, Aberderrin, Gulf of Oshiira, Inland Sea). When crossing international waters, the controlling authority is either the origin port's navy or a neutral convoy league.

\subsection{Mountain Passes \& Underways}
(Aeler, Himadri, Taharkan highlands). Any A may convert a surface route to an under-route. \SuitDiamond{} from Aeler always codifies outcomes — no roll — for sealed doors, breath time, and escorted segments.

\subsection{Desert Legs}
(Sahel, Red Desert, Fhara drylands). Water is the primary resource. A dry well hazard can end a journey.

\subsection{River Legs}
(Belworth, Ayokhan rivers, Oshiiran canals). Bridges, booms, and law in Vhasia/Viterra; reed-mazes and bell-lines in Mistlands; canal mothers and weigh-houses in Oshiira.

\subsection{Steppe Legs}
(Violet Steppe, Kuvani grazing lands). Remount stations and grazing compacts. The Ykrul and Kuvani both use story tolls.

\subsection{Shadow Corridors}
(Valewood, Ways Between, Thin Shore). Add a regional taboo: never count to nine, never speak the ninth name. A 9 in the seed triggers the Hollow's attention.

\section{Edgewalker's Notes on Leverage}
\label{sec:edgewalker-leverage}

Every Diamond you earn in these generators isn't a reward—it's a weapon. Some open doors. Some silence witnesses. Some are debts I've already bought from your victims.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Passage Tokens:} Pass-stones, harbour charters, toll waivers. Use them early—they tend to ``expire'' when the issuer has an unfortunate accident.
\item \textbf{Seals \& Stamps:} Official marks that prove you belong. A forged seal works once. A real one works until someone audits it.
\item \textbf{Story Debts:} Tulkani vow-cords, Lethai-ar witness cords, Ukwe leaves. Break them, and the Hollow visits.
\item \textbf{Spirit Objects:} Storm-charms, tide-beads, nkisi shards. They attract attention. Attention means bids.
\end{itemize}

Never use a Diamond if you can trade it for three smaller ones. Always keep a ninth—a secret, a story, a knot tied in a victim's hair—for when the toll exceeds your conscience. (It always does.)

\chapter{Story Beat (SB) Menus}
\label{chap:sbmenus}

\subsection{Universal SB Spend Menu}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{1 SB} — Shave time/position: add +1 segment to the current clock \emph{or} drop Position one step \emph{or} impose a small fee/toll.
\item \textbf{2 SB} — Escalate pressure: upgrade the active \SuitClub{} \emph{or} force a \emph{choice of Diamonds} \emph{or} tick a Front by 1.
\item \textbf{3 SB} — Lock a gate/taboo: close a route until a named \SuitDiamond{} is spent \emph{or} invoke a regional taboo.
\item \textbf{4+ SB} — Reshape the leg: rewrite the route, start a new complication clock, demand immediate payment on a Debt.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Regional SB Options}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Aeler (under-vaults):} Breath-time checks, engineer requisitions, cave-in detours.
\item \textbf{Oshiira (canals):} Audit traps, locust swarms, Widow's health failures.
\item \textbf{Ashaan (Veil):} Spirit-dust storms, bound spirits breaking free, Black Hand sweeps.
\item \textbf{Tulkani (wagon circles):} Debt collectors, Hollow walks on the ninth night, vow-cords tangling.
\item \textbf{Lethai-ar (bridges):} Snapped strands, web-law challenges, the ninth echo.
\item \textbf{Aelinnel (bells):} Miscounts, offended tide-spirits, bell-lines breaking.
\end{itemize}
