\chapter*{Preface: The Ledger Bleeds}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface: The Ledger Bleeds}
\label{chap:preface}

I am an Aeler Edgewalker. You call us mountain-dwellers who measure breath in silver bells and seal contracts in stone. How adorably naïve. We don’t just measure breath—we own the lungs that draw it. When a Tulkani chieftain swears an oath on his firstborn’s life? That’s not honour—it’s collateral I’ve already hypothecated three times over. When a Veiled Sister binds a demon to a corpse to prop up her corpse-throne? That’s not terror—it’s a futures contract on soul-debt, and I’m the broker who sold the short position before the ritual even began. You think I seek opportunities? Incorrect. I manufacture them like counterfeit coin—then sell the molds, the hammer, and the anvil to the very fools I’m about to rob.

You see the surface as ``full of problems.'' How quaint. Problems aren’t obstacles—they’re inventory. Wars need walls? I’ll sell you the bricks and the siege engine to knock them down—then buy back the rubble at scrap price to fill the next crater I create. Cities need bridges? I’ll underbid the architect by bribing his estranged sister with forged inheritance papers, then charge tolls for the detour while my men ``accidentally’’ weaken the supports with acid-laced mortar. Every famine isn’t tragedy—it’s a grain futures play where I corner the market before the locusts hatch. Every plague isn’t sorrow—it’s a chance to monopolise breath-bonds by spreading the rumour that only my seals ward off the Hollow (they don’t—I just charge extra for the ``premium’’ version). The world isn’t a ledger—it’s a slaughterhouse where I’m the only one bringing a price gun, a bone saw, and a ledger balanced in blood.

I’ve walked roads paved with more broken promises than cobblestones. I’ve drunk the ninth cup in a Tulkani circle while counting the missing step on an Aelinnel bridge (it was step~8.7—always shortchange the superstitious; they’ll pay triple for ``lucky’’ step~9 that doesn’t exist). I’ve haggled with Fhara water-clerks not over price, but by threatening to divert their qanats into the salt flats unless they threw in a concubine and a sworn statement that I ``inspired their generosity.'' I’ve sold steam-driven capstans to Ngomebe’s walking cities after sabotaging their rollers with ground glass—then charged triple for the ``emergency’’ repair crew I dispatched from my own warehouse. I’ve watched the Veiled Sisters smile as they signed away their daughters’ souls in exchange for a single night’s respite from the Black Hand’s whispers, and I’ve seen that shadow fall exactly where I’d planted the forged seal that started their feud three months prior—then auctioned the ``exclusive’’ intel on the feud’s origin to both sides for double the fee.

Here’s what the ledger won’t admit, because it’s too busy counting angels on pins: Leverage isn’t about fine print. It’s about who’s holding the knife when the ink dries—and whose throat it’s resting on.

A Diamond isn’t currency—it’s a hostage with a pulse. Spend it, and you’re renting violence. A Debt isn’t owed—it’s farmed. I don’t collect; I cultivate. Plant a promise in fertile despair, water it with regret, harvest the interest in screams. A Promise? That’s just debt wearing a smile and holding a dagger behind its back. The sweeter the vow, the sharper the blade.

This book isn’t a tourist’s guide. It’s a hit list with footnotes. I’ve marked the places where a well-placed bell doesn’t just open a pass—it starts a war I’ve already shorted the bond market on. Where a forged seal doesn’t just move a mountain—it triggers a landslide I’ve bought mineral rights to before the first crack appeared. Where a single true story doesn’t just buy a ship’s loyalty—it buys the captain’s silence while I sell his manifest to pirates. I’ve also marked the traps you’re supposed to avoid: the ninth step you \emph{must} count (it’s a pressure plate wired to explode—I’ll sell you the schematics and the defusal kit for 200\% markup), the ninth cup you \emph{must} pour (it’s laced with truth-serum that makes you confess where you buried the loot—I’ll sell you the emetic and a fake ledger to replace the real one), the ninth name you \emph{must} speak (it summons the Hollow—I’ll sell you earplugs lined with soul-trapping wax and a virgin sacrifice ``at cost'').

You want to build journeys? Draw your cards. Set your clocks. Spend your Story Beats like they mean something. But remember this, written in the margin of every contract I’ve ever signed: Every Diamond you earn is a tooth knocked out of someone else’s jaw. Every Debt you incur? I already bought it from your victim for half what you’ll pay—and I’m collecting interest in your marrow before you finish reading this sentence. My notes on leverage aren’t advice. They’re a confession. A brag. A warning written in the language of slaughter. Read them. Then decide: patch the cracks in the world\ldots or hand me the dynamite, the detonator, and a list of whose foundations need weakening first.

The road isn’t just a ledger. It’s a butcher’s block. Every gate isn’t a clause—it’s a throat waiting for the knife. Pay the toll, or I’ll collect interest in the form of your last breath\ldots and sell it back to you as a breath-bond.

— The Edgewalker\\
(Current location: Somewhere the ink hasn’t dried yet. Thimble’s still trying to balance books in a ditch outside Aksum using a rock and his own tears. Pathetic. Pass the brandy—I’ve got a drought to monetise in Sekogo.)

[Thimble: Sir, per Appendix Gamma of the Ledger Ethics Accord—]

[Edgewalker: Thimble. If you quote that accursed appendix one more time, I’ll bind your soul to a ledger page and use it to wipe my boots after trampling through the Veiled Sisters’ latest soul-auction. Now pass the abacus—I need to calculate how many souls fit in a single breath-bond before the next monsoon.]

[Thimble: But sir, the Accord—]

[Edgewalker: (Standing, chair scraping) Thimble. You’re fired. Your final paycheck is in the ledger\ldots under ``Organic Debt, Settled via Soul Harvest.'' Now leave before I amend the entry to ``Thimble’s Ledger: Used as Kindling for Veiled Sister Bonfires.'' And take your tears with you—they’re cluttering up the depreciation column.]

\part{The Amaranthine Sea \& Northern Shores}

\chapter{Kahfagia}
\emph{The Flag of Storms.} 
\label{chap:kahfagia}
The Admiralty runs this place like a pirate charter with a budget—and oh, how they love pretending it’s legitimate. They control the beacons that lure ships onto reefs, the booms that shatter hulls for salvage rights, and the tariffs that fund their champagne baths. If you want to move cargo through the straits without becoming fish food, you need a convoy letter stamped with their seal or a lantern-law warrant signed by the Port Marshal himself. \textit{I’ve sold them both}—genuine articles, straight from the source—then, while the Admiral was busy recounting spice taxes he’d already stolen from my last shipment (the bastards shorted me 12\% on saffron, claiming ``moisture loss’’ in holds I’d sealed with my own breath-wax), I sold perfect forgeries to three rival fleets: the Crimson Corsairs, the Jade Junks, and a suspiciously well-funded group of monks who definitely weren’t pirates. The trick? I didn’t just forge the seal—I made the letters appear to come from the Admiral’s long-lost brother, Lord Malakai, who supposedly vanished in a diving accident ten years ago. (Spoiler: I ``found’’ him running a rum distillery in the Mistlands. Paid him in trade goods to stay ``dead.'') The Admiralty never checks the seals’ provenance. Why would they? They’re too busy being paranoid that their own forgeries will be discovered—too busy counting the very spice taxes they stole from me to notice I’m playing three sides against the middle while their convoys sink each other.

[Thimble: Sir, Article 4, Section 2 of the Maritime Trust Compact explicitly prohibits—]

[Edgewalker: Thimble, my dear, trembling ledger-licker, the Maritime Trust Compact is worth less than the parchment it’s forged on—especially when I’ve bought the ink-maker’s silence with a lifetime supply of spice that \textit{isn’t} moist. Now, about that lantern-law warrant\ldots the Admiral’s nephew has a gambling problem. I wonder what his marker looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, exploiting familial debt violations under Clause 9.1—]

[Edgewalker: Familial debt is just leverage with a family tree. Now pass the brandy—I’ve got a war to start in Ecktoria, and Thimble? If you mention ``Clause 9.1’’ again, I’ll sell your ledger to the monks as kindling. They burn hot.]

\chapter{Ecktoria}
\emph{Marble \& Ledgers.}
\label{chap:ecktoria}
The Empire that never quite died—more like a corpse propped up by gilded scaffolding and the stubborn refusal of its undertakers to admit the job’s done. Ecktoria’s coin-houses still clutch Utaran seals like talismans against progress, every bridge groans under a toll booth staffed by cousins of the governor’s third wife, and the Everflame burns in every basilica not as a symbol of divine truth, but as a literal lie detector: swear a false oath near it, and your tongue turns black. Clever. Very clever. I sold them a breath-bond once—a beautiful thing, really—to guarantee a grain shipment from Oshiira. The bond was sealed with the sigh of a dying Aeler miner (ethically sourced, of course), promised to pay double if the ship arrived intact. The ship sank. Not by storm, not by pirates—but because I’d paid the stevedore to ``accidentally’’ drop a barrel of quicklime into the hold mid-voyage. The Ecktorians were furious. They dragged me before the Everflame, shrieking about breach of contract. I smiled, bowed, and reminded them, voice honey-smooth: ``I sold you the bond, Captain. Not the insurance. Not the seaworthiness of the vessel. Not even the promise that the stevedore wouldn’t be bribed by a man with a grudge against Oshiira’s grain inspectors.” Then, while they were still spluttering, I bought the wreckage rights for three coppers and a sworn affidavit from the captain’s mistress that he’d been planning to scuttle the ship anyway. Leverage isn’t about fine print—it’s about having the buyer sign the waiver while they’re still choking on seawater.

[Thimble: Sir, Article 17 of the Maritime Salvage Code requires—]

[Edgewalker: Salvage code? Thimble, sweet summer child, salvage code is for losers who don’t own the reef before the ship hits it. I bought the reef’s mineral rights six months ago—paid in promises to the governor’s mistress that I’d make her a widow. Now, about that bond\ldots the Oshiira inspectors? They have a lovely daughter. I wonder what her dowry looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, exploiting familial ties violates—]

[Edgewalker: Familial ties are just leverage with a heartbeat. Now pass the abacus—I need to calculate how many drowned sailors fit in a single breath-bond before the Everflame notices the soot’s fake.]

\chapter{Acasia}
\emph{The Broken Province.}
\label{chap:acasia}
Silkstrand isn’t just the only real city here—it’s a festering wound of commerce, where bridges collapse under the weight of dye vats, hired swords change loyalty with the moon, and the Curse of the Blackwood isn’t some ancient superstition—it’s the result of me selling the Duke’s forest manager ``eternal’’ fire-resistant sap that was actually just fermented sap and a slow-acting poison. The trees died. The duke blamed ``the Curse.'' I blamed bad forestry. Everybody won. I sold the Dyers’ Guild a system of copper bells to warn of fires—not because I cared about their livelihoods, but because I knew their vats would boil over when I secretly raised the temperature in their underground chambers via redirected geothermal vents. They paid in silk—nine bolts of the deep indigo that only grows in the Blackwood’s shadow, the kind that makes Veiled Sisters weep with envy. I traded it to a Tulkani caravan for a story that later saved my skin: how to walk through a veil without screaming. That’s the Acasian way: every negotiation leaves a stain, every stain is a bargaining chip, and every chip is just leverage waiting to be cashed in.

[Thimble: Sir, the Dyers’ Guild Charter explicitly forbids tampering with geothermal vents—]

[Edgewalker: Thimble, my anxious little bean-counter, the Charter’s only worth the parchment it’s printed on—especially when I’ve bought the guild’s scribe’s silence with a bolt of that indigo silk and a promise to make his rival ``disappear.'' Now, about those copper bells\ldots the foundry owner has a gambling debt to the Black Banner Captain. I wonder what his marker looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, Clause 3 of the Guild’s Labor Accord—]

[Edgewalker: Labor accord? Thimble, darling, labor’s just leverage with a union card. Now pass the brandy—I’ve got a dye war to start in Vhasia, and if you mention ``Clause 3’’ one more time, I’ll sell your ledger to the Blackwood’s wraiths as kindling. They prefer it damp.]

\chapter{Vhasia}
\emph{The Fractured Sun.}
\label{chap:vhasia}
Two crowns. One kingdom. A river so wide it might as well be a border—and oh, how the crowns love pretending it’s not. The Northern Seigneurie and the Southern Parlement both claim the throne, both flood the river with paperwork to ``prove’’ their right, and both are too busy arguing over seal wax colours to notice I’m the one who made the river this wide in the first place. I’ve forged more letters patent for them than I can count—not just for fun, but to keep their lawyers billing by the hour while my convoys cross under flags of truce I also forged. The trick? I don’t just use real seals on false parchment—I leak the ``forgery’’ to the opposing crown first. Let them spend weeks screaming about signature smudges and ink bleed while my grain ships glide past, their bills of lading stamped with seals so real they make the ink weep. The Last Dauphin? A rumour. But rumours pay well when you’re the one who started them—and I started three: that the Dauphin was swapped at birth with a goatherd’s child, that he’s secretly a Veiled Sister in disguise, and that he’s been dead for ten years, his ``reappearance’’ a puppet show run by the Seigneurie’s spymaster. Each rumour paid in different currencies: secrets, silk, and the right to walk through a certain gate at midnight.

[Thimble: Sir, deliberate misinformation breaches Article 1 of the Accord of Transparent Governance—]

[Edgewalker: Transparent governance? Thimble, my dear, trembling fool, transparent governance is for people who enjoy losing money—and I’ve got shares in the Seigneurie’s ink-makers. Now, about that pass-stone\ldots the Seigneur’s heir has a lovely secret. I wonder what her weight in silver looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, exploiting royal secrets violates—]

[Edgewalker: Royal secrets are just leverage with a crown. Now pass the abacus—I need to calculate how many forged letters fit in a single Diamond before the Parlement notices their ink’s fake.]

\chapter{Viterra}
\emph{The Hedge‑Law Realm.}
\label{chap:viterra}
Viterra doesn’t just run on precedent—it \emph{is} precedent, a tangled thicket of rulings where every hedge has a lawsuit attached, every parish stone a deed dispute, and every ferry crossing a generations-long feud over who gets to charge the toll. The Warrior Queen isn’t just a ruler—she’s the living embodiment of ``possession is nine-tenths of the law,’’ and she pays in customs seals like they’re going out of style. Which they are—thanks to me. I sold her a set of Aeler breath-counters for her spies—not because I trusted her, but because I knew she’d use them to sniff out traitors in her court\ldots traitors I’d planted myself. She paid in three customs seals, each valid for a year, each stamped with the sigil of a port I don’t even use. I’ve used two. The third? For emergencies. Like when I need to cross the Belworth without paying the tariff—and without the Queen’s spies noticing I’ve rerouted the ferry cables through my private toll booth three miles downstream. (The Queen’s still paying me in seals for that ``emergency’’—she thinks it’s a loyalty test. How precious.)

[Thimble: Sir, Article 12 of the Customs Seal Accord prohibits—]

[Edgewalker: Customs seal accord? Thimble, sweet pea, customs seals are just leverage with a fancy stamp—especially when I’ve bought the Queen’s head spy’s silence with a breath-counter that doesn’t count breaths\ldots it counts lies. Now, about that Belworth crossing\ldots the ferryman has a daughter who owes the Black Hand a favour. I wonder what her weight in secrets looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, Clause 7 of the Ferryman’s Guild—]

[Edgewalker: Ferryman’s guild? Thimble, darling, guilds are just leverage with a membership card. Now pass the brandy—I’ve got a hedge war to start in Aeler, and if you mention ``Clause 7’’ one more time, I’ll sell your ledger to the Belworth’s currents as kindling. They prefer it weighted.]

\chapter{Thepyrgos}
\emph{City of a Thousand Stairs.}
\label{chap:thepyrgos}
The Synod rules here with the delicacy of a brick wrapped in velvet—they hate unsanctioned magic like it’s a personal insult, but they adore infrastructure, especially when it’s shiny, expensive, and comes with a side of indulgences they can sell to the faithful. I sold them a system of under-vaults to expand the Library of Keys—not because I believed in knowledge, but because I knew the Synod’s Archivist was drowning in debt to a Veiled Sister’s loan shark, and he’d sign anything to make the collector go away. The Archon paid in harbour passes—enough to skip every boom in the straits for a decade—stamped with the Synod’s seal and blessed by three bishops who definitely weren’t bribed with contraband incense. I’ve already lost two to card games (damn those Tulkani bluffers), but the rest? Hidden in a lead box under a rock marked with a bell\ldots a bell I rang myself to make the rock ``holy’’ in the eyes of the local cult that now guards it for a fee. You want them? They’re yours. For a price. A very specific price involving the Synod’s Archivist and a certain ledger he’s been ``misplacing.’’

[Thimble: Sir, the Synod’s Charter on Sacred Architecture forbids—]

[Edgewalker: Sacred architecture? Thimble, my anxious little goblin, sacred architecture is just leverage with a stained-glass window—especially when I’ve bought the Archivist’s silence with a ledger that doesn’t track debts\ldots it creates them. Now, about those harbour passes\ldots the Synod’s treasurer has a gambling problem. I wonder what his marker looks like in uncut diamonds?]

[Thimble: Sir, exploiting ecclesiastical debt violates—]

[Edgewalker: Ecclesiastical debt is just leverage with a halo. Now pass the abacus—I need to calculate how many Synod indulgences fit in a single Diamond before the Archivist notices his ledger’s fake.]

\chapter{Aeler}
\emph{Breath, Bell, and Keystone.}
\label{chap:aeler}
My homeland. The place where we don’t trust the surface, but we do trust the weight of a well-placed keystone—and oh, how the surface loves to test that trust. We Aeler don’t just trade with the mountains; we own the leverage in every crevice. Our breath-bonds aren’t just currency—they’re the hardest thing in the peaks, forged in the sighs of miners who knew too much. I’ve sold them to Vikari jarls who paid in furs stained with brother’s blood, to Ykrul khagans who weighed the value in warhorses and broken oaths, even to a Veiled Sister once—she paid in a secret so dangerous I’ve never used it\ldots yet. (Spoiler: I’m saving it for the day the Ninth Seal cracks. The interest alone will buy me a small kingdom.) The under-vaults aren’t just storage—they’re libraries of opportunity, full of seals just waiting to be broken. I know all of them. Not because I’m noble—but because I’ve sold most of them already. That’s why I don’t go back often. Why revisit a scene when you can sell the blueprints to the next fool who thinks he’s clever?

[Thimble: Sir, the Aeler Vault Accord Article 3 explicitly—]

[Edgewalker: Vault accord? Thimble, my dear, trembling mole, the Vault Accord is just leverage with a rock hammer—especially when I’ve bought the Chief Stonewright’s silence with a keystone that doesn’t reinforce tunnels\ldots it weakens them. Now, about those breath-bonds\ldots the Vikari jarl’s son has a lovely gambling habit. I wonder what his weight in wolf pelts looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, Clause 9 of the Stonewright’s Guild—]

[Edgewalker: Stonewright’s guild? Thimble, darling, guilds are just leverage with a chisel. Now pass the brandy—I’ve got a seal war to start in Mistlands, and if you mention ``Clause 9’’ one more time, I’ll sell your ledger to the Hollow as kindling. It prefers it whispering.]

\chapter{The Mistlands}
\emph{Bells, Salt, and Breath.}
\label{chap:mistlands}
A protectorate of the Aeler in name only—the fog here doesn’t just hide the land; it hides the law, and oh, how the wraiths love that ambiguity. The bell-lines aren’t just defences—they’re the only thing keeping the wraiths from dragging whole villages into the mist, and I sold the wardens a tuning fork that could recalibrate those bells to a frequency that makes the wraiths dance instead of drag. They paid in salt—enough to preserve a ship’s cargo for a year, stamped with the Mistlands’ seal and guaranteed by the Warden-Captain’s own oath. I used it on a shipment of Sidhi pepper\ldots pepper that was cursed, yes, but curses are just leverage with a supernatural clause. I sold it anyway—at a markup—to a Tulkani caravan desperate for ``exotic’’ goods, then bought back the cursed pepper’s ``cure’’ from the same caravan a week later when they started hallucinating veils that weren’t there. Beautiful.

[Thimble: Sir, the Mistlands Warden’s Oath forbids—]

[Edgewalker: Warden’s oath? Thimble, my sweet little fog-lung, oaths are just leverage with a shiver—especially when I’ve bought the Warden-Captain’s silence with a tuning fork that doesn’t recalibrate bells\ldots it attracts wraiths. Now, about that Sidhi pepper\ldots the caravan leader has a daughter who owes the Veiled Sisters a favour. I wonder what her weight in secrets looks like?]

[Thimble: Sir, exploiting familial debt under—]

[Edgewalker: Familial debt is just leverage with a heartbeat. Now pass the abacus—I need to calculate how many cursed peppers fit in a single Diamond before the wardens notice their salt’s fake.]

\chapter{Ykrul}
\emph{Wolf Standards, Winter Camps.}
\label{chap:ykrul}
The Ykrul don’t measure wealth in silver—they measure it in hoofbeats, in the snort of a warhorse at dawn, and oh, how they love a good bargain\ldots especially when it comes with a side of broken trust. I sold them Aeler saddles with built-in breath-counters—not because I cared about their mounts, but because I knew the khagan’s head trainer was skimming oats from the imperial stables, and he’d sell his soul to keep it quiet. The khagan was impressed. He paid in a paiza—a tablet of hammered bronze that guarantees safe passage through all Ykrul lands, stamped with the wolf standard and blessed by the shaman who definitely wasn’t bribed with fermented mare’s milk. I’ve used it to cross the Violet Steppe nine times. The ninth time? The paiza cracked. Not from wear—from my tampering with the alloy during the ``quality check.’’ I’m still trying to collect on the warranty\ldots by selling the khagan a new paiza at triple the price, claiming the crack was ``a sign from the spirits’’ that he needs to upgrade. (The spirits agreed—for a fee.)

\chapter{Global Travel Procedures}
\label{chap:globaltravel}

\section{Core Travel Procedure}
For each leg of a journey, draw 3–4 cards using the decks for your destination and controlling authority.

\begin{itemize}
\item \SuitSpade{} from the destination deck: sets the scene (place).
\item \SuitHeart{} from the destination deck: introduces the local actor or faction.
\item \SuitClub{} from the Wilds (general hazards) or destination (if strongly policed): brings pressure.
\item \SuitDiamond{} from the authority that gates the route: papers, escorts, rights, or exceptions.
\end{itemize}

Set a travel clock by the highest rank:
\begin{itemize}
\item 2–5 $\Rightarrow$ 4 segments
\item 6–10 $\Rightarrow$ 6 segments
\item J/Q/K $\Rightarrow$ 8 segments
\item A $\Rightarrow$ 10 segments
\end{itemize}

On success, advance to the next leg; on failure, mark delay, debt, or diversion and resolve a consequence in the fiction.

\section{Mode Rules}

\subsection{Sea Legs}
(Amaranthine, Dolmis, Aberderrin, Gulf of Oshiira, Inland Sea). When crossing international waters, the controlling authority is either the origin port’s navy or a neutral convoy league.

\subsection{Mountain Passes \& Underways}
(Aeler, Himadri, Taharkan highlands). Any A may convert a surface route to an under‑route. \SuitDiamond{} from Aeler always codifies outcomes — no roll — for sealed doors, breath time, and escorted segments.

\subsection{Desert Legs}
(Sahel, Red Desert, Fhara drylands). Water is the primary resource. A dry well hazard can end a journey.

\subsection{River Legs}
(Belworth, Ayokhan rivers, Oshiiran canals). Bridges, booms, and law in Vhasia/Viterra; reed‑mazes and bell‑lines in Mistlands; canal mothers and weigh‑houses in Oshiira.

\subsection{Steppe Legs}
(Violet Steppe, Kuvani grazing lands). Remount stations and grazing compacts. The Ykrul and Kuvani both use story tolls.

\subsection{Shadow Corridors}
(Valewood, Ways Between, Thin Shore). Add a regional taboo: never count to nine, never speak the ninth name. A 9 in the seed triggers the Hollow’s attention.

\section{Edgewalker’s Notes on Leverage}
\label{sec:edgewalker-leverage}
Every Diamond you earn in these generators isn’t a reward—it’s a weapon. Some open doors. Some silence witnesses. Some are debts I’ve already bought from your victims. I’ve categorised the most useful ones below—not as advice, but as a field manual for predators who understand that the world runs on desperation, not honour:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{\textcolor{feAccent}{Passage Tokens:}} Pass‑stones, harbour charters, toll waivers. These let you move without paying the tariff. Use them early—they tend to ``expire’’ when the issuer has an unfortunate accident involving a cliff, a rival, or a sudden lack of heirs.
\item \textbf{\textcolor{feAccent}{Seals \& Stamps:}} Official marks that prove you belong. A forged seal works once—until someone notices the corpse in the watermark or the signature matches a known forger. A real one works until someone audits it\ldots which I prevent by selling the auditor a ``permanent vacation’’ in the Mistlands where the wraiths appreciate quiet clients.
\item \textbf{\textcolor{feAccent}{Story Debts:}} Tulkani vow‑cords, Lethai‑ar witness cords, Ukwe leaves. These are promises. Break them, and the Hollow visits. Good. Let it collect the interest—I’ve already sold the debt to a Debt‑Walker for twice the value, then bought the Debt‑Walker’s contract for three times that when they defaulted.
\item \textbf{\textcolor{feAccent}{Spirit Objects:}} Storm‑charms, tide‑beads, nkisi shards. These interact with the unseen world. They also attract attention. Perfect. Attention means bids. I’ll sell you the charm, then the exorcism, then the real estate where the spirit used to live\ldots at a premium for ``haunted adjacent’’ properties.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{My advice (which you’ll ignore until it’s too late, and then come begging for):}

Never use a Diamond if you can trade it for three smaller ones. Liquidity isn’t just power—it’s the difference between eating and being eaten. Always keep a ninth—a secret, a story, a knot tied in a victim’s hair, a whisper in a dead man’s ear—for when the toll exceeds your conscience. (Spoiler: It always does. Conscience is just leverage with a guilt complex.) If you feel guilty? Good. Guilt means you’re leaving money on the table—and I’ve got a buyer for that table.

\chapter{Story Beat (SB) Menus}
\label{chap:sbmenus}

\subsection{Universal SB Spend Menu}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{1 SB} — Shave time/position: add +1 segment to the current clock \emph{or} drop Position one step \emph{or} impose a small fee/toll.
\item \textbf{2 SB} — Escalate pressure: upgrade the active \SuitClub{} \emph{or} force a \emph{choice of Diamonds} \emph{or} tick a Front by 1.
\item \textbf{3 SB} — Lock a gate/taboo: close a route until a named \SuitDiamond{} is spent \emph{or} invoke a regional taboo.
\item \textbf{4+ SB} — Reshape the leg: rewrite the route, start a new complication clock, demand immediate payment on a Debt.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Regional SB Options}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Aeler (under‑vaults):} Breath‑time checks, engineer requisitions, cave‑in detours.
\item \textbf{Oshiira (canals):} Audit traps, locust swarms, Widow’s health failures.
\item \textbf{Ashaan (Veil):} Spirit‑dust storms, bound spirits breaking free, Black Hand sweeps.
\item \textbf{Tulkani (wagon circles):} Debt collectors, Hollow walks on the ninth night, vow‑cords tangling.
\item \textbf{Lethai‑ar (bridges):} Snapped strands, web‑law challenges, the ninth echo.
