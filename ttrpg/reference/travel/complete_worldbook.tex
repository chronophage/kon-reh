\chapter*{Preface: The Ledger Bleeds}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Preface: The Ledger Bleeds}
\label{chap:preface}

\noindent\textit{I am an Aeler Edgewalker. You call us mountain-dwellers who measure breath in silver bells and seal contracts in stone. How adorably naïve.}

We don't just measure breath---we own the lungs that draw it. When a Tulkani chieftain swears an oath on his firstborn's life? That's not honor---it's collateral I've already hypothecated three times over. When a Veiled Sister binds a demon to a corpse to prop up her corpse-throne? That's not terror---it's a futures contract on soul-debt, and I'm the broker who sold the short position before the ritual even began.

You see the surface as ``full of problems.'' How quaint. Problems aren't obstacles---they're inventory.

This book isn't a tourist's guide. It's a hit list with footnotes. I've marked the places where a well-placed bell doesn't just open a pass---it starts a war I've already shorted the bond market on. Where a forged seal doesn't just move a mountain---it triggers a landslide I've bought mineral rights to before the first crack appeared. Where a single true story doesn't just buy a ship's loyalty---it buys the captain's silence while I sell his manifest to pirates.

You want to build journeys? Draw your cards. Set your timers. Spend your Story Beats like they mean something. But remember: every Diamond you earn is a tooth knocked out of someone else's jaw. Every Debt you incur? I already bought it from your victim for half what you'll pay---and I'm collecting interest in your marrow before you finish reading this sentence.

The road isn't just a ledger. It's a butcher's block. Every gate isn't a clause---it's a throat waiting for the knife.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textit{--- The Edgewalker}

\chapter*{The Cartomancer's Compass}
\label{chap:cartomancer-compass}

\begin{quote}
\textit{``The world does not speak in words. It speaks in suits, ranks, and the spaces between. Every bridge, every bribe, every broken oath can be laid out on a table of felt and dealt from a worn deck. This is not superstition. It is method.''}\\
\hfill --- Mira of the Wandering Circle, Tulkani cartomancer
\end{quote}

\section*{A Tulkani Perspective on the Art}

I was seven when my grandmother first placed a deck in my hands. Not a new deck---a worn one, its edges soft as silk, its faces faded by a hundred thousand shuffles. ``This is your first threshold,'' she said. ``Learn to read it, and you will never be lost.''

The Tulkani do not build temples. We build fires. We do not carve scriptures into stone; we let the cards tell us what the road has seen. Cartomancy is our witch-work, our way of reading the Echo (what has been), steadying the Veil (what is), and leaning into Flow (what wishes to be). It is not fortune-telling. It is witnessing---a conversation with the world's memory, conducted through the language of suits and ranks.

The other peoples of Fate's Edge have their own names for what we do. The Aeler call us ``thread-readers'' and hire us to audit their ledgers. The Ykrul call us ``bone-speakers'' and consult us before every migration. The Ecktorian Inquisition calls us heretics and burns our decks when they find them. We keep shuffling.

This chapter is my gift to the road. It explains how to read the cards as we do---not as a tool for controlling fate, but as a way of listening to what the world has already decided to tell you.

\section*{The Four Suits: A Universal Grammar}

Every card in every generator carries the same four suit-meanings. They are the alphabet of the world's witness. In the Tulkani tradition, we say the suits are the four breaths of the world:

\subsection*{$\spadesuit$ Spades: Place --- The Stage}

Where does the scene happen? $\spadesuit$ is the threshold, the landmark, the ground beneath the action. It answers: \textit{Where are we?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (10--A):} A major landmark---a fortress, a capital, a haunted ruin. The place has a name that echoes in local songs.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6--9):} A functional location---a ford, a market, a shrine. The place has a purpose and a keeper.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2--5):} A liminal or neglected place---a crossroads, a collapsed bridge, a smuggler's cove. The place is defined by what it lacks.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, $\spadesuit$ entries are always places. See, for example, the bridges and canals of Silkstrand or the cairns and passes of Ubral.

\subsection*{$\heartsuit$ Hearts: Actor --- The Face}

Who drives the scene? $\heartsuit$ is the person, the faction, the voice that speaks. It answers: \textit{Who is here?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (J--A):} An elite, a ruler, a power-broker. The actor has authority and reach beyond the immediate scene.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6--10):} A professional, a functionary, a skilled operator. The actor has a trade and a price.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2--5):} A commoner, an outcast, a victim. The actor needs something or has been failed by someone.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, $\heartsuit$ entries are always people or factions. See the merchants and spies of the Brass Coast or the spirit-wardens of the Mistlands.

\subsection*{$\clubsuit$ Clubs: Pressure --- The Complication}

What pushes back? $\clubsuit$ is the storm, the burden, the knife in the dark. It answers: \textit{What goes wrong?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (10--A):} A catastrophe, a betrayal, an invasion. The complication changes the shape of the journey.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6--9):} A serious obstacle---a feud, a quarantine, a broken bridge. The complication costs time, resources, or reputation.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2--5):} A minor hazard---a sudden squall, a lost token, a raised toll. The complication is a nuisance, not a disaster.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, $\clubsuit$ entries are always complications or threats. See the river floods and witness-cord cuts of Ayokha or the market riots of Silkstrand.

\subsection*{$\diamondsuit$ Diamonds: Leverage --- The Reward}

What opens the door? $\diamondsuit$ is the token, the writ, the secret that changes Position. It answers: \textit{What do you gain?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High rank (J--A):} A major asset---a noble title, a keystone charter, a general pardon. The reward reshapes the party's standing.
    \item \textbf{Mid rank (6--10):} A significant tool---a pass-stone, a witness cord, a lighthouse charter. The reward solves a specific class of problem.
    \item \textbf{Low rank (2--5):} A minor advantage---a bridge token, a guest-cup, a rumor. The reward grants a single use of improved Position.
\end{itemize}

In the regional chapters, $\diamondsuit$ entries are always rewards or leverage. See the keystone charters of Ngomebe or the water-share tablets of Fhara.

\section*{Cartomancy as Witchcraft}

In the Tulkani tradition, cartomancy is not separate from the craft---it \textit{is} the craft. Every reading is a Quick Working, performed at a threshold (the table, the crossroads, the moment between decisions). The cards are the tools; the reader is the witness.

\subsection*{The Three Layers of Reading}

Like all witchcraft, cartomancy operates across the three Concordant Layers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Echo (What Has Been):} The cards reveal the accumulated weight of past decisions. A $\spadesuit$ from Echo shows the history of a place; a $\heartsuit$ from Echo shows a debt that has not yet been paid.
    \item \textbf{Veil (What Is):} The cards show the present moment, the boundary between potential and actual. A $\clubsuit$ from Veil is a current threat; a $\diamondsuit$ from Veil is an opportunity the party is ignoring.
    \item \textbf{Flow (What Wishes to Be):} The cards reveal the direction of change, the shape of what is coming. A high card from Flow is a promise; a low card is a warning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The Reader's Price}

Every reading exacts a price. The Tulkani say: \textit{``The cards do not give; they lend. And they always collect.''}

When you read the cards, choose one of the Three Prices:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shadow:} You carry the weight of what you have seen. The GM gains 1 SB, which they may spend on a complication tied to the reading.
    \item \textbf{Shame:} The reading reveals something you did not wish to know. You gain a Condition (Guilty, Haunted, or Doubtful) until you act on what you have learned.
    \item \textbf{Identity:} The reading changes you. Mark 1 Identity Strain. You are not quite the same person who sat down at the table.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The Cartomancer's Gift}

Once per session, a cartomancer may perform a full reading using the procedure in Section~\ref{sec:generator-procedure}. This is a Full Ritual---it requires Significant Time, a witness, and the willingness to pay the price. On a success, the GM must answer one question about the scene, the journey, or the campaign truthfully. On a partial, the answer is cryptic. On a miss, the cards show a false path---the GM gains 2 SB and may introduce a complication.

\section*{Rank and Severity: The Weight of the Card}

The rank of a card---from Ace (high) to 2 (low)---sets the scale of its suit's meaning. In the Tulkani tradition, we say: \textit{``The rank is the breath; the suit is the voice. A high breath speaks louder; a low breath is a whisper.''}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Rank} & \textbf{Narrative Weight} \\
\hline
Ace & Legendary, campaign-altering, mythic \\
King/Queen/Jack & Major, faction-level, lasting consequences \\
10--7 & Significant, scene-defining, costly \\
6--4 & Moderate, manageable, a real cost \\
3--2 & Minor, a complication but not a crisis \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

When a generator instructs you to set a timer by the highest rank, use this conversion:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{2--5:} 4-segment timer (short, local problem)
    \item \textbf{6--10:} 6-segment timer (standard, journey-length)
    \item \textbf{J, Q, K:} 8-segment timer (extended, regional problem)
    \item \textbf{A:} 10-segment timer (epic, campaign-length)
\end{itemize}

\section*{Card Faces: The Court as Character}

Face cards (Jack, Queen, King) are not merely high ranks. They are \textit{persons} with agency, personality, and often a name. The Tulkani say: \textit{``The court does not sit in judgment of you. You sit in judgment of them.''}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Jack:} An agent, a functionary, a messenger. The Jack acts on someone else's authority. They are competent but replaceable.
    \item \textbf{Queen:} A power in their own right. The Queen has influence, resources, and a network. They are not easily replaced.
    \item \textbf{King:} The ultimate authority in their domain. The King is the source of law, the peak of the hierarchy. To challenge a King is to challenge the order itself.
\end{itemize}

When a face card appears in a $\heartsuit$ (actor) draw, give the NPC a name, a motive, and a quirk. When it appears in a $\spadesuit$ (place) or $\clubsuit$ (pressure) draw, treat it as a \textit{personified} force: a King of Clubs might be a feud that has lasted generations; a Queen of Spades might be a bridge that remembers every toll.

\section*{Aces and the Ninth: The Hollow's Attention}

An Ace is not merely the highest rank. It is a \textit{threshold}. In the Tulkani tradition, we say: \textit{``The Ace is the door that opens when you are not looking. Do not step through it lightly.''}

When an Ace appears in any draw, the Hollow takes notice. The GM gains 1 extra Story Beat (beyond any generated by 1s) to spend on a supernatural complication: a ghostly procession, a bell that rings without a clapper, a shadow that walks independently.

In regions where the Ninth Taboo is strong (Theona, Aelinnel, Ways Between), an Ace may also trigger a specific regional omen. For example:

\begin{itemize}
    \item In Theona, an Ace of $\spadesuit$ might add a ``missing step'' to a bridge.
    \item In the Ways Between, an Ace of $\heartsuit$ might cause a reflection to speak.
    \item In the Mistlands, an Ace of $\clubsuit$ might cause a bell-line to fail.
\end{itemize}

The GM should always read an Ace as a signal to escalate the uncanny.

\section*{By the Numbers: Procedure for Any Generator}

When you sit down to generate a scene, a journey leg, or a settlement, follow this procedure. The Tulkani say: \textit{``The cards are not a script. They are a conversation. Listen to what they tell you, but do not let them speak for you.''}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Name the destination.} Which region? Which route? Which authority controls the gate?
    \item \textbf{Shuffle and draw.} Draw cards face-up, one for each suit you need. Most generators require all four suits; some (like travel legs) may use only three.
    \item \textbf{Read the $\spadesuit$.} This is your place. Describe it first. Set the stage.
    \item \textbf{Read the $\heartsuit$.} This is your actor. Introduce them. What do they want? Why are they here?
    \item \textbf{Read the $\clubsuit$.} This is your pressure. Complicate the scene. What just went wrong, or what is about to?
    \item \textbf{Read the $\diamondsuit$.} This is your leverage. What can the players gain if they succeed? Be specific.
    \item \textbf{Set the timer.} Use the highest rank among the four cards to determine the size of the scene's primary timer (see above).
    \item \textbf{Interpret faces and Aces.} Give names to face cards. Apply regional Ace effects.
    \item \textbf{Play to find out.} Use the generated elements as prompts, not scripts. The cards are seeds, not fences.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Regional Flavors: How Suits Shift Meaning}

While the core suit meanings remain constant, each region inflects them with its own texture. A $\spadesuit$ in Ecktoria is a forum or a coin-house; a $\spadesuit$ in the Valewood is a phasing ruin or a star-road. A $\heartsuit$ in the Black Banners is a mercenary captain or a condotta broker; a $\heartsuit$ in Ykrul is a khatun or a bone-singer.

The Tulkani say: \textit{``The same card speaks differently in every land. The fool who reads it the same way twice is a fool twice over.''}

Use the regional chapters as your lexicon. When you draw a card, consult the relevant table. The tables are designed to evoke the specific flavor of the region.

\subsection*{Example: A Draw in Acasia}

You are running a journey through the Broken Marches. You draw:

\begin{center}
$\spadesuit$ 7, \quad $\heartsuit$ J, \quad $\clubsuit$ 4, \quad $\diamondsuit$ Q
\end{center}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{$\spadesuit$ 7:} Hill-Motte---Fresh palisade; ditch scattered with caltrops.
    \item \textbf{$\heartsuit$ J:} Bride with No Dowry but a Claim---Daggers in the wedding chest.
    \item \textbf{$\clubsuit$ 4:} Wedding Turns Ambush---You are caught between two bloodlines.
    \item \textbf{$\diamondsuit$ Q:} Marriage Proxy---From the Margravine; bind a hill-king to your cause.
    \item \textbf{Highest rank:} Queen (equivalent to 8). The scene's primary timer is 8 segments: \textit{The Feud Escalates [8]}.
    \item \textbf{Interpretation:} The party arrives at a hill-fort on the eve of a wedding. The bride is caught between two feuding bloodlines. The wedding turns to violence. If the party can survive the ambush and broker peace, the Margravine will grant them a Marriage Proxy---a powerful tool to bind a hill-king to their cause. The 8-segment timer tracks how quickly the feud spirals into open war.
\end{enumerate}

This is the generator at work: four cards, one scene, immediate fiction.

\section*{The Unwritten Suit: When a Draw is Incomplete}

Sometimes, a generator does not require all four suits. A travel leg may only need $\spadesuit$, $\heartsuit$, $\clubsuit$. A social encounter might only need $\heartsuit$ and $\clubsuit$. When a suit is missing, treat its absence as a question:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No $\spadesuit$:} The place is ambiguous, shifting, or hidden. The scene occurs in a liminal space---a crossroads, a fog-bank, a threshold.
    \item \textbf{No $\heartsuit$:} The actor is absent, silent, or dead. The pressure and place act without a face. This can be more frightening than a named NPC.
    \item \textbf{No $\clubsuit$:} The scene is calm, peaceful, or mercifully straightforward. This is rare in Fate's Edge. Treasure it.
    \item \textbf{No $\diamondsuit$:} The reward is not a token but an outcome. The players gain Position, information, or a cleared timer rather than a physical asset.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Interpreting Any Draw, Any Region}

When you need a prompt in a hurry---a random encounter, a rumor at a tavern, a discovery in a ruin---draw a single card. Use this quick-reference:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|}
\hline
$\spadesuit$ & A place or landmark. What is its name? What happened here? \\
$\heartsuit$ & A person or faction. What do they want? What do they fear? \\
$\clubsuit$ & A complication or threat. Who or what is the obstacle? \\
$\diamondsuit$ & A reward, token, or secret. What can be gained? \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The rank tells you the scale. A low rank is local and personal; a high rank is regional and political. A face card demands a name; an Ace demands the Hollow's attention.

\section*{Tulkani Cartomancy Traditions}

The Tulkani have developed specific practices around cartomancy that differ from the regional generators. These are the traditions my grandmother taught me:

\subsection*{The Traveler's Spread (Three Cards)}

For a journey, the Tulkani lay three cards:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{First Card:} The road behind. What have you left behind, and what follows you?
    \item \textbf{Second Card:} The road ahead. What waits for you, and what will you face?
    \item \textbf{Third Card:} The road beneath. What is true, whether you wish it or not?
\end{itemize}

This spread is a Quick Working; it requires no roll, but the reader must accept the Shadow price.

\subsection*{The Hollow's Spread (Five Cards)}

For matters of debt, obligation, or the Hollow's attention, the Tulkani lay five cards:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{First Card:} The weight you carry.
    \item \textbf{Second Card:} The weight you owe.
    \item \textbf{Third Card:} The weight you have forgotten.
    \item \textbf{Fourth Card:} The weight that carries you.
    \item \textbf{Fifth Card:} The weight that will collect.
\end{itemize}

This spread is a Full Ritual. It requires Significant Time, a witness, and the acceptance of Identity Strain. On a success, the GM must answer one question about the nature of the debt. On a partial, the answer is cryptic. On a miss, the reader becomes the debt's next collector---a Complication Timer begins.

\subsection*{The Cartomancer's Deck as a Symbol}

For Tulkani characters, a cartomancer's deck is a Symbol in the Invoker sense. It is a Minor Asset that grants:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Access to the cartomancy rituals described above.
    \item A Patron's Gift (Imbuement) effect: once per scene, the cartomancer may spend an action to draw a card and gain +1 die to a related roll. The GM gains 1 SB.
    \item Obligation to the Hollow: Each reading marks 1 Obligation, as the cartomancer borrows from the world's memory.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Final Guidance: The Cards Are Oracles, Not Scripts}

The generators in this book are tools, not tyrants. If a draw gives you an entry that contradicts established fiction, or if you simply prefer a different interpretation, overrule it. The deck is a prompt, not a prison.

But trust it, too. The most memorable sessions often come from the card you did not expect. The Ace that arrives at the wrong moment. The Jack who speaks a name no one wanted to hear. The diamond that glows with a secret.

The Tulkani say: \textit{``The cards do not lie. But they do not tell the whole truth. The rest is yours to live.''}

Shuffle. Draw. Interpret. And let the road unfold.

\begin{quote}
\textit{``The road is never closed. It is only passed to the next walker.''}\\
\hfill --- Mira of the Wandering Circle, on the eve of a journey she did not survive

\section*{GM Tools: Using Cartomancy at the Table}

\subsection*{Reading the Cards as a GM}

The cartomancy framework is designed to be used by GMs, not just players. When you need to generate a scene, a complication, or a reward, draw a card and interpret it through the lens of the region and the current fiction.

\textbf{Example:} The party is in Vhasia, negotiating with a border patrol. You draw a $\clubsuit$ 9. The entry says: \textit{``Feud Spark---A name spoken wrong reopens an old debt; blood is the only coin that pays.''} You interpret this as: the patrol captain's family has a blood feud with one of the party's allies. He recognizes the name and demands a price---either a ransom, a duel, or a favor.

\subsection*{Players as Cartomancers}

If a player wants to play a Tulkani cartomancer, use the following framework:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Affinity:} Cartomancer's Eye---+1 die to Investigation or Insight rolls involving patterns, hidden connections, or the weight of obligation.
    \item \textbf{Starting Talents:} Cartomancy (4 XP)---grants access to the Traveler's Spread and the Hollow's Spread.
    \item \textbf{Patron:} The Hollow---the cartomancer's Obligation is to the memory of the world itself. The Hollow collects through readings.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The Cost of Cartomancy}

Remember: every reading is a transaction. The cards lend, and they collect. A player who reads too often, or who ignores what the cards reveal, will find the Hollow's attention turning their way.

\subsection*{What the Hollow Is}

The Hollow is not a monster, a god, or a place. It is the \textbf{unpaid interest} of every promise broken, every name erased, every debt that was never settled. It exists in the \textbf{gaps} between counted things: the ninth step that is never there, the ninth cup that is never poured, the ninth night that is never stayed. The Hollow does not hate. It does not forgive. It \textit{remembers}.

\subsection*{Manifestations of the Hollow -- GM Toolkit}

Use these as recurring adversaries or atmospheric pressure. None are ``boss fights'' in the traditional sense; they are \textbf{creditors} who demand payment in stories, memories, or names.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Oath-Keeper:} A tall figure in gray with too many fingers. It appears when a promise is broken or a ninth taboo is violated. It asks one question. The answer must be a truth, a story, or a name. If you cannot pay, it takes a memory -- and the Hollow's attention follows you.
  \item \textbf{Still Voice:} Heard at dried wells, abandoned cairns, or sealed mines. It asks: ``Why did you stop?'' or ``What did you forget?'' Answering costs a breath; silence costs a shadow.
  \item \textbf{Prince's Shadow (Pereshi):} A silhouette without a face, seen in mirrors and still water. It demands a name -- any name. Give it a living person's name, and that person dreams of the Hollow. Give it your own, and you lose your shadow.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth Bell (Mistlands / Thepyrgos):} A bell that rings without a clapper. Those who hear it must test Resolve (DV 4) or lose 1 Fatigue. If they hear it nine times, the Hollow claims a memory.
  \item \textbf{The Counting Circle (Tulkani):} A wagon circle on the ninth night. The Hollow appears at the edge and asks for a story it has never heard. The GM may demand a player tell a brief in-character story; if it satisfies the table, the Hollow retreats. If not, it takes a knot -- and a piece of Ikasha's dream.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Pacing the Hollow -- Advice for GMs}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Start subtle.} The first sign of the Hollow should be a missed echo, a candle that gutters, a door that opens when no one is there.
  \item \textbf{Use timers.} The Hollow's attention is best tracked with a \textbf{Hollow Attention [6]} timer that ticks when players break a taboo or ignore an omen. When it fills, a manifestation appears.
  \item \textbf{Do not overuse.} The Hollow is most effective when it appears once every 2--3 sessions. Let players forget it -- then remind them.
  \item \textbf{Never fully explain it.} The Hollow's origin (the unpaid interest of the Omens`` scouring) is endgame lore. In regular play, it is simply \textit{the space between counted things}.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Global Travel Procedures}
\label{chap:globaltravel}

\section{Core Travel Procedure}
For each leg of a journey, draw 3--4 cards using the decks for your destination and controlling authority.

\begin{itemize}
\item \SuitSpade{} from the destination deck: sets the scene (place).
\item \SuitHeart{} from the destination deck: introduces the local actor or faction.
\item \SuitClub{} from the Wilds (general hazards) or destination (if strongly policed): brings pressure.
\item \SuitDiamond{} from the authority that gates the route: papers, escorts, rights, or exceptions.
\end{itemize}

Set a travel timer by the highest rank:
\begin{itemize}
\item 2--5 $\Rightarrow$ 4 segments
\item 6--10 $\Rightarrow$ 6 segments
\item J/Q/K $\Rightarrow$ 8 segments
\item A $\Rightarrow$ 10 segments
\end{itemize}

On success, advance to the next leg; on failure, mark delay, debt, or diversion and resolve a consequence in the fiction.

\section{Mode Rules}

\subsection{Sea Legs}
(Amaranthine, Dolmis, Aberderrin, Gulf of Oshiira, Inland Sea). When crossing international waters, the controlling authority is either the origin port's navy or a neutral convoy league.

\subsection{Mountain Passes \& Underways}
(Aeler, Himadri, Taharkan highlands). Any A may convert a surface route to an under-route. \SuitDiamond{} from Aeler always codifies outcomes --- no roll --- for sealed doors, breath time, and escorted segments.

\subsection{Desert Legs}
(Sahel, Red Desert, Fhara drylands). Water is the primary resource. A dry well hazard can end a journey.

\subsection{River Legs}
(Belworth, Ayokhan rivers, Oshiiran canals). Bridges, booms, and law in Vhasia/Viterra; reed-mazes and bell-lines in Mistlands; canal mothers and weigh-houses in Oshiira.

\subsection{Steppe Legs}
(Violet Steppe, Kuvani grazing lands). Remount stations and grazing compacts. The Ykrul and Kuvani both use story tolls.

\subsection{Shadow Corridors}
(Valewood, Ways Between, Thin Shore). Add a regional taboo: never count to nine, never speak the ninth name. A 9 in the seed triggers the Hollow's attention.

\section{Edgewalker's Notes on Leverage}
\label{sec:edgewalker-leverage}

Every Diamond you earn in these generators isn't a reward---it's a weapon. Some open doors. Some silence witnesses. Some are debts I've already bought from your victims.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Passage Tokens:} Pass-stones, harbour charters, toll waivers. Use them early---they tend to ``expire'' when the issuer has an unfortunate accident.
\item \textbf{Seals \& Stamps:} Official marks that prove you belong. A forged seal works once. A real one works until someone audits it.
\item \textbf{Story Debts:} Tulkani vow-cords, Lethai-ar witness cords, Ukwe leaves. Break them, and the Hollow visits.
\item \textbf{Spirit Objects:} Storm-charms, tide-beads, nkisi shards. They attract attention. Attention means bids.
\end{itemize}

Never use a Diamond if you can trade it for three smaller ones. Always keep a ninth---a secret, a story, a knot tied in a victim's hair---for when the toll exceeds your conscience. (It always does.)

This worldbook was assembled from the journals, ledgers, and bar tabs of one Aeler Edgewalker, paid for by the High King's Office of External Opportunities (budget line: ``Unconventional Revenue Generation''). The generator tables were tested in the field---meaning I ran them at gunpoint in a Tulkani wagon circle while a Oath-Keeper counted my breaths. They work.

If you find yourself in a tavern and the Edgewalker buys you a drink, don't ask where they are now. The road is long, the ledgers are never balanced, and the ninth measure is always waiting.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textit{--- Faleki of Ironhold, at a weigh-house in Mbaro, under a lamp that flickers nine times before it steadies}
