\subsection{The Carrion-King (Decay, Renewal \& Transformation)}

\textit{Lore.} The Carrion-King is the master of endings that become beginnings. He does not destroy, but transforms—turning death into new life, decay into opportunity, and endings into fresh starts. His followers are harvesters of potential, seeing in every fall the seeds of future growth. The King has no will, only a hunger; no malice, only a rhythm. When something ends, he is there to turn it toward the next beginning. Those who serve him learn to read the slow geometry of rot, to hear the whisper of what is about to crumble, and to know that the most fertile ground is always the grave.

\begin{quote}
What crumbles feeds what grows. What dies becomes the soil of tomorrow's triumph. The King does not ask for sacrifice—he simply waits for it.
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Domain Focus}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Endings as Seeds:} extracting value from loss, failure, and death
  \item \textbf{Decay as Process:} accelerating, redirecting, or reading natural decay
  \item \textbf{Transformation Cycles:} turning one thing into another through death and rebirth
  \item \textbf{Rot as Resource:} using decay to create advantage (cover, concealment, reagents)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Thiasos and Codex (Runekeeper Options)}
A Runekeeper sworn to the Carrion-King does not bind a living creature in the usual sense. Their \textbf{Thiasos} is a small beast or mythological being that thrives on endings: a black beetle that feeds on rot and never dies, a pale grub that pupates into a moth only when a significant death occurs nearby, a crow with one white feather that knows where bodies are buried, or a small fox that appears only at dusk to lead the keeper to fresh carrion. This creature requires no food, but it must be allowed to scavenge or consume something that has died each week. If neglected, it becomes \textit{Compromised} (it refuses to return, or returns with a token of a death the keeper could have prevented).

The \textbf{Codex} of the Carrion-King is never a fresh book. It may be a leather binding made from the hide of a creature that died of old age, a set of bone tablets etched with rot‑runes, or a compost‑stained journal whose pages crumble at the edges. Because the King’s truths are written in decay, the Codex requires \textit{upkeep}: the Runekeeper must spend an hour each week burying it in fertile soil (or exposing it to the elements), or the Rites stored there become faint (treat as Compromised until restored).

\subsubsection*{Patron's Gift (Imbuement)}
Once per scene as an action, a Runekeeper of the Carrion-King may touch a held item (weapon, tool, or garment) and whisper a promise of ending. For the rest of the scene, the item grants \textbf{+1 die} to \textit{Survival} or \textit{Craft} rolls involving organic materials, and the first time each scene the bearer would take a Condition from poison, disease, or rot, they may ignore it (the King accepts the decay elsewhere). After the scene, the user marks \textbf{+1 Obligation} as the scent of turned earth clings to them.

\subsubsection*{Cantors \& Cults of the Carrion-King}

\textbf{The Cantor's Song.} A Cantor of the Carrion-King sings the \textit{dirge of transformation} – melodies that begin in mourning and end in fertile silence. Their voice carries the scent of turned earth and the weight of things laid to rest. They are called to funerals, to the edges of battlefields, and to compost heaps where the old year is buried. A Cantor of the King does not seek applause; they seek the moment when grief becomes acceptance, and acceptance becomes growth.

\textbf{The Cult of the Compost-Circle.} The King's cults are not secret, but they are overlooked – gardeners, gravediggers, bone‑setters, and midwives who have seen too many births and deaths to fear the space between. Their rituals are practical: the annual turning of the village burial mound, the blessing of a new compost heap, the singing of a child into the world while its grandmother is sung out. The Compost-Circle keeps no written creed, only a shared understanding: \textit{“Nothing is wasted. Not tears, not bones, not the silence after a name is spoken.”}

\subsubsection*{Witchcraft of the Carrion-King (Threshold)}

A witch who walks with the Carrion-King is a \textit{rot‑warden}, a keeper of the slow alchemy that turns decay into soil. Their magic is patient, overlooked, and often performed in the half‑light of a root cellar or a fog‑bound cemetery.

\textbf{The Witch's Tool: The Bone‑Needle.} A needle carved from the long‑bone of a creature that died of old age. It can stitch wounds that will not heal, but more often it is used to “sew shut” a corpse’s mouth, ensuring the dead do not speak of things they saw in the dark. Once per session, touching the needle to a threshold (a door, a grave marker, a coffin lid) seals it against spirits for a scene.

\textbf{A Hedge Gift: Turn the Soil (2 XP).} Once per scene, you may touch a patch of fertile ground (or a pile of compost, a flowerpot, a grave) and immediately know which plant or creature will thrive there next season, or what was buried there last. The GM must answer one factual question about the site’s recent natural history.

\subsection*{Playstyle Notes}
The Carrion-King rewards patience and opportunism. His servants are not grim reapers but gardeners of the inevitable. They know when to let something die and when to accelerate the process. The King does not demand – he merely waits. But when a follower hesitates to let go of a failing plan, a dying ally, or a crumbling empire, the King’s silence becomes heavy. Intrusions often take the form of omens (a black bloom on a wound, a sudden stench of compost) or demands to “clear the rot” – to end something so that something new may begin.

\noindent\textbf{Emphasises}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Endings as Resources:} failure, loss, and death are not punishments but opportunities
  \item \textbf{Cyclical Logic:} every death fertilises a birth; every collapse clears ground for new growth
  \item \textbf{Decay Reading:} sensing structural weaknesses, moral decay, or coming collapse
  \item \textbf{Transformation Craft:} turning a corpse into a crop, a failure into insight, a broken oath into fertile soil
\end{itemize}
