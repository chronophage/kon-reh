\subsection{Thrysos, King of Revels (Ecstasy \& Excess)}

\textit{Lore.} Where torches flare and cups spill, where dancers stamp until dawn, there strides Thrysos crowned with ivy and horn. He is both host and beast, a patron of license who laughs at law. In his train follow the masked and the wild, the broken who dance to forget, the bold who revel to remember. His rites intoxicate, inflame, and unmask---yet each cup leaves its mark.

Among the peoples of Fate's Edge, Thrysos is called by many names that honour the ecstatic release. In Linn, his followers are the \textit{Storm‑Dancers}, who whirl through gales to exhaustion. In Aelinnel, he is the \textit{Unbounded Guest}, whose arrival at a feast is marked by laughter and shattered glass. In Zakov, the \textit{Revel‑King} is invoked before every pirate carouse, with the understanding that a hungover crew is a vulnerable one. His rituals are never secret -- they are too loud for concealment -- but they are always dangerous, for Thrysos demands that every excess be paid for, if not tonight then at the first hangover of spring.

\noindent\textbf{Signs.} Wine that never sours; a mask that smiles without wearer; goat‑tracks circling thrice before vanishing.

\noindent\textbf{Cantor's Temptation.} His promise is freedom without end: no oaths, no chains, only song and cup and fire. Yet those who drink too deeply wake to find they serve not joy but appetite.

\begin{quote}
\emph{``Drink, and be unafraid.''}\\
\emph{``Every mask is true when worn long enough.''}\\
\emph{``In the dance we are all kings---until the dawn.''}
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Domain Focus}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ecstatic Release:} dance, intoxication, the shedding of inhibition
  \item \textbf{Masked Freedom:} identity as performance, truth through disguise
  \item \textbf{Feast and Famine:} excess, appetite, and the cost of indulgence
  \item \textbf{Ritual Frenzy:} communal ecstasy that transforms participants
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Cantors \& Cults of Thrysos}

\textbf{The Cantor's Song.} A Cantor of Thrysos sings in \textit{off‑key harmonies} – melodies that start wild and grow wilder, rhythms that mimic a galloping heart. Their voice is used to whip a crowd into a frenzy, to calm a hangover with a lullaby, or to make a mask cling to a face until the wearer is ready to take it off. Thrysos's Cantors are often found at festivals, in taverns at closing time, and leading the procession of the horned dancers.

\textbf{The Cult of the Horned Circle.} Thrysos's cults meet in clearings, barns, and the back rooms of inns after midnight. The Horned Circle is a fellowship of mask‑carvers, vintners, musicians, and those who have tasted the edge of ecstasy. Their most sacred vessel is a hollowed‑out log that was struck by lightning; they ferment wine inside it, and the wine \textit{whispers secrets} to those who drink it – forgotten grudges, hidden desires, the true names of those who will betray them. A ritual of the Circle involves each member donning a mask (hand‑carved, never bought) and dancing until the first dancer falls from exhaustion. The fallen is crowned with ivy and declared "guest of honour" – they lead the next circle. Their creed is carved into a single wine barrel that is never emptied: \textit{"The cup that never empties is the cup you share."} Outsiders are welcome to dance, but they must leave their mask behind when they go – and if they drink from the lightning‑log, they will hear the whispers for a year and a day.

\subsubsection*{Witchcraft of Thrysos (Lay / Revelry)}

A witch who walks with Thrysos is a \textit{mask‑carver} or \textit{wine‑smith} – one who crafts the tools of ecstatic release: a mask that lets the wearer dance without tiring, a drum that beats in time with the drinker's pulse, or a cup that never runs dry (but leaves a headache at dawn). Their magic works through the materials of celebration: a thyrsus that marks the boundary of a sacred dance, a press that squeezes wine that ferments in hours instead of weeks, or a needle that sews a costume that makes the wearer faster, louder, bolder. In Aeler, a True‑Mason might carve a "revel keystone" into a feast hall, but a hedge‑witch of Thrysos might instead drive a thyrsus into the floorboards to consecrate the space.

\textbf{The Witch’s Tool: The Thyrsus of the Unbound.} A staff of fennel or oak, topped with a pine cone and bound with ivy. Once per session, the witch may strike the thyrsus against the ground three times. For the next scene, any creature within Near who is engaged in revelry (dancing, drinking, performing) gains \textbf{+1 die} to social and physical actions, but any attempt to leave the zone requires a Resolve test (DV 3) – the revel holds them. The thyrsus can also be used to draw a circle on the ground; any mask placed within the circle becomes “true” for one night – the wearer cannot be recognised by anyone who was not present at the drawing.

\textbf{A Hedge Gift: Dancer's Cramp (2 XP).} Once per scene, the witch may snap their fingers and whisper a dancer's rhythm. One target within Near range feels an irresistible urge to move – they must tap a foot, sway, or take a step. This cannot force them into danger, but it can ruin stealth, break a staredown, or distract a guard for one exchange. The target may resist with Resolve (DV 3) to ignore the urge.

\subsection*{Playstyle Notes}
Thrysos rewards those who embrace risk, abandon convention, and find power in letting go. His followers become masters of ecstatic ritual, social manipulation through celebration, and the dangerous art of losing control. The corruption progression leads toward a beast of endless revel – powerful, charismatic, but never truly sober, never truly at rest. Ideal for players who enjoy wild scenes, social chaos, and characters who dance on the edge of self‑destruction.
