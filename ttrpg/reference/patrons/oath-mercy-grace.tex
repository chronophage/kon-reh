\subsection{Oath of Mercy and Grace (Healing \& Forgiveness)}

\textit{Lore.} The Oath of Mercy and Grace has no founder, no scripture, no single temple. It began in a hundred places---a battlefield where a medic bound wounds without asking whose side; a plague village where a stranger stayed to dig graves; a debtor's cell where a guard looked away and said, "I saw nothing." Those who swear the Oath do not worship a power; they embody a principle: that every act of care creates an obligation---not to the giver, but to the next person in need. You do not pay back. You pay forward. The mercy spirals.

The Oath appears in many guises across cultures, often as a healer's order or a mendicant fellowship within larger faiths:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Grey Circle (Everflame)} --- a heretical sect within the Church of the Everflame that tends the sick without demanding confession or tithe. Its members wear undyed wool and carry clay bowls. The orthodox clergy call them "fire‑dimmers"; the poor call them mercy.
  \item \textbf{The Hospitallers of the Unburdened Hand (Temple of Light)} --- a knightly order sworn to protect pilgrims and heal the wounded. They carry no banners and refuse to strike first. Their enemies mock them as "grey shields"; their patients never forget their faces.
  \item \textbf{The Pardoners of the Covenant (Mykkiel)} --- a reformist school within the Covenant that argues every contract contains an implicit clause of impossibility. "If a debtor cannot pay, the creditor must accept work in kind." Mykkielan judges consider them dangerous idealists; debtors call them deliverance.
  \item \textbf{The Tide-Kindlers (Linn sea-rites)} --- mourners who walk the shore after storms, burying the drowned without marking names. The sea takes no toll from them. Sailors whisper that a Tide-Kindler's blessing is the only prayer the deep will answer.
  \item \textbf{The Weepers (Death‑cult)} --- a mendicant fellowship that appears at funerals, often uninvited. They weep for the dead, believing that their tears can forgive the deceased's unpaid debts and close their page in the Ledger of Grace. Wealthy lords sponsor them as a status symbol; the poor chase them away as invasive nuisances. Their efficacy is a matter of fierce debate. (See sidebar.)
  \item Others know the Oath simply as \textit{the Spiral} or \textit{the Grey Work}---a nameless way of walking the world, tending wounds, and refusing to keep score.
\end{itemize}

Wherever it manifests, the Oath of Mercy and Grace remains the same: to swear it is to accept that you will receive care, and that you must give it in turn. The spiral does not close.

\begin{quote}
``You ask who owes what. We ask who needs what. The ledger is not the same as the meal.''
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Domain Focus}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Restorative Work:} Healing body, mind, and spirit; mending what is broken
  \item \textbf{Spiral Mercy:} Paying obligation forward through acts of care
  \item \textbf{Sanctuary:} Sheltering the hunted, feeding the hungry, accepting no payment
  \item \textbf{Unburdening:} Releasing others (and oneself) from the weight of unpaid debts
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Rites of the Oath of Mercy and Grace}

\paragraph{Rite of the Shared Bowl (Low, 4 XP)} \hfill \\
\emph{Action; Touch; Standard Push} \\
\textbf{Materials:} A clay bowl of clean water, shared with a willing creature. \\
\textbf{Effect:} You and the creature each clear 1 Fatigue. The creature gains \textbf{+1 die} on their next roll to resist fear or despair. \\
\textbf{Push It:} Extend the benefit to all creatures who drink from the bowl (up to six). Mark +1 Obligation. \\
\emph{Requires: Familiar (\textit{Invoke:} 1 Boon).}

\paragraph{Rite of Healing Waters (Low, 5 XP)} \hfill \\
\emph{Instant; Touch; Standard Push} \\
\textbf{Materials:} Water from a blessed source (a spring, a well, or a river where the Oath has been invoked), poured onto a wound. \\
\textbf{Effect:} Remove 1 Harm from a touched creature. If the wound is cursed or poisoned, the GM may call for a Spirit+Lore test (DV 4). \\
\textbf{Push It:} Also remove one Condition (e.g., Poisoned, Cursed, Blinded). Mark +1 Obligation. \\
\emph{Requires: Familiar (\textit{Invoke:} 1 Boon).}

\paragraph{Rite of the Unburdened Circle (Standard, 8 XP)} \hfill \\
\emph{Scene; Zone; Standard Push} \\
\textbf{Materials:} At least three willing participants, a circle drawn in ash or flour, a single candle. \\
\textbf{Effect:} Each participant clears 1 Obligation (to any patron or debt). One participant of the GM's choice gains a permanent String: \textit{Witnessed by the Circle} -- they may call on any other participant for one act of care, once per campaign. \\
\textbf{Push It:} The GM gains 3 Story Beats (the universe notes that a community of mutual aid is forming). Mark +1 Obligation. \\
\emph{Requires: Familiar + Codex (\textit{Invoke:} 1 Boon).}

\paragraph{Rite of the Sheltered Hearth (Standard, 9 XP)} \hfill \\
\emph{Scene; Zone; Standard Push} \\
\textbf{Materials:} A door or threshold, a lit candle, a whispered vow of sanctuary. \\
\textbf{Effect:} For one night, any fugitive, debtor, or refugee who crosses that threshold cannot be pursued or harmed within the zone. The sanctuary holds until dawn. \\
\textbf{Push It:} Extend the sanctuary to a full day. Mark +1 Obligation. \\
\emph{Requires: Familiar + Codex (\textit{Invoke:} 1 Boon).}

\paragraph{Rite of the Spiral's Witness (Standard, 10 XP)} \hfill \\
\emph{Instant; Near; Standard Push} \\
\textbf{Materials:} A grey wool cord tied around the caster's wrist. \\
\textbf{Effect:} Name a person crushed by debt or guilt. For one hour, they ignore all penalties from unpaid obligations (social, legal, or spiritual). They are not free; they are \textit{unburdened}. \\
\textbf{Push It:} The effect lasts until the next dawn. Mark +1 Obligation. \\
\emph{Requires: Familiar + Codex (\textit{Invoke:} 1 Boon).}

\paragraph{Rite of the Endless Spiral (High, 13 XP)} \hfill \\
\emph{Scene; Zone; High Push} \\
\textbf{Materials:} A circle of nine grey stones, a bowl of water, and a candle lit at each cardinal point. \\
\textbf{Effect:} All within the circle who accept the Oath's blessing clear 2 Fatigue and remove one Condition caused by despair or guilt. For the rest of the scene, any act of care performed within the zone costs the giver --1 Obligation (minimum 0). \\
\textbf{Push It:} The blessing persists for a full day. Mark +2 Obligation. \\
\emph{Requires: Familiar + Codex + Tier III (\textit{Invoke:} 2 Boons).} \\
\emph{Obligation:} 7 segments.

\subsubsection*{Cantors \& Cults of the Oath of Mercy and Grace}

\textbf{The Cantor's Song.} A Cantor of the Oath does not merely sing \textit{about} mercy---they become a vessel for the pain of others. Their voice reaches into a listener's chest and draws out the unspoken grief, the hidden shame, the debt that has calcified into self‑loathing. They sing, and the listener weeps. They sing, and the listener forgives themselves. The Cantor does not heal wounds; they \textit{share} them.

This gift is dangerous. A Cantor who takes on another's pain may find that pain lingers, echoing in their own dreams. They may forget which memories are theirs and which they borrowed. They may begin to crave the intimacy of shared suffering, seeking out the broken not to help them, but to feel their own emptiness filled by another's anguish. The most unstable Cantors become emotional parasites, draining the misery of crowds and leaving behind hollow peace---not because they have healed, but because they have taken everything.

The Oath watches such Cantors closely. It does not condemn them; it understands that the spiral of mercy includes the risk of drowning. But it does not always pull them back.

\textbf{The Cult of the Grey Circle.} The Oath's cults gather in wilderness communes, abandoned farmsteads, and hidden valleys where no lord's writ runs. The Grey Circle is a fellowship of healers, fugitives, and the simply exhausted who have sworn to keep no ledgers. A ritual of the Circle involves each member speaking one burden they carry -- not a debt, but a weight -- and then passing a clay bowl of water around the circle. Each drinks, then pours a few drops onto the ground for the next stranger who will need it. Their creed is never written, only passed from mouth to ear: \textit{"The spiral has no end. That is not a curse. That is the only honest shape of mercy."} Outsiders are welcome to drink and to stay -- but they will be asked to work, and they will be expected to leave when they are strong enough.

\textbf{The Weepers.} A specialized cult within the Grey Circle, the Weepers appear at funerals---sometimes invited, sometimes not. They wear grey hoods and carry no bowl, only a knotted cord. They do not speak. They weep. Their tears, they believe, can forgive the debts of the dead, closing pages in the Ledger of Grace that would otherwise remain open. Wealthy lords sponsor Weepers as a status symbol: \textit{I can afford to have my ancestors forgiven.} The poor chase them away as invasive nuisances. Varnek Karn's followers consider them dangerously naive; the Daughters of the Unbroken Cord call them heretics. The Weepers do not argue. They simply weep. Whether their tears accomplish anything is a question the Oath does not answer---it only asks: \textit{Would you rather be wept for, or forgotten?}

\subsubsection*{Witchcraft of the Oath of Mercy and Grace (Craft / Hearthwise)}

A witch who walks with the Oath is a \textit{herb‑wife} or \textit{bowl‑carrier} -- one who crafts the small vessels of mercy: a clay bowl that never cracks, a poultice that draws fever, a candle that burns without smoke. In Aeler, a True‑Mason might carve a "mercy stone" into a hospice lintel, so that the dying feel no pain crossing it. In Viterra, a hedgewitch might weave a grey cord to tie around a debtor's wrist, a reminder that they are not their debt.

\textbf{The Witch's Tool: The Bowl of Unburdening.} A simple clay bowl, unglazed, fired in a kiln that also baked bread for the hungry. Once per session, the witch may fill the bowl with water from any source and speak a name. For the rest of the scene, that water (drunk or poured on wounds) removes 1 Fatigue and grants +1 die to resist fear. The water tastes faintly of ash and salt.

\textbf{A Hedge Gift: Grey Cord (2 XP).} Once per session, the witch may tie a grey wool cord around a willing creature's wrist. For the next day, any time that creature performs an act of care for someone who cannot repay them, they clear 1 Obligation. The cord dissolves when the day ends.

\subsubsection*{Oath of Mercy and Grace Corruption Manifestations}

For a Cantor, reaching Tier 6+ carries an additional risk: they may no longer be able to distinguish their own memories from those they have absorbed. The GM may occasionally ask: \textit{Is that your grief, or someone else"s?} If the Cantor cannot answer, they mark 1 Fatigue as the boundary blurs further.

\subsection*{Playstyle Notes}
The Oath of Mercy and Grace rewards characters who prioritize healing, shelter, and the quiet work of unburdening others. Its followers become living embodiments of compassion, but the spiral of mercy can become a weight of its own -- the more you give, the more you are called to give. The corruption progression naturally leads toward a saint who cannot rest while suffering exists, a healer who burns out in the service of others. Cantors in particular walk a knife's edge: their gift is to share pain, but that sharing can consume them. Ideal for players who enjoy the tension between self‑care and self‑sacrifice, and for those who want to explore a faith based not on worship but on restorative work.

\subsection*{Rivalries}
Within the Church of the Everflame, the Grey Circle is viewed with deep suspicion by the orthodox clergy, who accuse them of "diluting the purity of the flame with works without faith." The Inquisitor Prime's followers, in particular, see the Oath of Mercy and Grace as a dangerous heresy: mercy without judgment is merely indulgence, they say, and forgiving a debt before it is paid undermines the very order that keeps the world from burning. Hospitallers of the Grey Circle have been arrested, their hospices shuttered, their records burned. Yet the poor still seek them out, and the flame still answers their quiet prayers.

Within the Covenant of Mykkiel, the Pardoners of the Uncounted Debt are a tolerated but distrusted minority. Mykkielan judges argue that the "clause of impossibility" is a license for fraud; the Pardoners counter that a debt that can never be paid is not a contract but a cage. The rivalry is legal, not violent -- but it has broken families and left debtors caught between two absolutes.

The Oath does not seek these rivalries. It simply continues its work. And those who hate it most often find themselves, one day, in need of mercy.
