\part{The Bound}

\section{Demons and the Infernal}

I use the term \emph{demon} with reluctance. The entities that the Church of the Everflame calls demons are not a unified species. They are a \emph{category of convenience}---spirits that refuse to abide by the covenants of mortal society, that cannot be negotiated with in the language of debt and obligation.

A ghost, a ghoul, a lycanthrope – they are \emph{endings} I can understand. A debt unpaid, a hunger unmet, a rage that forgot its own face. They can be pitied, sometimes even saved. But the things that come through the holes? The ones that were never alive, that owe nothing and take everything?

When I walk into a room where one of these dwells, I smell burnt clove and cold iron. My silver mirror \emph{sweats}. The Witness goes quiet – as if it, too, is afraid of being seen.

\subsection{Classification by Behavior}

The Church classifies by \emph{behavior} – a practical crutch. I classify by \emph{what they leave behind}.

\begin{description}
  \item[Class A: The Tempter] What they leave: a contract written in invisible ink, a ledger with one entry more than you remember signing.  
  They offer power, knowledge, revenge – but always through a \textbf{bargain witnessed by no one}. A contract sealed without a third party is a hole shaped like your own hand.  
  \emph{Counter:} A Runekeeper of Mykkiel can enforce the letter, but the price is always that the \emph{witness} becomes the next debtor.

  \item[Class B: The Ravager] What they leave: a scorch mark on the wall in the shape of a door, a bell that rings once for every corpse.  
  Summoned by violence or mass death, they do not speak or bargain. They \textbf{erase}. Fire and silver wound them, but the only true banishment is \textbf{overwhelming witness} – three or more souls who saw it arrive and refuse to look away.  
  \emph{Counter:} The moment the last witness looks down, the Ravager returns.

  \item[Class C: The Possessor] What they leave: nothing at first. Then, slowly, the host’s favorite food tastes like ash; their mother’s face blurs; they wake with their hands around a lover’s throat.  
  \emph{Counter:} An exorcism witnessed by a representative of the \emph{host’s} faith – not the Church’s, not mine. The demon must be argued out, not forced.  

  \item[Class D: The Parasite] What they leave: an emotion that no longer belongs to you. Fear that grows teeth. Grief that walks into the next room and closes the door. Ambition that starves the body.  
  \emph{Counter:} Starvation – rigorous emotional discipline, sometimes requiring the host to live in a sensory deprivation cell for a moon. I have seen a man forget how to be afraid. He was not saved. He was hollowed.
\end{description}

\subsection{Investigation Notes}

Demon summoning is forbidden in every nation that has a functioning legal system, and for good reason. Every summoning is a hole in the world.

Demon hunters require three things: silver, fire, and witness. I carry a censer that burns a compound of saltpeter, silver filings, and ironwood resin. The smoke reveals demonic possession: the host’s shadow lags; the demon’s shadow moves ahead.

\subsubsection{The Weight of a Name}

Every demon has a \emph{true name}. Not the word it answers to in the abyss – that would kill the exorcist to speak. The \emph{true name} is the \textbf{first lie it told to enter the world}.  

The Tempter’s name is the clause it hid. The Ravager’s name is the violence it used as a key. The Possessor’s name is the moment the host said “yes” to something they should have refused.

To learn the name, I do not summon the demon. I \emph{audit the hole}.  
I place a silver mirror at the threshold where it entered, light a censer of saltpeter, silver filings, and ironwood resin, and I ask the \emph{absence}:

\begin{quote}\itshape
“What was the first thing you borrowed that was not yours?”
\end{quote}

The smoke reveals the answer – not in words, but in a \textbf{memory of the host’s that is not the host’s}. I have seen a woman weep a child’s tears for a child she never bore. I have seen a man scream in a voice that was his, but cracked like a bell that had been rung too many times.

The name is always a wound. And once I know it, I owe the demon nothing – but the \emph{host} will owe me a story they will never tell.

\subsection{The Sentinel's Exorcism (A Procedure in Three Mirrors)}

\emph{This is the rite that never closes. Not because it fails – because it \textbf{works}, and the cost is always visible.}

\textbf{You will need:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{silver mirror} for the host (polished in moonlight, never in sun).
  \item A \textbf{silver mirror} for the demon (kept wrapped in black cloth until the moment).
  \item A \textbf{silver mirror} for the exorcist (my own – it has a crack across the left edge. I do not remember when the crack appeared).
  \item A \textbf{circle} of salt, iron filings, and powdered bone (the bone must be from a person who died unafraid).
  \item A \textbf{witness} – someone who loves the host, someone who fears the demon, and someone who is neutral. (Three is the minimum. I have used seven. The more witnesses, the slower the demon’s lies.)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Step 1: Draw the Circle} \hfill \texttt{[ACTION: 10 MINUTES]}

Walk the circle three times, counter‑clockwise, sprinkling the salt‑iron‑bone mix. Each pass, speak the demon’s true name once. The circle will \emph{tighten} – the air inside will feel cold; the host’s breathing will become shallow.  

\emph{If the circle hisses:} The demon is trying to speak through the host. Do not answer. Answering gives it a thread to pull.

\subsubsection{Step 2: Place the Mirrors} \hfill \texttt{[ACTION: 1 ACTION]}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Host mirror} (facing the host).
  \item \textbf{Demon mirror} (facing the host’s eyes – the demon’s reflection will appear there, always half a second behind).
  \item \textbf{Exorcist mirror} (facing the demon mirror).
\end{itemize}

The three reflections must form a triangle. I have seen the mirrors crack in sequence when the demon resists. A cracked mirror does not fail – it \emph{bites}.

\texttt{[TAG: WITNESS]} – The neutral witness must stand at the triangle’s apex. They do not speak. They do not blink. Their presence is the \textbf{anchor of the seen}.

\subsubsection{Step 3: The Three Calls} \hfill \texttt{[ROLL: SPIRIT + LORE vs. DV = DEMON’S TIER (4–6)]}

\textbf{First call:}
\begin{quote}\itshape
“I name you [TRUE NAME]. You are seen. You are witnessed.”
\end{quote}
The host convulses. The demon mirror shows a face that is not the host’s.  
\emph{On a miss:} The demon speaks through the host and gains \textbf{one thread} (it can force the host to take one action on its turn).

\textbf{Second call:}
\begin{quote}\itshape
“I name you [TRUE NAME]. You are bound. You are contained.”
\end{quote}
The demon mirror \emph{sweats}. Condensation drips down the silver.  
\emph{On a partial:} The demon thrashes – mark \textbf{1 FATIGUE} on the host.  
\emph{On a miss:} The demon breaks eye contact; the exorcist mirror cracks, and the demon gains \textbf{control for one exchange}.

\textbf{Third call:}
\begin{quote}\itshape
“I name you [TRUE NAME]. You are \textbf{unmade}.”
\end{quote}
The demon mirror shatters. The shards fly inward, toward the demon’s reflection. The demon screams – not with the host’s voice, but with the \textbf{sound of a door slamming in a house with no walls}.  

\emph{On a success:} The demon is forced into the central mirror (the exorcist’s). The host collapses, unharmed.  
\emph{On a critical success:} The demon is shattered so thoroughly that the Witness whispers the name of the person who summoned it.  
\emph{On a failure:} The demon possesses the exorcist mirror. I have seen this happen once. The exorcist’s reflection smiled and walked out of the glass. It took three days to trap it again.

\subsubsection{Step 4: Seal the Shards} \hfill \texttt{[ACTION: 1 HOUR]}

Each shard of the exorcist mirror must be wrapped in black cloth and \textbf{disposed of in separate bodies of water} – a river, a well, a cistern, the sea. The Sentinel seals each shard as it sinks.

\begin{quote}\itshape
“You are distributed. You are forgotten. You are no longer one.”
\end{quote}

If any two shards are brought together, the demon can reform. I have spent years tracking down shards from exorcisms I performed decades ago. The debt never fully closes.

\subsection{Case Study: The Censor’s Daughter (Ecktoria, 859 AR)}

I did not want this case. The girl was fourteen. Her father was a Censor – a man who weighed grain and judged contracts, who had never believed in anything except the ledger.

The demon was Class C. A Possessor. It had been with her for three years, since the night her mother died. The Inquisition had already tried to burn her twice. Both times, the fire would not light.

When I arrived, the girl was sitting in a stone cell, her hands chained to the wall. She did not look at me. The demon looked through her eyes.

\begin{quote}\itshape
“You are the one who breaks mirrors,” it said.
\end{quote}
\begin{quote}
“I am the one who listens to the hole,” I replied.
\end{quote}

I learned its true name by \textbf{auditing the hole} – not through the girl, but through her father’s ledgers. The demon’s name was the \textbf{interest rate} on a loan the Censor had taken to pay for his wife’s medicine. The loan had been sold to a collector, who had sold it to a broker, who had died. The interest had compounded into \textbf{a debt that could never be paid}. The demon was that debt, given will.

The exorcism took six hours. Three mirrors. Seven witnesses – because the girl’s father, the Censor, refused to be one of them.

The demon fought hardest at the \textbf{second call}. It used the girl’s voice to recite the names of every person her father had bankrupted. The witnesses wept. I did not look away.

When the third call shattered the mirror, the girl screamed – not in pain, but in \emph{relief}. Her father confessed to the loansharking. The demon’s shards were scattered into the Dolmis, the cistern of a Mykkielan chapel, a well in a village that no longer exists, and a bottle of ink I keep on my desk.

The girl survived. She works as an archivist now. She wears gloves, because her fingerprints sometimes change.

Her father died in a debtor’s cell. The interest had not been paid. It never is.

\subsection{The Exorcist’s Debt}

Every exorcism costs me something. Not fatigue – I can sleep. Not obligation – I owe the Sentinel nothing.

It costs me \textbf{witness}.

After I seal a demon, I lose a \textbf{memory} – not a skill, not a name, but a \emph{moment} of my life. The demon’s shard takes it as toll. I do not know what I have forgotten until I try to remember.

I know I have a daughter. I cannot recall her face.  
I know I was a marine. I cannot recall my ship’s name.  
I know I have been doing this for forty years. I cannot recall why I started.

The Witness does not take these memories. The \emph{absence} does. The hole that the demon crawled through is also the hole where my past goes.

One day I will forget my own name. That will be the last exorcism.

But not today.
