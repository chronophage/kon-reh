\section*{Appendix E ‚Äì On the Counting of Wounds (An Essay by Lerris Fenwood)}

\noindent I have killed seventeen creatures catalogued in this book. I have been wounded by twenty‚Äëthree. The wounds heal. The counting does not.

When I was young, my father taught me to count my breaths. ‚ÄúEight,‚Äù he said. ‚ÄúNever nine. The ninth breath belongs to the Hollow.‚Äù I did not believe him. Then I saw a man take the ninth breath in an Aeler tunnel. The air turned cold. The man‚Äôs shadow stood up and walked away. The man did not follow. He simply sat down and waited. The second entry is a silence I cannot name ‚Äì the debt I owe to the String‚ÄëMother. The third entry is a door I did not open. \section*{Rite of the Echoing Vessel}
\emph{Varnek Karn, High Rite}

\begin{ritualbox}
\textbf{Components:} A vessel that has touched the dead‚Äîa lock of hair, a burial coin, a piece of jewelry worn at the moment of passing. A bowl of still water drawn from the river where they died, or from a threshold between waters (a confluence, a ford, a place where fresh meets salt). A grey thread, spun from wool that has been neither washed nor dyed, knotted nine times. A mirror that does not lie‚Äîsilvered glass, still water, or the polished blade of a knife that has never drawn blood.

\medskip
\textbf{Obligation:} 3 segments. If the vision shows a debt unpaid, the caster must accept a Promise Timer [4] to settle it, or mark 1 Corruption. The Symbol of Varnek Karn grows cold during the rite; if it becomes warm, the dead are near, and the caster should not look away.
\end{ritualbox}

\vspace{1em}

You prepare the space at twilight, when the boundary between worlds is thinnest. You face east, toward the river, though you may not be near water. The dead do not need geography. They need direction.

You place the bowl on bare earth. If you are indoors, you set it on a stone that has never been worked by a tool. You fill it with water drawn from the place of death, or from the nearest running water you could reach. If you have neither, you use rainwater collected in a vessel that has never held wine. The dead are particular about vessels. They remember what has been drunk from them.

You take the grey thread. You knot it once, speaking the name of a dead you have known. You knot it twice, speaking the name of a dead you have forgotten. You knot it thrice, speaking the name of a dead you never met. You knot it four times, five times, six times, seven times, eight times‚Äîeach knot a breath held, a word withheld, a question you do not ask.

The ninth knot you do not tie. You lay the thread across the rim of the bowl, one end in the water, the other in your hand. The ninth knot is the question. The dead will tie it, or they will not.

You place the vessel‚Äîthe lock of hair, the burial coin, the locket‚Äîinto the water. You do not speak its story. The dead already know. The dead were there.

You wait.

The water stills. The mirror you have placed beside the bowl begins to fog, not from warmth but from breath‚Äîbreath that is not yours, breath that has not touched air in years. You do not wipe the mirror. You do not look away.

You see.
