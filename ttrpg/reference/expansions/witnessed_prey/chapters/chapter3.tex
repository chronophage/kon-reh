\chapter{Beings of the Road}
\label{chap:beings-road}

\epigraph{“The road does not forgive a missed roll. It only reschedules it.”}{—Dusana of the Raven Road (apocryphal)}

Not every hungry thing wears a ghost’s shroud or a demon’s crown. Some are simply travellers who forgot to stop, thresholds that learned to walk, or debts that grew teeth. I met the Hollowed Stag in a rain‑rutted lane south of Thepyrgos; it did not charge, it only watched. When I offered a memory of my mother’s kitchen, it dipped its antlers and left an acorn that later cured a child’s fever. The road is a ledger, and these are its irregular entries.

This chapter catalogues the wanderers you might meet between towns – creatures that guard no tomb, serve no master, and owe no patron. They are the road’s own children: lonely, patient, and always hungry for something you did not know you carried.

\section*{The Hollowed Stag (Forest Omen)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A stag with antlers of bleached bone, ribs visible through translucent hide. It never eats. It only walks.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 2 (lone)\\
\textbf{Harm}: 3\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Silent Approach [4]} – when ticks, the stag appears behind someone.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [BEAST] [SPIRIT] [DREAD]\\
\textbf{Twist}: It does not attack first. It \emph{watches}. If you flee, the timer advances. If you stand still, it passes. If you offer a memory, it leaves a single acorn that cures one poison.

\saikou{I once placed a silver mirror in its path. The stag looked at its own reflection – and the antlers turned to wood. It breathed once, then walked into the mist. I do not know if I banished it or only reminded it of what it had been.}

\subsection*{The Ragged Piper (Fen Spirit)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A drowned man in patched wool, playing a reed pipe that sounds like children laughing.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 3 (with swarm)\\
\textbf{Harm}: 2\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{The Dance [6]} – when full, all in earshot must follow the piper for one scene.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [SPIRIT] [CONTROL] [AUDIO]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Immune to steel. To banish, offer a truth the piper has forgotten – or cut the pipe with a copper knife.

\grey{I heard the Ragged Piper once, near the Linn salt marshes. My horse danced. I did not. I was holding a copper coin between my teeth. The piper laughed and played a different tune. I have not slept without that coin since.}

\subsection*{The Boiler-Wight (Aeler Ruin Guardian)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A brass-and-copper construct, furnace heart, steam for breath. Hisses the names of intruders.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 4 (ambush)\\
\textbf{Harm}: 3\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Overpressure [4]} – each tick adds +1 Harm to its next attack; at 4 it vents steam on all in Near.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [CONSTRUCT] [FIRE] [WARDEN]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Ignores anyone carrying a stamped Aeler work order. Can be reprogrammed by inserting a new brass key (found nearby).

\lerris{I bribed one with a rusty breath‑counter. It stopped hissing and began to weep oil. The Aeler do not make their guardians to last. They make them to remember.}

\subsection*{The Silt-Fisher (Coastal Horror)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A long pale arm emerging from tidal mud, fingers webbed, dragging things below.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 2\\
\textbf{Harm}: 2\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Tug [3]} – each tick pulls a victim one step closer to the silt. At 3, submerged.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [AQUATIC] [GRAB]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Can be reasoned with if offered a drowned coin or a song about the sea. Will trade a salvaged item for release.

\dusana{I once sang a shanty about a drowned captain. The Silt-Fisher stopped pulling and began to weep. It let go of my leg and offered me a rusted compass that always points to the nearest well. I still use it.}

\subsection*{The Star-Masked (High Pass Cultist)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: Face obscured by a tarnished silver mask etched with constellations that change.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 3 (with 2 followers)\\
\textbf{Harm}: 2\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Omen [6]} – each tick, a star falls. At 6, the cultist knows one secret about a PC.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [HUMANOID] [OCCULT]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Cannot be harmed by the same weapon twice. Worships \emph{the Ninth} (but never names it). Defeat by breaking the mask – reveals a terrified, ordinary face.

\saikou{I broke a Star‑Mask’s face with a silver mirror shard. The man underneath wept and thanked me. He had been wearing it for nine years. He did not remember his own name.}

\subsection*{The Mourner’s Candle (Haunting)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A single wax candle burning with a blue flame. No wick. No smoke. It whispers.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 1 (environmental)\\
\textbf{Harm}: 1 (chill)\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Wax Tear [4]} – when full, the candle cries hot wax, igniting a small area.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [UNDEAD] [FIRE] [TRAP]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Extinguishing it requires a spoken apology to the dead. Relighting it calls the ghost of the nearest corpse to answer one question.

\grey{I found one on a grave in Acasia. I apologised to the bones beneath. The candle went out with a sigh. I did not ask whose grave it was. Some debts are cheaper if you do not name the creditor.}

\subsection*{The Brick-King (Street-Tough’s Bane)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A seven‑foot figure of crumbling mortar and cobblestones, crowned with a rusted signpost.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 4\\
\textbf{Harm}: 3\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Collapse [6]} – each time it takes Harm, tick once. At 6, it crumbles into difficult terrain.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [CONSTRUCT] [IMMOBILE]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Cannot enter a building without an invitation. Will parley if you offer a new signpost crown (any metal pole).

\lerris{A Brick‑King blocked the road to Silkstrand. I gave it a broken lantern pole. It bowed, lifted the pole, and walked away. The next day, the pole was planted at a crossroad with a sign that read “Debt Paid”.}

\subsection*{The Umbral Hound (Shadow Wolf)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A sleek black canine with no eyes, only star‑shaped voids. Its growl silences all sound.

\noindent\textbf{Danger}: 3 (pack: +1 per extra hound)\\
\textbf{Harm}: 2\\
\textbf{Clocks}: \emph{Night Hunt [3]} – at full, the hound phases through walls to attack from an unexpected direction.\\
\textbf{Tags}: [BEAST] [SPIRIT] [STEALTH]\\
\textbf{Twist}: Cannot abide direct sunlight. In darkness, it gains +1 die. To drive off, light three candles at once – the hound will flee to extinguish the middle one.

\dusana{I lit three candles on a tombstone. The hound blew out the middle one, then sat and whined. I lit it again. The hound curled up and slept. It followed me for a week, silent and warm. It left when I crossed a river.}

\section*{Character Inspiration: Two Cards on the Road}
\noindent \textit{When creating a road‑weary traveller (PC or NPC), draw two playing cards. Combine a \textbf{Concept} (Heart/Diamond) with a \textbf{Complication} (Spade/Club).}

\begin{tabular}{@{}p{0.45\textwidth}p{0.45\textwidth}}
\toprule
\multicolumn{1}{c}{\textbf{Concepts (Heart/Diamond)}} & \multicolumn{1}{c}{\textbf{Complications (Spade/Club)}} \\
\midrule
2–3: Exiled noble & 2–3: Hunted by a rival \\
4–5: Grizzled scout & 4–5: Carries a cursed heirloom \\
6–7: Street‑wise fixer & 6–7: Owe a favour to a crime lord \\
8–9: Failed scholar & 8–9: Secretly ill (timer [4]) \\
10–J: Faith healer & 10–J: Witness to a forbidden rite \\
Q–K: Retired soldier & Q–K: Spouse missing (search timer) \\
A: Last of a bloodline & A: Marked by a spirit (visible) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\noindent Then roll or pick a third card for a \textbf{Goal} (any suit):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Red (Heart/Diamond):} Protect someone / find a treasure.
  \item \textbf{Black (Spade/Club):} Destroy a rival / avenge an insult.
  \item \textbf{Ace:} Discover the truth about [a place, death, or omen].
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textit{Example:} 6♦ (Grizzled scout) + 8♣ (Secretly ill) + Q♠ (avenge an insult) = a sick tracker who wants revenge on the poacher who killed her mentor.

\lerris{I keep a worn deck in my saddlebag for nights when the road offers no answers. The cards always give me a direction – even if it is the wrong one. That is still a direction.}

\grey{Do not trust a traveller who has never drawn a card. They are either lying or already dead.}
\saikou{The road is a shuffle. The encounter is the draw. The twist is the debt.}
\dusana{And the story? The story is what you do with the card you did not expect.}
