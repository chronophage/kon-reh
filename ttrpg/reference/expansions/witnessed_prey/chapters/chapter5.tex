\epigraph{“The fae do not lie. They simply keep a different ledger. What you call a trick, they call a fair price.”}{—Aqyl, son of Aqyl, from a lecture on courtesy}

The fae are not monsters. They are **neighbors**. They live in the hedge, the hollow, the space between the ninth step and the tenth. They borrow milk. They leave small gifts. They remember every promise you forget. I learned this in Aelinnel, where a bell‑tender taught me to count to eight before opening a door. I did not ask what the ninth step would open. She would not have told me.

This chapter catalogues the spirits of the threshold: Trow who trade in absences, Hags who price every kindness, the Green Court that watches from the hawthorn, and the Hollow’s own Debt‑Walker. They are not evil. They are **inevitable**. Treat them with courtesy, pay what you owe, and never, ever ring the ninth bell.

\section*{Trow – Shadow Bargainers}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: Enigmatic fey who keep to thresholds and almost‑there spaces.  
They favor the wizard’s guise: wide‑brimmed hats that drink lamplight, weathered grey robes stitched in moth silver, and long clay pipes that smoke without ember. Most hint at an unnamed patron – the \textbf{Grey Benefactor}, the \textbf{Under‑Guest} – but never speak that allegiance twice beneath the same roof.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier III, Cap 4. Incorporeal (when they choose), Intelligent, Bound by Courtesy.  
\textbf{Harm}: 4. \textbf{Fatigue}: Spirit.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Shadow‑Step} (move between witnessed thresholds in Near as a move action).  
\emph{Night‑Guest Writ} – once per leg, declare dusk‑right between two duskmarks; hostile checks to bar passage suffer DV +1 unless they bring a counter‑witness.  
\emph{Almost‑Presence} – in crowds, they slip notice; any social roll to spot them is at −1 die.

\noindent\textbf{Etiquette}: Bread‑and‑salt under a lifted lamp binds the Trow to guest‑right for one night and one truthful answer. Payment must be in honest memory – false recollection voids courtesy. A threefold witness (mortal, fey‑adjacent, liminal token) seals a pact.

\noindent\textbf{Complications}: If a Trow is cheated or a bargain broken, it will not attack. It will simply **omit** something – a name from a ledger, a door from a hallway, a day from a calendar. The omission is not noticed until it is needed.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: To unbind a Trow’s omission, you must speak the missing thing’s name aloud in the presence of a witness who remembers it. The Trow will nod and restore what was taken. To destroy a Trow, you must break its pipe. The smoke will rise and form a single, unanswerable question; the Trow will fade, but the question will echo in that place for a year.

\noindent\textbf{Clock}: \emph{Grey Courtesy [4]} – ticks each time players skip lamp, bread, or salt. On fill, hospitality voids; all bargains turn literal, and the Trow may demand a name as payment for any further conversation.

\medskip
\grey{A Trow once found me alone at a crossroads. He claimed that I cheated him so many times that he gave me his hat. I do not remember the cheating. I do not remember his face. I only remember the hat. It is still warm.}

\lerris{I once bargained with a Trow for a shortcut through the Valewood. I paid with the memory of my first horse. The Trow took the memory. The shortcut was there. I do not remember the horse’s name. I remember that I loved it.}

\section*{Neighbors – The Hollow’s Kin}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: The Neighbors are not a single species. They are a **category of attention** – the forest’s awareness of intruders, manifesting as silence, footsteps that aren’t there, a voice that speaks your language but does not belong to any throat. They appear when the ninth cup is poured or the ninth step is taken. They do not harm. They only remember.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier II, Cap 2. Incorporeal, Attentive, Patient.\\
\textbf{Harm}: Cannot be harmed. \textbf{Fatigue}: None.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Silence} – in the presence of a Neighbor, all sound is muffled. Stealth rolls gain +1 die, but communication beyond Close range is impossible. \emph{The Watching} – a character who fails a Resolve test (DV 4) in a Neighbor’s presence gains the Condition \emph{Marked} – the Neighbor knows their location for the rest of the scene.

\noindent\textbf{Ettiquette}: Do not test them. Do not ignore them. Acknowledge them politely and move on. A red thread tied at a crossroads and left as an offering will appease them for one crossing.

\noindent\textbf{Complications}: The Neighbors do not attack. They **wait**. They will follow a party through a forest, watching, until someone makes a mistake – a broken promise, a forgotten offering, a ninth cup poured.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: To dismiss a Neighbor, a character must speak a truth about themselves that they have never told anyone. The Neighbor will listen, nod, and fade. If no truth is offered, the Neighbor will wait until the party leaves the forest. It will remember them.

\noindent\textbf{Clock}: \emph{Neighbor’s Patience [6]} – ticks each time a taboo is broken (ninth cup, ninth step, unoffered red thread). When full, the Neighbor manifests as a **Debt‑Walker** (see below) and demands payment.

\dusana{I met a Neighbor in the Hollow Wood. It had no face. It asked for a story. I told it the story of the fox who forgot its name. The Neighbor listened. Then it said, “The fox is you.” I have not forgotten my name since.}

\section*{Green Court Fae}

\noindent The courts of the Hazel Queen and the Alder King are not places; they are **obligations**. To enter a court is to accept a role. To speak is to bind. The Green Knight does not fight; he offers trials of courtesy and pattern. The Lady of Thorns does not threaten; she smiles, and the smile is a snare.

\subsection*{Hazel Queen’s Emissary}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A fae courtier wearing leaves and law. Their skin is the colour of hazel bark; their eyes are acorns. They speak in couplets and measure every word.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier III, Cap 4. Corporeal, Courteous, Bound by Law.\\
\textbf{Harm}: 4. \textbf{Fatigue}: Spirit.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Leaf and Law} – the Emissary cannot lie, but they may omit. A successful Wits + Insight test (DV 4) reveals the omission. \emph{Courteous Snare} – any gift accepted from the Emissary creates a \emph{Debt Clock [4]}. When the clock fills, the Emissary may demand a service of equivalent value.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: To dismiss an Emissary, present a hazel token – a nut cracked in the presence of a witness. The Emissary will bow and leave. To kill one, you must break their acorn eyes. The Emissary will scream and crumble into leaves. The leaves will blow away, and a new Emissary will grow from the same tree within a year.

\subsection*{Alder King’s Debtor}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A shadow‑clad figure whose face is a mask of polished alder. They do not speak; they write obligations on slips of bark.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier III, Cap 5. Incorporeal (shadows), Silent, Bound by Obligation.\\
\textbf{Harm}: Cannot be harmed by mundane weapons. \textbf{Fatigue}: Spirit.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Obligation Writ} – the Debtor may write a single obligation on a slip of bark. The target must test Resolve (DV 5) or accept the geas. Refusal causes the Debtor to write the obligation in blood instead; the target takes Harm 1 (Stress) and the geas still applies.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: To dismiss a Debtor, return the obligation slip to the Alder King’s grove. The Debtor will follow and witness the return. To destroy a Debtor, you must burn the grove. The Debtor will burn with it. The Alder King will not forgive.

\subsection*{Green Knight}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: Antler helm, mirror‑bright blade. The Knight is not a person but a role – whoever wins the last duel becomes the Knight. The current Knight has worn the helm for a century and is tired.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier IV, Cap 5. Corporeal, Duelist, Bound by Pattern.\\
\textbf{Harm}: 7. \textbf{Fatigue}: Body 5.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Trial by Pattern} – the Knight challenges a character to a duel of proofs, not blades. The challenger must present three proofs: a correct count, a returned waymark, a courtesy satisfied. Each proof requires a Wits + Lore test (DV 4). Winning two proofs ends the trial without combat. Losing two proofs forces a duel.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: To defeat the Knight in combat, you must strike the antler helm. The helm will crack, and the Knight will yield. To win without combat, satisfy the three proofs. The Knight will bow and offer a way‑cord (a Diamond: safe passage through the Valewood once).

\subsection*{Lady of Thorns}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: Sovereign of a hawthorn court. Smiles like a snare, forgives in exact measures. She has never lied. She has never needed to.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier V, Cap 6. Corporeal, Courteous, Absolute.\\
\textbf{Harm}: 8. \textbf{Fatigue}: Spirit.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Thorn Sentence} – once per scene, the Lady may declare a character “bound by courtesy.” The character cannot take hostile actions for one exchange unless they speak a truth that wounds them (the GM chooses the truth).

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: The Lady cannot be fought. She cannot be bargained with on unequal terms. To gain her favour, a character must offer a gift of equal value to what they seek. To earn her enmity is to be pruned – the Lady will not kill; she will simply ensure that the character’s name is forgotten in her court.

\saikou{I met the Lady of Thorns once. She asked for my silver mirror. I refused. She smiled and said, “Then you will keep it. The debt is mine.” I do not know what she meant. The mirror has not fogged since.}

\section*{The Hollow’s Debt‑Walker}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: The Debt‑Walker is not a spirit of a person. It is the Hollow’s **attention** given shape. It appears when a character ignores three omens (a ninth cup, a ninth step, a ninth bell). It cannot be fought. It cannot be bargained with – except through the payment of a debt.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier IV, Cap 5. Incorporeal, Inexorable, Patient.\\
\textbf{Harm}: Cannot be harmed. \textbf{Fatigue}: None.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Demand} – the Debt‑Walker asks a single question: “What do you owe?” The character must answer truthfully. If they cannot answer, the Debt‑Walker takes a memory of their choice. \emph{Interest} – each time the Debt‑Walker appears, the debt grows. A forgotten promise to return a book becomes a curse on the bloodline.

\noindent\textbf{Complications}: The Debt‑Walker will not harm the debtor directly. It will harm those the debtor loves. It is not cruel; it is **efficient**.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: Pay the original debt – in coin, in kind, or in a story that serves as interest. The payment must be witnessed. If the original debtor is dead, the debt passes to the heir. I have seen a son pay his father’s gambling debt to the Hollow. The Hollow accepted a single coin and vanished. The son’s own debts were forgiven.

\noindent\textbf{Clock}: \emph{Hollow’s Patience [8]} – ticks each time a debt is ignored. When full, the Debt‑Walker claims the debtor’s name. The debtor becomes a stranger to everyone who knew them.

\lerris{I have seen the Debt‑Walker twice. The first time, I paid with a story. The second time, I had no story. It took the memory of my mother’s voice. I remember what she said. I do not remember how she said it.}

\section*{New Variants from the Margins}

\subsection*{The Unwitnessed Door (Threshold Entity)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A door that appears only to those who take the ninth step without meaning to. It leads to a room between places, where two mirrors offer two selves. The room does not guarantee which mirror is which. The Witness and Aveh are not in the habit of labeling their wares.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier IV, Cap – (cannot be fought). Incorporeal, Liminal, Indifferent.\\
\textbf{Harm}: None. \textbf{Fatigue}: None.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Reflection of Witness} – shows the self you are meant to be. \emph{Reflection of Aveh} – shows the self you should not be. The mirrors do not label themselves. The character must choose.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: A character who enters the Unwitnessed Door may choose a mirror and speak a name – either their own true name, or the name they believe they are meant to speak. The GM determines the outcome in secret.

\noindent\textbf{Clock}: \emph{The Door’s Memory [6]} – ticks each time the character uses their new self. When full, the door appears again, offering a second choice – or a return to the old self. The cost is a debt: the room will demand something in return for another chance.

\subsection*{The Counting Bell (Threshold Guardian)}

\noindent\textbf{Description}: A bronze bell that rings eight times. The ninth ring is never struck. If it is, the bell becomes a mouth, and the Hollow steps through.

\noindent\textbf{Stats}: Tier III, Cap 3. Corporeal (bell), Guardian, Alert.\\
\textbf{Harm}: Cannot be harmed directly. \textbf{Fatigue}: None.

\noindent\textbf{Traits}: 
\emph{Alert} – the bell’s ring can be heard for miles. A single ring advances the \emph{Alarm} clock by 1; eight rings fill it instantly. \emph{Ninth Ring} – if the ninth ring is struck, the bell cracks, and a Hollow’s Debt‑Walker manifests.

\noindent\textbf{Resolution}: To pass the bell without ringing it, a character must succeed on a Stealth test (DV 5) or a Wits + Mechanics test (DV 4) to silence the clapper. To destroy the bell, you must melt it in a forge that has never rung a bell. The bell will scream, then fall silent.

\saikou{I have heard the ninth bell once. I was in Thepyrgos. The bell did not ring. But I heard it anyway. The sound was not a sound. It was a silence with weight.}
