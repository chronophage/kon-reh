\section*{I. Introduction: The Theater of Banners}

\subsection*{A. Core Concept}

\paragraph{War as Social Theater.} In Fate's Edge, battles are brief flashes. The story lives in the hours before and after: payroll arguments, muddy roads, who eats, who prays, who runs. Camps, fords, and counting‚ąítents are the true stages of war.

\paragraph{Mercenaries as Narrative Bridges.} Bannered companies move between courts, ports, and steppes. They tie urban intrigue to caravan legs, wilderness marches, and political endgames. Hire them, join them, outmaneuver them‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíeither way, banners connect arcs.

\paragraph{The Condotta System.} Contracts (condotte) are adventures in legal clothing: clauses, riders, sureties, and scheduled betrayals. This module gives you fast procedures to play the deal, the march, and the clash with the same tools as core Fate's Edge (Position / DV / Effect, Strings, and timers).

\subsection*{B. Design Philosophy}

\paragraph{Camp Over Clash.} Put logistics, medicine, and morale on the table. The camp is where choices compound; the clash is where they"re cashed.

\paragraph{Contracts as Stories.} A charter's margins foreshadow your next session. Every clause is a future scene; every omission, a future wound.

\paragraph{Dynamic Loyalties.} Allegiances shift without spreadsheets. One die per scene nudges fortune, pay, politics, and position.

\paragraph{Cross‚ąíArc Integration.} Banners bring Diamonds (leverage), Clubs (pressure), Hearts (people), and Spades (places) to any arc: court, road, hedge, harbor.

\section*{Lore: The Black Banners}

``The contract is the road. The colors are the promise. The pay‚ąíchest is the prayer.''

The Black Banners are not one company but a league‚ąí‚ąí‚ąía loose commonwealth of mercenary outfits that share camp law, pay customs, and a habit of turning up wherever the map is loud. Their colors are always some device on black: a lantern, a thorn, a bell, a fishbone, a broken scale. Black means coin before crown; witness before oath.

They are the thread that stitches frontiers to courts, caravan roads to siege lines. Ask five people who the Black Banners are and you'll hear five truths: quartermasters who can feed a ghost army; paymasters who count twice; raiders with rules; peacekeepers who charge rent; undertakers who sing.

\rule{\textwidth}{0.4pt}

\subsection*{Origins & Reputation}

\begin{itemize}
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Birth at the Crossroads.} The first ``black'' standards reportedly gathered during a famine campaign when city militias and steppe hosts both ran out of grain. A broker chalked clauses on wagon boards; those who signed swore to take coin, keep order, and leave exits. Black cloth was used because no city would lend its colors.
    
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Four Sacks of Ecktoria.} The empire counts four humiliations as ``sacks.'' The banners will say‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíprivately‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíthat the gates opened for Vilikari cohorts and Banners logistics, not for steppe charges. It was a war of ledgers, ferries, and night roads...and the empire paid late every time.
    
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Fierce, but priced.} Their reputation is a machine: frightening enough to deter trouble, disciplined enough to be rentable, and bound by camp law that courts grudgingly respect.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Articles of the Camp (the Eight You Hear Aloud)}

\begin{enumerate}
    ‚ĄĻtem Payday comes before parley. No pay, no move.
    ‚ĄĻtem Leech and bell are sacred. Healers and chaplains travel untouched.
    ‚ĄĻtem Colors at dawn, not at night. Sacks and changes of allegiance happen in light, with a witness.
    ‚ĄĻtem Prisoners eat. Ransom or release; no starving.
    ‚ĄĻtem The broker is a bridge. Don't kill bridges.
    ‚ĄĻtem Take no road you won't defend. (Some add: ``Leave two exits; take one.'')
    ‚ĄĻtem No fire in the hospital quarter. Even if the enemy runs through it.
    ‚ĄĻtem Count twice. Coin, bodies, promises.
\end{enumerate}

Breaking an Article earns a Black Mark‚ąí‚ąí‚ąía stigma that travels faster than mail. Three Black Marks and no reputable broker will carry your charter without triple surety.

\subsection*{How They See War}

\begin{itemize}
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Camp before clash.} A battle is the interest on debts a captain accrued in camp: food, route, weather, rumor. If those sums are wrong, steel won't save you.
    
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Contracts as maps.} A condotta (charter) isn't just payment‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíit's terrain in sentences: rights of passage, shares of salvage, witness duties, who feeds prisoners and at what rate. Good captains can march through a clause.
    
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Loyalties in motion.} They do change sides, but by rule: daylight, drum, witness, surety paid. The gutter word is ``treachery.'' The banner word is ``rider fulfilled.''
\end{itemize}

    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Quartermaster.} The real warlord. Controls Supply, hates surprises, loves rain barrels.

\subsection*{Using the Black Banners in Your Campaign}

Drop a bannered company anywhere you need stakes with rules. They carry their own courts, hospitals, road crews, and songs. In intrigue arcs, they're leverage; in caravan legs, they're escorts or competitors; in wilderness play, they keep the wolves fed and the fires small; in political campaigns, they are the deniable arm of policy, standing under black to make the colors behind them look clean.

The secret of the Black Banners is simple: they turn war back into agreements. And agreements‚ąí‚ąí‚ąílike bridges‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíare only as strong as the people who keep them in the wind.

\section*{II. Core Mechanics Framework}

\subsection*{A. The Four‚ąíSuit System}

\paragraph{Hearts (People).} Faces for your theater:
\begin{itemize}
    ‚ĄĻtem Captain: command, parley, risk.
    ‚ĄĻtem Quartermaster: supply, pay, scavenge.
    ‚ĄĻtem Surgeon/Leech: harm control, disease, morale.
    ‚ĄĻtem Scout/Sergeant: terrain, ambush, tempo.

\paragraph{Spades (Places).} Position and Effect live in terrain: frozen fords, rain‚ąícut roads, half‚ąíbuilt siegeworks, forest lanes under truce. Spades name the rule of the ground and what it does to dice.

\paragraph{Clubs (Pressures).} The SB menu of war: false orders, cut lines, camp‚ąífever, gear breakage, rival aid that arrives late and loud, political "help." Clubs are how the Keeper spends setbacks.

\paragraph{Diamonds (Leverage).} Fictional assets that bypass a roll once when you pay the price: safe‚ąíconducts, bridge priority stamps, debt notes, dispensation writs, blood‚ąítruce witnesses. Put at least one Diamond in every scene.

Use in play: At the top of a leg, draft one of each suit. Highest rank sets a major timer (4/6/8/10). Any Ace = winter & loyalty motif: expect hunger, omen, promotion‚ąíby‚ąíattrition.

\paragraph{Movement, Speed, Terrain, Scale.}
\textbf{Speed} is your Movement Points (MP) budget each round; you spend MP \emph{per hex} moved in the Maneuver phase.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Base Costs (per hex):} Open/Field = 1 MP; Road = 1 MP (ignore first Difficult modifier); Difficult (forest, rubble, marsh) = 2 MP; Very Difficult (bog, scree, shallow water) = 3 MP; Impassable = 0 MP (no entry).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Elevation:} Moving \emph{up} a level costs +1 MP; moving \emph{down} is free but may require a test if the hex is Hazardous.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Scale Modifiers (per hex):}
    \begin{itemize}
      ‚ĄĻtem \emph{Large/Huge} in \textit{Constricted or Difficult} terrain (woods, streets, rubble): +1 MP \emph{per hex}.
      ‚ĄĻtem \emph{Small} skirmish units: ignore the first +1 MP from \textit{Difficult} once per round (they flow).
      ‚ĄĻtem \emph{Road Columns:} Any Scale on a Road ignores Scale penalties while \emph{in column}; entering/leaving column costs 1 MP.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\textit{Example:} A Large unit moving through \textbf{Difficult Forest} pays \textbf{3 MP per hex} (2 MP Difficult + 1 MP Scale). If also going \emph{up} elevation, that hex costs \textbf{4 MP}. These costs are \emph{per hex}, not a one‚ąítime surcharge.

\paragraph{Action & Reaction Economy.}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Movement (Maneuver)} spends \emph{Speed} (MP) according to terrain/Scale.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Primary Action (1)} in the Actions phase, as specified by the Order (attack, rally, breach, etc.).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Reaction (up to 1/round, optional)} may trigger outside your turn when a listed trigger occurs.
\end{enumerate}
\textbf{Reactions are in addition to your primary action} but limited to \emph{one per round per unit}. Mark a small $‚čĄ$ beside the unit when its reaction is spent; clear all reaction markers in the End Step.

\paragraph{Common Reaction Triggers (choose one to resolve).}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Free Strike:} An enemy leaves your ZoC without Disengaging (DV 4‚ąí‚ąí6).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Brace:} An enemy \emph{enters} your front arc from \(‚Č•\) 2 hexes away; gain Position +1 versus that charge/attack.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Overwatch Fire:} If your unit set \textit{Volley/Overwatch} this round, make one prepared attack when the first enemy crosses your lane.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Counterpush:} If an enemy resolves a \textit{Shock Push} into your hex, test to Shove 1 hex back on a hit (opposed).
\end{itemize}
