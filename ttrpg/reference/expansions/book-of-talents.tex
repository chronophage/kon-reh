\section{Rules Clarifications \& Addenda}

\begin{fatebox}[Diamonds \& Assets (SRD Addendum)]
\textbf{What is a Diamond?} A one-use, fiction-backed asset that \emph{bypasses a roll} or grants \textbf{DV --1} where stated. It must be plausibly introduced on-screen.

\medskip
\textbf{Creation:} If a talent creates a Diamond (e.g., \emph{Cutpurse}), you gain a \emph{Light-value} Diamond (small item, document, leverage) usable \textbf{once per leg or scene} as specified. It does \emph{not} occupy inventory unless it's physical; if physical, it can be found, stolen, or lost like any asset.

\medskip
\textbf{Limits:} Max \textbf{2 unspent Diamonds/PC}. Additional Diamonds overwrite one of your choice.
\end{fatebox}

\begin{fatebox}[Tags — Quick Impact Rule]
Unless a talent specifies otherwise, a relevant scene/tag provides:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Edge Tag} (e.g., High Ground, Consecrated, Darkness): \textbf{Position +1} \emph{or} \textbf{Effect +1} (GM/fiction chooses the most honest).
  \item \textbf{Hindrance Tag} (e.g., Slippery, Echoing, Choking Smoke): \textbf{Position --1} \emph{or} \textbf{DV +1}.
\end{itemize}
Conflicting tags on the \emph{same axis} cancel one step. Two or more allied tags cannot stack beyond \textbf{+1 Position} \emph{and} \textbf{+1 Effect} total without a talent that says so.
\end{fatebox}

\begin{fatebox}[Environmental Tags — Scope \& Duration]
Effects like \emph{Ruin} or \emph{Remedy} create an \textbf{Environmental Tag} that lasts \textbf{for the scene} (combat/social) or \textbf{one leg} (travel), whichever the action targeted. The tag can be removed early by:
\begin{itemize}
  \item An opposed success that directly counters the fiction (e.g., \emph{clear smoke}, \emph{shore rubble}), or
  \item A \textbf{3+ SB} GM spend reframing the environment.
\end{itemize}
Scene-end clears temporary tags unless a clock or consequence promotes them to \emph{campaign tags}.
\end{fatebox}

\subsection*{Niche Notes (to reduce overlap)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Healer vs.\ Bard:} Healer sets \emph{Harm math} (DV, step-downs). Bard sets \emph{scene math} (Position shifts, social pivots). They stack, not duplicate.
  \item \textbf{Face vs.\ Bard:} Face manipulates \emph{structures} (Boards, clauses); Bard manipulates \emph{mood and tempo} (Rally, Crescendo).
  \item \textbf{Invoker vs.\ Runekeeper:} Invoker anchors \emph{oaths/warded space}. Runekeeper stabilizes \emph{process/sites} with risk of loops.
\end{itemize}

\section{Runekeepers as Emulators}
\label{sec:runekeeper-emulators}

Runekeepers bind processes, contracts, and sites to a Patron’s domain and then \emph{route} power through method, seals, and audit. With the same chassis (Familiar + Codex + Thaisos), a Runekeeper can convincingly cover many party roles by choosing Patrons, rites, and talents that echo a classic archetype. Patrons are vast intelligences of domain and story—entreating them is power \emph{and} obligation. \paragraph{Advancement Notes.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Monk/Caster} builds love \emph{Counterweave}, \emph{Focused Casting}, and \emph{Geometer’s Focus} to stabilize big plays without stealing the spotlight from martial beats.
  \item \textbf{Monk/Summoner} builds scale minions using simple clocks: [3–4] for a single ally (Fragile/Powerful tags), [6] for a cohort. Keep tags honest and spend ticks for discrete maneuvers.
  \item Consider \textbf{Heroic Moment (7 XP)} as a late pick on stunt-heavy monks; it pairs well with \emph{Flow Step} chains and “save-the-escort” turns.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Table Guidance.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Summoned allies should be \emph{scene-bound assets}, not replacement PCs: give them clear tags, 1–2 maneuvers, and a compact [3–6] clock.
  \item When a rite establishes a \emph{method} (Runekeeper), start a small \textbf{Hunger/Attention} clock appropriate to Patron; pay it off in fiction with witness, rest, or a minor offering.
  \item Keep mobility central: monks change Position more than they change raw Effect. Let that shine.
\end{itemize}
