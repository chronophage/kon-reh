\section{Pereshi --- ``The Roof of the Way''}
\label{chap:pereshi}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Flame-Keeper (Elite):}  
  ``The fire does not forget. It has witnessed every oath sworn in this valley, every treaty sealed with a dropped coal, every lie told in its light. I do not tend the flame. I listen to what it remembers. And it remembers everything.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Caravan Scribe (Commoner):}  
  ``My grandfather copied a tax ledger that had not been opened in three centuries. He found a mistake — a single miscounted bale of silk. That mistake started a war. We do not write for the living. We write for the dead who cannot correct their errors.''
\end{quote}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
The Pereshi highlands are a vast, windswept plateau, cut by deep river valleys and crowned with snow-capped peaks. Ancient fire temples stand beside caravanserais; terraced vineyards climb the hillsides. The people are scholars, shepherds, and traders — proud, suspicious, and bound by a code of hospitality that demands three days of feasting for any guest, and three generations of vengeance for any insult. The Pereshi have no king; their land is a patchwork of petty lords, each ruling from a stone fortress, each claiming descent from the mythic Sun Kings of the First Age. Here, the old empire lies buried under the weight of its own libraries. Every caravanserai has a scriptorium; every fortress has a fire temple; every bridge has a toll that can be paid in verse. The Pereshi are the bureaucrats, magi, and memory-keepers of the Silk Way — because they never throw away a document, and they never forget a debt.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of woodsmoke and old parchment. The way every valley has a different accent. The constant call to prayer from fire-temple towers — but the prayers are in a language no one speaks outside the highlands.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never step on a fallen book. Written words carry the weight of law. To step on a book is to trample a contract, a genealogy, a verdict. The spirits of the words will haunt you.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of the Archive}

Power here is measured in fire-temple charters, caravan tariffs, and the length of a family's library. The petty kings of the highlands compete not through armies but through scribes — who can produce the oldest charter, the most complete genealogy, the most beautifully illuminated tax receipt. The Covenant has a strong presence in the sacred cities, but the Pereshi follow their own law: the word of a Flame-Keeper outweighs a judge's gavel. Travelers are expected to recite a verse when crossing a bridge — the spirits of the water demand it — and to present a red rose before petitioning a lord. The high passes are measured in breath-counts, not miles, and the air is thin enough to make a foreigner's head spin.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The scriptorium of a mountain caravanserai, where lamps burn at noon and scribes copy tax ledgers by candlelight. A Flame-Keeper looks up from a brazier: ``You have come for a tariff receipt, or for a verse to cross the pass, or to consult a book that should have been burned. First, tell me a truth. Then I will decide which fire shows you the way.''

\section*{Spades --- Places}
\label{sec:pereshi-places}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Fire Temple of the Unquenched} --- A stone tower with a copper dome. The fire inside is said to be the first flame.
  \hfill \textit{It has never gone out — but it flickers when a king dies. The flame's colour changes with the season.}
\item \textbf{Library of Unfinished Books} --- A scriptorium where scribes copy texts that were burned by conquerors.
  \hfill \textit{The books are blank. The scribes are ghosts. They copy from memory. Their memory is perfect.}
\item \textbf{Bridge of the Ninth Verse} --- A stone arch over a chasm. Each stone is carved with a line of poetry.
  \hfill \textit{The ninth verse is missing — it was stolen by a king. The bridge groans when a traveller recites a false verse.}
\item \textbf{Caravanserai of the Three Valleys} --- A walled courtyard with three gates, each from a different valley.
  \hfill \textit{The gate you enter determines the price you pay. The price is always a story.}
\item \textbf{Vineyard of the Blind Vintner} --- Terraced hillside where the vintner lost his sight to a curse.
  \hfill \textit{He tastes the grapes to know the weather. He is never wrong. The wine tastes of prophecy.}
\item \textbf{Hospice of the Lost Steps} --- A monastery where pilgrims who lost their way are healed.
  \hfill \textit{The healers ask no payment — only a story of how you got lost. The stories are added to the library.}
\item \textbf{Well of the Ancestors} --- A deep pool in a grove of cypress trees. The water is black.
  \hfill \textit{Drink it, and you remember a past life — but you forget one year of this one. The year is always a good one.}
\item \textbf{Forge of the Silent Smith} --- A cave where a hermit makes swords. The swords have no edge.
  \hfill \textit{They are legal documents, folded steel. Present one in court, and the judge must read it.}
\item \textbf{Shepherds' Moot} --- A circle of standing stones on a hilltop. Shepherds gather to settle disputes.
  \hfill \textit{The stones have ears. Oaths spoken here echo for three days. The shepherds listen to the echoes.}
\item[J] \textbf{Fire Temple of the Ninth Coal} --- A sanctuary where the flame is kept in a brass brazier with nine coals.
  \hfill \textit{The ninth coal is never lit. Lighting it would summon the Hollow. The Flame-Keeper sleeps with a bucket of sand.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Pass of the Unforgiven} --- A narrow defile where a treaty was broken a thousand years ago.
  \hfill \textit{The wind carries the names of the oath-breakers. It never stops. The names are getting quieter.}
\item[K] \textbf{Sun King's Throne} --- A carved stone seat on a peak. Whoever sits there at dawn can see three valleys.
  \hfill \textit{The curse is that they cannot leave. The throne is warm. It grows warmer the longer you sit.}
\item[A] \textbf{The Archive of the First Empire} --- A buried city, sealed under a landslide. The scrolls inside are still legible.
  \hfill \textit{The seal was placed by the last emperor. He wrote on the door: ``Let no one read these lies.''}
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph*{(Keeper/Lord/Archivist)} Flame-Keeper with a brass dipper and a rose; Lord Rostam with a dove cage; Archivist Kaelen with ink-stained fingers and no shadow.

\begin{enumerate}

  \hfill \textit{He never carries a weapon. His camels are his guards. The camels have names.}
\item \textbf{Blind Vintner} --- Hands stained purple, eyes white with cataracts. He tastes the grapes.
  \hfill \textit{``This vine remembers the drought of '92,'' he says. ``This one remembers a wedding.''}
\item \textbf{Temple Beggar} --- Blind, always sitting by the fire. He can see the future in the flames.
  \hfill \textit{He charges a single coal. The coal must be from the ninth brazier. He never asks twice.}
\item \textbf{Oath-Breaker's Ghost} --- Visible only at twilight. He repeats the words of the broken treaty.
  \hfill \textit{He has been repeating them for a century. The words are getting softer. Soon he will be silent.}
\item \textbf{Silent Smith} --- A giant of a man, deaf and mute. He communicates by striking his anvil in code.
  \hfill \textit{The code is a forgotten language. The language is the law. He never makes a mistake.}
\item \textbf{Healer of the Lost Steps} --- A woman with a staff of olive wood. She never asks your name.
  \hfill \textit{She knows it anyway. She writes it in a ledger. The ledger is in a locked room.}
\item[J] \textbf{Poet of the Ninth Verse} --- An old man in a patched coat. He recites the missing verse for a cup of tea.
  \hfill \textit{The tea must be made with snowmelt. The snow must be from the peak of the Unforgiven Pass.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Shepherd King} --- Young, wearing a cloak of uncut wool. He counts his sheep by naming them.
  \hfill \textit{He has never lost a name. The sheep answer when he calls. They remember their ancestors' names too.}
\item[K] \textbf{The Sun King's Shadow} --- A figure that sits on the throne at night. It is not the king.
  \hfill \textit{It is the memory of the king. It gives bad advice. The advice is always true — for a dead person.}
\item[A] \textbf{The First Emperor's Ghost} --- A skeleton in a crown, sitting in the buried archive.
  \hfill \textit{He asks: ``Did anyone read my lies?'' If you say yes, he will weep. The weeping floods the archive.}
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Clubs --- Complications/Threats}
\label{sec:pereshi-complications}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Flame Gutters} --- The temple fire flickers. A sign of a coming death. The death is yours — unless you pay a coal to the beggar.
  \hfill \textit{The beggar's price is a story. The story must be about a death you caused.}
\item \textbf{Forged Scroll} --- A caravan tariff receipt is discovered to be a copy. The lord demands compensation.
  \hfill \textit{The forger is the lord's own scribe. The scribe is trying to frame a rival. You are the pawn.}
\item \textbf{Bridge Recitation Failed} --- A traveller cannot recite a verse. The water-spirits block the crossing.
  \hfill \textit{The spirits demand a verse from a dead poet. The poet is the traveller's ancestor.}
\item \textbf{Landslide (Pass Blocked)} --- A rockslide covers the high pass. The alternative route is three days longer.
  \hfill \textit{The landslide was deliberate — a rival caravan master wants to delay you. His camels are ahead.}
\item \textbf{Blood Feud (Stolen Sheep)} --- Two clans blame you for a shepherd's death. The shepherd died of old age.
  \hfill \textit{The clans do not care. They need an excuse for war. You are the excuse.}
\item \textbf{Poet's Demand} --- The old man asks for a new verse to replace the one he lost. The verse must be true.
  \hfill \textit{If it is not true, he will know. He will not speak again. The missing verse will stay missing.}
\item \textbf{Archive Fire} --- A lamp falls in the Library of Unfinished Books. The ghosts scream.
  \hfill \textit{The smoke forms names. The names are those who burned the original texts. One is yours.}
\item \textbf{Oath-Breaker's Curse} --- The ghost appears at your camp. He repeats the broken treaty. The words are louder.
  \hfill \textit{He wants you to witness his death. If you refuse, you will break an oath before dawn.}
\item \textbf{Sun King's Curse} --- Someone sits on the throne. They cannot leave. The party must find a new king — or break the throne.
  \hfill \textit{The throne is a single piece of stone. Breaking it requires a verse strong enough to crack granite.}
\item[J] \textbf{Ninth Coal Lit} --- Someone lights the forbidden coal in the fire temple. The flame turns blue.
  \hfill \textit{The blue flame shows the future — but the future is always a funeral. The funeral is yours.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Unforgiven Pass's Toll} --- The wind demands a name. Give a name, and the wind will carry it to the oath-breakers.
  \hfill \textit{The oath-breakers will come. They will ask why you spoke their name. The answer must be a verse.}
\item[K] \textbf{Silent Smith's Price} --- He offers a sword (a legal document folded into steel). The price is a memory.
  \hfill \textit{He takes it with his tongs. The memory is gone forever. The sword is sharp — for the first cut.}
\item[A] \textbf{Buried Archive Opens} --- A earthquake cracks the seal of the First Empire's library. The scrolls are exposed to air.
  \hfill \textit{They begin to crumble. The ghosts of the scribes ask for help. The help must be given before the last scroll turns to dust.}
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:pereshi-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2–5 → 4, 6–10 → 6, J/Q/K → 8, A → 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (tokens, receipts, seals) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{fire-omens}—flames that burn green, scrolls that ignite without heat, a bridge that recites a verse in a dead language.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Fire Temples and the Ninth Coal:} Every fire temple keeps a brazier with nine coals. The ninth coal is never lit — it is a promise to the Hollow. Lighting it summons the Debt-Walker. Flame-Keepers inherit the secret of which coal is the ninth; it changes with each keeper.
\item \textbf{Verse Law:} In Pereshi courts, a well-recited verse carries more weight than a witness. The old epics contain every legal precedent; a litigant who can quote the correct verse wins automatically. The problem is that the epics are incomplete — and the missing verses are hidden in the tombs of forgotten kings.
\item \textbf{Caravan Tariffs as Currency:} A paid tariff receipt can be traded, sold, or used as proof of credit. The Pereshi have no central bank; the receipt is the coin. Forged receipts are a capital crime — the forger is forced to copy the entire epic by hand, blindfolded.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross-Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fire Temples and the Covenant (Mykkiel):} Covenant judges recognise the authority of flame-keepers. A fire-temple token can be used as a witness in a Covenant court — the flame itself is considered an incorruptible witness.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth Verse and the Ukwe (Sekogo):} The missing verse of the Pereshi epic is said to be carved on the Ukwe's oldest root. The Lethai-ar know the verse but will not recite it — doing so would call the Hollow.
  \item \textbf{Silent Smith's Swords and the Aeler (Ngomebe):} The silent smiths are said to be descendants of Aeler vent-priors who stayed in the highlands. Their folding technique is a lost art; the Ngomebe masons would pay a fortune for a working forge.
  \item \textbf{The Buried Archive and the Kalrantha Amulet (Dhahara):} The First Emperor's scrolls contain a description of the amulet's creation. The Veiled Sisters have been searching for the archive for a century. The party may find it first — or find it already looted.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Pereshi Superstitions (burn for +1 die once)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bread to the Fire:} Crumble a crust into the temple brazier. Ignore the first \emph{Flame Gutters} penalty.
  \item \textbf{Knot the Scroll:} Tie a single ribbon around a book you are returning. Cancel \emph{Forged Scroll} or take –1 die on your next legal roll (your choice).
  \item \textbf{Name to the Bridge:} Whisper a dead poet's name into the water below. Gain +1 Effect when reciting a verse this scene, but mark \emph{Obligation} to the Bridge's spirits.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Among the Pereshi, power flows through fire, ink, and the weight of ancient precedent. A lord's authority rests on the age of his library; a flame-keeper's influence on the purity of her fire. The petty kings are constantly forging genealogies, and the archivists are constantly exposing them. Power is not inherited — it is cited.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Pereshi society is measured in scrolls, coals, and the memory of the flame. Patronage often takes the form of fire-temple tokens, tariff receipts, or royal seals — each tied to a specific lord, a specific temple, a specific debt. To emphasize this:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to physical tokens (coals, seals, scrolls) that can be challenged, stolen, or declared forgeries.
\item Let rival lords issue conflicting tariff receipts, forcing players to choose whose toll to pay.
\item Use the fire temples, libraries, and bridges as arenas for social contests, where a well-recited verse is a weapon and a forgotten name is a death sentence.
\end{itemize}
In Pereshi society, the fire forgets nothing — and the books remember every mistake.
\end{tcolorbox}
