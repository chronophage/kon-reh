\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of Ash and the Warmth of the Coal}

Power here is measured in fire‚ąíshrine charters, caravan tolls, and the length of a family's library. The petty lords of the highlands compete not through armies but through scribes who can produce the oldest charter, the most complete genealogy, the most beautifully illuminated reckoning. The Covenant has a strong presence in the sacred cities, but the Pereshi follow their own law: the word of a Flame‚ąíKeeper outweighs a judge's gavel.

Travelers are expected to recite a verse when crossing a bridge‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíthe water spirits demand it‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand to present a dried sprig of \textit{feverfew} before petitioning a lord. The high passes are measured in breath‚ąícounts, not miles, and the air is thin enough to make a foreigner's head spin. But the thinnest air of all is in the sealed archive where the Prince's name is buried. No one has opened it in nine generations. The lock has no key. The key is a memory no one wants to have‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand lately, the lock has been weeping rust. The rust tastes of iron and old smoke.

\subsection*{The Unfired Crown: How the Pereshi Rule Themselves}
Ňāabel{subsec:pereshi‚ąíunfired‚ąícrown}

The Pereshi do not kneel to a king. They kneel to fire, to ash, and to a name they erased. Each valley or fire‚ąíshrine city governs itself through a council of elders, a Flame‚ąíKeeper, and a war captain (if needed). There is no central treasury, no standing army, no code of laws beyond custom and the witness of the ninth coal. What unites them is not a crown but a memory of conquest: every Pereshi child learns the story of the Ash Prince, the burning of the great library at Khvarena, and the year the fires went out. They also learn the year the fires were relit‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand the ritual that severed the Prince's name from the world.

The Flame‚ąíKeepers of the nine largest shrines meet once every nine years at Mount Khvarena, the holiest peak. They do not elect a leader. They sit in a circle, each holding a coal from their own hearth. They speak of weather, of harvests, of bandit raids. They do not speak of the Prince‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíbut each of them, before sleep, places a hand on the ninth coal that sits in a brass brazier at the centre of the circle. If it is warm, they know the Shadow is stretching. If it is hot, they light a signal fire and send riders to every valley: \textit{The name stirs. Renew the seals.}

No outsider has ever attended a meeting of the nine. The lowland merchants have tried to send spies; the spies never returned, and their names were erased from every reckoning. The Tulkani do not try; they leave a knotted cord at the edge of the shrine and wait for a reply. The reply is always a single burnt knot.

\subsection*{The Fear of the Lowlands and the Tulkani Unease}
Ňāabel{subsec:pereshi‚ąífear‚ąíand‚ąíunease}

\textbf{The lowland merchants fear the Pereshi.} In the oasis cities, water‚ąíclerks whisper that every Pereshi carries a ``name‚ąícoal''‚ąí‚ąí‚ąía fragment that can lift a name from memory, unraveling every bond it once held. A merchant whose name is struck from the tallies is not bankrupt; he is \textit{forgotten}. His wells dry up. His caravans lose their way. His children cannot remember his face. The lowland guilds settle their reckonings with the Pereshi with unusual haste, and they never, ever allow a Pereshi fire‚ąíkeeper to light a lamp in their counting houses. Some say that the well of the lost oasis was cursed not by the desert nomads but by a Pereshi exile who wanted to make a trading post vanish.

\textbf{The Tulkani have an uneasy relationship with the Pereshi.} They share a border of silence. Both respect the ninth night, but for different reasons. The Tulkani say the Pereshi stole fire from the Dreamer (the sleeping goddess). The Pereshi say the Tulkani forgot how to build a hearth. They trade stories at the edge of the high passes, but never stay the night. A Tulkani who enters a Pereshi fire‚ąíshrine must leave a knot behind‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand the Pereshi burn it. The smoke carries the dream to the sky, but the knot is gone, and the Tulkani cannot retie it. Some Tulkani elders whisper that the Pereshi know how to sever a name because they once tried to sever the Dreamer's name‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand failed. They do not say this aloud. The Hollow might be listening.

\textbf{Everyone else underestimates the Pereshi.} Lowland clerks call them ``backward highlanders with no coin.'' Armored knights sneer at their unpaved roads and unarmored sentinels. Grain factors pity them. The Pereshi prefer it this way. Let the lowlands send their reckoners; the passes are high, the cold is deep, and every seal of passage requires a verse that no foreigner can recite correctly. Those who try are turned back at the bridge‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíor find that their names have been quietly written in ash and left to the wind.

\subsection*{The Ash Prince‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíHeart as Coal, Name as Ash}
Ňāabel{subsec:pereshi‚ąíforgotten‚ąíprince}

Once, the Ash Prince‚ąí‚ąí‚ąía conqueror from the southern magocracy, a man whose armies swept from the red desert to the eastern hills‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíturned his gaze to the Pereshi highlands. He burned the great library at Khvarena. He melted the shrine bells into arrowheads. He tried to erase the fire‚ąífaith, to replace it with the silence of the Veiled Ones. For nine years he ruled from a garrison in the lowlands, sending patrols to cut down any who still lit a lamp before dawn.

Then his empire cracked. The cursed gem that had given him power over fate began to devour him from within. His generals turned on each other. His shadow began to walk independently. He retreated to a fortress in the eastern passes, and the Pereshi followed‚ąí‚ąí‚ąínot with armies, but with priests.

The ritual took nine nights. They did not kill him. They \textit{unmade} him. They spoke his true name into a lead scroll, sealed it with nine coals, and buried it beneath the largest fire‚ąíshrine. They cut his shadow from his body and pinned it to a stone throne (now lost in the red desert). And they took his heart‚ąí‚ąí‚ąístill beating, still warm‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand placed it in a brass brazier. The ninth coal is not a relic. It is his petrified heart. Once a year, on the anniversary of his fall, it pulses. The Flame‚ąíKeepers feel it. They do not speak of it.

The Prince's shadow still walks because the heart still dreams of conquest. The Pereshi do not fear him‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíthey \textit{remember} him. He is the Weight the Fire Lifted. But the weight grows heavier with time. Every broken name, every forgotten oath, every coal that cracks adds another shadow to the flame. If the ninth coal ever goes cold, the Prince will not return‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíhe will never have been gone.
