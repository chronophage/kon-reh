\section{Ecktoria --- ``Marble \& Fire''}
\label{chap:ecktoria}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The flame remembers. The marble cracks. The law forgets — but the statues watch.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Grand Magistrate (Elite):}  
  ``The law is written in marble and witnessed by the flame. Every judgment echoes in the stones — but the stones have grown quiet. The patricians do not listen. They have forgotten what the flame tastes like. They seal their windows with silk and call it governance.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Ferrymaster (Commoner):}  
  ``I've seen the city burn three times. The marble remembers the heat. The flame remembers the lies. The rich have stopped looking at the flames. They only look at each other — and at the cracks in their own mirrors. The fire isn't burning anymore, friend. It's \textit{smiling}.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Imperial decay, bureaucratic horror, memory as weight, Renaissance decadence — a city walled off from its own rot, where the wealthy dance while the poor cough ash.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The Coin-House floor, marble cracked with soot lines, where votive candles gutter beside melted icons. A factor with smoke-stained fingers offers a folded note. ``The Lame King moves tonight. The Basilica burns with him. The Amber Palace hasn't answered a summons in a month. Find out who lit the fire — or the Vigil will audit your soul at dawn. And stay away from the Arena. The statues have been \textit{whispering}.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Once the heart of the Utaran Empire, Ecktoria has worn its laurels into chains. Three centuries of sack, fire, and slow decline have not stripped its marble forums nor its gold-sealed coin-houses — but each day, more of its weight slips into the hands of mercenaries and whispers. The Black Banners march on its purse, avenging old Ykrul wounds by proxy. Processions still crown its stairways, yet ash lines every step. The city burns not only with memory, but with a grief that refuses to name itself.

Yet the true sickness is not in the streets. It is in the \textbf{Amber Palace}, where the old patrician families have walled themselves away. They sit on laurels preserved from the empire, drinking wine that costs a village's harvest, whispering deals with merchants who have never seen a loom. \textbf{Maelstraeus}, the demon of commerce, has threaded their accounts with invisible tolls; every contract they sign forgets a clause. \textbf{Livaea}, the demon of desire, has wrapped their hearts in silk; they marry for influence, divorce for scandal, and love only the reflection of themselves in gilded mirrors — reflections that no longer blink. The common folk call them the \emph{Unburdened} — not because they carry no weight, but because they have forgotten what weight \textit{feels} like.

The \textbf{Vigil} — once the empire's secret police, now a fractured ghost of an institution — fights a silent war over the dream of restoration. One faction, the \textbf{Censer-Knights}, serves the Everflame and the Synod, hunting heresy and latent magic with brass censers and bells that ring at dawn. Another, the \textbf{Unburnt}, answers only to the old patrician houses, ensuring that no whisper of rebellion reaches the Amber Palace. A third, the \textbf{Red Vigil}, has sold itself to Maelstraeus, auditing not coin but \textit{memory} — they collect the names of those who have forgotten what they owe. They do not fight each other openly. They meet in the hypogeum of the Arena, under the gaze of statues that weep marble tears. The statues are not decorative. They are the city's \textit{witnesses}.

Beyond the capital's walls, the province is a patchwork of struggling towns, abandoned fortresses, and grainlands whose harvests are disputed by rival tax-collectors. The Everflame Orthodoxy preaches unity, but each sermon is a coded signal for a political camp. The statues watch. The fire judges. And in the sewers, the Vigil's informers listen for the name of the next person to vanish — not into death, but into the margins of a record no one will ever read.
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of old incense and fresh bread — and beneath it, the faint sweetness of rot. The way crowds part for a procession — and close again for a knife. The marble steps, worn smooth by centuries of petitioners. The way the wealthy never step on the cracks. The cracks are where the petitions of the poor are wedged. The cracks are where the Hollow reads.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never refuse a toast in a coin-house. The tallies are the true judges, and a declined cup is a declined trust. You will not borrow again. But more than that: never accept a gift from a patrician without naming its price aloud. Maelstraeus and Livaea both listen, and a gift accepted in silence is a contract written in water — it will drown you eventually.

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Flame That Remembers — And The Dancers Who Forgot}

Here, the past does not fade — it is polished into monuments. Every statue, every obelisk, every triumphal arch is a legal precedent carved in stone. But precedent is not justice. Precedent is \textit{memory}, and memory is a weight that the city carries like a cracked keel. Ecktoria is not one city but three: the marble forums of the old patricians (where the Unburdened hold masques that last for weeks, their faces painted with ash from the Everflame), the soot-stained alleys of the mercenary camps (where Black Banner captains drink with Vigil informers), and the basilicas where the Everflame's priests balance piety against profit (and sometimes sell indulgences for the sin of being born poor). The Grand Magistrate presides over a skeleton empire, but the real power moves in coin-houses, grain rolls, and the whispered judgments of the Censor's Hall — and in the \textit{dreams} of patricians who no longer remember the scent of rain.

The \textbf{Vigil} does not patrol the streets. It \emph{permeates} them. Every tavern has an informer; every bathhouse has a spy; every marriage is witnessed by someone who reports to a faction of the Vigil. The \textbf{Censer-Knights} hunt unlicensed magic; the \textbf{Unburnt} hunt sedition; the \textbf{Red Vigil} hunts \emph{what was forgotten}. They are not allies. They are not enemies. They are three creditors fighting over the same soul — and the soul is the city's.

The \textbf{patricians} — the Old Blood — no longer govern. They \emph{perform} governance. A session of the Parlement might see two senators debate the price of grain while their slaves hold silver trays of honeyed figs. A decree might be passed that doubles the salt tax, then rescinded an hour later because a duchess sneezed. They are not cruel. They are \emph{distracted}. Livaea has taught them to crave the next pleasure before they have finished the current one. Maelstraeus has taught them that every transaction is a game, and every game is winnable if you have enough coin — but they have forgotten that the house always wins. The house is the Hollow.

The \textbf{Everflame} still burns in the basilicas, but its light has grown dim. The High Priest speaks of unity, but his sermons are interrupted by coughing from the pews — not from illness, but from the ash that falls like snow. The common folk whisper that the flame no longer judges truth; it only \textit{records} it, filing confessions in a vault that no one ever opens. The Vigil has a key. The patricians have a copy. The poor have nothing but their names, which the Censor's Hall writes in ink that fades when you look away.

\subsection*{The Everflame — Faith of Ash and Incense}
The state religion of Ecktoria is a syncretic faith that venerates the Eternal Flame as the divine principle, but its power flows through three great Orders, each devoted to a different Patron. The Basilica of the Unquenched Flame is the heart of the city: a cavernous space of gold mosaics, weeping saints, and the perpetual smell of beeswax and myrrh. Mass is held at dawn and dusk, the congregation coughing through the ash that falls from the censers. The priests sell indulgences in the side chapels — a silver coin for the remission of a small sin, a gold coin for a large one, a horse for a murder. The choir sings in Utaran, a language most parishioners no longer understand, but the melody is beautiful enough to make them weep — and to make the statues turn their heads.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Order of the Oath of Flame \& Light (Orthodox)} — The public face of the church. They lead processions, bless marriages, and anoint the dying. Their rites are those of vows, radiance, and protection. They are the ones who light the great candles at dawn. Many are sincere; most are tired. The tired ones are the ones the Vigil watches.
    \item \textbf{Order of the Inquisitor Prime (Prosecutorial)} — The \textbf{Censer-Knights}. They hunt untutored magic, root out demonic cults, and enforce the purity of the flame. Their rites are those of judgment, purification, and burning censure. They carry brass censers that smoke with bitter herbs, and bells that ring once for a warning, thrice for a raid. They are feared, but also respected — for they are the only ones who still take the flame \textit{seriously}. And the flame takes them seriously in return.
    \item \textbf{Order of the Oath of Mercy and Grace (Hospitalier)} — The healers and confessors. They run hospices, tend to the sick, and hear the confessions of the dying. Their rites are those of healing, forgiveness, and the spiral of care. They are often at odds with the Inquisitor Prime — mercy can look like leniency. The common folk love them; the patricians ignore them. The Vigil watches both.
\end{itemize}

The Everflame is both beautiful and ugly. Its gold leaf covers the same marble where petitioners once scratched their names. Its hymns are sung by choirboys whose families sold them for a loaf of bread. Its relics — a nail from the True Cross, a tear of Saint Lucia — draw pilgrims from across the empire, but the priests charge admission, and the poor sleep on the steps. The flame itself is a wonder: a pillar of blue-white fire that never gutters, tended by the High Priest alone. It is said to show the truth to any who stare into it long enough — and to show the same truth to the Vigil, who watch from the shadows. The truth is always the same: \textit{you have forgotten something.}

\subsection*{At a Glance}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Atmosphere:} Marble grandeur, ash-tipped ambition, the slow rot of imperial forms, and the cloying sweetness of a city drowning in its own nostalgia.
    \item \textbf{Key Conflict:} Legitimacy versus wealth; old families, new mercenaries, and the Everflame's judgment — but beneath it all, the battle between Maelstraeus (greed) and Livaea (desire) over the souls of the powerful.
    \item \textbf{Notable Threats:} Bread riots, arena spills, ducal conflicts, inquisitorial visits, the Red List — and the Vigil's three factions, who may arrest you for unpaid weights, sedition, or simply being \textit{remembered}.
    \item \textbf{Common Rewards:} Procession permits, coin-house credit, laurel deeds, a Golden Edict carved in precedent — and, if you are brave, a secret from the Vigil's own files. The secret is always about a statue.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The Three Ecktorias: Faction Spectrum}
Ecktoria is not a single polity but a battlefield of three competing visions. Most citizens and factions fall somewhere between these poles, shifting allegiance as the grain price changes. Superimposed on them are the whispers of Maelstraeus and Livaea, who have no faction — only those who owe.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Old Blood (Patrician Traditionalists)} -- The ancient senatorial families who remember when Ecktoria ruled the world. They worship precedent, hoard marble, and see mercenaries as necessary filth. Their power is in law, lineage, and the silent networks of the Censor's Hall.  
    \emph{Clocks: \textsc{Patrician Patience} [4], \textsc{Blood Price} [6].}  
    \emph{Patron whispers:} Livaea — they marry for influence, betray for pleasure, and mistake cruelty for wit. They mistake silence for consent.

    \item \textbf{Black Purse (Mercenary \& Trade Leagues)} -- The new power: condotta brokers, coin-house factors, and mercenary captains who have bought what birth could not give. They care about supply lines, not bloodlines. Their loyalty is to the next contract, and their god is the tally — but the tally has no god.  
    \emph{Clocks: \textsc{Contract Spiral} [4], \textsc{Payday Fever} [6].}  
    \emph{Patron whispers:} Maelstraeus — they measure profit in margins, margins in suffering, and suffering in silver. They do not measure the weight of marble.

    \item \textbf{The Everflame (Church \& Inquisition)} -- The priests, inquisitors, and relic-keepers who claim that the Eternal Flame is the only true sovereign. They offer legitimacy to whoever pays the tithe — and withdraw it from whoever forgets. Their power is in pulpits, processions, and the fear of excommunication.  
    \emph{Clocks: \textsc{Heresy Index} [4], \textsc{Sanctuary Violation} [6].}  
    \emph{Patron whispers:} Neither — the Everflame remembers, but the Vigil has learned to filter its truths. The flame's truth is ash; the Vigil's truth is silence.
\end{itemize}

Superimposed on these are the \textbf{Ykrul creditors} (who hold Ecktoria's weight like a leash), the \textbf{Black Banners} (who hold its streets like a lease), and the \textbf{Vigil} (who hold its secrets like a knife — and sometimes use it). A wise politician learns to balance all three — and keeps a boat ready on the Dolmis.

\subsection*{The Vigil — Three Faces of the Secret Police}
The Vigil is not one order but three, each claiming the mantle of the old imperial secret police. They do not cooperate. They do not war. They \emph{audit} each other. They also audit the dead.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{The Censer-Knights (Orthodox, Everflame)} — Hunters of untutored magic. They carry brass censers that smoke with bitter herbs and bells that ring once for a warning, thrice for a raid. They answer to the Synod and the High Priest, but their true loyalty is to the \emph{precedent} — the law that magic must be licensed, indexed, and taxed. A Censer-Knight raid is heralded by a single, low bell. They are the most feared and the most public. They are also the most forgetful.  
    \emph{Clocks: \textsc{Candle-Smoke Sweep} [4], \textsc{Heretic Index} [6].}

    \item \textbf{The Unburnt (Patrician)} — Bodyguards and spies of the Amber Palace. They wear red sashes and carry no visible weapons. Their job is to ensure that no whisper of rebellion, no bread riot, no unpaid toll ever reaches a patrician's ear. They are experts at \emph{redaction} — not killing witnesses, but making them forget. They have forgotten their own names.  
    \emph{Clocks: \textsc{Palace Secrets} [4], \textsc{Redacted Memory} [6].}

    \item \textbf{The Red Vigil (Maelstraeus's Own)} — Collectors and enforcers. They serve no noble, no church, no magistrate — only the record. They audit the wealthy for the wealthy, finding hidden obligations, unpaid tithes, and compound tolls that have grown teeth. A Red Vigil agent never demands coin. They demand \emph{names}. They also demand silence — the silence of the forgotten.  
    \emph{Clocks: \textsc{Toll Due} [4], \textsc{Roll of Names} [6].}
\end{itemize}

The Vigil's three commanders meet once a month in the hypogeum of the Arena, under the gaze of statues that weep marble tears. No record is kept of these meetings. No one admits they happen. The common folk say that if you see a Vigil agent, it is already too late — but they cannot agree on \emph{which} Vigil. The statues know. The statues do not tell.

\subsection*{Spades — Places (revised tone)}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Petition Steps} --- Milk-stone worn smooth by a thousand hopeful knees. The petitions are written in ink and wax. The steps answer in silence, and the silence is a verdict: ``Your claim is under review.'' A clerk sweeps the steps at dawn. He never reads the petitions. He weighs them. The heaviest goes to the bottom of the pile. The pile is the city's memory.
\item \textbf{Tally-Ford Ferryhouse} --- Rope-drums thrum all day; the ferrymaster watches coin and banners. The rope-drums have a pattern. It spells the names of those who paid late — and the names of those who forgot they owed. The ferrymaster has a brother in the Red Vigil. They do not speak.
\item \textbf{Everflame Cloister} --- Candle markets and side-chapels. The flame has a voice. It is the voice of the last liar who tested it. Lately, it speaks in a whisper. The whisper is always the same: \textit{``I remember. I remember. I remember.''} Those who listen too long forget their own names and start reciting the names of the city's forgotten dead.
\item \textbf{Triumph Stairs} --- Marble worn smooth by ambition. The stairs have a name: ``The Rise of the Forgotten.'' No one remembers why. The patricians still climb them at noon, their retainers counting the steps aloud. They never count to nine. The ninth step is a gap. The gap is where a senator fell — or was pushed — or simply forgot to step. His ghost is still climbing. He has been climbing for a century.
\item \textbf{Censor's Hall} --- Red-lacquered tablets record every name, every shame. The tablets are redacted in invisible ink. The truth is underneath. The Vigil has the reagent. The reagent is made from the tears of the last censor who told the truth. He is still crying.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Heads — People \& Factions (revised tone)}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Torchbearer Child} --- Ash-smudged hands, knowing every shortcut through the ruins. The ruins are not old. They are last year's fashionable quarter, abandoned when the mortgage was ``reclassified.'' She has never been lost. The ruins speak to her in the language of eviction notices. She reads them aloud to the rats. The rats pay her in stolen bread.
\item \textbf{Decumanus-Master} --- Surveyor who ``moves'' stones. His rod is a finger bone. The bone belonged to the last surveyor who argued. He does not rewrite the map. He rewrites the \textit{memory} of the map. ``This street was always a cul-de-sac. You simply forgot.'' The Vigil pays him in forgotten names.
\item \textbf{Censer-Knight Commander Vorenus} --- His lantern burns green. He says it shows the truth. His shadow has two heads — one for each faction he has betrayed. He does not see the third head. The third head is his own, from a timeline where he kept his oath. It is weeping.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Clubs — Complications/Threats (revised tone)}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Blocked Bridge} --- A funeral procession for a pauper who died in debt. The law says the corpse cannot cross until the toll is paid. The family has no coin. The law does not accept stories. The corpse is beginning to smell, and the Hollow is beginning to notice a weight that cannot be buried. The Vigil is taking notes on who stops to help.
\item \textbf{Border Dispute} --- A surveyor has moved a boundary stone three feet east. The new map shows your warehouse now stands on a public thoroughfare. The old map is in the archive. The archive wing collapsed last spring. The memory of the boundary now exists only in the cracked floor of the basilica, and the basilica is accepting sealed bids for ``reparative memory.'' The Vigil has already bid.

\subsection*{Diamonds — Rewards/Leverage (revised tone)}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Procession Permit} --- Move sacred flame through a crowded quarter; law walks with you. The permit is a torch. Carry it, and the city will part — or burn. The Vigil will follow. The flame will flicker when you lie. The permit is signed by a clerk who died three years ago. His ghost still signs.
\item \textbf{Golden Edict} --- An exception carved in marble; precedent travels like law. The edict is a crack. The crack in the law is now an opening. The Vigil is already inside. They will leave a note. The note will say: \textit{``We remember. Do you?''}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Quick use notes}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2--5 $\rightarrow$ 4, 6--10 $\rightarrow$ 6, J/Q/K $\rightarrow$ 8, A $\rightarrow$ 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (papers/favor/precedence) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{marble and fire} motifs — statues that turn their heads, flames that burn blue, law that remembers every name — and also echo the \textbf{Vigil's presence}: a cough in the crowd, a seal on a document you did not stamp, a mirror that shows a different face. The different face is always older. It is always watching.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:ecktoria-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Imperial Ruins:} Every district bears half‑collapsed arcades, serving as markets, courts, or shelters for mercenary companies. The Vigil knows every hidden passage.
\item \textbf{Competing Factions:} Dukes, guilds, and priests stage processions that collide at bridges and squares, each claiming legitimacy. The Vigil collects bets on the outcome.
\item \textbf{Monuments as Weapons:} Statues, obelisks, and colonnades are not only symbols — they are barricades, rallying points, and high ground when the city riots. The Vigil has a map of which statues can be toppled.
\item \textbf{Everflame Orthodoxy:} The basilicas preach unity, but each sermon is a coded signal for a political camp. The High Priest plays balance while cultivating his own following. The Vigil decodes every sermon.
\item \textbf{Black Banner Contracts:} Foreign mercenaries fill the gaps left by a shrinking citizen levy, their loyalty secured only by steady coin. The Vigil audits their pay.
\item \textbf{The Amber Palace Quarantine:} The patricians have not left the palace in a decade. No one is certain if they are prisoners or patrons. The Vigil maintains the perimeter — and delivers the wine.
\item \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Saints \& Cults):}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{St. Marcellus of the Gilded Scale (Mykkiel)} — \textit{The Justiciar}. His order enforces unwritten laws; reading a sealed precedent binds the reader to enforce it. His shrine is the Censor's Hall. The Vigil keeps a copy of every precedent.
    \item \textbf{St. Valeria of the Serpent (Isoka)} — \textit{The Shedding Saint}. Pilgrims who confess at her chapel forget one sin — and one kindness. Her mosaic shows a snake eating its tail. The Vigil uses her chapel to erase memories.
    \item \textbf{St. Lucia of the Undying Flame (Oath of Flame \& Light)} — \textit{The Witness of Embers}. Any oath sworn over her shrine's flame cannot be broken; the breaker sees their own face in every wave until they drown. The Vigil swears oaths there — and never breaks them.
    \item \textbf{The Reckoner (Maelstraeus)} — \textit{The Tally Keeper}. A mystery cult of coin‑house factors. They offer credit at the cost of a hidden toll that compounds in blood. The Red Vigil are their collectors.
    \item \textbf{The Ember Prefect (Heretic Cult)} — A schism that believes the flame should not be contained. They meet in the arena hypogeum, chanting the \textit{Unspoken Ninth Citation}. The Grand Magistrate has outlawed them three times. The Vigil has infiltrated them twice.
    \item \textbf{The Velvet Mask (Livaea)} — A secret society of patricians who worship pleasure as the only truth. Their rites are said to last nine days. No one has ever left a rite unchanged. The Vigil watches their parties — and reports to the Unburnt.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Bitter Root:} Daughters of the Cord run a hospice in the Shatterline Bazaar. They ask no payment — but they cut threads. A renegade Sister hides among the arena slaves, trying to untie her cord from a coin‑house weight. The Vigil has her file.
  \item \textbf{Malachai, the Chained Angel:} A silver coin that never spends circulates among the grain‑prefects. Take it, and your store never runs dry — but the coin will reappear in the mouth of a starving child. The Red Vigil uses it to mark those who owe.
  \item \textbf{The Grey Wanderer's Grimoire:} Ecktorian psions are called ``Flame‑Readers.'' They see the future in the Everflame's flicker. The High Priest employs three — and has never trusted a forecast that didn't smell of smoke. The Vigil employs their apprentices.
  \item \textbf{The Threshold Compendium:} The Imperial Arena is a Class IV Poltergeist site — the collective rage of every gladiator who died for the crowd. Saikou Ira would ring a bell eight times and leave a wreath of laurel. The Vigil rings the bell nine times. The Arena answers.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any Ecktorian court, the ninth citation of precedent voids all previous. The wise stop at eight and settle. Those who cite the ninth hear a faint laughter from the marble walls — and their cases are never heard again. The Vigil cites the ninth often. The laughter does not bother them.
\end{itemize}

  \end{enumerate}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Ecktoria's decline is not collapse, but transformation. Wealth bleeds into patronage webs: a senator's indulgence, a censor's indulgence, a noble's purse. The city's great houses keep mercenary captains on retainer, while priests sell ritual processions as public theater and private favor. And everywhere, the Vigil watches — not to stop the rot, but to take notes.

Yet beneath the decay, two demons whisper. \textbf{Maelstraeus} teaches that every contract is a weapon, every obligation a leash. \textbf{Livaea} teaches that every desire is a chain, every pleasure a payment. The patricians who once ruled the world now rule only the Amber Palace, and the Amber Palace is a gilded cage. They do not see the bread riots. They do not hear the Vigil's footsteps. They dance. They drink. They forget that the flame remembers everything.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Patronage should function as both opportunity and trap. Any gift (a purse, permit, or charter) binds the receiver to service, but patrons can die, fall from favor, or suddenly proscribe what they once allowed. The Vigil offers a different kind of patronage: \emph{protection} in exchange for \emph{information}. A party that pays the Vigil in secrets may find doors opening — and other doors closing behind them. To emphasize the decadence and dread:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to visible symbols (seals, ribbons, laurel crowns, Vigil marks) that can be stolen, forged, or voided.
\item Let rival patrons issue conflicting "safe‑conducts," forcing players to choose whose favor matters more.
\item Use processions, funerals, or trials as arenas for social contests, where patronage weights surface and shift allegiances.
\item Introduce the \textbf{Vigil's Faction Clock [6]} to track which Vigil faction (Censer-Knights, Unburnt, Red Vigil) has the most leverage over the party. When it fills, that faction makes a demand.
\end{itemize}
Patronage in Ecktoria is not mere background — it is the engine of politics, coin, and survival. And beneath it all, Maelstraeus and Livaea are writing a contract. The city is the collateral. The poor are the interest. The patricians are the signature — and they signed in blood long ago.
\end{tcolorbox}
