\label{chap:sekogo}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The jungle remembers every debt. The mask is the law. The honey is a promise.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Leopard Society Hunter (Elite):}  
  ``The jungle has an immune system. When a fever rises, we cut it out. When an oath is broken, we collect the price. We are not cruel. We are necessary. The tree remembers every unpaid debt, and we are its teeth.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Honey-Gatherer (Commoner):}  
  ``The bees know my name. They do not sting me because I sing to them the song of the first blossom. My grandmother taught me that the honey is not a gift; it is a loan. One day, the grove will ask for it back.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Afro-jungle folk horror, spirit diplomacy, mask‑law.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A honey-orchard at the jungle's edge, where the trees hum and the air is thick with bees. A masked figure in a leopard pelt steps from the shadows: ``The Ukwe has asked for a name. A caravan master broke his oath to the grove. Find him before the roots do — or become his witness.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Sekogo lies between the Sahel grasslands and the southern plateau — a dry savannah that grows wetter as you travel east, until the baobabs give way to deep jungle and the air turns thick with the smell of damp earth and overripe fruit. Here, the Green Council rules through consensus, honey-orchards are sacred, and the old spirits still walk. Slavery is forbidden; the jungle itself would rise against any slaver who crossed its border. But the Council is divided: Conservators want to protect the old pacts, while Wakers argue that the jungle has grown sluggish and needs to be roused with fever-mists, thorn-spirits, and older, bloodier rites. At the eastern edge lies the Crimson Valley, home to the Lethai-ar — chestnut-brown elves whose silk bridges span red canyons and whose web-law keeps both empires from using the valley as a shortcut to war.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The sound of bees — a constant, low drone that never stops, even at night. The way the trees lean toward you when you speak a secret. The smell of honey and rotting fruit, sweet and sharp at once.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never cut a marked tree. Each such tree is a living witness. The bark grows over the names of debtors, but the wood remembers. Cut it, and the Ukwe will add your name to its ledger.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of the Root}

Power here is measured in debts witnessed, honey tithes paid, and the balance between jungle and council. The Leopard Society hunts those who break the deepest taboos — oath-breakers, slavers, outsiders who take more than they give. The Earth Society keeps the Calendar of Debt, a sacred list written on bark and buried at the Ukwe's roots. The nganga work with minkisi — power objects inhabited by spirits — hammering nails into carved figures to release magic, each nail a new debt to the spirit within. Trickster spirits — children of Ananse, the spider‑trickster — weave illusions and swap debts for laughter. The water spirit Mami Wata dwells in the deep pools of the Crimson Falls, offering bargains to those who dare to meet her gaze. And in the oldest groves, the Ninki Nanka — a serpent with a mirror‑back — slithers between roots, its reflection showing the lies you have told. At the eastern edge lies the Crimson Valley, home to the Lethai-ar — chestnut-brown elves whose silk bridges span red canyons and whose web‑law keeps both empires from using the valley as a shortcut to war.

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:sekogo-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2–5 → 4, 6–10 → 6, J/Q/K → 8, A → 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (favors, debts, shards) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{grove-omens}—bees that fly backward, roots that bleed sap, a mask that watches from the trees. If an A+ appears (special high‑rank result), a spirit of the deep jungle (Mami Wata, Ananse's Child, the Leopard King) manifests directly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:sekogo-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Three Societies:} Sekogo is governed by three overlapping authorities: the Leopard Society (hunters who enforce taboos), the Earth Society (elders who keep the Calendar of Debt), and the nganga (spirit-doctors who work with minkisi). A single elder often belongs to two societies, and the tension between their roles is where drama lives.
\item \textbf{The Ukwe (Speaking Tree):} A living ledger whose roots extend across Sekogo. Every broken promise, unpaid tithe, and unburied body stains its leaves. It answers questions — but never with kindness, and never for free. Accepting its gift incurs a debt that the tree may call at any moment.
\item \textbf{The Crimson Valley and Web-Law:} The Lethai-ar guard the valley with fierce neutrality. Their skin-tattoos (Marks) record promises witnessed. When a Lethai-ar dies, their Marks are cut from the skin and buried at the Ukwe's roots. The web-law is unwritten; each warden carries a different clause in memory.
\item \textbf{Mask Law:} In Sekogo, a mask is not a disguise — it is a role. Wearing the mask of a Leopard Society hunter, a Waker, or a spirit entitles you to speak for that office, but also binds you to its taboos. Breaking a mask's taboo (e.g., a Leopard mask showing mercy to a slayer) causes the mask to crack and the wearer to lose 1 die on all social rolls until they atone.
\item \textbf{Ananse's Bargain (Trickster Mechanic):} Once per session, a character may call upon Ananse's Child to ``renegotiate'' any debt, oath, or contract. Roll Spirit + Lore (DV 4). On a success, the terms shift in your favour — but the GM gains 1 SB and may add a hidden clause that will trigger at the worst possible moment. On a miss, the debt doubles.
\item \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Root Father} — Grimmir. *The ancient pact between the jungle and the first honey-gatherer.*
    \item \textbf{The Masked One} — Mykkiel. *The law that wears a leopard's face; the judge who hunts.*
    \item \textbf{The Spider's Whisper} — Inaea. *The trickster who spins webs in the spaces between oaths.*
    \item \textbf{The Water Mother} — Raéyn. *Mami Wata, who holds the debts of the drowned.*
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross-Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Ukwe's Debt and the Hollow (Eastern Realms):} The Hollow is said to be the shadow of the Ukwe — the space where debts go when the tree forgets them. Aelaerem hedge-keepers leave offerings at the Ukwe's roots to keep the Hollow from wandering.
  \item \textbf{Lethai-ar Silk and the Way of Silk:} The silk bridges of the Crimson Valley are made from spider-sap and oath-hair. Tulkani traders prize the silk for vow-cords; Fhara water-clerks use it to seal well-contracts. A bolt of Crimson silk can buy safe passage across half the continent.
  \item \textbf{Leopard Society and Ashaani Slavers:} The Leopard Society has a standing grudge against Ashaani slavers. A masked hunter may offer the party a deal: help free a group of captives from a Black Hand caravan, and the Society will forget an old debt.
  \item \textbf{Nganga and the Kalrantha Amulet (Dhahara):} The nganga of Sekogo believe the Kalrantha amulet is a rogue nkisi — a spirit-object that was never properly sealed. They want to drive a nail into it to bind the demon. The Veiled Sisters want the amulet for themselves.
  \item \textbf{Ananse's Child and the Grey Wanderer's Grimoire:} The trickster spirit is known to trade fragments of forgotten prophecies for stories. Some psions claim that Ananse's Child taught the first Echo-Readers the art of reading debts in the wind.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Among Sekogo, power flows through debts, honey, and the consent of the jungle. The Green Council rules by consensus, but the Leopard Society enforces taboos, the Earth Society keeps the Calendar, and the nganga negotiate with spirits. No single faction holds all the power — and that is by design.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Sekogo society is measured in unpaid obligations and witnessed promises. Patronage often takes the form of favors, debts, or shards — each carrying a future price. To emphasize this:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to objects that create obligations (leopard claws, Ukwe leaves, nkisi shards) that can be called in at inconvenient moments.
\item Let rival societies offer conflicting judgments, forcing players to choose whose law to follow.
\item Use the honey-orchards, druid circles, and silk bridges as arenas for social contests, where a gift of honey is a treaty and a broken promise is a death sentence.
\item Introduce trickster spirits (Ananse's Child) to complicate simple debt resolutions — they are excellent wild cards.
\item Remember that Mami Wata is not evil; she is ancient. She collects memories, not lives. A bargain with her may be the only way to escape a blood‑debt.
\end{itemize}
In Sekogo society, the jungle remembers everything — and the Ukwe forgets nothing.
\end{tcolorbox}
