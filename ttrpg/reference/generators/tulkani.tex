\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The knot remembers. The story answers. The circle holds.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Caravan Judge (Elite):}\\
  ``The road does not forgive a broken promise. It only reschedules the telling. A vow spoken under the open sky is witnessed by the wind, the grass, and the wheels of every cart that passes. I do not write judgments. I tie knots. The knots remember. When you are ready to share, untie one. If you are not ready, tie another. The circle waits.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Wagon‚àíKeeper (Commoner):}\\
  ``My wagon is my hearth, my court, my grave. The Hollow cannot cross the circle of wheels unless invited. The collector cannot enter without a story. The lord cannot claim his due unless we camp on his land for nine nights. On the eighth night, we leave. That is the law ‚àí‚àí‚àí not because we fear the lord, but because the ninth night belongs to someone else.''
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Itinerant community, story‚àídriven kinship, folk horror at the edge of the Hollow.

\noindent\textbf{Starting Location:} A wagon circle at a crossroads, where nine painted wagons form a ring. A Caravan Judge sits on an overturned barrel, a knotted cord in her hand. ``You are standing on ground that belongs to the lord of the eastern valley. He will be here at dawn to press his claim. But you are in the circle. You are safe until the ninth hour. Tell me a story ‚àí‚àí‚àí a true one ‚àí‚àí‚àí and I will tell you how to leave before the lord arrives. And if you hear a whisper from the Dream‚àíWagon, do not answer. That is not a voice for waking ears.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme & Atmosphere}]
The Tulkani have no fixed homeland; they are wanderers who follow the seasons and the trade routes. Their wagons are their hearths, their circles are their courts, and their knots are their memories. They are welcome everywhere and trusted nowhere ‚àí‚àí‚àí a position they wear with wry humor. A Tulkani caravan can appear at a lord's border, camp for seven nights, and leave before the eighth ‚àí‚àí‚àí because on the eighth night, the lord can demand rent. On the ninth night, something else comes.

Their courts are circles of wagons; their judges are elders who carry braided cords. Each knot is a vow, a story, or a memory shared ‚àí‚àí‚àí but never merely a record. They are the keepers of an older rhythm, one that predates the crowns and the tariffs. Their neutrality is sacred: a Tulkani camp is sanctuary for any traveler, regardless of allegiance, as long as they respect the circle and offer a story when asked.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of woodsmoke and cooking oil. The painted wheels of wagons arranged in a circle, each wheel a different color. The way everyone touches a knotted cord before speaking of a journey.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never sleep outside the circle at night. The wagon ring is sanctuary. Outside it, the Hollow walks. A Tulkani will not rescue you ‚àí‚àí‚àí they will only mourn you at dawn.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{The Curse of the Ninth Night: Why We Wander}
≈Çabel{subsec:tulkani‚àíorigin}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!2,colframe=red!70!black,title={A Fragment of the Dream‚àíCord of the First Night (as sung by the Caravan Judges)}]
\textit{``The Spider spun the net, the Snake shed the truth, the Raven closed the sky. But the one who dreamed ‚àí‚àí‚àí she whispered through the cracks. `Take these threads,' she said. `Tie them in eights. The ninth is for me. I will sleep, and you will carry my waking. Never stay nine nights in the same breath. The Hollow counts. The Hollow follows. If it catches you, give it a story ‚àí‚àí‚àí not silver, not blood. A story it has never heard. That is the toll. That is the mercy.'''}
\end{tcolorbox}

No Tulkani speaks openly of the Dreamer. The old ones say that before the first caravan, before the roads were named, three sisters watched the world burn and rebuilt it from ash. Two of them decided that mortals must never know the fire. The third ‚àí‚àí‚àí the one who dreamed of a different path ‚àí‚àí‚àí was sealed into a sleep that would last as long as the mountains. But before she closed her eyes, she taught a handful of herders and smiths how to knot her dreams into cord. ``You will be my memory,'' she told them. ``And the Hollow will be my shadow. Keep moving, or it will ask you what I whispered.''

Thus the Tulkani became wanderers. They do not stay nine nights because on the ninth night, the Hollow catches up. It does not attack. It asks for a story. If the Tulkani can tell one that the Dreamer herself never heard, the Hollow retreats ‚àí‚àí‚àí until the next camp, the next ninth night. If they cannot, it takes a knot from the judge's cord, and a piece of the Dreamer's memory fades forever.

The Tulkani worship Kuva, the sky father, openly. They light no shrines to the Dreamer. But every circle has a \textbf{Dream‚àíWagon} ‚àí‚àí‚àí a painted wagon with no wheels, its door a curtain of knots. Inside, a single candle burns beside a bed of straw. The old ones say that if you listen at midnight, you can hear the Dreamer breathing. They also say that the Raven's shadow falls across the wagon on the ninth night of every ninth season. No one has ever seen it and remained in the circle.

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Measure of the Knot}

Power here is measured in memory‚àícords, shared stories, and the number of nights camped on a lord's land. The Tulkani have no king, no judges, no written law ‚àí‚àí‚àí only the Caravan Circle, where disputes are settled by storytelling. A liar is exposed not by evidence but by the knots on his cord: if a knot is tied in false witness, it will tighten until blood is drawn. The Dreamer's cult is not a religion; it is a survival tactic ‚àí‚àí‚àí the dream inside the wagon, the whisper in the creaking wheel. The Tulkani give no permanent coin, but they answer in stories. A lord who demands silver will be told a story so boring that his crops fail. A lord who offers hospitality will be told a story so beautiful that his harvest doubles. The Tulkani do not keep accounts; they keep faith in reciprocity. What you give, you get back ‚àí‚àí‚àí maybe tomorrow, maybe in the next valley. But the circle remembers.

\subsection*{Additional Features: Itinerant Mechanics}
≈Çabel{sec:tulkani‚àímechanics}

\begin{itemize}
‚Ñπtem \textbf{The Ninth Night Rule:} A Tulkani caravan may camp on a lord's land for seven nights without payment. On the eighth night, the lord may ask his due ‚àí‚àí‚àí typically a story, a promise, or a small service. On the ninth night, the Hollow comes. No Tulkani stays nine nights in one place. A party camping with Tulkani must leave by the eighth dusk or face the Hollow's attention.
  ‚Ñéfill \textit{Mechanic:} Each night the party camps in a Tulkani circle, mark a segment on a \textbf{Ninth Night Timer [$8$]}. At $7$ segments, the lord (or local authority) arrives to ask his due. At $8$ segments, the Hollow manifests. The party can leave before $8$ to reset the timer.
‚Ñπtem \textbf{Story as Kinship:} Tulkani do not use coin. What passes between them are stories, promises, and memories. A good story can buy a meal, a horse, or safe passage. A bad story can start a feud. The GM should encourage players to tell brief in‚àícharacter stories; the quality (or a Presence + Sway roll) determines its worth. On a success, the story is worth $1$~Measure of value; on a critical success, $2$. On a failure, the listener is offended ‚àí‚àí‚àí the party gains $‚àí1$~Position on their next social roll in that circle. ‚Ñπtem \textbf{Land Rent and Neutrality:} Tulkani circles are neutral ground. No lord may collect taxes or enforce laws inside a circle, regardless of whose territory it sits on. ‚Ñπtem \textbf{Memory‚àíCords and Reciprocity:} Every Tulkani carries a memory‚àícord ‚àí‚àí‚àí a braided strand of horsehair and silk, with knots tied for every promise made and kept. ‚Ñπtem \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}

    ‚Ñπtem \textbf{The Cord‚àíCutter (Isoka):} The clean break; the shedding of false promises.

\subsection*{Story Bonds: Promises and Stakes}
≈Çabel{subsec:tulkani‚àístory‚àíeconomy}

The Tulkani treat stories as the most valued exchange. When dealing with Tulkani, the party may offer a \textbf{Story Promise} instead of coin or goods. A Story Promise is a verbal contract: the party agrees to tell a true story (on a future occasion) to the Tulkani elder or to a specific person the elder names. The promise is witnessed by the circle ‚àí‚àí‚àí a knot is tied on a cord.

\begin{itemize}
‚Ñπtem \textbf{Stakes:} The Tulkani may ask for something held in trust ‚àí‚àí‚àí a minor secret, a lock of hair, or a written name ‚àí‚àí‚àí set against the promise. If the promise is broken, the stake is given to the Hollow (it vanishes, and the party gains $+2$ to the \textbf{Hollow Attention} timer).
‚Ñπtem \textbf{Echo:} A story promised but delayed echoes. Each season the promise goes unfulfilled, the GM may ask the party to tell a \emph{small} story (a paragraph) to the table as a reminder. If they refuse, the Hollow's attention ticks $+1$.
‚Ñπtem \textbf{Forgiveness:} A story promise can be forgiven if the party tells a story that makes the Caravan Judge weep ‚àí‚àí‚àí or laugh so hard she cuts the cord herself. This is a high‚àíDC social roll (typically Presence + Performance vs.\ DV~$5$) and may require an actual in‚àícharacter performance.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage & Power}]
Among the Tulkani, power flows through knots, stories, and the memory of the circle. A Caravan Judge's authority is recognized by every lord from the steppe to the coast ‚àí‚àí‚àí not because of armies, but because the Hollow respects the judge's cord. The Tulkani have no temples, no kings, no borders. They have the ninth night, and that is enough.

\textbf{For the GM:} Power in Tulkani society is measured in knots tied, stories told, and nights counted. Patronage often takes the form of memory‚àíbeads, dream‚àícords, or safe‚àíhaven oaths ‚àí‚àí‚àí each holding the spirit of a promise. To emphasize the itinerant mechanics:
\begin{itemize}
‚Ñπtem Require players to negotiate land rent with local lords, using stories or promises as their answer. A failed negotiation may lead to the Hollow.
‚Ñπtem Use the \textbf{Ninth Night Timer } as a tension mechanic: every night in a Tulkani camp advances the timer. At $8$, the Hollow manifests unless the party leaves.
‚Ñπtem Let memory‚àícords be physical props ‚àí‚àí‚àí actual knotted strings that players can hold, tie, or cut. A cut cord is a dramatic moment.
‚Ñπtem Emphasize that storytelling is a skill: reward creative players with mechanical bonuses, and let failed stories have consequences ‚àí‚àí‚àí a bored lord may tighten his claim, an offended spirit may curse.
‚Ñπtem Treat Tulkani society as a web of reciprocity, not a reckoning of accounts. What you give, you get back ‚àí‚àí‚àí but the circle decides when.
\end{itemize}
In Tulkani society, the knot remembers everything ‚àí‚àí‚àí and the ninth night forgets nothing.
\end{tcolorbox}
