\section{Viterra --- ``The Hedge-Law Realm''}
\label{chap:viterra}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{Every hedge is a boundary. Every boundary is a claim. Every claim is a blade.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Warrior Queen (Elite):}  
  ``The hedge that is not defended is only a suggestion. And the law that is not enforced is only a memory. I will have both remembered — in stone, in blood, and in the ledgers of every parish from the Belworth to the Dolmis.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Fen Reeve (Commoner):}  
  ``The dikes hold back more than water — they hold back chaos. Every rod measured, every stone placed, keeps the flood from washing away not just our fields but our very way of life. Across the Belworth, the Vhasians still claim our hedges are theirs. Let them try. We have lawyers. We have swords. Sometimes they are the same people. The Queen's peace is a contract; she signs it each morning with a sword. I just hope the ink holds.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Post‑imperial consolidation, legal warfare, border adventurism, the fragile crown.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The Hall of Dawning tilt‑yard at first light. Dawn‑knights drill in armor polished like mirrors. Beside them, advocates rehearse arguments with the same intensity. A Queen's Justiciar slides a parchment across an oaken rail. ``Three charters, one road across the Belworth. The Vhasian claimant says the bridge is theirs. The local reeve says it's crown land. The abbey says it's a pilgrimage, not a toll. Carry the Queen's writ, break the Quiet Tolls, and make them agree — by clause or by cut. The Earls are watching, and the Queen has only just secured the throne. Fail, and you'll give her enemies the excuse they need.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Viterra breathes with the careful rhythm of precedent and petition — but also with the raw, unhealed scar of a kingdom that threw off the Utaran yoke and is still learning to stand alone. Once a proud, independent kingdom before the legions came, Viterra never forgave the empire. It was the first to break away when the Utaran Imperium faltered, its hedge‑laws and parish courts preserving a stubborn, deep‑rooted identity that parchment and wax could never erase. Now, under a young and untested Warrior Queen, Viterra is trying to remember what it means to rule itself.

But the crown is not secure. Queen Veralyn Everblood took the throne only after the sudden death of her brother, King Baldwyn Brightstide, who reigned for barely two years before succumbing to a swift, mysterious illness. His death was a wound the kingdom has not healed. The succession was contested: the earls of the northern marches, who remember when their ancestors ruled as petty kings, demanded concessions; the barons of the western borderlands, many with Vhasian blood and Vhasian gold in their pockets, whispered that a woman could not hold the crown. The Queen has spent her first year in the saddle, riding from keep to keep, sealing charters with her own seal, settling feuds with her own justiciars, and — when necessary — proving her right to rule with a blade in her hand. The throne is secured, for now. But the kingdom is a patchwork of loyalties, and every hedge hides a knife.

The realm's true sinews lie not in steel but in writs, charters, and the ancient rights that bind lord to tenant, crown to county, and church to common. Every hedgerow, every parish stone, every ferry crossing carries the weight of custom older than memory. Yet there is no single, unified law. The Queen's law competes with the earls' law, with the barons' law, with the ancient custom of the fens, with the Ubral clan law that still holds in the high passes, and with the Vhasian claims that bleed across the Belworth. A deed that is valid in Valora may be worthless in the Marches. A judgment that holds in a crown court may be overturned by a hedge‑moot at dusk. The very question \emph{which law is law} is the central argument of the kingdom.

Viterra is not a nation at peace. It is a realm that wages law as war, and war as law. The Queen's Peace is not a promise — it is a contract, renewed each morning by every magistrate, every justicar, every clerk who opens a ledger. And the ink is still wet on many pages.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of damp parchment and fresh ink. The clatter of writ‑runners' boots on cobblestones. The way every noble's hand rests on a seal ring — and every commoner's hand rests on a hatchet.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never cross the Belworth without a charter recognized on both banks. Viterran law and Vhasian claim fight every crossing, and the river takes the losers.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: Hedge, Crown, and Claim}

Here, law is a blade. The Warrior Queen rules from Valora, her throne a bench of precedents. She is young, untested, and surrounded by counselors who mistake caution for loyalty — and by courtiers who still whisper the name of her dead brother. The old Fenwood dukes — whose ancestors sacked Lence and left the Vhasian Sun‑court fractured — still hold vast estates in the northern marches, and their claims across the Belworth keep the border smoking like a low fire. The earls of the north, descendants of the old hill kings, watch her every move, waiting for a misstep. The barons of the west, many of whom swore oaths to Vhasian claimants before they swore to her, smile with teeth that glitter with coin from across the river. Every spring, the Queen's justiciars ride west with warrants and escorts, demanding homage from villages that have paid Vhasian tithes for a generation. Every autumn, Vhasian routiers raid the grain convoys, and the legal duels that follow are fought with steel and citation alike. The border is not a line on a map. It is an argument. And the Queen intends to win it.

North, the Ubral clans watch from their cairns, ready to rekindle feuds if a Viterran levy crosses the passes. South, Thepyrgos sends clerks and spies, testing the edges of Viterran jurisdiction with every disputed toll. And in Theona, the Three Greens mumble old curses while Viterran bailiffs try to count their cattle — and always miscount by one. The Queen's rule is not yet secure. Every charter she signs is a challenge. Every justiciar she sends is a declaration. Every duel fought in her name is a step toward a throne that is still being carved.

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:viterra-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2--5 $\rightarrow$ 4, 6--10 $\rightarrow$ 6, J/Q/K $\rightarrow$ 8, A $\rightarrow$ 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (writs/charters/priority) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{hedge‑law} motif — boundaries blur, charters contradict, precedent splinters, and the careful web of legal authority shows its cracks. Legal warfare escalates to physical conflict.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:viterra-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Hedge-Law Momentum:} When a valid \emph{writ, charter, or warrant} is presented in the proper parish, step \textbf{Position} up one category on social or logistical actions in that locale for the scene. The first contradiction starts a \textbf{Border-Lace} [4] clock; advance each time competing papers are invoked.

\item \textbf{Beacon \& Bell Chain:} If two adjacent \emph{beacons} or \emph{brotherhood bells} are under your influence, convert one \textbf{Club} draw into a travel delay for \emph{others} while the party proceeds with improved \textbf{Effect} for movement and logistics. The signal draws scrutiny; the GM may bank 1 SB tied to local authority.

\item \textbf{River-Right Window:} At a ferry, tidework, or customs stair, play any \textbf{Diamond} tied to crossings or seals to open a short \emph{window of lawful passage}: one convoy passes uninspected, or one cargo clears at priority. The window closes immediately if a storm, audit, or levy card is revealed.

\item \textbf{Legal Duel Protocol:} When legal disputes cannot be resolved through argument, parties may challenge each other to a duel. The winner's position becomes legally binding. Requires proper sanction and seconds (see \textbf{Duel Sanction} Diamond).

\item \textbf{Vhasian Claims:} Any Viterran noble with land across the Belworth may invoke a \emph{Ducal Warrant} to attempt to collect rents or enforce judgments in Vhasia. Doing so starts a \textbf{Border Snarl} clock, as the Vhasian claimants are unlikely to cooperate.

\item \textbf{Theona Contest:} The Warrior Queen's authority over the Three Greens is nominal at best. Any attempt to enforce Viterran law in Theona triggers an \textbf{Isle Refusal} complication, and the local taboos may twist the law into knots.

\item \textbf{The Succession's Shadow:} The sudden death of King Baldwyn Brightstide and the contested succession left deep scars. Any legal dispute that touches on inheritance, legitimacy, or royal authority is automatically one step more volatile. The GM may spend 1 SB to introduce a witness, a document, or a rumor related to the succession crisis.

The Queen's justiciars respect her precedent: \emph{A severed debt is not a gift. It is a thread that now binds you to the living.''}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}

\begin{itemize}

They carry no weapons, only a set of iron scales and a copy of the \emph{Hedge‑Law Tables}. A ruling from the Order is binding in both Viterra and the Vhasian marches, but any party who lies before them forfeits their right to appeal. His shrines are stiles and bridge posts; travellers who touch the iron ring on his shrine and speak a destination find the road holds firm. The Temple of Light has absorbed his cult, but the fen folk still leave offerings of eels at his cairns.

  \item \textbf{The Cog‑Cults of the Northern Hills (The Clockwork Monad):} In the high parishes near the Ubral border, secret fraternities of millers and surveyors believe that law is a machine that can be perfected. They meet in abandoned watermills, measuring boundaries with brass chains and reciting the \emph{Canons of Proportion}. The Everflame calls them heretics; the Queen's justiciars ignore them because their charts are always accurate.

  \item \textbf{The Salt Sworn of the Eastern Coast (The Pale Shepherd):} Along the Dolmis shore, a syncretic cult tends to the drowned. They build cairns of salt stone and leave no offerings — only the names of the lost, carved into driftwood. The Temple of Light has tried to suppress them three times; each time, the mission ships have been found adrift with no crew and a single name carved into the mast: \emph{Oblivion}.

  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any Viterran court, the ninth citation of precedent voids all previous citations. The wise stop at eight and settle out of court. Those who cite the ninth are said to hear a faint laughter from the marble walls — and their cases are never heard again.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Legal Combat System (Summary)}
\label{sec:viterra-legal-combat}
When legal disputes cannot be resolved through negotiation, parties may engage in formal legal duels. These combine verbal sparring with physical combat:

\textbf{Phase 1: Pleadings}
\begin{itemize}
\item Each advocate presents their case (Wits + Sway vs. DV 3)
\item Success grants +1 die to the next phase
\item Failure creates 1 SB that the opponent can spend
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase 2: Cross-Examination}
\begin{itemize}
\item Attack opponent's arguments (Wits + Insight vs. DV 3)
\item Success inflicts Fatigue 1 on the opponent
\item Critical success forces opponent to answer truthfully
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase 3: Trial by Combat (if unresolved)}
\begin{itemize}
\item Physical combat with legal stakes (Body + Melee or relevant combat skill)
\item Winner's position becomes legally binding
\item Loser suffers legal consequences (fine, loss of claim, imprisonment) plus physical harm
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
In Viterra, power flows through the careful application of law, the management of ancient rights, and the delicate balance between imperial legacy and reclaimed sovereignty. The Warrior Queen maintains authority through a combination of military might and legal precision, while the old duchies and local officials wield influence through their knowledge of traditional customs and hedge‑law. The true power lies with those who can navigate this complex legal landscape and maintain the appearance of legitimate authority while building genuine support.

But remember — in Viterra, law is warfare. Every document is a weapon, every courtroom a battlefield, and every advocate a soldier. The pen cuts as deep as any blade, and legal victory can be as decisive as military conquest. And beneath it all runs the shadow of the dead king, Baldwyn Brightstide, whose brief reign and sudden death left a wound that has not healed. The Queen's rule is lawful — but is it \emph{right}? The earls whisper. The barons scheme. The ghost of the past walks the hedgerows at dusk.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Patronage in Viterra revolves around legal documents, local rights, and the ability to command respect from institutions still finding their post‑imperial identity. Rewards often take the form of writs, charters, and official positions that can be leveraged into greater influence. To emphasize the fragile crown and the contested succession:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to visible symbols (seals, writs, charters) that can be challenged, stolen, or voided — especially if they bear the dead king's seal.
\item Let rival duchies and Vhasian claimants issue conflicting authorities, forcing players to choose whose favor matters more.
\item Use the courts, parishes, and muster grounds as arenas for social contests, where knowledge of law and custom determines success.
\item When legal conflicts escalate beyond words, transition naturally to physical confrontation with appropriate stakes.
\item Introduce the \textbf{Succession's Shadow} as a recurring complication: old loyalties, forged documents, and the ghost of the dead king can upend any legal certainty.
\end{itemize}
In Viterra, your papers are your power, and your power determines whether you rule or are ruled. But when words fail, steel speaks — and the law says both are valid. The Queen's Peace is a contract. She has signed it in blood. Will you?
\end{tcolorbox}
