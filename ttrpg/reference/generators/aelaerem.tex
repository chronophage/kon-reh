\section{Aelaerem (Halflings) --- ``Hearth \& Hollow''}
\label{chap:aelaerem}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Apple-Matron (Elite):}  
  ``A proper harvest feast requires three things: the finest cider aged in the right barrel, a guest‑loaf baked with intention, and the wisdom to know when to invite the quiet ones to sit by the fire.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Miller (Commoner):}  
  ``My geese know a stranger's step from a mile off, and they've never been wrong. If they start honking at noon, you best check your larder – someone's coming who wasn't invited. And when the Hollow walks, the only safe place is a lit hearth with the ninth cup unpoured.''
\end{quote}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
In Amedell, the Aelaerem keep their bargains with bread, bells, and careful counting. Lanes run under ash and hawthorn; doors reddened with thread promise a seat and a story to any who step right. Their magic is housekeeping writ large – thresholds groomed, names jarred like jam, the Hollow fed with silence and the ninth cup left unpoured. When the Neighbors stir beneath the barrows, the folk answer with kettle songs and lantern law, and the fields behave because someone asked them to. Hospitality here is not kindness—it is a contract. Every meal is a promise, every gift a debt, every candle an argument against the dark.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of baking bread and old apples. The way every threshold has a red thread tied at eye level. The silence of children when a stranger passes the village stone.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never refuse an offered cup. The Hollow counts refusals as invitations. Pour the ninth cup for the empty chair, and you may feed it—or feed it.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: Warm Hearths, Cold Thresholds}

Here, the land is not wilderness—it is a living ledger of favors and omissions. The Aelaerem do not build walls; they build hedges, stiles, and the careful geometry of guest‑right. A red door means bread, salt, and one safe night if you come honest. A cup‑mark stone on the verge accepts butter as a prayer. The watch‑geese know your step before you do, and the scarecrow turns to face the road when the Hollow is hungry. The Neighbors—the things that live under the barrows, in the uncounted ninth step, in the space between the candle and its shadow—are not enemies. They are neighbors. And neighbors, if treated politely, will stay on their side of the hedge.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A cider‑press barn perfumed with crushed apples and rain, where masks hang from beams awaiting the next festival. The Apple‑Matron sits on a stool of woven twigs. Behind her, a Moot Oak leaf bleeding red sap onto a stone. “The Hollow walks early this year,” she says. “The Moot Oak bleeds wine. Will you speak for the field, or let the Neighbors claim it? Speak true—the geese are listening.”

\paragraph*{(Lanes/Orchards/Mills/Downs/Barrows)} Willow ford with flat stones and a bowed pollard that remembers faces; cider‑press barn with sweet reek, old songs, doors that stick after dusk; chalk sheep‑downs with a turf maze cut like a wheel.

\section*{Spades --- Places}
\label{sec:aelaerem-places}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Willow Ford} --- Flat stones, bowed pollard that remembers faces.
  \hfill \textit{The willow has a name. It will tell you if you sit beneath it and listen.}
\item \textbf{Cider-Press Barn} --- Sweet reek, old songs, doors that stick after dusk.
  \hfill \textit{The press was built from a barrow oak. The oak still dreams.}
\item \textbf{Chalk Sheep-Downs} --- Turf maze cut like a wheel; sheep know the pattern.
  \hfill \textit{The maze is a prayer. Walk it backward to ask a favor.}
\item \textbf{Millpond Under Alders} --- The wheel turns some nights without water.
  \hfill \textit{The water is not water. It is the memory of rain.}
\item \textbf{Bluebell Wood Path} --- Rabbit‑gates and snares set by careful hands.
  \hfill \textit{The snares are for the Hollow. They catch only shadows—mostly.}
\item \textbf{Hedge-Tunnel Lane} --- Between fields; nine stiles, locals count “eight‑and‑one.”
  \hfill \textit{The ninth stile is a gap in the hedge. Step through, and the lane behind you changes.}
\item \textbf{Cup-Mark Stone on the Verge} --- Butter left in niches goes missing kindly.
  \hfill \textit{The butter vanishes before dawn. The Hollow leaves a pebble in exchange.}
\item \textbf{Barrow-by-the-Beech} --- Bees go quiet at noon.
  \hfill \textit{The bees are not sleeping. They are listening.}
\item \textbf{Market Green} --- Maypole and stocks; carved village stones turned inward.
  \hfill \textit{The stones face the green. They watch the maypole dance—and the dancers.}
\item[J] \textbf{Dovecote Hill} --- The scarecrow faces the road, not the rows.
  \hfill \textit{The scarecrow has a face. It is the face of the last person who broke guest‑right.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Mother's Orchard} --- Rows straighten if you don't look—curve if you do.
  \hfill \textit{The orchard is a promise. Break it, and the apples will taste of ash.}
\item[K] \textbf{Moot Oak} --- Lantern nails hammered deep; benches at the roots.
  \hfill \textit{The nails are from a hundred feuds. Each one holds a grudge.}
\item[A] \textbf{Hollow Field} --- A ring where no plow will hold a line, not for any coin.
  \hfill \textit{The field is a mouth. It has not fed in a century.}
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph*{(Keepers/Elders/Mummers/Quiet Powers)} Hedge‑witch midwife who ties red thread and unties feuds; miller and his watch‑geese—better sentries than men.

\section*{Hearts --- People \& Factions}
\label{sec:aelaerem-people}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Hedge-Witch Midwife} --- Ties red thread, unties feuds, and knows which births the Hollow watches.
  \hfill \textit{Her thread is from her own hair. She never cuts it—only knots it.}
\item \textbf{Miller} --- Watch‑geese that remember faces; better sentries than men.
  \hfill \textit{The geese have names. One is “Stranger.” It has never been wrong.}
\item \textbf{Orchard Reeve} --- Tally‑stick, cider‑stained smile, and a list of who owes what harvest.
  \hfill \textit{His tally‑stick is an apple branch. The notches grow back.}
\item \textbf{Beekeeper} --- Keeps odd honey aside for “winter churches.”
  \hfill \textit{The honey is from barrow flowers. It tastes of earth and old promises.}
\item \textbf{Shepherd} --- Bone whistle that calls dogs—and other things.
  \hfill \textit{The whistle is from a wolf. The wolf still answers.}
\item \textbf{Lantern-Warden} --- Trims lamps and knows which shadow is wrong.
  \hfill \textit{She lights the lamps in a pattern. The pattern is a prayer to keep the Hollow back.}
\item \textbf{Mummers' Captain} --- Chest of masks, stricter rules than church.
  \hfill \textit{The masks are not carved. They are grown.}
\item \textbf{Traveling Tinker} --- Bright kettles, dull knife for cutting oaths.
  \hfill \textit{The knife was his father's. His father used it to cut a contract with the Hollow.}
\item \textbf{Bailiff of the Moot Oak} --- Polite as a noose, carries a ledger of grudges.
  \hfill \textit{His ledger is bark. The writing grows deeper each year.}
\item[J] \textbf{Wold-Wardens} --- Elders who swear by hedges, not crowns.
  \hfill \textit{They measure time in hedge‑layers. A hedge that has grown for a century has a hundred opinions.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Apple-Matron} --- Hostess of harvest feasts; power sits where she pours.
  \hfill \textit{She pours the first cup for the eldest, the second for the guest, the eighth for the fire. The ninth cup is for the empty chair.}
\item[K] \textbf{Thresher-King} --- A title that moves but never leaves; harvest robes, seasonal authority.
  \hfill \textit{He is chosen by the last sheaf. The sheaf is always a little damp.}
\item[A] \textbf{The Pale Shepherd} --- Comes when lambs are born and when people go missing.
  \hfill \textit{He has no face. His hat is a shadow.}
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph*{(Folk Omens/Rites/Beasts/Night)} Unseasonal fog; the road walks you back to your own gate. Scarecrow turns; it watches the lane, not the field. Black sow through the orchard; hooves scuff every charm.

\begin{enumerate}

  \hfill \textit{The scarecrow's head follows you. The crows are silent.}

  \hfill \textit{The sow was a woman once. She broke a vow.}

  \hfill \textit{The verse is about a barrow that opened. The children think it is a lullaby.}

  \hfill \textit{The blue light is a toll‑keeper. Payment is a story—or a secret.}

  \hfill \textit{The masks are warm. They have a heartbeat.}

  \hfill \textit{The mist has a smell—old wool and older bread.}

  \hfill \textit{The thirteenth pew is empty. The dust on it is fresh.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Harvest Tithe Demanded} --- Hands gloved in leaves.
  \hfill \textit{The hands do not belong to anyone in the village.}
\item[K] \textbf{Moot Oak Bleeds Sap} --- The color of wine; talk turns to knives.
  \hfill \textit{The sap is warm. It tastes of blood and honey.}
\item[A] \textbf{The Hollow Opens} --- Burrows and barrows connect; footfalls answer underground.
  \hfill \textit{The footsteps are counting. They stop at eight.}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{enumerate}

  \hfill \textit{The mark is a notch on a barrel. The tapster will remember you—or pretend not to.}

  \hfill \textit{The ribbon is red. Tie it to a branch, and the thorns will part—once.}

  \hfill \textit{The honey is from a queen who died last year. Her ghost still guards the comb.}
\item \textbf{Shepherd's Whistle} --- Dogs and door‑bolts heed you for one scene.
  \hfill \textit{The whistle is a knucklebone. Blow it, and the dogs will obey—or they will hide.}
\item \textbf{Lantern-Writ} --- The lamps stay lit along your path despite wind.
  \hfill \textit{The writ is a bell. Ring it, and the lamps will not flicker—until dawn.}
\item \textbf{Mummers' License} --- Lawful mask and after‑dark crossing for a feast day.
  \hfill \textit{The license is a wooden mask. Wear it, and the Hollow will see a stranger—not a guest.}
\item \textbf{Orchard Right} --- Pick at Mother's Orchard without offense; the fruit hides a truth.
  \hfill \textit{The fruit is a secret. Bite it, and the secret will become yours—or it will become you.}
\item \textbf{Mill Token} --- The wheel turns at any hour, grain or rumor ground.
  \hfill \textit{The token is a millstone chip. Feed it to the wheel, and it will grind anything—truths included.}
\item[J] \textbf{Apple-Matron's Blessing} --- Hands help unseen when you ask no coin.
  \hfill \textit{The blessing is a cup. Pour it out in trouble, and the fields will send rain—or roots.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Private Moot Under the Oak} --- Elders hear you alone; precedent sticks.
  \hfill \textit{The moot is held at midnight. The oak's roots are the jury.}
\item[K] \textbf{Thresher-King's Guard} --- Six red‑hooded harvesters escort you; doors open.
  \hfill \textit{Their hoods are the color of dried blood. They do not speak.}
\item[A] \textbf{Pale Shepherd's Clause} --- Once, the Hollow will not notice you; pass uncounted.
  \hfill \textit{The clause is a silence. The Hollow will step aside—and will remember that it did.}

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:aelaerem-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2–5 $\rightarrow$ 4, 6–10 $\rightarrow$ 6, J/Q/K $\rightarrow$ 8, A $\rightarrow$ 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (blessings/tokens/host‑rights) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo Hearth \& Hollow motifs—quiet bells, red thread, watch‑geese, footsteps under the soil—so the dread feels woven through the hedges.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Bread \& Lantern:} Presenting a guest‑loaf and a lit lantern at a threshold shifts your next social action one step safer while you remain a guest of that hearth. The lantern must be lit from the host's fire.
\item \textbf{Neighborly Courtesies:} When you keep local taboos (count “eight‑and‑one,” turn village stones inward, leave butter at cup‑marks), cancel the first SB generated by rites or negotiations in that scene.
\item \textbf{Listen at the Stile:} A quiet pause at a stile, barrow, or hedge‑tunnel yields a true omen—ask one short question about what the Hollow wants \emph{here and now}; the answer arrives as a sign, a sound, or a small animal.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Bitter Root:} Daughters of the Chain run a hospice in a hollow field. They ask no payment—but they cut threads. A renegade Sister hides among the hedge‑witches, trying to untie her cord from the Hollow's ledger.
  \item \textbf{The Threshold Compendium:} The cup‑mark stones are Class II Anchor sites—the spirits of old offerings. Saikou Ira would leave butter in every niche—and a silver coin for luck.
  \item \textbf{Malachai, the Chained Angel:} A silver coin that never spends circulates among the mummers. Take it, and your mask will never slip—but the coin will taste of dust every time the Hollow walks.
  \item \textbf{The Grey Wanderer's Grimoire:} Aelaerem psions are called “Threshold‑Readers.” They taste the weight of an invitation. The Apple‑Matron employs one—and has never trusted a guest they didn't vet.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any Aelaerem scene, the ninth guest to arrive at a feast is never human. The wise count eight and leave a chair empty.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Aelaerem Superstitions (burn for +1 die once)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bread to the Barrow:} Crumble a crust at a cup‑mark stone. Ignore the first \emph{Hive‑Swarm} penalty.
  \item \textbf{Knot the Ribbon:} Tie a single knot in a red thread at a threshold. Cancel \emph{Out‑of‑Season Mumming} or take –1 die on your next festival roll (your choice).
  \item \textbf{Name to the Hollow:} Whisper a dead person's name into an empty chair. Gain +1 Effect when invoking guest‑right this scene, but mark \emph{Obligation} to the Pale Shepherd.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Hearth Magic}]
The Aelaerem weave their power through careful observance and small courtesies. Their magic lies not in grand gestures but in the precise attention to thresholds, seasons, and the proper ordering of things. Red thread binds more than wounds—it ties names to places, promises to people, and the living to the Hollow that walks beneath their feet. To offend a hedge‑witch is to invite the Neighbors' attention; to earn an elder's blessing is to walk with the earth's favor.
\end{tcolorbox}
