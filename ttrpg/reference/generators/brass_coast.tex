\section{The Brass Coast −−− ``The Shore of Silver and Spice''}
łabel{chap:brasscoast}

\textbf{Key Demographic: } Humans
\textbf{Capital City:: } Tamralipta

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{Three powers, one coast, and a memory written in salt. The wrong spark will burn the whole shore.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Raja Govind (Native Hill Lord):}  
  ``My grandfather watched the Kahfagians raise their first customs house on our beach. My father paid their tolls in sweat. I will see them drown in their own silence — or I will drown trying. The Sidhi are guests. The Admiralty are thieves. The Brass Coast belongs to those who salt their own fish.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Sidhi Freedman (Commoner):}  
  ``My grandmother was born in Galanina. My mother was born in a slave ship. I was born in a lighthouse. The Admiralty calls me a subject. The Covenant calls me a freedman. But the rajas — they call me \textit{guest}. And a guest who stays too long becomes a target.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Mercantile noir, post−colonial pressure cooker, ghost−lit coast, three−way cold war.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A moonlit beach where three boats meet: a Kahfagian cutter, a Sidhi lighthouse skiff, and a Dhaharan spice dhow. A native pearl diver approaches you: ``The Admiralty raised the salt tax again. The Sidhi say they will strike the ropeways. And my raja is sharpening his father's sword. Pick a side — or the tide will pick for you.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme & Atmosphere}]
The Brass Coast is Dhahara's southern rim: a humid, feverish coastline of palm groves, salt pans, and crowded port cities. Three powers bleed into each other here, and none can draw a clean breath.

\noindent
\textbf{The Admiralty (Kahfagian)} holds the warm−weather ports and the toll schedules. Their warships patrol the shipping lanes, and their magistrates judge by maritime law. They call it a ``client province.'' The locals call it a noose. The Admiralty is not evil — it is \emph{hungry}. Its thalassocratic empire needs new markets, raw materials, and a captive shoreline to fuel its oligarchs' ever−growing appetites. The Brass Coast is a vein they intend to mine dry.

\noindent
\textbf{The Sidhi} are a foreign diaspora — refugees from Galanina who fled the Ashaani slavers generations ago. They control the ropeways, the lighthouse courts, and the freedman's seal. They are welcome nowhere and trusted less, but without them the spice trade stops. Their story is one of forced migration: driven from their homeland, they moved from place to place, unwelcome at every stop, until at last they reached this coast. Like the displaced of another age, they made a new life from salt water and stubborn memory. They resent the rajas for the cold shoulders and the whispers — ``foreigners,'' ``drifters,'' ``they keep to themselves.'' And they resent the Admiralty for trying to subjugate them all over again, trading chains of iron for chains of tolls. The Sidhi make the spice flow — and wherever flow is strong, hands reach to dam it.

\noindent
\textbf{The Native Dhaharan Rajas} are the hill lords who have lived here since before the Admiralty's first anchor. They grow the pepper, mine the iron, and salt the fish. They watch the coast from inland fortresses, and they remember when no foreign flag flew over their beaches. Some accept bribes. Some sharpen blades. All of them are waiting. They resent the Admiralty for the slow colonization — the tolls, the land seizures, the quiet erasure of their courts. But they also resent the Sidhi. Centuries have passed, yet the Sidhi remain ``outsiders'' in the rajas' eyes — a people who keep to their own lighthouses, marry within their own circles, and speak a creole tongue that grates on the ear. The rajas envy the Sidhi's quiet prosperity: the ropeways humming with trade, the seal that opens every port. They do not trust a people who have no homeland and yet have thrived. And that envy sharpens their knives.

The air smells of pepper, cinnamon, and the low tide of a thousand ships. Here, a man can buy anything — a ship, a spice lot, a freedman's seal — and sell anything except his own shadow. But the price is never just silver. The Admiralty takes tolls. The Sidhi take stories. The rajas take blood.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The heat pressing down like a damp cloth. The way the sea is the same color as the sky, so the horizon disappears. The brass hooks on Sidhi belts, the brass scales on Admiralty desks, and the brass earrings in native chieftains' ears — three metals, one coast.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never board a ship that has a green lantern at the mast. It means the captain is under a death vow — and you are the payment. Also, never refuse a cup of palm wine offered by a raja's envoy. It is a test of honor, and the hills have long memories.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of the Toll, The Heat of the Hills}

The Brass Coast is not a colony — it is a slow−burning argument. The Admiralty holds the ports, the Sidhi hold the ropeways, and the rajas hold the land. Each side believes the other two are temporary. Each side is wrong.

\noindent
\textbf{The Admiralty's Powder Keg:} The Kahfagian governor, Admiral Voss, rules from a whitewashed fort that was once a Sidhi stronghold. His marines drink coconut wine and mock the ``lazy natives.'' His clerks adjust tolls with the tide. But his ships are few, his garrison is underpaid, and the rajas have started hiring pirate dhows to intercept his grain convoys. The Admiralty needs new markets because its old ones are failing; the Brass Coast is meant to be the solution. Instead, it has become a wound that will not close.

\noindent
\textbf{The Sidhi's Fragile Web:} The Sidhi run the ropeways, the lighthouse courts, and the freedman's seal. They are the only neutral party — and neutrality is a knife edge. A Sidhi strike would collapse the spice trade. A Sidhi betrayal would hand the coast to the Admiralty. And the rajas whisper that the Sidhi are just another foreign master, trading one chain for another. The Sidhi do not forget the long road that brought them here — the forced marches, the closed gates, the children buried in unnamed graves. They will not be pushed again. But the rajas' envy and the Admiralty's hunger leave them few roads out.

\noindent
\textbf{The Rajas' Slow Burn:} The native Dhaharan hill lords speak a dozen dialects and worship a hundred spirits. They do not agree on much — except that the coast belongs to them. Some, like Raja Govind of the Kalinga foothills, play the Admiralty and the Sidhi against each other. Others, like the Veiled Queen of the Mangrove Delta, have already sent envoys to the Ashaani Breath−less. And in the smoky kitchens of every hill fort, young warriors sharpen their grandmother's steel and wait for the sign. They resent the Admiralty's tolls, yes — but they also resent the Sidhi's quiet prosperity. ``They do not marry our daughters,'' a raja's son might say. ``They do not pray to our gods. They are strangers who have grown rich on our land.'' That resentment is a fire the Admiralty fans when it suits them.

\noindent
The green flame of the Sidhi lighthouses, the brass scales of the Admiralty, and the iron swords of the rajas — the Brass Coast is a tripwire. Any spark could set it ablaze.

\subsection*{The Three Powers: Faction Timers}
Use these timers to track the coast's simmering tensions.

\begin{itemize}
ℹtem \textbf{Admiralty Grip [6]:} Ticks up when the party enforces Kahfagian tolls, sinks a pirate dhow, or exposes a Sidhi smuggler. At 4: Admiral Voss offers the party a Letter of Marque (legal piracy against rival powers). At 6: He declares martial law — all ports closed, all ships searched, all outsiders suspect.
ℹtem \textbf{Sidhi Ropeway Control [6]:} Ticks up when the party helps a Sidhi lighthouse keeper, frees a Sidhi prisoner, or protects a Sidhi caravan. At 4: The Sidhi offer a Lighthouse Charter (safe harbour in any Sidhi port). At 6: They call a ropeway strike — trade halts, and every faction blames each other.
ℹtem \textbf{Raja's Rebellion [8]:} Ticks up when the party smuggles weapons to the hills, assassinates an Admiralty official, or reveals a Sidhi secret. At 4: A raja sends a secret envoy with a tiger's claw (an invitation to an alliance). At 8: The hills rise — native war parties raid coastal towns, and the Admiralty responds with fire ships.
\end{itemize}

These timers are not mutually exclusive. A clever party might feed one to delay another — or light all three at once.

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
łabel{sec:brasscoast−mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
ℹtem \textbf{The Three Faction Timers (see above).} These are the campaign's pressure gauge. Use them to determine which faction acts first when the party dithers.
ℹtem \textbf{The Sidhi Diaspora Network:} Sidhi characters gain +1 die on social rolls with other Sidhi, but −−1 die on social rolls with Admiralty officials. With native rajas, they are neutral (no modifier) — but the rajas will always treat them as ``guests,'' never as allies.
ℹtem \textbf{Native Hill Networks:} Native characters gain +1 die on social rolls with rajas and hill folk, but −−1 die on social rolls with Admiralty officials. With Sidhi, they are cautious (no modifier) — the Sidhi are useful, but they are still foreigners.
ℹtem \textbf{The Toll Timer [6] (renamed Admiralty Grip).} Already covered above.
ℹtem \textbf{Green Flame Justice:} Sidhi lighthouse courts operate on the principle of ``witnessed truth.'' Any party member may request a green flame judgment instead of a conventional trial. Roll Presence + Lore (DV 4). On success, the judge rules in your favour; on a miss, the green flame shows a hidden crime you committed — and the penalty is doubled. However, if the crime was committed against a native, the rajas will learn of it.
ℹtem \textbf{Ropeway Leverage:} The ropeways are the arteries of the coast. A character who controls a ropeway (by owning a share, bribing a foreman, or holding a Sidhi charter) may impose a ``ropeway toll'' on any cargo moving through that district: +1 Supply to the party or −−1 Supply to a rival. Using this leverage ticks the Sidhi Ropeway Control timer by 1.
ℹtem \textbf{Native Smuggling Routes:} A character with a native contact (e.g., a pepper factor or a salt widow) can use native smuggling routes to move goods without Admiralty inspection. This ticks the Raja's Rebellion timer by 1 but also ticks the Admiralty Grip timer by 1 if discovered.
ℹtem \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}
  \begin{itemize}
    ℹtem \textbf{The Green Keeper} — The Witness. \textit{The flame that shows the truth, even when it burns — especially when it burns a foreigner's lie.}
    ℹtem \textbf{The Hook} — Maelstraeus. \textit{The price of a second chance; the weight on a freedman's soul. The rajas say the Hook is Admiralty silver.}
    ℹtem \textbf{The Tide Father} — Khemesh. \textit{The pressure beneath the keel; the toll that the sea collects. The widows say he is a native god, stolen by the Sidhi.}
    ℹtem \textbf{The Lantern Bearer} — The Traveler. \textit{The light that guides lost ships — and lost rebellions. The Veiled Queen invokes him before every raid.}
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross−Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem \textbf{Sidhi Freedmen and the Breath−less (Ashaan):} The Sidhi of the Brass Coast have family who were taken as slaves to Ashan. A freedman's seal can be used to smuggle a Breath−less agent out of Galanina. Captain Idris has done this nine times. The tenth may be his last — and the rajas are watching to see if the Sidhi are still loyal to their own.
  ℹtem \textbf{Spice Trade and the Fhara (Way of Silk):} Pepper from the Brass Coast is a major commodity on the Fhara caravan routes. A Fhara well−judge may accept a Sidhi lighthouse charter as proof of credit. The charter must be witnessed by a living flame. The rajas have begun forging their own charters — and the Fhara cannot tell the difference.
  ℹtem \textbf{The Undercliff and the Kalrantha Amulet (Dhahara):} The smuggler's cove is a known transit point for artifacts stolen from the Himadri temple. The Sea King's ninth wife claims to have seen the amulet pass through. She also claims the amulet spoke to her. It asked for a drink of water. The Veiled Queen believes the amulet is the key to driving out the Admiralty — but only if she can control it.
  ℹtem \textbf{The Drowned Priest and the Hollow (Eastern Realms):} The ghost who walks the shore at low tide is said to be a Oath−Keeper who lost his way. Aelaerem leave offerings of salt to keep him from following them home. The salt must be from the pans of the Silent Widows. Any other salt will attract him — and the rajas have started using him as a weapon, luring him toward Admiralty patrols.
  ℹtem \textbf{The Green Flame and the Dreamer (Ikasha):} The Sidhi lighthouse lamps burn green — the color of a promise kept, a debt paid, a memory not yet drowned. Some Breath−less agents whisper that the first green flame was lit from a coal stolen from the Dreamer's brazier, before Ikasha was sealed. The Sidhi do not confirm this. They do not deny it. They only tend the flame and ask no questions.
  ℹtem \textbf{The Serpent's Judgment (Isoka) at the Freedman's Seal:} When a Sidhi freedman receives their brass seal, they are made to cut a thread in the presence of a lighthouse keeper. The thread represents the slaver's claim. If the cut is clean, the freedman is declared unbound. If the thread frays, the keeper demands a name — the name of the slaver who still holds a memory of them. This ritual is older than the Covenant. Some say it was taught by the Serpent herself, before she turned away from Ashaan.
  ℹtem \textbf{The Raven's Shadow on the Ropeways:} At low tide, when the brass lanterns gutter, a shadow sometimes passes over the ropeways — a shadow with the shape of a bird, though no bird flies at night. The Sidhi workers touch their freedman's seals and whisper a single word: ``Remember.'' They do not say whose shadow it is. The Brass Coast rajas say it is Ikasha's dream, still looking for a shore. The Admiralty calls it a trick of the light. The Sidhi know better.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage & Power}]
On the Brass Coast, power flows through tolls, ropeways, and the loyalty of the hill folk. The Admiralty holds the ports, the Sidhi hold the ropes, and the rajas hold the land. No single faction can win — but any faction can light the fuse.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Use the three faction timers as your campaign's heartbeat. Every session, tick at least one timer based on the party's actions (or inaction). Let the players see the powder keg growing. When a timer fills, something explodes — a rebellion, a strike, a massacre. The party's job is not to prevent the explosion — it's to survive it and choose who rises from the ashes.

\noindent
\textbf{Native Focus:}  
The rajas are not victims — they are players. They have their own spies, their own fleets (via the Sea King), and their own magical traditions (tiger spirits, salt widows, elephant oracles). Whenever the party interacts with the Brass Coast, ask: ``How would a native see this?'' The Sidhi are refugees; the Admiralty are occupiers. The rajas are the hosts — and they are tired of guests.
\end{tcolorbox}
