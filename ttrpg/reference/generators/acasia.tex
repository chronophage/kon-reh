\section{Acasia --- ``Broken Marches''}
\label{chap:acasia}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The Empire conquered it first. The Empire left it last. Now neither remembers why.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Margravine of the Broken March (Elite):}  
  ``The tithe must be paid, not merely offered. A promise written in ash and sealed with iron will is worth more than a thousand gold coins scattered on the wind. The Empire is dead. Its debts are not.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Hedge-Witch (Commoner):}  
  ``Don't cross the Pale Causeway at dusk, especially not if you've got a silver tooth. The bridge'll remember it, and next time you pass, it'll want more than just your footfall. In Acasia, every road has a memory. The question is who pays the toll. The Empire forgot. That's why it fell.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Low‑fantasy wasteland, mercenary realism, cursed borderlands.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The Pale Causeway's last inn, walls soot‑blackened by coal and a century of gossip. A bounty poster flutters beside a half‑empty hearth. The innkeeper is a one‑eyed woman who pours small beer without asking. A stranger at the bar offers a sealed contract: retrieve a dowry chest from a Blackwood matriarch before the Margravine's feast day. ``The chest contains a claim, not coin,'' the stranger whispers. ``But a claim in Acasia is worth more than gold---it is a key to a bridge that does not loop. The Empire's surveyors marked it. Then they died.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Acasia was the first province the Utaran Empire swallowed when it turned its eyes east. The legions marched through the Pale Causeway, crushed the hill‑kings, and planted their eagles in the Blackwood. For centuries, Acasia was the empire's eastern breadbasket and its first line of defence. Grain flowed west; coin flowed east. The roads were straight, the bridges paid, and the Curse was just a farmer's whisper.

Then the empire rotted from within. When the last Utaran emperor died and the provinces broke away, Acasia lingered for another hundred years — a ghost province whose governors still stamped Utaran seals, whose courts still cited Utaran law, whose coins still bore the emperor's face. No one came from the west to collect the taxes. No one sent legions to enforce the peace. The Acasians kept the forms, because they had never learned to keep anything else. They had no native king, no unifying faith, no common tongue beyond the legionary's Latin. They were the empire's children, and when the empire died, they became orphans.

For a century they clung to the ruins. Then the plagues came. Then the famines. Then the Curse — a slow rot of roads that led nowhere, bridges that looped back on themselves, villages that woke to find their neighbours' houses standing empty. The old imperial surveys became maps of places that no longer existed. The grain blights turned the storehouses to dust. The free companies, once hired to keep the peace, became the only law.

That was two hundred years ago.

Now Acasia is a wound that will not close. It sits between Ecktoria to the west and Vhasia to the east, claimed by both, ruled by neither. Petty kings raise their banners in burned keeps. Free Companies trade loyalty like grain, swapping colours mid‑march. The Margravine of the Broken March keeps a tally of feuds, binding bloodlines to her feast days. The Lame King travels on a throne carried by debtors, making law by pointing. And the Curse — the nameless thing that makes crossroads turn back on themselves — is not a myth. It is the weight of broken promises, and Acasia is drowning in them.

South, on the marshy islands of the Amaranthine coast, the city of Silkstrand squats on reclaimed silt, trading in silk, silver, and secrets. The Acasians call it ``the Sink'' — a place where the Curse thins, but where law is measured in coin, not custom. Few Acasians go there. Fewer return unchanged.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of mildew on old parchment and wet ash. The way every milestone has been recarved — first in Utaran, then in a half‑dozen failed scripts. The silence of a village that heard you coming and chose not to open its gates.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never sign a contract on a bridge. The river is a witness, and it never forgets a signature. The Empire's last governor signed a treaty on the Pont‑du‑Tithe. No one knows what it said. The bridge remembers.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: Famine, Ambition, and the Ghost of Empire}

Here, the past is not a memory — it is a predator. The Utaran Empire left behind roads that lead to nowhere, laws that contradict each other, and a hunger that no harvest can fill. Acasia's petty kings hold court in burned keeps, their crowns forged from iron nails pulled from famine coffins. The Margravine keeps a tally of feuds, binding bloodlines to her feast days. Free Companies trade loyalty like grain, swapping colors mid‑march. And beneath it all, the Curse waits. It is not a monster. It is a geometry: the wrong turn, the bridge that leads back to the same shore, the bell that tolls for no funeral. In Acasia, the land itself has become a contract, and the fine print is written in mildew.

The Curse is not ancient. It is a legacy. Some say it began with the last Imperial Survey, when the surveyors tried to map every bridge, every ford, every toll—and in the measuring, broke something. Others say it was the plagues, when the dead were buried in unmarked pits and the priests forgot the proper rites. The hedge‑witches say it was the empire's own fault: they ruled Acasia for centuries but never taught the Acasians to rule themselves. When the empire left, the land had no king, no law, no memory. The Curse filled the gap.

Now the Margravine and the Lame King fight over the scraps, while free companies sell their swords to whichever claimant pays first. The hedgewitches tie red thread around bridges and leave offerings of salt at crossroads, hoping to keep the Curse from spreading. The villagers pray to saints the Empire forgot. And the roads — the old imperial roads — still march west toward a capital that no longer exists.

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:acasia-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2--5 $\rightarrow$ 4, 6--10 $\rightarrow$ 6, J/Q/K $\rightarrow$ 8, A $\rightarrow$ 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (papers/claims/safe‑conducts) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo plague and curse motifs---crossroads that turn back on themselves, bells tolling for no funeral, banners blackened by mildew, the ghost of the empire in every milestone.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Feud as Currency:} Any insult, accident, or rumor can birth a feud; the GM may replace an SB result with a feud‑token tied to a petty lord or house. Feud‑tokens act as reusable complications: once per scene, the GM can spend a feud‑token to have a rival appear, a bridge close, or a village refuse aid.
\item \textbf{Rot \& Tithe:} Each market or harvest scene carries a risk of rot---add a pressure if food or coin are exchanged without a blessing or a seal. A failed roll may double the tithe or spoil stored supplies.
\item \textbf{The Curse Remembers:} When roads or negotiations stall, the GM may quietly repeat imagery---crossroads, whispers, mildew---to remind players that Acasia itself resists resolution. If the party ignores three such omens, the \emph{Curse Stirrs} complication triggers automatically.
\item \textbf{Empire's Ghost:} Any ancient imperial structure (milestone, bridge, fortress) may still be haunted by the echo of its original purpose. A character who makes an offering (a coin, a salute, a whispered oath) may gain +1 die on a single roll within sight of that structure. The offering must be sincere. The empire does not accept bribes. It accepts memory.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Bitter Root:} Daughters of the Cord run a hospice in a burned abbey. They ask no payment---but they cut threads. A renegade Sister hides among the charcoalers, trying to untie her cord from a Rothari death warrant. She says the empire's last debt is still unpaid.
  \item \textbf{The Threshold Compendium:} The Pale Causeway is a Class II Anchor site---the ghosts of workers who died building it still carry stones. Saikou Ira would leave a silver coin in every crack. The workers do not spend the coins. They stack them. They are building something.
  \item \textbf{Malachai, the Chained Angel:} A silver coin that never spends circulates among the toll‑collectors. Take it, and your passage is always free---but the coin will taste of blood every time the Causeway floods. The coin was minted in the empire's last year. The emperor's face is worn smooth.
  \item \textbf{The Grey Wanderer's Grimoire:} Acasian psions are called ``Feud‑Readers.'' They taste the weight of old grudges in the water. The Margravine employs two---and has never trusted a settlement they didn't whisper. They say the Curse is not a thing. It is a conversation. The empire is still talking.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any Acasian scene, the ninth toll paid at a bridge is never the last. The wise pay eight and barter for the ninth. The ninth toll is collected by a child with no name. The child is the Curse's accountant.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Acasian Superstitions (burn for +1 die once)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bread to the Milestone:} Crumble a crust at a broken milestone. Ignore the first \emph{Peat-Fog} penalty.
  \item \textbf{Knot the Contract:} Tie a single knot in a writ's ribbon. Cancel \emph{Bridge Feud} or take --1 die on your next negotiation (your choice).
  \item \textbf{Name to the Blight:} Whisper a dead tax collector's name into a grain sack. Gain +1 Effect when avoiding tithes this scene, but mark \emph{Obligation} to the Margravine.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Decline \& Patronage}]
Patronage here is as brittle as candle wax. The Rothari Clan holds their marches by fear and iron law---patronage from them is real, but cruel, demanding service in blood or levy. Elsewhere, noble names are little more than titles printed on parchment and sold like promises. The Margravine keeps a tally of feuds, binding bloodlines to her feast days, while mercenary captains trade loyalty for coin, switching colors with each bell.

To seek a patron in Acasia is to choose which hunger you will feed: the Rothari's iron teeth, a Free Company's purse, or the hollow blessing of a saint who may never have lived. Yet even false crowns cast shadows long enough to shelter those desperate enough to kneel. The empire is dead. Its debts are not. And the Curse is patient.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Boss Archetypes in Acasia}
\label{sec:acasia-bosses}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The False King (Feud/Authority)} --- A petty lord or pretender who crowns themselves on the ruins of empire. \emph{Phases}: Levy Demanded $\rightarrow$ Feud Declared $\rightarrow$ Hasty Coronation. \emph{Cracks}: a \textbf{Blood-Peace Charter} suspends his authority; a \textbf{Marriage Proxy} binds him to a rival.
\item \textbf{The Witch of the Blackwoods (Witchcraft/Curse)} --- A hedge‑witch, matriarch, or revenant who manipulates feuds and curses. \emph{Phases}: Tithe of Fear $\rightarrow$ Curse of the Crossroads $\rightarrow$ Hollow Wedding. \emph{Cracks}: a \textbf{Curse-Redemption Rite} breaks her hold; a \textbf{Mine-Share} gives the commune courage to defy her.
\item \textbf{The Tithe Collector (Greed/Extortion)} --- A bureaucrat‑priest with writs, escorts, and the ability to summon feuds or famine with a single decree. \emph{Phases}: Audit of Souls $\rightarrow$ Seizure of Grain $\rightarrow$ The Reckoning. \emph{Cracks}: a \textbf{Toll-Exemption Plaque} voids his claim; a \textbf{Wine-Right} buys off his debtors.
\item \textbf{The Rotting Child (Mystery/Curse)} --- A folkloric horror tied to Acasia's plague of false crossroads. \emph{Phases}: Laughter in the Fog $\rightarrow$ The Bridge That Loops $\rightarrow$ The Feast of Ends. \emph{Cracks}: a \textbf{Tithe-Remission Writ} fed to the fog; a \textbf{Monastery Letter} read aloud at the crossroads.
\item \textbf{The Imperial Ghost (Undead/Precedent)} --- The shade of the last imperial governor, still trying to collect the taxes. \emph{Phases}: Audit of the Damned $\rightarrow$ Summon the Debtors $\rightarrow$ The Emperor's Peace. \emph{Cracks}: produce the original imperial charter (in the Sealed Dowry Chest), pay a wergild in the empire's own debased coinage, or convince the ghost that the empire is truly dead.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Encounter Seeds (Quick Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Dowry That Walked:} A Blackwood matriarch's dowry chest has been stolen. The chest contains a claim to a bridge that never floods---and a curse that the Margravine wants broken. The chest was last seen in the hands of a free company captain who claims he is the heir to the empire.
\item \textbf{The Lame King's Feast:} The Lame King invites the party to a feast. The feast is a trial. The food is memory. The wine is lies. The king wants to know who killed the last imperial surveyor. The surveyor is sitting at the table.
\item \textbf{The Curse of the Pale Causeway:} The Causeway is repeating. Every crossing brings the party back to the same inn. The innkeeper is missing a finger. She says she gave it to the bridge. The bridge wants more. The bridge remembers the empire's last toll.
\end{itemize}
