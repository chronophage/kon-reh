\section{Taharka --- ``The Monsoon Crown''}
\label{chap:taharka}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The mountain listens. The horse remembers. The monsoon keeps the ledger.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Ridge-Watcher Captain (Elite):}  
  ``The passes remember every army that tried to climb them. The mountains do not forgive haste, and they do not forget a foreign banner. We do not guard the highlands. We listen to them. They tell us who to let through.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Horse Trader (Commoner):}  
  ``Before you praise the rider, praise the horse. Before you praise the horse, praise the mountain that grew its lungs. A Taharkan foal is born breathing thin air. That is why our cavalry has never been broken. The monsoon gives us wind; the horse gives us wings.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Monsoon trade, highland fortresses, spirit‑ridden passes, oral law.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A rock-chapel at the head of the Silver Pass, where the air is cold and the wind smells of snow. A Ridge-Watcher captain blows a horn of ibex horn: ``The Crown Council has declared a muster. The passes are closing to all foreign traffic. You have until dusk to declare your business — or turn back. The shetani are restless. They taste fear on the wind.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Taharka grows rich on monsoon trade — spices, coffee, and frankincense that travel east to the distant ocean and west to the Gulf. The kingdom has never been conquered; its passes are a graveyard for Ashaani armies and a barrier that Oshiiran grain barges cannot cross. The highlands are a fortress of rock and thin air, where every valley speaks a different dialect and every ridge has a watchtower. On the coast, the monsoon winds bring dhows from Dhahara, Ayokha, and the eastern isles, and a trade creole — a blend of Bantu, Arabic, and Cushitic tongues — has grown from a dozen voices, the language of the wharves where a merchant can bargain in verse and a captain can curse in seven accents. The old spirits — the \textit{shetani} — live in springs, baobabs, and the bones of ancestors. The \textit{popobawa} hunts at the dark of the moon. The \textit{ngoma} drums speak faster than horns. And the passes themselves are alive, breathing with the memory of every army that froze or fell.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of roasting coffee and frankincense. The way the air thins as you climb, until each breath is a measured gift. The constant lowing of pack mules on terraced paths.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never refuse a second cup of tea. The first is hospitality; the second is a test of honour; the third is a promise. Refuse the second, and you have insulted the house — and the house’s ancestors, who are listening through the floorboards.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of the Pass}

Power here is measured in passes, horse lines, and the favour of the monsoon. The Crown Council rules from the high plateau, but each valley has its own lord, each pass its own toll-keeper. The Ridge-Watchers are the eyes of the kingdom, stationed in rock-chapels that double as signal towers. The horse is not a mount; it is a lineage, a treaty, a weapon. To control the highland breeds is to control the passes — and to control the passes is to remain free. The lowland coast operates under a different law: the \textit{ujamaa} of the dhows, a mutual aid network where every captain owes a share of every voyage to the community. A captain who cheats the \textit{ujamaa} finds his ship’s name struck from every mooring post in the archipelago.

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:taharka-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2–5 → 4, 6–10 → 6, J/Q/K → 8, A → 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (bonds, stones, measures) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{monsoon-omens}—horses that speak in riddles, coffee that tastes of snow, a pass that closes behind you, a spider that weaves the future into its web.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:taharka-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Ridge-Watcher Signals:} A network of signal towers on every peak. A fire, a mirror, or a horn can send a message across the entire kingdom in a day. The Ridge-Watchers are neutral in Crown politics; they serve only the passes. A character who spends an action to light a signal fire may reduce the next weather hazard by 1 segment, but the fire also attracts shetani (GM gains 1 SB).
\item \textbf{Ujamaa of the Dhows (Mutual Aid):} Along the coast, every captain owes a share of every voyage to the community. A character who contributes to the ujamaa (by sharing cargo, repairing a dock, or telling a true story) gains a \emph{Community Favour} token. Spend it to reroll a failed social roll with any coastal merchant or captain. Hoarding wealth without contributing, however, gives the GM 1 SB per session as the community closes ranks.
\item \textbf{Shetani Taboos:} The old spirits — shetani — live in springs, baobabs, and the bones of ancestors. Breaking a taboo (counting to nine near a spirit tree, refusing a cup of tea, wearing shoes in a shrine) draws their attention. The GM may then spend 2 SB to have a shetani appear and demand a story that ends with the number eight. A good story pacifies the spirit; a poor one curses the offender with \emph{Shetani's Mark} (–1 die on all rolls until they make an offering of honey and blood).
\item \textbf{Horse Lineage as Leverage:} Every highland horse belongs to a lineage that traces back to a legendary stallion. Knowing a horse’s lineage gives +1 die on social rolls with its owner. A character who can recite the lineage of a horse from memory may treat the horse as a temporary ally (Cap 2) for one scene. Forging a lineage is a capital crime — the Crown Council has executed three forgers in the last decade.
\item \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mzimu wa Kwanza} (The First Spirit) — The Witness. *The ancestor who remembers every oath, every betrayal, every cup of tea refused.*
    \item \textbf{Bwana wa Nyasi} (Lord of the Grass) — The Traveler. *The wind that guides the monsoon dhows and the rider lost in the high passes.*
    \item \textbf{Shetani wa Tisa} (The Shetani of Nine) — The Ninth. *The spirit that lives in the missing step, the uncounted wave, the breath after eight.*
    \item \textbf{Bibi wa Kahawa} (Grandmother of Coffee) — Inaea. *The web of trade that binds coast to highlands, merchant to farmer, dhow to mule.*
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross-Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Monsoon Trade (Dhahara/Ayokha):} Taharka's spice and coffee travel east on the monsoon winds, reaching the Dhaharan coast and the Ayokhan river markets. A delay in Taharka means famine in a dozen ports. The dhows are blessed by Bibi wa Kahawa; a captain who breaks the blessing finds his ship becalmed for nine days.
  \item \textbf{The Pass Ghost and the Hollow (Eastern Realms):} The Pass Ghost (Liyel) is a spirit of the threshold — a border between living and dead. The Aelaerem say it is a cousin to the Hollow, bound by an oath to the first king. If you see it point at you, you have one day to settle your debts. The Hollow collects the rest.
  \item \textbf{Ashaani Spies and the Veiled Sisters (Ashaan):} Ashaani agents often pose as spice merchants. The Ridge-Watchers have learned to test them by offering tea — a foreigner who does not know the ritual of three cups reveals themselves. The Veiled Sisters have begun sending agents who have studied the ritual. The Ridge-Watchers are now testing with a fourth cup — the silence cup.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth Heir and the Kalrantha Amulet (Dhahara):} The exiled prince is rumoured to have bought his horse from a Dhaharan smuggler — a horse that carried a certain green glass amulet. The Crown Council wants to know where the amulet is now. The prince says he sold it for a pass-stone. The pass-stone is now in the hand of the Astrologer.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Among the Taharka, power flows through passes, horse lines, and the favour of the monsoon. The Crown Council rules by consensus, but each valley lord commands his own Ridge-Watchers. The spice merchants of the coast operate independently, paying tariffs to the Crown but answering to no single lord. The \textit{ujamaa} of the dhows is a parallel economy of mutual aid — a captain who cheats it finds his name erased from every mooring post.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Taharkan society is measured in altitude and access. Patronage often takes the form of pass-stones, horse-bonds, or coffee measures — each tied to a specific valley, bloodline, or trade route. To emphasize the East African flavour:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to physical tokens (carved stone, braided hair, clay cups) that can be challenged, stolen, or voided.
\item Let rival lords issue conflicting pass-rights, forcing players to choose whose path to follow.
\item Use the rock-chapels, horse cemeteries, and monsoon wharves as arenas for social contests, where hospitality is law and a cup of tea is a contract.
\item Introduce shetani as recurring obstacles — they are not enemies but creditors of a different kind, demanding stories rather than silver.
\end{itemize}
In Taharkan society, the mountain remembers every step — and the horse remembers every rider. The shetani remember every miscount.
\end{tcolorbox}
