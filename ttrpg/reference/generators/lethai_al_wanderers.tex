\section{Lethai-al --- ``The Storm-Touched Wanderers of Nihori''}
\label{chap:lethai-al}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{We do not own the land. We walk it. The road is our shrine. The storm is our law.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Procession Elder (Elite):}  
  ``The sea does not forgive, but it does forget. If you can wait long enough, the waves will erase your debt. But we do not wait. We walk. From shrine to shrine, from storm to storm, we carry the names of the drowned. The road is a prayer. Every step is a syllable.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Tide-Charm Keeper (Commoner):}  
  ``My grandmother drowned in the ninth wave. Her name became a tide-charm. I keep it in a pouch of oiled leather. When the storm grows angry, I whisper her name. The sea remembers her. It calms for a moment — long enough to breathe. We are wanderers. The road is our home. The dead travel with us.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Processional pilgrimage, wandering monks, duellists at crossroads, storm-folk.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A procession trail winding along the sea cliffs, marked by weathered torii gates and small stone shrines. A group of Lethai-al walk in single file, chanting a counting song. One carries a palanquin with a covered object — a tide-charm shrine. The leader stops: ``You stand on the processional road. The drowned are watching from the waves. Do you walk with us? If so, you must carry nothing. The road provides. The road takes away.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
The Lethai-al of Nihori once guarded a drowned kingdom — an archipelago that sank when a forbidden name was spoken. Their blood still carries the salt of that ancient catastrophe. Now they are wanderers in the oldest sense: masterless, landless, bound to no lord but fiercely bound to each other and to the storm spirits that swirl above the waves. They walk from shrine to shrine, from port to port, following the processional roads that circle the island. They do not farm. They do not build. They carry everything they own on their backs or in wooden carts. Their duels are not to the death — they are to the first draw of blood, after which the loser must join the procession and carry the winner's burden for a season. Their courts are crossroads. Their temples are the road itself. The Eastern Ninth lives here: the Hollow does not hide under hills — it breathes in the salt foam. The ninth wave is never the largest; it is the one that remembers your name.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of salt and incense. The way the Lethai-al never sit with their backs to the sea. The constant, low murmur of waves being predicted — not by instruments, but by the tilt of a head, the twitch of a finger. The worn wooden plaques hung from their belts, each carved with a dead ancestor's name.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never walk under a torii gate without making an offering. The gate is a threshold. The road on the other side is not the same road you left.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Processional Road}

Power here is measured in shrines visited, storms survived, and the number of dead ancestors carried as tide-charms. The Lethai-al have no king, no courts, no written law. Their authority is personal and processional: a Procession Elder's word is law because she has walked the circuit more times than anyone else. Their guilds are not guilds — they are processional bands, each with its own rhythm, its own counting song, its own patron shrine. A band that walks too fast offends the spirits; one that walks too slow loses the tide. The Lethai-al do not believe in ownership of land; they believe in passage rights, storm oaths, and the memory of the drowned. Every crossroads is a court. Every storm is a messenger. Every sunrise is a departure.

\textbf{Starting Location (alternative):} A crossroads shrine at the edge of a fishing village. A procession of Lethai-al has stopped for the night. Their carts are arranged in a circle, painted with wave patterns. A Procession Elder sits by a small fire, offering cups of sake to travellers who pause. ``The road is long. The dead are many. Stay one night. Tell us a story. If we like it, we will carry it to the next shrine. If we do not, you will walk alone tomorrow.''

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:lethai-al-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2–5 → 4, 6–10 → 6, J/Q/K → 8, A → 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (charms, arrows, cups) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{processional omens}—a gate that creaks without wind, a cart wheel that turns backward, a name that echoes from the sea.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:lethai-al-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Processional Circuit:} The Lethai-al walk a circuit of 108 shrines. A character who walks the circuit (over several sessions) may, at the end, gain +1 die to all rolls involving storms, spirits, or the sea. The circuit takes nine months. A character who completes the circuit nine times becomes a Procession Elder.
\item \textbf{Walking Court (Crossroads Justice):} When two Lethai-al dispute, they settle it at a crossroads. Each party speaks their truth once. Then they walk away in opposite directions. The road decides. A character who lies at a crossroads gains the condition \emph{Wave-Walker's Shame} (-1 die to all social rolls until they walk the circuit again).
\item \textbf{Storm-Reading:} Once per journey, a character with Lethai-al training may predict the weather for the next 24 hours (Wits + Survival vs. DV 3). On success, choose one leg to ignore weather penalties. On a miss, the storm finds you anyway, and the GM gains 1 Story Beat to spend on a thunderclap or flash flood.
\item \textbf{Tidal Resilience:} Once per scene, when a character would take Harm from a water-based or storm-based effect, they may reduce it by 1 level (to a minimum of 0). Mark 1 Fatigue.
\item \textbf{The Eastern Ninth (Hollow in Salt Foam):} When a Lethai-al drowns, their name becomes a tide-charm. Living kin may whisper it to calm a storm, but the Hollow will collect the debt at the next full moon. The Hollow's attention ticks whenever a tide-charm is used.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross-Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tide-Charms and the Tulkani (Way of Silk):} Tulkani story-tellers sometimes carry Lethai-al tide-charms as proof of a witnessed drowning. A charm can be traded for a story that the Tulkani have not heard. The story must be about the sea.
  \item \textbf{Storm-Spirits and the Veiled Sisters (Ashaan):} The Veiled Sisters have tried to bind storm-spirits for use in their fleet. The Lethai-al have foiled nine attempts. A Procession Elder may ask the party to sabotage a Sister's ritual.
  \item \textbf{The Drowned Capital and the Kalrantha Amulet (Dhahara):} The sunken city is said to contain a temple that holds a fragment of the demon Varash. The amulet's creator visited the capital before it sank. The fragment whispers the forbidden name.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth Wave and the Hollow (Eastern Realms):} The Hollow's Oath-Keepers ride the ninth wave. Lethai-al who survive the ninth wave are said to owe the Hollow nothing. Aelaerem leave offerings of salt at the high tide line to appease the wave.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Among the Lethai-al, power flows through the procession, the road, and the memory of the drowned. A Procession Elder's word is law because he has walked the circuit more than anyone else. A Tide-Charm Keeper holds the names of the dead; she can calm a storm or call one. There are no kings, no courts, no written laws — only the road, and the road does not negotiate.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Lethai-al society is measured in shrines visited, storms survived, and tide-charms kept. Patronage often takes the form of storm-charms, broken arrows, or processional passes — each tied to a specific ancestor, duel, or shrine. To emphasize the cultural mechanics:
\begin{itemize}
\item Use \textbf{Processional Circuit} as a long-term character goal. Completing it is a major achievement.
\item Enforce \textbf{Walking Court} — a character who lies at a crossroads suffers social penalties until they make amends.
\item Let \textbf{Storm-Reading} be a journey-saving ability, but with a risk of additional complications.
\item Track the \textbf{Hollow's Attention} when tide-charms are used. The Oath-Keepers may appear at the worst moment.
\end{itemize}
In Lethai-al society, the sea forgets nothing — and the ninth wave remembers every name.
\end{tcolorbox}
