\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Processional pilgrimage, wandering monks, duelists at crossroads, storm‚ąífolk, ancestral debt.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A procession trail winding along the sea cliffs, marked by weathered torii gates and small stone shrines. A group of Lethai‚ąíal walk in single file, chanting a counting song. One carries a palanquin with a covered object ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí a tide‚ąícharm shrine. \vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme & Atmosphere}]
The Lethai‚ąíal of Nihori once guarded a drowned kingdom ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí an archipelago that sank when a forbidden name was spoken. Their blood still carries the salt of that ancient catastrophe. Now they are wanderers in the oldest sense: masterless, landless, bound to no lord but fiercely bound to each other and to the storm spirits that swirl above the waves. They walk from shrine to shrine, from port to port, following the processional roads that circle the island. They do not farm. They do not build. They carry everything they own on their backs or in wooden carts. Their duels are not to the death ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí they are to the first draw of blood, after which the loser must join the procession and carry the winner's burden for a season. Their courts are crossroads. Their temples are the road itself. The Eastern Ninth lives here: the Hollow does not hide under hills ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí it breathes in the salt foam. The ninth wave is never the largest; it is the one that remembers your name.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of salt and incense. The way the Lethai‚ąíal never sit with their backs to the sea. The constant, low murmur of waves being predicted ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí not by instruments, but by the tilt of a head, the twitch of a finger. The worn wooden plaques hung from their belts, each carved with a dead ancestor's name.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never walk under a torii gate without making an offering. The gate is a threshold. The road on the other side is not the same road you left.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Processional Road}

Power here is measured in shrines visited, storms survived, and the number of dead ancestors carried as tide‚ąícharms. The Lethai‚ąíal have no king, no courts, no written law. Their authority is personal and processional: a Procession Elder's word is law because she has walked the circuit more times than anyone else. Their guilds are not guilds ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí they are processional bands, each with its own rhythm, its own counting song, its own patron shrine. A band that walks too fast offends the spirits; one that walks too slow loses the tide. The Lethai‚ąíal do not believe in ownership of land; they believe in passage rights, storm oaths, and the memory of the drowned. Every crossroads is a court. Every storm is a messenger. Every sunrise is a departure.

\textbf{Starting Location (alternative):} A crossroads shrine at the edge of a fishing village. A procession of Lethai‚ąíal has stopped for the night. Their carts are arranged in a circle, painted with wave patterns. A Procession Elder sits by a small fire, offering cups of sake to travelers who pause. ``The road is long. The dead are many. Stay one night. Tell us a story. If we like it, we will carry it to the next shrine. If we do not, you will walk alone tomorrow.''

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}

\begin{itemize}
‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Processional Circuit:} The Lethai‚ąíal walk a circuit of 108 shrines. A character who walks the circuit (over several sessions) may, at the end, gain +1 die to all rolls involving storms, spirits, or the sea. The circuit takes nine months. A character who completes the circuit nine times becomes a Procession Elder.
