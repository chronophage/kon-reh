\section{Linn --- ``The Frost Ledger''}
\label{chap:linn}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The sea writes in salt. The frost binds in iron. The skald remembers in verse.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{High Jarl (Elite):}  
  ``The sea keeps better counsel than any scribe, and the storm remembers every oath sworn upon its winds. But the frost — the frost is the ledger. It preserves what the tide would wash away. A debt sworn in winter is owed until the ice melts. And the ice never truly melts.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Net-Wife (Commoner):}  
  ``The water speaks to those who know how to listen — the way it moves tells you if the fish are running, if the weather's turning, or if something's coming that wasn't invited to the feast. My grandmother taught me to read the frost on the oar-blades. That's how I know who will live through the winter and who will feed the seals.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Norse coastal saga, oath‑driven honour, horror of the unwitnessed death, the tyranny of seasons.

\textbf{Starting Location:} A longship house lined with storm‑charms and whale‑bone runes, salt wind clawing at the doors. A Net‑Wife binds a storm‑token into a PC's palm. ``The Whale‑Road sings,'' she says. ``But the Mistlands whistle back. Bring us three keel‑lengths of ironwood from the Valewood edge, or the tide will take our boats in the spring. The frost ledger says you owe us for the shelter you took last winter. Pay, or the ice will remember.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
North of the Aberderrin Sea, where fjords split the coast and skerries break the tide, dwell the Linn. They are traders and raiders in equal breath, their longships crowding the whale‑road and river mouths. They descend the Ylolka like an axe‑blade and cut into southern shores, or slip through the Thin Strip where the Valewood dares not touch salt. Yet even these storm‑oath people dread the Mistlands and the Valewood, whose fog and trees have no need of ships or harbors to claim their due.

The Linn measure their lives in seasons, not years. There is a season for raiding (summer, when the seas are open), a season for planting (spring, when the frost breaks), a season for governance (autumn, when the ships are beached and the Thing meets), and a season for survival (winter, when the frost ledger is written). In winter, the fjords freeze, the longships are dragged into sheds, and the people gather around the longhall fires to settle debts, tell sagas, and wait for the sun to return. The frost ledger is not a book — it is a set of carved tally-sticks, each notch a debt, each knot a promise. When a Linn says a debt is ``written in frost,'' they mean it is unchangeable until the ice melts. And the ice, in the high north, never truly melts.

The gods of the Linn are not distant. \textbf{Adar}, the All‑Father, walks the ridges in a grey cloak, his one eye watching for those who break oaths. He is a manifestation of the Oath of Flame and Light, but the Linn know him as the god of wisdom, death, and binding promises. \textbf{Xhak'thul}, the Storm Father, rides the clouds, his hammer cracking the sky. He is the patron of raiders and sailors, a Thor‑like figure who laughs in the teeth of the gale. His cult is loud, proud, and dangerous — a Xhak'thul devotee will swear a storm‑oath by drinking seawater and shouting at the lightning. The Temple of Light calls him a demon. The Linn call him captain.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of tar and salt fish. The creak of oars from unseen boats. The way everyone — jarl to thrall — touches a knotted cord before speaking of the sea. The tally‑sticks hanging from every belt, each notch a debt owed or collected.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never name a drowned person aloud while underway. The sea collects names, and if it hears you speaking one it doesn't recognize, it may reach up to correct the ledger. Also, never refuse a cup of winter mead — it is a test of your honour and your debt‑worthiness.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: Skerry Stormcall, Frost Ledger}

Power here is spoken, not written. A jarl's authority rests on the weight of his oath‑chest and the sharpness of his skald's tongue. Longships are not vessels; they are promises carved in timber. Every voyage is a gamble against tide, reef, and the attention of things that sleep beneath the waves. The Linn do not fear death — they fear dying without a witness, swallowed by fog where no verse will remember their name.

But beneath the sea‑lore runs the frost ledger. Each Linn keeps a tally‑stick, notched for every debt they owe and every debt owed to them. When the winter gales ground the longships, the Thing convenes to settle the ledger. A jarl whose tally is too deep in the red may find his hall burned; a thrall who has collected enough notches may buy his freedom. The frost ledger is the true law of the Linn — not the spoken word, but the carved one.

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:linn-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Frost Ledger (Debt Clock):} Every Linn community keeps a tally‑stick or a frozen block of ice carved with notches representing debts. A character who accepts a favour, shelter, or gift from a Linn must accept a \textbf{Frost Notch} — a 4‑segment debt clock. When the clock fills, the community may demand a service (a raid, a story, a season of labour). A character who refuses to accept a Frost Notch is considered an oath‑breaker and suffers –1 die on all social rolls with Linn for the remainder of the journey. The Frost Notch can be cleared by performing a season‑appropriate task (planting in spring, raiding in summer, governing in autumn, surviving in winter).
\item \textbf{Storm-Oaths:} A Linn may swear an oath by touching salt water. Breaking a storm‑oath inflicts 1 Fatigue (the sea's disappointment) and gives the GM a free SB to represent the sea's attention. Keeping an oath until its completion grants +1 die on the next roll involving the sea or sailing. Oaths sworn during a storm (under Xhak'thul's eye) are doubly binding — breaking them inflicts 1 Harm.
\item \textbf{The Season's Turn:} The GM may declare that the current season affects certain actions. In \textbf{Spring} (planting), foraging and healing rolls gain +1 die. In \textbf{Summer} (raiding), combat and sailing rolls gain +1 die. In \textbf{Autumn} (governance), social and legal rolls gain +1 die. In \textbf{Winter} (survival), all survival rolls are Desperate, but clearing a Frost Notch is easier (the community is too cold to argue). The season changes when the GM spends 3 SB, or after a major story beat.
\item \textbf{Saga Reputation (Community Track):} Every Linn settlement has a Saga Track [6] that reflects the community's oral memory. The party can increase it by performing deeds worthy of verse (rescuing a ship, slaying a sea monster, keeping a difficult oath). At 3 segments, locals offer hospitality without question. At 6, the party gains a permanent epithet and may call on one favour from any Linn jarl.
\item \textbf{Mist-Dread:} Linn characters suffer –1 die on navigation rolls when entering the Mistlands or Valewood, but gain +1 die on rolls to avoid supernatural fear (they have learned to dread the right things).
\item \textbf{Skald's Burden:} A PC who publicly composes a verse about an event may choose to increase or decrease the Saga Track by 1 (as they shape memory). On a failed Performance roll (DV 3), the verse backfires and the track moves the opposite direction.
\item \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Adar the All-Father} — Oath of Flame and Light. *The one‑eyed god of wisdom, death, and binding oaths. He watches from the ridges. His tally‑stick is the frost ledger itself.*
    \item \textbf{The Storm Father} — Xhak'thul. *The god of thunder, raiding, and the wild sea. His devotees swear storm‑oaths by drinking seawater. He laughs at lightning.*
    \item \textbf{The Salted Anchor} — The Sealed Gate. *The harbour that never closes, the oath that never rusts.*
    \item \textbf{The Wave Mother} — Raéyn. *The tide that carries and the storm that answers.*
    \item \textbf{The Drowned Chorus} — Khemesh. *The pressure beneath the keel, the debt the sea collects.*
    \item \textbf{The Unwritten Verse} — The Witness. *The memory that survives the drowning.*
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Draugr Jarl (Unfinished Business):} The Thing‑Holm is haunted by a jarl who died before his oath was fulfilled. He rises when a storm‑oath is broken, demanding a blood‑price paid in stories. The Temple of Light has sent three missionaries to lay him to rest; all three drowned in calm water. His tally‑stick is frozen into the ice of the Thing‑Holm. If it ever thaws, he will walk again.
  \item \textbf{Adar’s Ravens (The Witness):} Adar is said to have two ravens, Memory and Thought, who fly across the world each day and return to whisper in his ear. A Linn skald who prays to Adar may ask one question about a forgotten debt; the GM must answer truthfully, though cryptically. The price is a memory — the skald forgets something unimportant, but it may become important later.
  \item \textbf{Xhak'thul's Hammer (The Savage Heart):} The Storm Father's hammer is said to be lodged in a cliff on the highest skerry. A character who touches the hammer and survives the lightning may invoke Xhak'thul's wrath once per session: all enemies within Near must test Resolve (DV 4) or be Stunned for one exchange. The cost is that the character is marked by the storm — they will be struck by lightning at the worst possible moment (GM's discretion).
  \item \textbf{The Tide‑Washed Covenant (The Hospice of the Salted Anchor):} A grey‑robed healer runs a hospice on the edge of the Mistlands, treating wounded raiders and fog‑mad sailors alike. She asks no payment, but she writes every name in a ledger that never dries. Linn captains leave offerings of fresh water before a voyage; those who refuse find their ship plagued by the drowned. The ledger is said to be a copy of the frost ledger — but written in salt water, not ice.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any longhouse, the ninth horn blown at a feast summons the Hollow. The wise stop at eight. Those who blow the ninth are said to see a pale figure standing at the high table — waiting to collect a debt. The figure is Adar, checking his tally‑stick.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bread to the Wake:} Toss a crust over the gunwale before a voyage. Ignore the first \emph{Black Squall} penalty. `[SUPERSTITION]`
  \item \textbf{Knot the Oar:} Tie a single knot in your oar's leather grip. Cancel \emph{Keel-Rot Rumor} or take –1 die on your next rowing check (your choice). `[SUPERSTITION]`
  \item \textbf{Name to the Deep:} Whisper a dead enemy's name into the tide. Gain +1 Effect against his kin for the scene, but mark \emph{Obligation} to the Sea‑Queen. `[SUPERSTITION]`
  \item \textbf{Drink to the Storm:} Pour a cup of mead into the sea before a raid. Xhak'thul will grant you favourable winds — but he will also expect a share of the plunder. `[SUPERSTITION]`
\end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Season Turns:} The GM may spend 2 SB to advance the season to the next phase, with associated bonuses/penalties.

\begin{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Among the Linn, power flows through oaths, kinship, and the spoken word — and through the notches carved in ice. Jarls maintain authority through successful raids and wise judgments, while skalds wield influence through their ability to immortalize deeds in verse — or damn names through satire. The Sea‑Queen holds sway over the coastlands, but her rule depends on the respect of the High Jarl and the Thing‑speakers who interpret ancient laws. And beneath all of them lies the frost ledger — the true account of who owes what, written in a language that only the Volva can read.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Linn society is fluid and performance‑based. Patronage often takes the form of foster‑bonds, oar‑shares, or raid‑truces — each binding the recipient to service and reputation. To emphasize this:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to visible symbols (tokens, banners, verses) that can be challenged, stolen, or voided.
\item Let rival jarls issue conflicting claims, forcing players to choose whose favor matters more.
\item Use the Thing‑holm and harbor gatherings as arenas for social contests, where oaths are sworn and broken.
\item Introduce the Frost Notch as a recurring pressure. Every gift from a Linn comes with a debt. Players who ignore their notches may find themselves summoned to the Thing in winter.
\item Let the seasons change. A winter scene should feel different from a summer raid. Use the Season's Turn to reinforce the cyclical nature of Linn life.
\end{itemize}
In Linn society, your word is your bond, and your reputation is your greatest treasure. But the frost ledger is patient. It waits for spring.
\end{tcolorbox}
