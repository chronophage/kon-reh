\section{Silkstrand --- ``City of Bridges and Silk''}
\label{chap:silkstrand}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{Every bridge is a contract. Every canal keeps a record. But the real bargains are sealed in whispers, not wax.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{The Matron (Elite):}  
  ``In this city, every bridge is a bargain and every current carries a rumor. The deals struck in our shadows never quite fade, and the waters remember every handshake. Trust the silk, not the smile. And never mistake a favor for a gift — threads can be pulled from either end.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Bobbin-Runner (Commoner):}  
  ``I can cross this city faster than any grown‑up, and I know which planks creak and which merchants lie. The bridges remember every step, and the canals carry more secrets than fish. The nobles think they rule, but the Ragman owns the stairs they climb. Stay off the water after midnight — the night‑boats don't carry cargo, they carry whispers between patrons.''
\end{quote}

\noindent\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Mercantile intrigue, Renaissance urban drama, layered loyalties, high stakes.

\noindent\textbf{Starting Location:} Three‑Queens Bridge, crowded with market stalls, its stones worn smooth by centuries of footsteps. A Guildmistress with a silver whistle offers a stamped chit. ``The Exchange boils today,'' she says. ``And something in the canals remembers your face. Bring me three bolts of untaxed crimson before the closing bell, or the Archivolt will have your name on a dead notary's list. And watch your back on the Ropewalk — the Ragman's bravos are hungry. If you need a patron, ask for the Contarini factor. If you need a knife, ask for the Tide Father's man. Just know which one you'll owe afterwards.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Silkstrand is a city strung across canals and arched bridges — a place where every crossing is a negotiation and every current carries coin. The silk looms never sleep, the Exchange never closes, and the Archivolt's notaries seal fates with a drop of wax. Merchants rise and fall with the tide of the market, bravos carve reputations on the planks of Three‑Queens Bridge, and the city's underworld runs on whispers and borrowed keys.

But trade is the \emph{veneer}. The real power moves through older channels: the client–patron relationships that stitch the city together from the waterline up. The old patrician families — Contarini, Morosini, Dandolo — hold charters signed by emperors who are now dust. The crime lords — the Ragman, the Tide Father, the Silk Mother — hold the alleys, the night‑boat landings, and the weights that cannot be recorded. The Matron rules from her palazzo stairs with velvet and iron, but she rules because she balances these threads, not because she cuts them.

What you notice first: The clash of water against ancient stone. The constant rumble of cart wheels on bridge planks. The way every handshake lingers a heartbeat too long — as if both parties are checking for hidden blades — and the way every favor is named aloud, so witnesses can remember who owes what.

The one rule that kills newcomers: Never name a rival while crossing a bridge at night. The water remembers, and the curse you send may come back wrapped around your own throat. But the deeper rule: never accept a favor without naming its weight. A gift unspoken is a thread that can be pulled from either end — and the other end is always held by someone who has been playing longer than you.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: Silk, Steel, and Shadow}

Here, fortune is written in silk and sealed with a handshake — but the handshake is witnessed, and the witness takes a share. Silkstrand is not lawless; it is \emph{layered}. Above the tide lies the world of guild charters, notarial wax, and the Matron's velvet justice. Below the bridges, in the weeping arches and the midnight boat lanes, a quieter economy runs on whispers, borrowed keys, and the careful naming of obligations. The old patrician families hold the long view — a favor called in after twenty years is still a favor. The crime bosses hold the short view — a knife in the ribs settles an account by morning. Every alliance is a knot that can be pulled loose. Every fortune is a thread that can be cut. And every thread has two ends.

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:silkstrand-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Weight of a Favor (Patron–Client Economy):} In Silkstrand, favors are the true currency. Each favor has a \textbf{weight} — a measure of what it cost to grant and what it will cost to call in. Favors are recorded not in ledgers but in \textbf{threads} — a red thread tied around the wrist of the debtor, a black thread around the wrist of the patron. When the favor is repaid, the threads are cut. A character may start with 1d3 favor threads (minor favors owed or owed to them). The GM may award threads for significant aid. A thread can be spent to gain +1 Position on a social roll with the patron's faction, or to demand a minor service (safe passage, a night's shelter, a warning). Holding more than nine threads is dangerous — the ninth thread is always the Hollow's.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Minor Favor (1 thread):} A night's shelter, a message delivered, a blind eye turned.
    \item \textbf{Significant Favor (3 threads):} An introduction to a patron, a forged document, a small loan.
    \item \textbf{Major Favor (6 threads):} A change in policy, a life spared, a contract voided.
    \item \textbf{Unpayable Weight (9 threads):} The Ragman's favor. You do not want to owe this. The ninth thread is never cut.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Patron–Client Mini‑Generator (d6):} When the party seeks a patron or encounters a client relationship, roll to determine the nature of the bond.
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Blood Bond:} The patron and client share a family tie — or a family feud. The obligation is old, deep, and bitter.
    \item \textbf{Silk Bond:} The patron holds the client's debt in the form of a silk contract. The terms are written, but the interest is not.
    \item \textbf{Shadow Bond:} The patron is a crime lord (the Ragman, the Tide Father, the Silk Mother). The client owes a service that cannot be named aloud.
    \item \textbf{Stone Bond:} The patron is a patrician (Contarini, Morosini, Dandolo). The client holds a charter or a seal. The obligation is public but the weight is private.
    \item \textbf{Bridge Bond:} The patron and client met on a bridge at night. The favor was sworn without witnesses. Only the water remembers.
    \item \textbf{Ninth Bond:} The patron is the Hollow. The client does not know they owe. The ninth thread is already tied.
  \end{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Duel Etiquette:} Duels fought on bridges draw crowds eager to wager on blood. Refusing stains a name worse than losing. The winner may claim the loser's bridge token (if any) as a trophy. A duel may also be fought to settle a favor's weight — the loser's obligation is transferred to the winner's patron.
\item \textbf{Canal Omens:} Canals run strange colors at dawn; red foretells riots, blue brings calm, black means a curse has awakened. A successful Wits + Lore test (DV 3) allows a character to interpret the omen and gain +1 die on the next roll to avoid or exploit the coming event. Green water means the Hollow is near.
\item \textbf{Matron's Seal:} Any contract bearing her seal is law — but the Matron is known to rescind seals without warning, leaving obligations tangled. A seal may be challenged by producing an older Matron's seal; the older one always holds. The ninth seal is the Matron's own — she has never used it.
\item \textbf{Exchange Volatility Clock [6]:} Tracks market instability. Each time a major cargo is seized, a trade route is cut, or a merchant defaults, tick the clock. At 3: prices of all goods increase by 1 Supply. At 6: a panic hits — the Exchange closes for 1d3 days, and all outstanding contracts are voided unless witnessed by the Matron. The ninth time this clock fills in a decade, the Ragman calls in his share.
\item \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}
  \begin{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Saint Lazarus of the Unwritten Accord (The Witness):} The Archivolt notaries keep a sealed archive of \emph{unwritten contracts} — promises that were never stamped. The Order of the Witness claims that reading even one of these binds the reader to fulfil it. The Matron has never opened the archive. The Red Clerk has, and his seal now weeps blood. The ninth contract in the archive is blank — it awaits the Ragman's name.
  \item \textbf{Saint Fina of the Silken Veil (Isoka):} The Basilica of Azerin has a side chapel dedicated to the \emph{Shedding Saint}, whose mosaic shows a silkworm breaking its cocoon. Pilgrims who leave a confession there wake the next morning having forgotten one sin — but also one kindness they received. The Guildmistress permits the chapel because the offerings keep the silk guilds wealthy. The ninth confession each day is heard by the Hollow, not the saint.
  \item \textbf{The Weavers of Mercy (The Silk‑Shrouded):} A hospice near the Salt Gate is run by grey‑robed healers who ask no payment. They are tolerated because they treat wounded condottieri for free, but the Black Purse faction suspects they also erase obligations — ledgers left near their doors have been found blank. The Order's rule: ``You do not pay back. You pay forward. But woe to the one who strikes a healer.'' The ninth bed in the hospice is always empty — it is for the Ragman, should he ever need mercy.
  \item \textbf{The Scroll of Unbroken Promises (The Syndicate Seal):} A small but influential order of notaries based in the Archivolt. They adjudicate disputes over contracts, cargo claims, and bridge tolls. Their judgments are always delivered in triplicate, and they accept no bribes — only binding oaths. Those who lie before a Covenant judge find that their next three shipments are impounded. The ninth judgment each year is never delivered — it is the Ragman's veto.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any Silkstrand scene, the ninth bridge crossed in a day leads somewhere unexpected. The wise stop at eight and take a boat. Those who cross the ninth are said to hear a faint laugh from the water — and their contracts are never quite fulfilled. The ninth bridge is the Ragman's. He built it. He did not pay.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
In Silkstrand, power flows through commerce, connections, and the careful weaving of influence — but the loom belongs to the patrons, not the merchants. The Matron maintains control through a web of guilds, factors, and condottieri, each bound by contracts and customs that shift like the tides. The old patrician families hold the long view — a favor called in after twenty years is still a favor. The crime lords hold the short view — a knife in the ribs settles an account by morning.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Patronage in Silkstrand revolves around access — to bridges, to warehouses, to the Exchange floor itself — but access is granted through favors, not purchased with coin. Rewards often take the form of tokens, seals, or safe passage that can be leveraged into greater influence, but each such reward carries a thread — a favor owed to the patron who granted it. To emphasize the layered client‑patron dynamic:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to visible symbols (seals, tokens, contracts) that can be challenged, stolen, or voided — and that carry a thread of obligation to a specific patron (a Contarini seal, a Ragman's knot, a Matron's ribbon).
\item Let rival patrons (Contarini vs. Ragman, Morosini vs. Tide Father) issue conflicting charters, forcing players to choose whose favor matters more — and whose weight they are willing to carry.
\item Use the bridges, markets, and Exchange as arenas for social contests, where reputation and connections determine success — and where a well‑timed favor can turn the tide.
\item Track \textbf{favor threads} as a tangible resource. Holding threads is power; holding too many is danger. The ninth thread is always the Hollow's.
\end{itemize}
In Silkstrand, your word is your bond, and your bond determines whether you rise or drown. But your bond is also a thread, and every thread has two ends. One end is in your hand. The other is held by someone who has been playing longer than you.
\end{tcolorbox}
