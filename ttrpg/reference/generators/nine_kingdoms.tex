\section{The Nine Kingdoms --- ``The Peacock Throne and Its Rivals''}
\label{chap:ninekingdoms}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{Nine thrones, one shadow. The peacock watches. The well remembers.}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Maharaja of Ahimsa (Elite):}  
  ``The Peacock Throne is not a seat. It is a promise made by the first sun king: that the nine kingdoms would remain nine, never one, never fewer. I do not rule. I balance. A peacock's tail has nine eyes. Each eye watches a different kingdom. When one closes, the throne weeps.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Temple Dancer (Commoner):}  
  ``My dance is not for the king. It is for the monsoon. If my ankle bells ring true, the rains come. If they falter, the wells dry. They have never faltered. I do not sleep before a performance. I dream the rhythm.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Courtly intrigue, elephant warfare, monsoon ritual, honour as currency.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The Peacock Throne Hall in Ahimsa, where a golden peacock with ruby eyes watches the court. The Maharaja's chamberlain approaches: ``The Maharaja wishes to renew the Nine-Kingdom Pact. But the King of Vajrapur has sent a forged genealogy. The Queen of Mahishmati has refused to attend. And the ninth kingdom — the one that is always missing — has sent a messenger. The messenger is a ghost. Speak carefully.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Between the Sarpies plateau and the Brass Coast lies a fertile, contested lowland: the land of the Nine Kingdoms. This is the heart of Dhahara, where ancient dynasties still claim descent from the sun, and where the Peacock Throne of Ahimsa casts its shadow over half a dozen rival courts. Here, rivers are borders, elephants are war machines, and every harvest is a negotiation between kings who have never met but whose grandfathers swore blood oaths. The Covenant has a strong presence in the sacred cities; the Aeler have counting houses in the iron towns; the Sidhi sail up the rivers to trade. But the Nine Kingdoms belong to the Dhaharan — to their poets, their temple dancers, their bandit kings, and their queens who rule from behind silk screens. The number ``nine'' is traditional; the actual number of kingdoms fluctuates with wars, marriages, and the mood of the Peacock Throne.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of jasmine and dust. The way every crossroads has a painted stone — a boundary marker, a warning, a prayer. The constant murmur of bazaars where haggling is a form of theatre.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never praise a king's elephant. The elephant is sacred; your praise will be taken as an insult to the king's own mount. Compliment the mahout instead. The mahout will tell the elephant.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of the Peacock}

Power here is measured in river rights, elephant herds, and the thickness of a dynasty's archive. A king's authority rests on his genealogy — how many generations back to the sun, how many treaties sealed, how many marriages made. The Peacock Throne of Ahimsa is the nominal centre, but the other eight kings vie for influence, and the balance shifts with every monsoon. The Covenant judges provide legal arbitration; the temple dancers control the ritual calendar; the bandit kings of Kalinga pay no tribute to anyone. The number nine is sacred, but also a warning: when a ninth kingdom falls, the Hollow comes.

\subsection*{Additional Features (Cultural Mechanics)}
\label{sec:ninekingdoms-mechanics}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Nine Kingdoms Pact Clock [8]:} Tracks the cohesion of the alliance. Advances when a kingdom sends a false genealogy, when a king refuses to attend the renewal, or when a border raid occurs. At 4 segments: tariffs increase between kingdoms. At 8: the pact collapses — war is declared, and the Peacock Throne becomes a target.
\item \textbf{The Honor Dial (Reputation Track):} Each PC has a personal Honor score from –3 to +3. Gain Honor by keeping oaths, protecting the weak, and donating to temples. Lose Honor by breaking promises, insulting elders, or harming sacred animals. High Honor (+2 or +3) grants +1 Position in social encounters with nobles and priests. Low Honor (–2 or –3) doubles market prices and may trigger assassination attempts (GM’s discretion).
\item \textbf{Elephant Bonding:} A character who spends a downtime scene with a war elephant may form a bond. The elephant gains a name and a personality. Once bonded, the elephant grants +1 die to the mahout’s Command rolls in battle, but the mahout suffers –1 die to social rolls with elephant‑hating factions (e.g., the Aeler counting houses, who fear the beasts).
\item \textbf{Ritual Calendar:} Temple dancers control the monsoon schedule. A party that earns the favour of a temple dancer (by protecting a shrine, funding a festival, or telling a true story) may ask for a “weather window”: one journey leg where rain, fog, or heat does not apply. Using this favour ticks the Honor Dial by –1 (the dancer’s rivals will spread rumours).
\item \textbf{Local Patrons (Syncretic Names):}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Peacock's Eye} — The Witness. *The ruby that sees all false genealogies. It does not blink.*
    \item \textbf{The Iron Queen} — Mykkiel. *The law that binds the nine kingdoms, even when kings forget.*
    \item \textbf{The Monsoon Lord} — Khemesh. *The pressure that brings the rain — and the flood that follows.*
    \item \textbf{The Dancing Star} — The Traveler. *The rhythm that guides the dancer’s feet and the monsoon’s arrival.*
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross-Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Nine Kings' Tomb and the Kalrantha Amulet (Dhahara):} The tomb is said to contain a map to the Himadri temple where the amulet was sealed. A rival king may offer the party a noble title in exchange for the map. The map is tattooed on the inside of the Ninth King's ghost. You will need to ask politely.
  \item \textbf{Covenant Judges and the Sidhi (Way of Silk):} High Judge Vasu has ruled on several cases involving Sidhi freedmen. He is known to be fair but harsh. A Sidhi port-master may ask the party to deliver a bribe — or a warning. The warning is written in disappearing ink. The ink is your own blood.
  \item \textbf{Elephant Herds and the Kuvani (Way of Silk):} The Kuvani have tried to buy war elephants from the Nine Kingdoms. The price is always refused — but a Kuvani remount master may offer a story worth the trade. The story must be about a horse that outran a king. The elephants will listen. They are not impressed.
  \item \textbf{The Immortal Peacock and the Hollow (Eastern Realms):} The peacock is said to be the Hollow's original form — before it learned to hunger. Aelaerem hedge-keepers leave offerings of peacock feathers at the Hollow's gates to remind it of what it once was. The feathers are always accepted. The Hollow never thanks them.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
Among the Nine Kingdoms, power flows through genealogy, elephant herds, and the honour of the court. A king's authority rests on his dynasty's archive; a queen's on her marriage alliances; a temple dancer's on the rhythm of the monsoon. The Peacock Throne is the centre, but the other eight kingdoms are not satellites — they are competitors.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in the Nine Kingdoms is measured in iron, buffalo, and the thickness of a family's scroll collection. Patronage often takes the form of peacock feathers, iron gate passes, or Covenant seals — each tied to a specific kingdom, clan, or court. To emphasize this:
\begin{itemize}
\item Use the \textbf{Honor Dial} as a visible track. Let players gain or lose Honor based on their actions in native courts. High Honor opens doors; low Honor closes them — and may bring assassins.
\item Let the nine kingdoms be a web of shifting alliances. A favour from the Maharaja may be an insult to the Rani. Players must navigate the politics.
\item Use the stepwells, elephant stables, and throne halls as arenas for social contests, where a well-recited genealogy is a weapon and a broken promise is a declaration of war.
\end{itemize}
In the Nine Kingdoms, the peacock remembers every eye — and the well shows every death.
\end{tcolorbox}
