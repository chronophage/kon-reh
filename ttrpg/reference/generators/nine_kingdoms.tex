\subsection*{Quick Reference}

\begin{quote}

\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Elephant warfare, monsoon ritual, honor as ancestral dharma, high−altitude survival horror, industrial colonialism.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The Peacock Throne Hall in Ahimsa, where a golden peacock with ruby eyes watches the court. The Maharaja's chamberlain approaches: ``The Maharaja wishes to renew the Nine−Kingdom Pact. But the King of Vajrapur has sent a forged genealogy. The Queen of Mahishmati has refused to attend. And the ninth kingdom−−−the one that is always missing−−−has sent a messenger. The messenger is a ghost. Meanwhile, Viceroy Thrain has sent a breath−counter and a demand: the iron due must be met. Speak carefully. The peacock is watching.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme & Atmosphere}]
Between the high passes of the Aeler viceroyalty and the Brass Coast lies a fertile, contested lowland: the land of the Nine Kingdoms, the heart of Dhahara. Here, ancient dynasties still claim descent from the sun, and the Peacock Throne of Ahimsa casts its shadow over half a dozen rival courts. Rivers are borders; elephants are war machines; every harvest is a negotiation between kings who have never met but whose grandfathers swore blood oaths.

North, in the high passes, the Aeler viceroys rule on behalf of the distant High King. Here, the air is thin and cold, the rivers run white with snowmelt, and the passes are measured in breath−counts rather than miles. Iron mines scar the hillsides, worked by Dhaharan laborers and overseen by Aeler engineers. The Aeler have established a viceroyalty here−−−not conquest, but a long lease, renewed every nine years.

But both north and south share a common truth: the number ``nine'' is a wound−−−and a promise. There have never been nine kingdoms. The number is a ritual, a prayer that the Hollow will not notice that one of the nine is always missing. That missing kingdom changes. Sometimes it is a real kingdom that fell to plague or war; sometimes it is a kingdom that never was, a placeholder kept empty to satisfy the silence.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of jasmine and dust, and the ringing of iron hammers. The way every crossroads has a painted stone−−−a boundary marker, a warning, a prayer. The constant murmur of bazaars where haggling is a form of theater. The silence after a bell rings in the mountains−−−the silence is for the mountain to answer.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never count your breaths aloud in a mine. The mountain listens, and if it hears you reach the ninth, it will take one−−−a breath, a memory, a shadow. In the lowlands, never praise a king's elephant. The elephant is sacred; your praise will be taken as an insult to the king's own mount. Compliment the mahout.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of the Elephant and the Gaze of the Hollow}

Power here is measured in river rights, elephant herds, the thickness of a dynasty's archive, and the number of breaths a miner can hold. In the lowlands, a king's authority rests on how many generations back to the sun−−−but also on how many of those generations kept their promises. The Peacock Throne of Ahimsa is the nominal center, but the other eight kings vie for influence, and the pattern shifts with every monsoon.

And somewhere, in the gap between the eighth and the ninth−−−between the eighth breath and the ninth breath, between the eighth throne and the ninth throne−−−the Hollow waits to ask what the missing kingdom promised.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage & Power}]
Among the Dhaharan, power flows through genealogy, elephant herds, the honor of the court, and the slow pull of ancestral promise−−−and in the high passes, through iron, tea, and the lease. The Peacock Throne is the center, but the other eight kingdoms are competitors. And the missing ninth is the question that never stops echoing.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Dhahara is measured in iron, buffalo, the thickness of a family's scroll collection, the number of forgotten promises in its bloodline, and the number of breaths a miner can hold.

\begin{itemize}
ℹtem Use the \textbf{Ancestral Honor Dial} as a visible track. High Honor opens doors; low Honor closes them−−−and may bring the Hollow's silent witnesses.
