\section{Vhasia --- ``The Fractured Sun''}
\label{chap:vhasia}

\subsection*{Quick Reference}
\textbf{Tagline:} \textit{The sun may be fractured, but the embers still burn. Which claimant will you warm your hands on?}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Cardinal-Regent of the Broken Sun (Elite):}  
  ``The sun may be fractured, but our authority is absolute within our domains. Every coin minted here carries our blessing, and every law written bears our seal – the rest is just politics. The rest is also ash.''
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
  \textbf{Road Warden (Commoner):}  
  ``I've served under three different banners on this same stretch of road, and the only constant is that someone always wants to collect tolls. My horn calls honest warning – best heed it. The Fenwoods burned Lence ten years ago, and now every duke with a coin purse thinks he's king. The Queen‑Mother plots, the Dauphin hides, and the Two Crowns sharpen their swords. Me? I just want to get home before the snows.''
\end{quote}

\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Courtly intrigue, chevauchée warfare, fractured legitimacy, a kingdom that cannot mourn.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The Pont‑du‑Tithe, its golden arches catching fire in the sunset as pilgrims chant from both banks. A Duchy Constable in splendid armor guards the toll gate. ``The Chevauchee burns,'' he says, gesturing to distant smoke. ``Two Crowns march to meet it. Whose seal do you carry? Speak quickly – the levy waits for no one. And keep your hand away from your sword. The bridge remembers the last blood spilled here.''

\vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme \& Atmosphere}]
Vhasia is a realm of broken coronations and split loyalties, still bleeding from a wound a decade old. Once a proud province of the Utaran empire, the unified Sun‑court shattered like its heraldic sunburst when the Fenwoods of Viterra and their allies sacked the capital of Lence and cast down the old Blood line. Duke Braedyn Fenwood – father of the woman who would become Viterra's Warrior Queen – led the assault, his iron‑shod cavalry grinding the Sun‑king's army into the mud of the Ford of Ashes. They burned Lence until the mirrors melted and the very stones wept. They left the throne empty – not destroyed, but open. And they left a wound that has not healed.

Now rival dukes, duchesses, abbesses, and parlements claim the fractured light. Everywhere, signs of fracture: chipped sun‑discs on milestones, worn tabards bearing half a crown, coins minted in rival cities with different suns. In Vhasia, legitimacy is as fragile as a mirror, and every oath risks breaking along the fault lines of politics, faith, and fortune.

The memory of the sack haunts every negotiation. The Queen‑Mother, Elianor Everblood (née Longblood), lost her husband and her firstborn in the flames. She rules from a riverside keep, weaving alliances and hiding a secret – a Dauphin, real or false, who may be the key to restoring the Sun‑court. Her son Valdais, Duke of Adria, claims the throne by blood and steel. The Two Crowns – rival dukes crowned in rival cathedrals – march their levies through fields that can feed neither. Routiers sell their swords by the season, and parlement clerks drown justice in procedure. Vhasia is not a nation at war. It is a nation that forgot how to be at peace – and that has not yet learned how to grieve.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The smell of old smoke and fresh ink. The way every noble's seal has a hairline crack. The constant, nervous laughter of courtiers who remember whose head rolled last season.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never carry a coin that bears only one sunburst. The realm has three mints, and the wrong metal on the wrong road is treason. Also, never mention the Fenwood name without naming the Queen of Viterra first. The wound is still raw.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: Crowns, Courts, and Companies}

Here, the past is a weapon and the future is a debt. The Fenwoods came from Viterra with fire and iron, broke the Sun‑king's army at the Ford of Ashes, and burned Lence until the mirrors melted. Duke Braedyn Fenwood led them; his daughter Veralyn – now the Warrior Queen of Viterra – was his wife's attendant and witness to the sack, a fact that complicates every border negotiation. The Vhasian nobility has not forgotten. They cannot forgive. But they cannot afford a war with Viterra, either.

So they fight each other instead.

Now every duke with a long purse and a short conscience backs a claimant. The Queen‑Mother weaves alliances from her riverside keep, holding a Dauphin – perhaps her grandson, perhaps a commoner with a convincing birthmark – as a bargaining chip. The Two Crowns – Valdais Longblood of Adria in the north, and a rival claimant backed by the southern parlements – march their levies. Routiers sell their swords by the season, and parlement clerks drown justice in procedure. Vhasia is not a nation at war. It is a nation that forgot how to be at peace. And ten years later, the smoke from Lence still hasn't cleared.

\subsection*{The Players of the Great Game}
\label{sec:vhasia-players}

The succession crisis has four major factions, each with a claim, a secret, and a price.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Queen‑Mother Elianor Everblood (née Longblood):} Widow of the last Sun‑king, mother of the claimant Valdais, and estranged from both. She holds the Dauphin – a child of uncertain legitimacy – in seclusion. Her price is a guarantee that her grandson will not be killed. Her secret: she is not sure if the child is truly of the Blood.
  \item \textbf{Duke Valdais Longblood of Adria:} The northern claimant. Nephew – or half‑brother, depending on the rumor – to the last Sun‑king. A tournament champion with a flawless record and a suspicious habit of opponents falling ill before jousts. His claim rests on blood and steel. His secret: his mother, Elianor, does not trust him.
  \item \textbf{The Two Crowns (Rival Dukes of the South and West):} Backed by the southern parlements and the western march lords. Their claim rests on election and expediency. Their secret: they have been known to accept Viterran gold – but never the Fenwood seal.
  \item \textbf{The Last Dauphin (The False/Real Heir):} A child of nine years, hidden in the Queen‑Mother's keep. His legitimacy is a matter of faith. His existence is the only thing keeping Valdais from marching on his mother's castle. His secret: even Elianor does not know if he is truly her grandson – but she acts as if he is.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Spades — Places}
\label{sec:vhasia-places}
\begin{enumerate}
\setcounter{enumi}{1}
\item \textbf{Wayside Shrine} --- Pilgrims tack petitions to a scorched sun‑disc; each parchment curls in different winds.
  \hfill \textit{The petitions are never answered. The shrine is a mailbox for the dead – especially those who fell at Lence.}
\item \textbf{Vine-Terrace} --- Clos above a millstream; watchmen nap, halberds traded for bottles.
  \hfill \textit{The best vintage is called ``Crownbreaker.'' It tastes like smoke and regret. The year of the sack was a particularly good harvest.}
\item \textbf{Bastide Square} --- Neat as a checkerboard; merchants pay homage to different ``hours'' of the Sun.
  \hfill \textit{The square has a gallows. It rotates to face whichever claimant lost the last duel. The wood is still green.}
\item \textbf{Royal Forest} --- Antler posts mark laws no king remembers; poachers still hang.
  \hfill \textit{The forest's deer have grown bold. They wear crowns of shed antlers. Some say they are the ghosts of the old nobility.}
\item \textbf{Pont-du-Tithe} --- Toll‑bridge flanked by chapels and crossbows; chains blessed by rival bishops.
  \hfill \textit{The chains were forged from melted sunbursts. They ring in different keys. The Fenwood army crossed here. The bridge still groans when Viterrans approach.}
\item \textbf{Great Fair} --- Outside a chartered town; counting sheds ring like bells, rumors louder still.
  \hfill \textit{The fair has a Court of Miracles where counterfeiters sell to informers. The best forgery is a sunburst coin that rings true – until you test its weight.}
\item \textbf{Salt Pans} --- Wind‑pumps on an estuary; gulls own the law, smugglers own the night.
  \hfill \textit{The salt is grey. It tastes of old tears and older debts. The smugglers carry more than salt – they carry the Queen‑Mother's letters.}
\item \textbf{Siege-Château} --- Hoardings patched, cistern low, banners cut into ragged suns.
  \hfill \textit{The banner's ragged edges are from a Fenwood sword. The owner still limps. He lost his son at Lence. He fights for Valdais – but he does not love him.}
\item \textbf{Cathedral Yard} --- Cranes creak, stones fly, scaffolds bristle with rival masons' marks.
  \hfill \textit{The mason's marks are clan signs. A scratched‑out mark means a dead foreman. One mason's mark is a sunburst with a crack – the mark of the Queen‑Mother's architect.}
\item[J] \textbf{Parlement Hall} --- Hung with faded tapestries; sunbursts spliced and re‑stitched by claimants.
  \hfill \textit{The tapestries show battles that never happened – and some that shouldn't have. One tapestry shows the sack of Lence from the Fenwood perspective. The Queen‑Mother has ordered it burned three times. It keeps reappearing.}
\item[Q] \textbf{Queen's Causeway} --- Mile‑long oath paved in quarried stone; half‑sunk each spring.
  \hfill \textit{The sunken half is a graveyard for vows. The stones are slippery with old lies. The Queen‑Mother walks here at dawn. She speaks to the dead.}
\item[K] \textbf{Sun Palace (Lence)} --- Shuttered mirrors, brambled gardens; courtiers playing at kingship.
  \hfill \textit{The mirrors were covered after the sack. No one wants to see what they show now. The courtiers pretend the king still lives. The servants know better.}
\item[A] \textbf{King's High Road} --- Milestones chipped of crowns; yet every army still marches it.
  \hfill \textit{The road is paved with the bones of tax collectors. They are not missed. The Fenwood army marched here. Their footprints are still visible in the stone.}
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Quick use notes}
\label{sec:vhasia-quick-use}
\begin{itemize}
\item Draw until you have all four suits: Spade = place, Heart = actor, Club = pressure, Diamond = leverage. Highest rank sets the main Clock (2--5 $\rightarrow$ 4, 6--10 $\rightarrow$ 6, J/Q/K $\rightarrow$ 8, A $\rightarrow$ 10).
\item Diamonds are codified outcomes (charters/patents/rights) that change position rather than call for a roll.
\item If any A appears, echo \textbf{fractured sun} motifs – light that splinters, oaths that crack like glass, smoke that smells of Lence, and a child who may be a prince or a puppet.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Additional Features}

\paragraph{Fractured Seals:}
When brandishing a writ, badge, or charter tied to a Sun‑court claimant, step Position up one step for the scene's next interaction at a gate, court, or checkpoint. Each use advances a \textbf{Legitimacy [4]} clock linked to the claimant; when it fills, rivals challenge the authority on sight. The seal's crack widens with each challenge. Eventually, it will break.

\paragraph{Parlement Games:}
If a scene touches petitions, guild disputes, or feudal appeals, the first Diamond played becomes a policy lever: treat it as two Diamonds toward swaying a decision or rewriting a custom. Afterward, start or advance a \textbf{Whispered Precedent} clock; when it fills, backlash spreads as other courts cite the case against you. The Queen‑Mother's clerks are masters of this. They have never lost.

\paragraph{Gabelle Knife-Edge:}
At bridges, fairs, or salt depots, convert Diamonds tied to tolls, leases, or safe‑conducts into immediate passage or tariff relief for the company (no roll). Each conversion ticks a \textbf{Resentment [4]} clock for the locale; when it completes, expect boycotts, ambush rumors, or an audit by rival officials. The salt farmers remember Lence. They do not forget.

\paragraph{Vhasian Chivalry \& the Round of Five Virtues:}
The knights of the western cantons (Ethor, Adria) follow the Round of Five Virtues: \textbf{Courage, Courtesy, Mercy, Prowess, Truth}. In a formal joust or a legal duel, the GM may award \emph{Victory by Virtue} rather than by blood: the combatant with the higher Virtue score wins the dispute, even if they lost the physical exchange. This is the law of the tilt – and the law of the court. But at Lence, the Fenwoods showed no mercy. The knights still argue about whether chivalry died there.

\paragraph{The Armor Closes / The Song Escapes (Chivalric Clocks):}
The Cantor Alain, bearer of the Cracked Ring, sings a warning at every tourney:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Armor Closes [6]} – advances when knights choose protection over mercy, when gorgets are buckled tight, when chivalry becomes siege mentality. When full, the cantons close their borders and the raids become wars.
  \item \textbf{Song Escapes [8]} – advances when a Cantor sings the truth, when mercy is shown, when armour is unbuckled. When full, a moment of genuine connection occurs – though it may be fatal.
\end{itemize}
In Rouen, Alain still sings the \emph{Planctus Armatus} each spring, and the congregation weeps for Sir Corin, who unbuckled his gorget to hear a thief's voice and was undone by the mercy he offered. Sir Corin died at Lence. He was trying to save a child. The child is now the Dauphin – or so the Queen‑Mother claims.

\subsection*{Lore Echoes (Cross‑Expansion Hooks)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Seint Michel, the Judge of the Scar (Mykkiel):} The Order of the Gilded Scale maintains the \emph{Chivalric Ledger} – a record of every oath sworn and broken in the cantons. A page torn from this ledger can serve as a single‑use Claim String (gain +1 Position in any court). The Order's justiciars are feared and respected; they never unbuckle their gorgets, and they have never lost a case. The Queen‑Mother keeps a copy in her riverside keep, wrapped in a grey shroud. The page that records the Fenwood oath of safe passage – broken at Lence – is still blank. The Order will not write it. They are afraid of what will happen if they do.

  \item \textbf{Seint Isabeau, Who Slumbers Amongst the Lilies (Ikasha):} In the Ethorian marches, knights whisper prayers to Seint Isabeau when they long to set aside their steel. Her shrine is a hawthorn grove where armour must be left at the gate. Pilgrims who enter bare‑skinned and speak a true regret find that their next wound heals in a single night – but they dream of a woman in white who asks, ``Will you remember how to be touched?'' Those who return to the grove a second time are never seen again. The knights who survived Lence do not go near the grove. They are afraid of what they might forget.

  \item \textbf{Seint Evroul of the Cairn (Mor'iraath):} In the high passes of the Ethorian marches, a rusted knight in an unopened helm haunts the old barrows. He is the patron of those who carry a blood‑debt and cannot rest. To call upon him, one must pour a cup of salt water at a cairn and name the wrong done to your house. He will witness one blow; if it lands, the foe is \emph{Marked by the Cairn} – any spirit may locate them for a year. If it misses, the blow turns inward. The Marshal‑Exile carries a scar from such a bargain; he does not speak of it. He named the Fenwoods. The blow missed. He still does not understand why.

  \item \textbf{The Order of the Sunken Cup (The Hospice of the Broken Blade):} A grey‑robed hospice in the ruins of Lence treats the wounded of both crowns. They ask no payment, but they record every name in a ledger that never dries. The Restorationists suspect they are spies for the Queen‑Mother; the Two Crowns accuse them of erasing debts. Sister Oria, the hospice's custodian, is also the keeper of the Lantern‑Rites House on the Belworth – neutral ground where even claimants must leave their blades at the door. The ledger has a page for every person who died at Lence. The page is still being written. The ink is still wet.

  \item \textbf{The Ninth:} In any Vhasian court, the ninth citation of precedent voids all previous. The wise stop at eight and settle. Those who cite the ninth are said to hear a faint laughter from the marble walls – and their cases are never heard again. The laughter is the dead king's. He was the ninth king of his line. He died at Lence. He is still laughing.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Vhasian Superstitions (burn for +1 die once)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bread to the Sun:} Crumble a crust onto a cracked sun‑disc. Ignore the first \emph{Chevauchée} penalty. The sun‑disc should be from Lence. The older the crack, the better.
  \item \textbf{Knot the Seal:} Tie a single knot in a charter's ribbon. Cancel \emph{Parlement Divided} or take –1 die on your next legal argument (your choice). The ribbon should be black. Black is for mourning.
  \item \textbf{Name to the Salt:} Whisper a dead claimant's name into a salt pan. Gain +1 Effect when collecting debts from their faction this scene, but mark \emph{Obligation} to the Queen‑Mother. The salt pan should be on the Belworth. The dead claimant's name should be spoken only once. The Queen‑Mother will know.
  \item \textbf{The Lence Candle:} Light a candle in the ruins of Lence. For the next scene, any oath sworn by candlelight is binding – but the candle will gutter if the oath is false. The dead will witness. They do not forgive.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thematic SB Spend Table}
\label{sec:vhasia-sb}

\paragraph{Minor Complications (1 SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Exposure:} Rival officials or court guards take notice.
\item \textbf{Noise:} Parlement clerks or routier patrols are alerted.
\item \textbf{Trace:} Trackers or tax collectors mark your route.
\item \textbf{Delay:} A setback costs time or a favourable legal window.
\item \textbf{Supply Strain:} Mark +1 segment on a relevant resource clock.
\item \textbf{Lence Echo:} A flash of smoke, a distant scream, a child's face in a window. The memory of the sack surfaces. The GM gains 1 SB to spend on a fear effect.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Moderate Setbacks (2 SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Alarm Raised:} A duchy constable or abbess‑chatelaine begins responding.
\item \textbf{Position Lost:} Legal challenge or road closure costs you advantage.
\item \textbf{Foe Appears:} A rival claimant or free‑company arrives.
\item \textbf{Gear Trouble:} A piece of equipment becomes Compromised/Neglected.
\item \textbf{Lock/Barrier:} A simple obstacle now requires a test.
\item \textbf{Claimant's Agent:} A spy working for Valdias, the Queen‑Mother, or the Two Crowns reveals themselves. They demand a favour or a bribe.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Serious Trouble (3 SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Reinforcements:} Additional routiers, parlement guards, or ducal forces arrive.
\item \textbf{Key Gear Breaks:} A crucial tool or weapon becomes unusable.
\item \textbf{Major Twist:} A writ is voided, an interdict declared, or a levy called.
\item \textbf{Rail Tick:} Advance a relevant campaign/front clock by 1 segment.
\item \textbf{Condition Applied:} Mark Fatigue 1/Harm 1 or an appropriate Condition.
\item \textbf{The Dauphin's Name:} Someone speaks the child's name aloud. The Queen‑Mother's spies are everywhere. The party is now on her radar.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Major Turns (4+ SB)}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Trap Springs:} A prepared danger activates fully.
\item \textbf{Authority Arrival:} The Two Crowns, Queen‑Mother, or Marshal‑Exile intervenes.
\item \textbf{Scene Shift:} A chevauchée hits, the river floods, or the parlement erupts.
\item \textbf{Patron Omen:} An omen appears, a blessing is lost, or a curse manifests.
\item \textbf{Narrative Pivot:} The story takes an unexpected turn.
\item \textbf{The Sack Relived:} Smoke rises from Lence. The Fenwood banners appear on the horizon. It is a false alarm – or is it? The party must test Resolve (DV 4) or lose 1d4 Fatigue from remembered terror.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Region-Specific SB Options}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Fractured Authority:} Seals break mid‑signing, legal papers rewrite themselves, badges refuse honour.
\item \textbf{Legal Warfare:} Court decisions reverse, precedents are cited against you, writs expire prematurely.
\item \textbf{Sun Symbolism:} Sun‑discs crack underfoot, light splits into contradictory shadows, oaths shatter like glass.
\item \textbf{Lence's Shadow:} A ghost appears – a soldier, a child, a king. It asks a question. Answering costs a memory. Refusing costs a curse.
\end{itemize}

% ====== PATRONAGE & POWER ======
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage \& Power}]
In Vhasia, power flows through the remnants of imperial authority, fractured legitimacy, and the careful manipulation of legal and religious institutions. Former Utaran provincial structures still provide a framework for governance, but competing claimants to the Sun‑court have created a complex web of overlapping jurisdictions and conflicting loyalties. The true power lies with those who can navigate this legal maze and maintain the appearance of legitimate authority.

But beneath the charters and the writs runs a deeper current: the memory of Lence. The Fenwood sack was not just a military defeat; it was a wound to the soul of Vhasia. Every negotiation, every duel, every sealed document carries the weight of that loss. The Queen‑Mother plays a long game, her Dauphin a card she may never play. Valdais wants the throne his father could not hold. The Two Crowns will accept any solution that leaves them richer. And the common folk – the farmers, the salt‑panners, the road wardens – just want the raiding to stop.

They will wait. They have waited ten years. They can wait longer.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Patronage in Vhasia revolves around legitimacy, legal documents, and the ability to command respect from institutions that no longer have a clear central authority. Rewards often take the form of charters, writs, and official positions that can be leveraged into greater influence. To emphasize the post‑sack grief and the fragile peace:
\begin{itemize}
\item Tie rewards to visible symbols (seals, badges, writs) that can be challenged, stolen, or voided – especially if they bear the old king's seal or the Fenwood mark.
\item Let rival claimants issue conflicting authorities, forcing players to choose whose favor matters more – and whose memory of Lence they are willing to invoke.
\item Use the parlements, cathedrals, and châteaux as arenas for social contests, where knowledge of law and custom determines success – and where the mere mention of the Fenwood name can shift the tide.
\item Introduce the \textbf{Lence's Shadow} mechanic: whenever a scene involves a legal dispute, a military maneuver, or a negotiation with a noble who lost family in the sack, the GM may spend 1 SB to invoke the memory of the burning. The Position worsens by one step, and the losing party may gain the \emph{Haunted} Condition.
\end{itemize}
In Vhasia, your papers are your power, and your power determines whether you rule or are ruled. But your memory of Lence – and how you use it – determines whether anyone will follow you.
\end{tcolorbox}

% ====== CAMPAIGN CLOCKS ======
\subsection*{The Vacant Throne \& Campaign Clocks}
\label{sec:vhasia-campaign}
For long‑form political campaigns:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Vacant Throne Clock [10]:} Advances when a claimant gains a major advantage (battle, noble defection, foreign alliance). Retreats when the party brokers a truce or exposes a weakness. When full, a claimant takes Lence and declares themselves king. The Queen‑Mother will not live to see it.
  \item \textbf{Armor Closes [6]:} Advances when knights choose protection over mercy, when gorgets are buckled tight, when chivalry becomes siege mentality. When full, the cantons close their borders and the raids become wars. The Fenwoods taught Vhasia that mercy is a weakness. The knights have not forgotten.
  \item \textbf{Song Escapes [8]:} Advances when a Cantor sings the truth, when mercy is shown, when armour is unbuckled. When full, a moment of genuine connection occurs – though it may be fatal. The Cantor Alain has been trying to fill this clock for ten years. He is still trying.
  \item \textbf{Claimant's Coup (per claimant):} A 6‑clock that advances when that claimant wins a Parlement vote, a joust, or a skirmish. When full, they march on Lence. The Dauphin – real or false – watches from the Queen‑Mother's keep.
  \item \textbf{Lence's Reckoning [8]:} Tracks the party's engagement with the memory of the sack. Advances when they aid a survivor, recover a relic, or speak the truth about what happened. When full, a survivor reveals a secret: the old king's last words, the location of a hidden treasure, or the true parentage of the Dauphin. The secret will change the succession.
\end{itemize}

% ====== BOSS SEEDS ======
\subsection*{Boss Seeds (three‑phase foes)}
\label{sec:vhasia-bosses}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Red Marshal (Exiled Sun‑court hammer)} – \emph{Phases}: Chevauchee Call $\rightarrow$ Banner Turn $\rightarrow$ Lists to War. \emph{Cracks}: Letters Patent buys one round of obedience; General Pardon lets a unit stand down. He was at Lence. He ran. He has not forgiven himself.
\item \textbf{Cardinal-Regent of the Broken Sun (scarlet gloves)} – \emph{Phases}: Interdict Blade $\rightarrow$ Seal Both Ways $\rightarrow$ Confessional Leak. \emph{Cracks}: Queen's Audience cancels Interdict Blade once; a Synod Indulgence treats a church check as controlled for one scene. He was not at Lence. He blessed the Fenwood banners before they marched. He does not speak of it.
\item \textbf{The Parlement Spider (chief clerk; quills like daggers)} – \emph{Phases}: Motion to Delay $\rightarrow$ Counter‑Citation $\rightarrow$ Filed at Dawn. \emph{Cracks}: Sunburst Warrant opens a ``royal window''; a Burgess Charter lets a town court overrule once locally. He keeps the lists. He knows which nobles voted for the Fenwood peace. He does not show the list to anyone.
\item \textbf{The Two Crowns (rival dukes)} – \emph{Phases}: Contradictory Edicts $\rightarrow$ Levy in Hours $\rightarrow$ Coin Trial. \emph{Cracks}: Sunburst Warrant + public mass unites them for one scene; General Pardon calms levy unrest once. They were not at Lence. They sent levies. The levies never returned. They pay the widows' pensions. They do not visit the graves.
\item \textbf{The Queen‑Mother (Elianor Everblood)} – \emph{Phases}: Weave the Web $\rightarrow$ Unveil the Dauphin $\rightarrow$ The Sun Rises Again. \emph{Cracks}: produce proof of the Dauphin's illegitimacy, convince Valdais to kneel, or find the old king's true heir (a child hidden in the Mistlands). She has outlived three husbands and two sons. She will outlive you.
\end{itemize}

% ====== LEGENDARY SITES & TRIALS ======
\subsection*{Legendary Sites \& Trials}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Tournament Ordeal (Bastide Square)} – Declare Purse, Patent, or Pardon as stakes. Win to cash a matching \Diamond; lose and spawn \emph{Tournament ``Accident''}. The square was built on the site of a mass grave from Lence. The dead watch. They are not impartial.
\item \textbf{Cathedral Scaffold Assize} – Argue law atop creaking beams; each good point moves Position up; each slip marks Exposure. A \emph{Paréage Charter} lets you call a recess once. The cathedral was rebuilt after the sack. The old stones are in the foundation. They still remember the fire.
\item \textbf{Pont-du-Tithe Vigil} – Keep the bridge peaceful through dusk: clear 3 segments on \emph{Bridge Peace} by feeding pilgrims, quieting relic zeal, and paying one tithe from your purse. The bridge was the first structure the Fenwoods burned. The replacement stone was brought from Viterra. It still smells of smoke.
\end{itemize}
