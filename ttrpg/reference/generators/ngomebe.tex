\textbf{Genre / Mood:} Walking city survival, ancestral stone magic, industrial partnership, ecological heresy.

\textbf{Starting Location:} The edge of a moving city's path, where the stone is still warm and the dust has not yet settled. A Mason‚àíKin elder approaches, wiping granite dust from his face: ``A keystone has cracked. The city will stop before dawn. The ancestors are restless. \vspace{2mm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=blue!60!black,title={Theme & Atmosphere}]
The Ngomebe are a people of deep, iron‚àídark skin and older ties to the southern ranges. Their cities walk ‚àí‚àí‚àí colossal stone structures on rollers and sledges, dragged by teams of oxen, magic, and Aeler‚àídesigned capstans. This partnership is one of the great cross‚àícultural achievements of the age: Aeler engineers maintain the bearings; Ngomebe masons carve the keystones. A walking city is not a curiosity; it is a living argument against stillness, a monument to survival in a land where staying in one place invites famine, war, or the slow forgetting of ancestral stone.

Spirits are not distant ‚àí‚àí‚àí they live in the rock, the rollers, the cracks. The ancestors speak through keystone fissures. The \textbf{tokoloshe} fouls the bearings at night. The \textbf{impundulu} (lightning bird) shatters entire sledges with a single strike. The Ngomebe do not fight these spirits. They bargain with them: a grindstone of fresh meat, a pot of home‚àíbrewed sorghum beer, a promise whispered into a roller‚àípit.

The Ngomebe are no more united than the stone is silent. \textbf{Mason‚àíKin Houses} feud openly over migration routes and carving rights. The \textbf{Aeler Covenant} guards its technical secrets even as it swears partnership. \textbf{Root‚àíbinders} gather in the groves, preaching that motion is a curse. Among the \textbf{Sledge‚àíBorn} laborers, songs of stillness spread in whispers. And the \textbf{Izinyanga} keep their own counsel, reading cracks that houses and engineers would rather ignore.

  ‚Ñπtem \textit{Northwest ‚àí‚àí‚àí Sekogo:} The Ukwe's roots reach into Ngomebe's southern savanna. Sekogo druids trade honey for keystone shards, believing the stone contains trapped memories of the jungle's oldest trees.
  ‚Ñπtem \textit{Northeast ‚àí‚àí‚àí Aelinnel of the Southern Coast:} Fae‚àítouched gnomes who broker the pact that keeps the walking cities from sinking into soft ground. Their dawn bone‚àíroll determines each day's safe path. The Ngomebe tolerate them; the gnomes hold a bond older than memory.
  ‚Ñπtem \textit{East/Southeast ‚àí‚àí‚àí Aelaerem of Taharka (Highland halflings):} Small, red‚àíthreaded folk who tend the passes and offer shelter to weary caravans. They share a reverence for ancestral stone and trade coffee for iron.
  ‚Ñπtem \textit{South ‚àí‚àí‚àí The Salt Scar:} A vast, cracked waste where abandoned keystones lie like frozen waves. The \textbf{Still Voice} haunts these ruins, asking every traveler why their city stopped.

\vspace{2mm}
\textbf{What you notice first:} The groan of stone on stone. The smell of warm granite, ox sweat, and sorghum beer offerings. The way the ground trembles slightly, even a mile away, as the city inches across the plateau.

\textbf{The one rule that kills newcomers:} Never carve a name into living rock. The stone remembers. It will whisper that name to every spirit of the earth, and the weight will follow your bloodline for seven generations.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Regional Mood: The Weight of Movement}

Power here is measured in keystones, migration routes, and the loyalty of the Mason‚àíKin. A walking city is a polity, a temple, and a weapon all at once. Yet its people are far from united. The \textbf{Mason‚àíKin Houses} control the rights to carve, to move, and to settle, but they feud endlessly over precedence and pride. The \textbf{Root‚àíbinders}‚àí‚àí‚àíheretics who plant acorns in the rollers‚àí‚àí‚àípreach that the cities should stop and let the forest reclaim the stone, and their numbers swell among the weary. The \textbf{Aeler Covenant} engineers are neither masters nor servants; they are partners in a bond older than memory, though some among them whisper that the Ngomebe would be lost without Aeler steel. Beneath them all, the \textbf{Izinyanga} speak for the dead, and the dead speak through the keystone's cracks. Even the \textbf{Sledge‚àíBorn}‚àí‚àí‚àíthe ox‚àídrivers and grease‚àícarriers‚àí‚àí‚àíhave begun to murmur that the weight of motion is too heavy to bear forever. When a city stops, it is because the ancestors have been offended. When a city moves true, it is because the \textbf{Rain Queen} has blessed the path.

\subsection*{The Walking City Timers (Campaign Engines)}
Every city that moves is a living system. Track its health with these visible timers:

\textbf{Migration Timer [10]} ‚àí‚àí‚àí Advances each day the city moves, retreats when it stops. At 2: fertile land $‚Üí$ +1 die to foraging. At 6: narrow pass $‚Üí$ navigation Desperate. At 10: destination reached $‚Üí$ Keystone Blessing.  
\textbf{Roller Integrity [8]} ‚àí‚àí‚àí Ticks when rollers crack or are sabotaged. At 4: speed halves. At 8: the city stops.  
\textbf{Ancestor Favor [8]} ‚àí‚àí‚àí Starts at 4. Ticks up when the ancestors are honored, down when they are offended. At 0: the Still Voice comes. At 8: the ancestors bless the city (one free Keystone Sense use per scene).  
\textbf{Root‚àíbinder Attention [6]} ‚àí‚àí‚àí Ticks when the party interacts with Root‚àíbinders or damages the forest. At 6: the Root‚àíbinders attack.

\subsection*{The Still Voice ($1\text{d}6$)}
When Ancestor Favor reaches 0 or the city stops, the Still Voice appears. Roll $1\text{d}6$:
\begin{enumerate}
‚Ñπtem ``Why did you stop?'' Answer truthfully, or it takes a memory (lose 1 XP, redeemable by a quest).
‚Ñπtem The Voice points at a character. That character now carries a \textbf{Dead Keystone Shard} ‚àí‚àí‚àí it weighs nothing, but the Hollow knows where they are.
‚Ñπtem The ground beneath the city cracks. The city sinks one foot. Roller Integrity $‚àí2$.
‚Ñπtem The Voice demands a story about a city that never stopped. If you cannot tell one, it takes the name of your home village.
‚Ñπtem A keystone fragment rises from the earth. Touch it to ask one question about the city's past. The Voice answers in a whisper.
‚Ñπtem The Voice weeps dust. The dust is bitter. If you taste it, you see the apocalypse that made the cities walk. Mark 1 Corruption.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{The Cracking Wind (Umbhemisi)}
The Salt Scar is not a desert. It is the graveyard of cities that stopped walking. Now a red dust ‚àí‚àí‚àí not sand, but ground bone and forgotten oaths ‚àí‚àí‚àí has begun to crawl north. The elders call it \textbf{Umbhemisi}, the Cracking Wind. Where it touches a keystone, the stone weeps rust. Where it touches a roller, the wood splits. The cities that hear its song are moving south, but the dust follows.

\textbf{Umbhemisi's Advance [8]} ‚àí‚àí‚àí A campaign timer that ticks when:
\begin{itemize}
‚Ñπtem The party remains in one place for more than a week (+1)
‚Ñπtem A keystone is cracked by sabotage or neglect (+1)
‚Ñπtem The Still Voice is summoned (+2)
‚Ñπtem A city stops moving (+2)
\end{itemize}
At 4 segments: Red dust storms appear on the horizon. All navigation rolls suffer $‚àí1$ die.  
At 8 segments: Umbhemisi reaches the city. Roller Integrity ticks $‚àí1$ per day, and the Cracking Wind itself may manifest as a boss (use the \textbf{Dust Walker} stats from the Nine Rollers sidebox).

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=black!3,colframe=black!40!white,title={Patronage & Power}]
Among the Ngomebe, power flows through keystones, migration rights, and the loyalty of the Mason‚àíKin Houses. An elder's authority rests on the age of his House's keystone and the number of cities that follow his route. The Aeler engineers hold technical authority; the Root‚àíbinders offer a heretical alternative; and the Ancestral Healers speak for the dead, who still vote through the cracks in the stone.

\textbf{For the GM:}  
Power in Ngomebe society is measured in movement and stone. Patronage often takes the form of keystone charters, sledge‚àíwax, or stone‚àísong fragments‚àí‚àí‚àíeach tied to a specific city and House. To emphasize this:
\begin{itemize}
‚Ñπtem Tie rewards to physical tokens (chips of keystone, lumps of wax, recorded songs) that can be challenged, stolen, or voided.
‚Ñπtem Let rival Houses issue conflicting claims, forcing players to choose whose city shelters them.
‚Ñπtem Use the roller‚àípits, keystone chambers, and Mason‚àíKin halls as arenas for social contests, where migration routes are debated and saboteurs are judged.
‚Ñπtem Introduce \textbf{Ancestral Cracks} as a way for players to gain clues from the dead ‚àí‚àí‚àí but at the cost of the ancestors' attention.
\end{itemize}
In Ngomebe society, the stone remembers everything ‚àí‚àí‚àí including the weight you carry. The ancestors do not forget. The tokoloshe does not forgive. And the city that stops dies.
\end{tcolorbox}

  \subsection*{The Hook}
  
  The party arrives at the edge of a moving city ‚àí‚àí‚àí not one of the great metropolises, but a smaller Walker named \textbf{Khomani's Progress}, named after the mason who carved its keystone three centuries ago. The city is dying.
  
  \textit{Elder Morvath} meets you at the sledge‚àítrack, his face gray with granite dust and exhaustion. ``The city has not stopped in forty days. We cannot stop. The ancestors will not let us. But the rollers are cracking. The oxen are dying. And something is following us ‚àí‚àí‚àí something that walks in the dust and counts our breaths.''
  
  He leads you to the keystone chamber. The great stone is warm. Too warm. A network of fine cracks spreads from its center like veins. The cracks are singing ‚àí‚àí‚àí a low, mournful hum in a language none of you recognize.
  
  ``The Ninth Roller is failing,'' Morvath says. ``It was carved by Khomani herself. It has never been replaced. If it fails, the city stops. If the city stops, the Hollow comes. We have nine days until the roller cannot turn. Find a replacement ‚àí‚àí‚àí or learn to sing the stone‚àísong well enough to heal it.''
  
  \vspace{2mm}
  \subsection*{The Mechanics}
  
  The party has \textbf{nine days} (or scenes) to save \textit{Khomani's Progress}. Each day, they may choose one action from the table below. The city's condition is tracked by three timers:
  
  \begin{longtable}{|p{0.3\textwidth}|p{0.5\textwidth}|}
  ‚Ñéline
  \textbf{Clock} & \textbf{Description} \\
  ‚Ñéline
  \textbf{Roller Integrity [9]} & Starts at 9 (the Ninth Roller). Ticks down each day. At 0, the roller fails. \\
  ‚Ñéline
  \textbf{Ancestor Favor [8]} & Starts at 4. Ticks up when the party honors the ancestors, ticks down when they offend. At 8, the ancestors help. At 0, the Still Voice comes. \\
  ‚Ñéline
  \textbf{Root‚àíbinder Attention [6]} & Ticks up when the party interacts with Root‚àíbinders or damages the forest. At 6, the Root‚àíbinders attack. \\
  ‚Ñéline
  \end{longtable}

  \subsection*{The Pursuing Threat}
  
  On the third day, the party discovers what has been following the city: a \textbf{Dust Walker} ‚àí‚àí‚àí a spirit of abandoned keystones, drawn to cities that have lost their way.   \vspace{2mm}
