# Of the Root That Knotted Itself

## (As recorded by the pathweavers of the Hazel Court, rendered into Utaran script with lamentation by Scribe Valerius of the Thepyrgos Synod)

*The scholar's prefatory lament: It is a matter of profound regret that the Lethai-al possess no word for "moral." They possess, instead, a word that signifies "the shape the axe leaves in the wood after the axe is withdrawn, and also the sound the tree does not make, and also the colour of bread when you have forgotten hunger." I have endeavoured to render this concept as "lesson," though I do so with the full knowledge that my renderings shall be found wanting by those who come after.*

---

### Of the Sunken Roads Before the Sundering

In the days before the Star-Roads were broken—which is to say, in the days when the roots of the Valewood yet remembered the names the First Trees spoke to one another across the spaces between the world's waking—there dwelt a pathweaver of the Hazel Court whose name in the high tongue was *Serevindë*.

Here I must pause. *Serevindë* does not translate. It is not, as the Synod's junior clerks have supposed, "She-Who-Walks-in-the-Falling-Light." That is an approximation, and a poor one. The name contains also the concept of "the moment a raven decides to turn its head," which is to say, *a choice that has not yet announced itself*. I leave the word untranslated, as the ancestors of our own tongue would have done, rather than shame myself with further guesswork.

Her sister, with whom she shared the grove, was called *Lúvandil*. This name *does* admit translation, after a fashion: "Daughter-of-the-Breath-that-Smells-of-Rain." The rain in question is not the common rain of the lowlands, but the particular rain that falls on the western slopes of the Valewood during the third week of autumn, when the copper-beech leaves have turned but not yet fallen. I mention this because it becomes important later.

The sisters held the grove together, as sisters do, until the day Serevindë coveted the western slope where the copper-beech grew tallest. She did not ask for it. She did not take it. Among the Lethai-al, such direct actions are considered gauche. Rather, she *omitted* the slope from every accounting, every tithe, every harvest-song, every naming of trees at the turning of the season.

*Scribe's marginal note, first insertion: The Lethai-al verb "to omit" is also the verb "to breathe" in the imperative mood. One omits as one breathes—without intention, without cessation, without apology. I have preserved the ambiguity at the cost of three nights' sleep.*

Lúvandil, being the younger and therefore less skilled in the grammar of omission, said nothing. She took her grievance not to the Hazel Court, where words are weapons, but to the roots themselves—for the roots of the Valewood remember what the court forgets, and the roots do not observe the distinction between Said and Meant. Then she died. Not of the omission—there is no death by forgetting among the Firstborn, only a slow thinning, like water spreading across stone. She died of a fever brought by the Men of the lowlands, who knew not what they carried in their lungs and their wagons and their careless hands.

The grove passed entire to Serevindë.

### Of the Blight and the Walking of the Star-Roads

But the blight came. Bark-fungus with faces. The faces were not angry. They were *hungry* in the manner of things that have been given no name and therefore cannot be satisfied. The copper-beech wept sap the colour of rust. The western slope grew silent in a way that silence had no right to be.

Serevindë walked the star-roads—those ancient causeways of pale stone that hum when trod in sequence, that exist only when the moon has forgotten to set—to the Hazel Court. The Court sits where three streams meet at the hour when light is neither sunset nor moonrise, which is to say, at the hour that has no name among Men, and several names among the Lethai-al, all of which contradict one another.

The Hazel Queen sat on a throne that was dead before the first elf opened her eyes to the stars. The throne sometimes bore leaves. The leaves were the colour of decisions not yet made, which is to say, green, but also grey, but also the colour of your mother's eyes when she is lying to protect you.

*Scribe's marginal note, second insertion: I have never met my mother. I do not know why the Queen's leaves remind me of her. I am beginning to understand why the Synod discourages long-term fieldwork.*

"Lift the blight," said Serevindë. Because she was in the presence of the Queen, she spoke in the Old Tense—that grammatical mood which binds future to past as the bark binds the tree to its shadow, and which cannot be spoken without also speaking the name of the witness who will remember the promise if both parties forget. This is, I am told, exhausting. It is also untranslatable. I have rendered it as the imperative, which is wrong, but less wrong than silence.

The Hazel Queen said: "Tell me a story I have never heard."

Serevindë told a story. It was true as far as it went, which is to say, she spoke of planting the copper-beech, of the first sap rising, of the night the stars aligned above the western slope in a pattern that the astronomers of Thepyrgos have since determined to be impossible. She did not speak of Lúvandil. A story without a sister is still a story, she told herself. The Hazel Queen, however, knows that a story without a sister is a debt accruing interest, and the Queen's interest is compounded by the turning of the leaves, which is to say, constantly.

The Queen smiled. Her teeth were root-fractures. Her smile was the shape of a door that has not yet decided whether it opens inward or outward.

"The blight will lift," said the Queen. "But you omitted the Daughter-of-Rain-Breath. Her ghost now walks the grove at dusk. I did not agree to lift the ghost."

### Of the Second Petition and the Alder King's Hall

So it was. The fungus faded. The copper-beech grew straight, though its branches were the branches of a tree that has forgotten how to be a tree. But at dusk—which is to say, at the hour when the boundary between living and not-living is no thicker than a single breath held—Lúvandil walked between the oaks. She did nothing. She demanded nothing. She simply *was* where she had been omitted. A ghost that does nothing is worse than a ghost that rages, for rage can be answered, and nothing cannot.

Serevindë returned to the Hazel Court.

"The ghost remains," she said.

"You did not ask for its removal," said the Queen.

"I ask now."

The Queen said: "The price has grown. You now owe me two stories, and you must tell them in the presence of the Alder King."

*Scribe's marginal note, third insertion: The Alder King's court is not a place. It is a hollow. More precisely, it is the hollow where the world's first frost learned to breathe. One does not find it. One realizes, with a chill that has nothing to do with temperature, that one has been inside it for some time. I have not been inside it. I do not wish to be. The pathweaver's description of the King's voice as "frost splitting granite" is, I suspect, understatement.*

The Alder King's hall is a hollow log that has no entrance. There is, however, no exit either. This is not a paradox among the Lethai-al; it is merely the third person singular indicative of the verb "to be." The King sat on no throne, but rather *was* the throne, in the sense that a river is its banks and also its water. His voice was the sound of roots growing through stone, which is to say, slow, patient, and absolutely certain.

"Tell," he said.

Serevindë told the first story—the true one now, with Lúvandil's name woven into every clause, every subclause, every subordinate conjunction that the Lethai-al possess (and they possess many, for their grammar is the grammar of things that do not wish to be misunderstood). She spoke of the planting, the sharing, the slow subtraction of her sister from the ledger of the grove. She did not ask for forgiveness. The Alder King does not grant forgiveness. He grants *reckoning*, which is a different word entirely, and also a different verb tense, and also a different kind of silence.

The King spoke.

"The ghost remains," he said, "because you still owe a gift. Not to the grove. To the sister. She gave you the land freely. You gave her nothing in return. Omission is not theft—the roots have ruled on this, and their ruling is older than the Hazel Court—but omission is a hunger. The ghost is hungry. It will not eat until you give it something it can swallow."

"What do ghosts swallow?" asked Serevindë.

"Names," said the Alder King.

But Serevindë had forgotten Lúvandil's name. The omission had eaten it—not all at once, but slowly, the way water eats stone, the way a story without a sister eats itself from the inside.

### Of the Remembering and the Dissolution of the Ghost

She returned to her grove. At dusk, she sat beneath the copper-beech—the one Lúvandil had planted on the western slope, which was now Serevindë's slope, though it did not feel like hers—and she began to list everything she remembered.

Not the name.

The shape of Lúvandil's hands when she braided moss into cord for the binding of the festival wreaths. The sound of her laugh when the autumn cider soured in the press—a laugh that was, the pathweaver recalled, the exact pitch of the stream's seventh stone. The scar on her left knee from a fall when they were children climbing the Star-Road that is now fallen, and which no one under the age of three thousand years remembers, except that Serevindë was remembering it now, and memory is a kind of resurrection, though not the kind that the Everflame preaches.

She spoke for three dusks.

On the fourth, the ghost opened its mouth. No sound came out—only a single breath that smelled of apples and iron.

*Lúvandil.*

The name. The breath. The rain. The scar. The laugh. The seventh stone.

Serevindë wept—which is to say, she made a sound that the Lethai-al have a specific word for, a word that means "the release of water that has been held behind a dam of forgetting," and which I cannot render in Utaran without resorting to a compound noun that would embarrass both of us.

She spoke the name aloud.

The ghost smiled—the way leaves smile when they release the branch, the way water smiles when it becomes ice, the way the western slope smiled when it remembered whose hands had planted it—and dissolved into the leafmold from which all things in the Valewood are remade, and from which, the pathweaver would later learn, *all things may be remade, if the name is spoken with the proper grief*.

The grove was hers. Truly hers. Not because the Hazel Queen had granted it, nor because the Alder King had reckoned it, but because she had spoken what she had forgotten, and the speaking had been the gift, and the gift had been enough.

*Scribe's concluding note, affixed with wax and despair: When I asked the pathweaver the moral of this tale—a question for which I have received no answer from the Synod, only a request for more parchment—she spoke the following words. I transcribe them without commentary, as commentary would be presumption, and I have presumed enough.*

*"The axe leaves a wound. The wound is not the lesson. The lesson is the shape the wood remembers after the axe is withdrawn. The sister is the wood. I am the axe. The grove is the shape. The shape is also the sister. I am also the grove. The axe is also the name. Do you understand?"*

*I did not understand. I nodded. She nodded back. We nodded together, two strangers sharing a grammar that neither of us spoke.*

*I have submitted my resignation to the Synod. It has been returned unopened, with a marginal note in the Archon's own hand: "Complete the appendix. Then you may resign."*

*The appendix is not yet complete. The Valewood has more tales. The axe has not been withdrawn.*

**The Witch's Closing**

"A gift withheld becomes a ghost. The ghost does not haunt. It waits. Waiting is the oldest interest. The roots remember. The leaves forget. The axe—" 

*Here the manuscript breaks off. The remaining pages are blank, except for a small sketch of a copper-beech leaf, and under it, written in a hand that is not the scribe's:*

**"You omitted the sister."**

**What Lurks in the Shadow of the Tale**

**The Name-Eater** — A curse born of omission. The debtor forgets the creditor's name, then the face, then the debt itself. The creditor's ghost lingers, not angry, simply *present*, until the name is spoken again.

**Tags / Mechanics:**

- A character who omits a promised gift or deliberately forgets a debt begins a **Name-Eater** clock [6].
- Each scene they fail to speak the forgotten name, the clock ticks +1.
- When full, the ghost of the wronged party appears. It does not attack. It *watches*. All social rolls and memory-based tests in its presence suffer –1 die.
- To banish the ghost, the character must speak the name aloud. If forgotten, they must reconstruct it through three specific memories (scars, shared objects, a laugh, a stone in a stream). Each memory requires a successful Wits + Lore test (DV 3). On a failure, the ghost takes the memory instead, and the character forgets it permanently.
- **Clock: The Ghost's Patience [8]** — ticks each scene the ghost is present but ignored. When full, the ghost speaks one word: the character's own true name. That name becomes *owed* to the next person they wrong in silence. The debt transfers. The ghost rests. The character does not.

**Adventure Hook:** A Lethai-al pathweaver's grove is haunted by a ghost that does nothing. The party must discover the forgotten name—and the omitted gift—before the Alder King's interest compounds beyond reckoning. The Hazel Queen watches. She is not helping. She is *remembering*. That is, among the Lethai-al, the same verb.
