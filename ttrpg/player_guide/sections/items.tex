\section{The Philosophy of Gear}
In Fate’s Edge, your character’s possessions are described in the fiction, not tracked on a ledger. You have enough coin, barter, or credit to afford:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Ordinary meals, lodging, and travel rations
  ℹtem Basic tools (rope, lantern, writing kit, lockpicks)
  ℹtem Small bribes (a drink, a gift)
  ℹtem Routine transportation (ferry, caravan passage)
\end{itemize}
If the GM ever asks ``Do you have enough?'' the answer is usually \textbf{yes} – unless scarcity is the point of the scene.

\section{Mundane Equipment}
łabel{sec:mundane−gear}

\subsection{Weapons and Armor}
Weapons and armor are covered in §\ref{sec:weapons−armor} (Core Mechanics). Briefly:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem \textbf{Light weapons (4 XP):} fast, concealable.
  ℹtem \textbf{Medium weapons (8 XP):} balanced.
  ℹtem \textbf{Heavy weapons (12 XP):} slow, powerful.
  ℹtem \textbf{Light armor (4 XP):} converts 1 Harm → 1 Fatigue.
  ℹtem \textbf{Medium armor (8 XP):} converts 2 Harm → 1 Fatigue, –1 die physical skills.
  ℹtem \textbf{Heavy armor (12 XP):} converts 3 Harm → 2 Fatigue, –2 dice physical skills, no sprint in rough terrain.
\end{itemize}
You may acquire these with XP at character creation or later.

\subsection{Adventuring Gear}
You are assumed to have the following unless you are in a survival situation:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Backpack, waterskin, 1d6 days of rations, a knife, flint and steel, a small lantern or candle
  ℹtem Tools for any skill you have at level 1+ (e.g., lockpicks for Subterfuge, healer’s kit for Medicine)
  ℹtem A set of clothing appropriate to your culture
\end{itemize}
If you need something unusual (a climber’s kit, a disguise, a rare herb), you may:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Spend 1 Boon to declare you already have it (narrative permission).
  ℹtem Spend 1 XP to acquire it (as a one‑time resource).
  ℹtem Acquire it as a minor asset (4 XP) if you want permanent access.
\end{itemize}

\section{Magic Items}
Magic items are not bought with gold. They are acquired by spending XP, completing quests, or crafting. A magic item is a \textbf{permanent asset} that grants a mechanical benefit. It requires \textbf{upkeep} (see below).

\subsection{Cost Basis: Talent Equivalents}
A magic item’s effect is roughly equivalent to a \textbf{Talent} (see \ref{ch:talents}). Use the Talent XP cost as the base price, then apply a \textbf{discount} because items can be lost, stolen, or broken.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
⊤rule
\textbf{Effect Power} & \textbf{Talent Cost} & \textbf{Magic Item Cost (XP)} \\
∣rule
Minor (e.g., +1 die to a narrow skill once/scene) & 2–3 XP & 2 XP \\
Major (e.g., +1 die to a broad skill always) & 4–6 XP & 4 XP \\
Prestige (e.g., once/session reroll a failed save) & 7–10 XP & 6 XP \\
Epic (e.g., teleport once/day) & 11+ XP & 8 XP \\
⊥tomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\emph{Discount reason:} A Talent is permanently attached to your character; an item can be taken away. The discount also covers the need for upkeep.

\subsection{Attunement and Upkeep}
A character may be \textbf{attuned} to up to 3 magic items at a time (attunement takes a short rest). During each downtime, you must pay \textbf{upkeep} for each magic item you keep attuned:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem \textbf{Efficient:} Pay XP equal to the item’s cost divided by 3 (rounded up, minimum 1). You spend minimal effort.
  ℹtem \textbf{Intensive:} Pay 1 XP and describe a downtime scene where you maintain the item (cleaning, recharging, appeasing its spirit).
\end{itemize}
If you neglect upkeep, the item becomes \textbf{Neglected} (no benefit until you pay the upkeep retroactively). If neglected for two consecutive downtimes, it becomes \textbf{Compromised} (requires a quest to restore).

\subsection{Losing Magic Items}
If you lose a magic item (theft, destruction, sacrifice), you lose the XP you spent on it. The GM may allow you to recover it as a story hook. You may also choose to ``retire'' an item (sell it, trade it, give it away) and regain half its XP cost (rounded down) to spend on something else.

\section{Crafting Magic Items}
łabel{sec:crafting}

Crafting a permanent magic item is a \textbf{campaign‑level project}. It requires:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem A character with relevant skills: \textbf{Arcana 3+} and \textbf{Craft 2+} (or appropriate specialty).
  ℹtem A workspace (e.g., a workshop asset, a consecrated forge).
  ℹtem Rare components (obtained through adventuring – the GM will decide).
  ℹtem A \textbf{Downtime Project Timer } (6–12 segments). Each segment requires a scene or a downtime action.
  ℹtem The XP cost of the item (see table above) – paid when the timer is completed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Crafting Procedure}
łabel{subsec:crafting−procedure}
\begin{enumerate}
  ℹtem \textbf{Design:} Describe what the item does. The GM sets the Tier (Minor/Major/Prestige/Epic) and XP cost.
  ℹtem \textbf{Quest:} The GM frames a short arc to obtain a rare component (e.g., ``a phoenix feather'', ``the heart of a storm elemental'').
  ℹtem \textbf{Downtime Timer :} Roll \textbf{Wits + Craft} or \textbf{Wits + Arcana} each downtime. Each success advances the timer by 1. Partial successes create complications (lost components, attracting attention). On a failure, the timer may tick backwards.
  ℹtem \textbf{Completion:} When the timer fills, you pay the XP and the item is complete. The GM may add a final, optional cost (e.g., ``it also consumes a memory'').
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Crafting Consumables}
One‑use items (potions, scrolls, runestones) are much easier. Crafting a consumable:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Takes a single downtime action (no timer).
  ℹtem Costs \textbf{1 XP} per consumable (or 2 XP for a powerful one).
  ℹtem Requires an appropriate skill roll (DV 3) – on a failure, the components are wasted.
  ℹtem No upkeep required.
\end{itemize}
Examples: \emph{Healing potion} (restores 1 Fatigue), \emph{Sight scroll} (grants +1 die to Investigation for one scene), \emph{Thunderstone} (ranged attack, Harm 1 to a small area).

\section{Upgrading Mundane Gear}
You may also spend XP to give a mundane item a permanent magical property. Use the same costs as magic items. The process is faster than crafting from scratch (no rare component quest, just a downtime timer of 4–6 segments).

\section{Summary for Players}
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Ordinary gear is free – describe what you carry.
  ℹtem Weapons and armor cost XP (light 4, medium 8, heavy 12).
  ℹtem Magic items cost XP based on Talent equivalents (2–8 XP).
  ℹtem Magic items require upkeep (XP or downtime scene) each downtime.
  ℹtem Crafting a magic item is a long project involving a timer, rare components, and XP.
\end{itemize}

\section{Artifacts: Items That Owe You (And You Owe Them)}

\emph{The coin that never spends is the one you don’t remember taking. The blade that never dulls is the one you never truly own.}

Most magic items are tools – you pay XP, you keep them, you maintain them. \textbf{Artifacts} are different. They are not crafted; they are inherited, discovered, or earned through story. An artifact has a will of its own, however faint. It remembers every hand that held it, every oath spoken over it, every debt left unpaid.

\subsection{What Makes an Artifact?}

An artifact is a magic item with three special properties:

\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem \textbf{Obligation.} When you first claim an artifact (or when you use its deeper powers), you mark Obligation to the artifact itself. This Obligation is tracked like Patron Obligation (Capacity = Spirit + Presence). The artifact may demand tasks, sacrifices, or simply that you carry it to places it wishes to go.
  ℹtem \textbf{Rites.} An artifact grants one or two specific Rites (see \ref{ch:world−powers−obligation}) that you can invoke without a Patron, a Codex, or a Symbol. Using a Rite from an artifact costs the normal Obligation (in addition to any Obligation from attuning to the artifact). The Rites are tied to the artifact’s nature – a sword that remembers a betrayed king might grant \textit{Rite of the Oath Unbroken}; a lantern that once lit a heretic’s pyre might grant \textit{Rite of the Revealing Flame}.
  ℹtem \textbf{Indestructible (mostly).} If an artifact is lost, stolen, or destroyed, it reappears intact at the next dawn – somewhere nearby, often in the hand of the one who owes it the most Obligation. The only way to be rid of an artifact permanently is to fulfill all Obligation to it, then pass it to a willing heir (or bury it in a place of true rest, with GM approval).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{No Upkeep, but No Escape}

Unlike ordinary magic items, artifacts require \textbf{no upkeep} (no XP or downtime scenes). They maintain themselves – or rather, they maintain their hold on you. However, you cannot simply stop attuning to an artifact; it will return at dawn until your Obligation to it is cleared.

\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem \textbf{Attunement:} An artifact counts toward your attunement limit (3 items). Breaking attunement is a ritual (downtime scene) that requires a \textbf{Spirit + Lore} roll (DV 4). On a failure, you mark 1 Fatigue and the artifact reappears the next dawn.
  ℹtem \textbf{Obligation Overflow:} If your Obligation to an artifact exceeds your Capacity, you suffer Fatigue as normal (see §\ref{sec:obligation−overflow}). Additionally, the artifact may manifest – a cold weight in your pack, a whisper in your ear, a reflection that doesn’t match the room.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Subtle Power, Not Apocalyptic}

Most artifacts are \textbf{subtle}. They do not reshape battlefields or raise armies. A true artifact might:

\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Allow you to hear the last words of anyone who died holding it (once per session).
  ℹtem Show you the nearest hidden door or secret passage when you spend 1 Boon.
  ℹtem Grant +1 die to all Sway rolls when bargaining with a specific faction (e.g., the Salt Prince’s court).
\end{itemize}

Apocalyptic artifacts – those that could end kingdoms or unmake reality – are almost unheard of. One or two are whispered about in the oldest ledgers, but no living witness has confirmed their existence. The Grey Wanderer themselves is said to carry a shard of the First Oath, but if they do, they have never used it.

\subsection{Acquiring an Artifact}

You cannot buy an artifact with XP. You find them:

\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem In the hoard of a forgotten dragon.
  ℹtem At the bottom of a sunken temple, still clutched by a skeleton.
  ℹtem As an inheritance from a dying mentor (``Take my mother’s mirror. It will show you what I could not face.'').
  ℹtem As a reward from a Patron who wishes to bind you more tightly.
\end{itemize}

The GM determines the artifact’s Rites and the starting Obligation (usually 1–3 segments). You do not pay XP for the artifact itself, but you may need to complete a quest to unlock its full power.

\subsection{Player Advice}

Artifacts are \textbf{story hooks}, not power−ups. If you find one, ask yourself:
\begin{itemize}
  ℹtem Why does this thing want me to carry it?
  ℹtem What will it demand when my Obligation fills?
  ℹtem Who else wants it – and what will they pay?
\end{itemize}
Embrace the artifact as a co−star in your character’s drama. The Rites it grants are useful; the strings attached are the real treasure.
