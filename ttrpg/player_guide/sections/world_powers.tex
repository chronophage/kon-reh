\section{Of Patrons, Runes, and Invokers}
\label{sec:invoker-lore}

\begin{quote}
``You wish to walk the road of power? Then listen well. The world is old, and older still are the voices beneath it. We call them \textit{Patrons}, though they were never sworn to us. They are the tides that move unseen, the keepers of forgotten bargains, the sleepers beneath the stone and the stars. To call upon them is to dip a hand into a river that has carved mountains.''
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Patrons}
\index{Patrons}

Patrons are vast intelligences---not gods, though some worship them as such. They are embodiments of \textit{concepts} and \textit{forces} rather than sovereigns. Raéyn, mistress of the tides and the sea-routes. Khemesh, the crushing inevitability of the deep. Each offers power, but always with cost: fatigue, scars upon fate, or a slow unweaving of one’s own story.

To entreat a Patron is to risk being marked. Their Rites are gifts and snares both.

\textbf{In Fate's Edge, power has its price.}  
When a being rises beyond mortal scale, its identity dissolves and reforms into something larger, older, and less free. This transformation is known as \textbf{Iconic Ascension}.

\medskip
\textbf{Icons are not individuals.} They are \emph{forces}—living embodiments of desires, patterns, and long-term agendas. Their power is immense, but their agency is small. An Icon cannot freely choose its actions; its nature compels it.

\medskip
\textbf{Icons cannot touch the world directly.} Their presence is too vast, their will too diffuse. Instead, they act through intermediaries:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Chosen} who carry fragments of their purpose
    \item \textbf{Devotees} who interpret their omens
    \item \textbf{Agents} who benefit from their favor
\end{itemize}

\medskip
Icons shape events not with hands, but through \textbf{boons}, \textbf{omens}, and \textbf{shifts in fate} granted to those who serve their desires—whether fleeting hungers or ancient ambitions woven into the world's fabric.

\medskip
\textbf{To serve an Icon is to wield a living idea.} Their gifts are potent, but their influence reshapes those who bear them. Their will echoes in mortal choices long after their voices fade.
\end{tcolorbox}

\paragraph{No True Acolyte}
Interpreting a patron's will is often a dangerous prospect in and of itself. Many a Runekeeper has found themselves on the opposite end of machinations from others from the same patron.

\paragraph*{Runebound Resilience (18 XP Epic Talent)}%
\textit{Prerequisite: Tier III+, Codex, Spirit 4+}\\[3pt]
You have learned to transform the very essence of harm into deeper communion with your Patron.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Harm Conversion:} Once per round, you may convert up to 2 Harm levels to +2 Obligation instead of taking the damage
  \item \textbf{Obligation Endurance:} While at 6+ Obligation segments, gain +1 die to all Rite invocations
  \item \textbf{Patron's Shield:} When reduced to Harm 3, you may immediately Invoke any known Rite without marking additional Obligation
  \item \textbf{Debt Fortification:} At 8+ Obligation segments, ignore first Harm 1 each round
  \item \textbf{Once per scene}
\end{itemize}

\noindent
\textbf{Narrative Integration:}\\
\textit{``Your flesh has become a ledger, and every wound is a prayer. The deeper your Patron's claim, the stronger your resistance to mortal frailty.''}


\paragraph*{Patron's Favored (18 XP Prestige Talent)}%
\textit{Prerequisite: Tier III+, Codex, Familiar, 8+ total Obligation segments}\\[3pt]
Your Patron has marked you as specially favored, granting you unprecedented access to their power with reduced cost.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Low Rite Discount:} Reduce Obligation cost for Low Rites by 1 (minimum 0)
  \item \textbf{Empower:} Once per scene, spend 1 Action + 1 Boons:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Target gains +1 Corruption level per round for 3 rounds. Corruption lasts until the end of the scene.
      \item While empowered, gain +1 die to all rolls
    \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Empower Push:} Spend 1 Action + 1 Boon + 3 Obligation:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Target(s) gains +3 Corruption levels until end of scene
      \item While empowered, gain +1 die to all rolls
      \item Target gains +Corruption fatigue at the end of the scene.
    \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Enhanced Gift:} Your \textbf{Patron's Gift} now provides +2 dice instead of +1
  \item \textbf{Debt Bound:} Cannot Empower again until all Obligation is cleared
\end{itemize}

\noindent
\textbf{Narrative Integration:}  
\textit{The Patron's favor comes with a price—but for the truly devoted, that price is worth the power. You carry their blessing openly now, and their influence spreads like wildfire to those who would walk in your shadow.}

\begin{quote}
``Every Rune is a promise. Every line a covenant. Do not mistake the Runekeeper’s silence for weakness; their memory is the foundation of our craft.''
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Invokers}
\index{Invokers}

\textbf{Invokers} are those who dare. Neither archivists nor worshippers, they are travelers on the knife-edge between story and ruin. An Invoker learns the Rites of a Patron, weaves them into their own Art, and bends fate for a moment. Yet invocation is not command: it is negotiation. The Patron always leaves its mark. The stronger the Rite, the deeper the scar.

Invokers are often wanderers, exiles, or seekers. To common folk they are feared---witches, oathbreakers, meddlers with things not meant for mortal hands. But when the village falls to plague, when the sea closes its roads, when the dead refuse their rest, it is an Invoker who is called upon.

\subsection{Closing Words}
The dance between Patron, Runekeeper, and Invoker is a triangle of peril and necessity. Without Patrons, there is no power. Without Runekeepers, no record. Without Invokers, no action. Together, they shape the crooked, perilous art we call Invocation.

\paragraph{Clearing Corruption}
Corruption may be reduced through \textit{purging rituals}, such as exorcisms, sacred songs, or rites of contrition. 
These require a test (typically \textbf{Lore + Spirit}) against a DV equal to the character’s current corruption level.  
On success, reduce corruption by 1. On failure, the corruption manifests violently, imposing a temporary Condition or advancing its narrative expression.  

Optional: A \textbf{Story Beat} may also be spent to attempt such a ritual, representing the personal cost of atonement. Patrons may demand specific acts of service, sacrifice, or obligation as part of the purging process.

\subsection*{Patron's Gift and Borrowed Grace: XP Free, Sustainable Magic Enhancement}

\subsubsection*{Patron's Gift (Runekeeper Talent)}

\textbf{Patron's Gift -- Daily Magical Enhancement}\\[3pt]
\textbf{Cost:} Free (requires Thiasos/Familiar representing the bond to a Patron) \\
\textbf{Activation:} 1 Action, once per scene \\
\textbf{Effect:} Gain +1 die to one skill check related to your Patron's domain and +1 Melee or Ranged. \\
\textbf{Requirements:} Must be thematically appropriate to Patron's domain. \\
\textbf{Limitations:} Cannot stack with other magical effects and is subject to talent stacking rules. The enchanted item can only be used by the Runekeeper. \\
\textbf{Cost:} Generates 1 Story Beat (Diamonds - magical/spiritual disturbance). \\[6pt]

\paragraph*{Key Tips for Effective Patron's Gift Usage}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Daily Reliability:} Use for routine tasks that benefit from minor magical assistance.
  \item \textbf{Domain Focus:} Choose applications that align strongly with your Patron's themes.
  \item \textbf{Narrative Integration:} Let the Story Beat cost inform interesting complications.
  \item \textbf{Complementary Use:} Fill gaps where full Rites would be excessive.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Borrowed Grace}

\textbf{Borrowed Grace} is a minor imbuement through which an Invoker momentarily channels their Patron’s favor into a single decisive action.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Type:} Invoker Talent — Lesser Imbuement \\
\textbf{Cost:} 1 Boon \emph{or} 1 Fatigue; 1 Action \\
\textbf{Duration:} One action \\
\textbf{Requirement:} The Invoker must visibly display the symbol of their Patron. \\

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Effect:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Gain \textbf{+1 die} to a single combat action, \emph{or}
  \item Gain \textbf{+1 Effect} to a single social or stealth action.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Limitations:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Borrowed Grace may be used \textbf{once per scene}.
  \item The effect cannot be stacked, extended, or shared.
  \item If the Patron’s symbol is \emph{Compromised}, Borrowed Grace may still be invoked at \textbf{–1 die}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Power Level Guidance}

These tools are designed to provide a \textbf{+1 die reliable enhancement} without the overhead of full Rite/Spell casting. 
They should feel like:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Consistent character enhancement rather than dramatic power spikes.
  \item Reliable support rather than game-changing effects.
  \item Sustainable advantages rather than resource-intensive solutions.
\end{itemize}

When used properly, \textbf{Patron's Gift} and \textbf{Borrowed Grace} eliminate the ``resource management tax'' that can make magical characters feel less effective than their non-magical counterparts, while maintaining the thematic richness that makes magical characters special.

\begin{itemize}

  \item \textbf{Standard}: +2 Fatigue
  \item \textbf{High}: +3 Fatigue
\end{itemize}

\section{Clearing Obligation Segments (Extended)}
\label{sec:obligation-clearing-extended}
\index{Obligation}\index{Acts of Service}\index{Boon}\index{Focused Devotion}

Obligation measures the growing narrative weight of a Patron’s attention. It is not debt in a transactional sense, but rather the sense that a Patron has invested, noticed, and expects relevance. This section expands the SRD guidance with philosophy, examples, and optional rules.

\subsection{Core Principle}
\label{subsec:obligation-principle}
Obligation is cleared through \textbf{meaningful engagement} with the Patron. The system assumes Patrons are narrative entities: they do not care about coin, but about resonance. Clearing requires a demonstration that the character is still walking their path.

\subsection{Off-Screen Clearing (Primary Method)}
\label{subsec:obligation-offscreen}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Act of Service.} Clear 1 segment by performing a resonant service aligned to the Patron’s themes or goals.\index{Acts of Service}
\item \textbf{Requirement.} The player must provide an \emph{Intricate Description} of the act—detail, symbolism, and impact.
\item \textbf{Scope.} The act need not be arduous, but it must be thematically precise (e.g., weaving a shadow tapestry for Ikasha; negotiating a shrewd pact for the Clockwork Monad).
\item \textbf{Reward.} Successful clearing often comes with narrative color: whispers of approval, subtle omens, or a temporary advantage in Patron-facing scenes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{On-Screen Clearing (Optional Mechanic)}
\label{subsec:obligation-onscreen}
\paragraph{Talent: Focused Devotion (3–5 XP).}\index{Focused Devotion} Once per scene, spend 1 Boon to clear 1 segment of Obligation from a Patron. This cannot remove segments accrued during the same scene. It represents a desperate or ritualized gesture to stave off attention without fully satisfying it.

\subsection{Narrative Implications}
\label{subsec:obligation-implications}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Player Agency.} The player defines how service manifests, reinforcing creativity within the Patron’s thematic frame.
\item \textbf{GM Collaboration.} The GM adjudicates sufficiency: Is the service aligned? Is it intricate enough?
\item \textbf{Ongoing Relationship.} Clearing segments emphasizes that the Patron bond is active, not static. The relationship evolves with acts of recognition.
\item \textbf{Scene Economy.} The cap on on-screen clearing ensures the mechanic cannot erase costs just accrued. It forces the long view.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Worked Examples}
\label{subsec:obligation-examples}
\paragraph{Example 1: Ikasha, Angel of Serpents.} A character clears a segment by shedding their old cloak, burning it, and narrating how it symbolizes leaving behind a former self. The GM rules it sufficient, noting the subtle scent of scales in the air.

\paragraph{Example 2: Clockwork Monad.} The player narrates spending an hour meticulously recalibrating a piece of equipment, using precise measurements and ritual cleaning. The GM agrees: the service is thematically aligned and clears 1 segment.

\paragraph{Example 3: Oath of Flame \& Light.} The group holds a dawn vigil, speaking aloud a vow they have kept. The intricate description convinces the GM, who clears 1 segment from each participant's Obligation to that Patron.

\subsection{Optional Modules}
\label{subsec:obligation-modules}
\paragraph{Overflow Consequences.} When Obligation fills and overflows, the Patron demands immediate service. This may be delivered as a Complication Point (or Story Beat) trigger: the Patron intrudes, offering aid with a binding cost.
\paragraph{Segment Scaling.} Particularly devoted characters may require greater services: clear 2 segments with a dramatic act (pilgrimage, sacrifice, or political maneuver), subject to GM approval.
\paragraph{Shared Obligation.} In multi-character bonds, the GM may allow a group act of service to clear multiple segments, but only if each player contributes to the Intricate Description.

\subsection{GM Guidance}
\label{subsec:obligation-gm}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Be Generous with Hooks.} Reward creativity with flavorful omens, not just a cleared box.
\item \textbf{Maintain Tension.} Do not allow trivial or repetitive services to count. Require new symbolic angles each time.
\item \textbf{Keep On-Screen Costly.} Remind players that Focused Devotion costs a Boon and cannot erase fresh debt.
\item \textbf{Tie to Patron Themes.} If a service drifts off-theme, let it fail or only partially clear.
\end{itemize}

\begin{tcolorbox}[title={Summary},colback=gray!5,colframe=black]
Obligation is cleared through \textbf{thematic service, intricate description, and narrative engagement}. Off-screen acts remain the primary path; on-screen clearing is a costly safety valve. Treat every act as a chance to show how characters evolve under their Patron’s gaze.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{GM Notes}
\label{subsec:acts-service-gm}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Novelty Over Volume.} Repeating the same petty act does not keep clearing segments; vary the symbol or site.
\item \textbf{Precision Beats Difficulty.} A sharp thematic hit at “petty” scale can clear 1 segment as surely as a muddled “modest.”
\item \textbf{Hook the World.} Many of these acts conveniently create future clocks, NPC ties, or bargains—use them.
\item \textbf{Patron’s Largess} A Patron’s will is mysterious, but their displeasure is rarely subtle. For narrative reasons, the GM may rule that a Patron is especially offended—perhaps by a neglected omen, a rival gaining favor, or simple caprice—and exact an additional toll beyond the usual cost.
\end{itemize}

Fear tagged \emph{Beast, Blood, Prey} becomes primal. On 1 (Fight), victim charges the nearest target without thought. \\

Sealed Gate & Boundaries, Closure & Amplify & 
Fear tagged \emph{Confinement, Threshold, Trapped} worsens. On 4 (Flee), victim may injure themselves clawing at sealed barriers. \\

The Traveler & Ways, Journeys, Crossroads & Resonance & 
Fear tagged \emph{Lost, Path, Journey} also inflicts Complication Points. Terror of losing the way becomes mechanical hindrance. \\

Thrysos & Revels, Frenzy, Excess & Reroll &

Varnek Karn & Necromantic Archives, Death & Amplify & 
Fear tagged \emph{Death, Undead, Necromancy} becomes horrific. On 2 (Hide), victim feigns death among corpses. \\

The Witness & Truth, Revelation & Amplify & 
Fear tagged \emph{Truth, Knowledge, Revelation} intensifies. On 6 (Submit), target may confess dark secrets under perceived Iconic judgment. \\

\subsection{Implementation Guidelines}
\label{subsec:patron-fear-guidelines}

\subsubsection{Tag Matching}
When a Fear effect is triggered, compare the effect's Magic Tags against the active Patron's Domain Tags. If any tags match, the Patron's influence applies.

\subsubsection{Multiple Patrons}
If multiple Patrons are present with overlapping domains, the GM may:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Choose the most thematically appropriate influence
  \item Apply influences sequentially (reroll then amplify)
  \item Allow players to choose which influence affects them
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Player Agency}
Players should be informed when Patron influence is active and given the opportunity to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Spend resources (Boons, Willpower) to resist the influence
  \item Narrate how their character resists or embraces the effect
  \item Choose from available influence options when multiple apply
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Embracing Corruption}
\index{Corruption!Embracing} \label{subsec:embracing-corruption}

A character with \textbf{2+ levels of Corruption} may choose to \textbf{Embrace} it, transforming creeping decay into a permanent \textbf{Talent}. Embracing never reduces Corruption --- it reshapes it. The deeper the corruption, the greater the power and the cost.

\begin{center}
\begin{longtable}{@{}l l l@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Corruption Level} & \textbf{Talent Tier} & \textbf{Cost} \\
\midrule
2 (Early) & Minor Talent & +1 Permanent Corruption (cannot Fade below 1) \\
3 (Deepening) & Major Talent & +2 Permanent Corruption, constant visible tell \\
4+ (Severe) & Transcendent Talent & Permanently altered, locked at Stage 3+ \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}
\end{center}

\noindent Embraced Talents always reflect the Patron’s themes (e.g., shadow, indulgence, empathy, paranoia). They grant great power, but mark the character irrevocably as claimed.

\section{Witchcraft, Cults, and Religions}
\label{sec:witchcraft-cults-religions}

\index{witchcraft}\index{cults}\index{religions}

The patrons of Fate's Edge are vast and impersonal, but mortals rarely approach them alone. Over centuries, traditions have coalesced into \textbf{witchcraft traditions} (systemic, low‑magic practices that lean on thresholds and the Ninth), \textbf{cults} (small, often secretive groups focused on a single patron or a specific interpretation), and \textbf{religions} (large, institutional faiths that blend patronage with ethics, law, and social order).

This section gives players the tools to belong to—or rebel against—these organisations. It does not replace the per‑patron witchcraft entries (see each patron’s \textit{Witchcraft} sidebar); instead, it shows how those practices are woven into living faiths.

\subsection{What Is Witchcraft?}
\label{subsec:witchcraft-def}

In the broadest sense, witchcraft is the \textbf{quiet work} of thresholds, names, and small debts. A witch does not invoke a patron with a full rite; they \textbf{tend} edges: doorways, riverbanks, birthbeds, graves. Witches are respected, feared, or ignored depending on the culture, but they are everywhere.

Mechanically, witchcraft is represented by:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Hedge Gifts} (see §\ref{ch:assets-followers} and the \textit{Hedge Gift} talent).
  \item \textbf{Quick Workings} and \textbf{Full Rituals} (threshold magic, usually without a patron’s direct attention).
  \item \textbf{Patronage}: many witches also serve a patron (e.g., Inaea, Isoka, Morag, Ikasha) and use the patron’s \textit{Witchcraft} tools (see each patron’s entry).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Player Advice:} If you want to be a witch, take the \textit{Craft of the Hedge} talent (4 XP) and choose a patron who offers witchcraft tools. Your character knows the old customs, the proper knots, and when to pour the ninth cup (and when to stop at eight).

\subsection{Religions Organised Around Patrons}
\label{subsec:organised-religions}

Religions in Fate's Edge are built on a \textbf{substrate of patrons}. A single church may revere several patrons under a unified theology, or it may focus on one while acknowledging others as saints or aspects.

\subsubsection{The Church of the Everflame (Ecktorian Orthodox)}
\label{sec:everflame}

\textbf{Dominant in:} Ecktoria (heartland), Vhasia (syncretic cults), eastern Viterra (along the Belworth).  
\textbf{Also present:} Synods in Thepyrgos.  
\textbf{Primary patrons:} Oath of Flame \& Light (core), Inquisitor Prime (inquisitorial arm).  
\textbf{Secondary patrons:} Mykkiel (de‑emphasised, mostly in legal contexts).

The Everflame is the state religion of the fallen Utaran Empire’s direct successor. It teaches that the Flame is the original witness of all oaths, and that keeping one’s word is the highest form of worship. The Everflame does not deny other patrons, but considers them \textit{lesser fires}—aspects of the one true flame.

\textbf{Key tenets:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Oath is the Seed.} A promise made before a lit candle is binding in this life and the next.
  \item \textbf{The Flame Judges.} Liars, oathbreakers, and heretics are purified by fire.
  \item \textbf{The Candle Never Dies.} A votive light can be passed from generation to generation; to let it go out is to abandon one’s ancestors.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Structure:} Hierarchical, with the \textbf{Grand Magistrate} (secular) and the \textbf{High Priest of the Everflame} (spiritual) sharing power. Provincial bishops (\textit{Censers}) oversee local clergy, while \textbf{Inquisitors of the Prime} hunt forbidden magic (see Inquisitor Prime).

\textbf{Player Hooks:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item You are a runaway novice who saw the Inquisition burn an innocent.
  \item Your family’s candle has guttered. You must relight it at the Great Bazaar of Lence.
  \item A Censer has demanded you spy on a rival parish; refusal means excommunication.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Temple of Light (Evangelical)}
\label{sec:temple-light}

\textbf{Dominant in:} Viterra (heartland), Thepyrgos (intellectual core).  
\textbf{Spreading into:} Linn territory (via “education” and trade).  
\textbf{Primary patrons:} Oath of Flame \& Light, Inquisitor Prime (more prominent than in the Everflame).  
\textbf{Secondary:} Mykkiel (de‑emphasised, sometimes seen as a cult within the Temple).

The Temple teaches that the Light is personal, not institutional. Every believer can speak directly to the Light through prayer and good works. It is more evangelical than the Everflame, sending missionaries to “illuminate” the uninitiated. Its relationship with the Everflame is cold—each considers the other a corrupt or misguided branch.

\textbf{Key tenets:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Inner Light.} Every person carries a spark of the divine. Cultivate it.
  \item \textbf{The Open Hand.} Hospitality to strangers is a sacrament. Never turn away a traveller.
  \item \textbf{The Purge of Shadows.} Witchcraft that hides in darkness must be exposed (but the Temple is less burn‑happy than the Everflame).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Structure:} Decentralised. Local \textbf{Lanterns} lead congregations; travelling \textbf{Beacon‑bearers} spread the word. The \textbf{Synod of Thepyrgos} provides theological guidance but does not command.

\textbf{Player Hooks:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item You are a Beacon‑bearer sent to convert a Linn fishing village – but their storm‑rites work better than your prayers.
  \item A rival preacher has declared your congregation heretical. Prove the Light shines through your works.
  \item You found an old Everflame shrine; the local priest wants it destroyed, but your patron (the Oath) demands you restore it.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Covenant of Mykkiel (The Law Giver)}
\label{sec:covenant-mykkiel}

\textbf{Centred in:} Galalina (a city‑state north of the Amaranthine).  
\textbf{Also present:} Pereshi (minority), isolated communities in Viterra and Vhasia.  
\textbf{Primary patron:} Mykkiel (the Arbiter of the Covenant).  
\textbf{Secondary:} Oath of Flame \& Light (for solemn vows), Inquisitor Prime (inquisitorial functions, but seen as extreme by mainstream Covenant).

The Covenant is a prescriptive, legalistic faith that worships the Law itself as divine. It is **jealous**—it tolerates no other patrons (though it acknowledges they exist) and demands absolute obedience to its codices. To break the law is to sin; to sin is to invite ruin.

\textbf{Key tenets:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The Word is Stone.} Scripture is literal, unchanging, and must be obeyed without exception.
  \item \textbf{Every Debt Shall Be Paid.} Justice is mechanical: restitution, punishment, and reconciliation in that order.
  \item \textbf{No Unwitnessed Promise.} A vow sworn without two witnesses is void; the Covenant does not protect the careless.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Structure:} Hierarchical, with a \textbf{High Justiciar} (elected by the College of Scribes) as supreme authority. Provincial \textbf{Archons} judge civil cases; \textbf{Silent Notaries} record every transaction.

\textbf{Player Hooks:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item You are a Notary who discovered a loophole that would free a guilty murderer. Do you seal the record or tear it out?
  \item A rival Archon has accused you of “interpretive drift” – a crime punishable by exile.
  \item The Covenant has declared a holy crusade against a heretical sect. You must choose: enforce the law or flee.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Pereshi (Dualist Flame‑Cults)}
\label{sec:pereshi}

\textbf{Locations:} Scattered across the Amaranthine coast, especially in Zakov, Linn, and the Vilikari marches.  
\textbf{Primary patron:} Oath of Flame \& Light (as the “Good Flame”).  
\textbf{Secondary:} Mor’iraath (as the “Devouring Void”), occasionally Malachai (as corruptor).

The Pereshi are not a single religion but a family of **animistic, dualist cults** that believe the universe is a struggle between the \textit{Good Flame} (warmth, life, community) and the \textit{Devouring Void} (cold, loneliness, forgetting). Fire is not worshipped as an element but as a \textit{witness} – a living thing that remembers what it consumes.

\textbf{Key tenets:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fire Eats Guilt.} Sacrifice a written sin to flame; the fire will carry it to the Good Flame.
  \item \textbf{Never Let the Hearth Die.} A cold hearth is an invitation to the Void.
  \item \textbf{The Ninth Spark is Taboo.} Do not light a ninth candle from the same source; that flame belongs to the Void.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Structure:} Decentralised, led by \textbf{Hearth‑Elders} (often witches or runekeepers). No central authority; each settlement maintains its own rites.

\textbf{Player Hooks:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Your village’s hearth has gone cold. You must venture into the ruins of a sunken temple to retrieve a coal from the First Flame.
  \item A Pereshi elder has declared you a “Void‑touched” because you once received a blessing from Malachai. Prove your loyalty or be cast out.
  \item The Good Flame has stopped answering prayers. A rival cult claims the Void is winning – and they want to sacrifice you to tip the balance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cults: Small, Focused, Dangerous}
\label{subsec:cults}

Cults are small groups devoted to a single patron or a heretical interpretation of a patron. They are often secret, sometimes dangerous, and always hungry for influence.

\subsubsection{Example: The Congregation of the Unmasking (The Witness)}
\textit{See the Witness’s entry.} This apocalyptic cult believes the world will end when the last hidden truth is spoken aloud – and they work to hasten that revelation, exposing every lie regardless of cost.

\subsubsection{Example: The Serpent’s Chorus (Isoka)}
\textit{See Isoka’s entry.} A snake cult that worships transformation through venom and shedding. Members ritually “shed” their old identities, sometimes becoming unrecognisable even to themselves.

\subsubsection{Example: The Gilded Ledger (Maelstraeus)}
A merchant cult that treats commerce as prayer. Its members meet in counting-houses, not temples, and believe that a perfectly balanced ledger is a holy text. They trade in secrets, debts, and futures – and they always collect.

\textbf{Player Hooks for Cults:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item You owe a favor to a cult – but you don’t know which one. A messenger appears with a sealed letter.
  \item Your patron’s cult has split; both sides demand your allegiance.
  \item You infiltrated a cult for information, but their rituals are beautiful and their community is warm. The line between spy and convert is thinning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Joining or Creating a Cult or Religion}
\label{subsec:joining-cults}

As a player, you may decide that your character belongs to (or is fleeing from) one of these organisations. Work with your GM to determine:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Your rank:} Novice, initiate, full member, exile, heretic.
  \item \textbf{Your patron:} Most organisations have a primary patron; your character may follow that patron or a secret secondary one.
  \item \textbf{Your obligation:} Cults and religions come with duties. The GM may represent these as an \textbf{Obligation Clock} (4–6 segments) tied to the organisation, separate from patron obligation. Failing to pray, tithe, or attend rites ticks the clock.
  \item \textbf{Your perks:} Membership may grant a \textbf{Hedge Gift} (e.g., access to a cult’s safe house, a networking Boon once per session, or a regional contact). Work with the GM to design a minor benefit appropriate to your tier.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Running Religious Conflicts (GM Guidance)}
\label{subsec:religious-conflicts}

This section is primarily for players, but GMs may find it useful when framing campaign arcs.

\textbf{Common Flashpoints:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Schism:} A cult splinters; both sides claim your character as their own.
  \item \textbf{Inquisition:} The Everflame or the Temple of Light sends investigators into a region where folk‑witchcraft is common.
  \item \textbf{Holy War:} The Covenant of Mykkiel declares a crusade against a “lawless” land (e.g., Zakov or the Violet Steppe).
  \item \textbf{Syncretism:} A local practice merges two patrons (e.g., a Pereshi cult that also honours the Pale Shepherd). The orthodox authorities call it heresy.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Mechanical Tools:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Religious Clock [4–8]:} Tracks the organisation’s patience, suspicion, or zeal. The GM ticks it when players defy dogma or attract attention.
  \item \textbf{Patron Intrusion:} The organisation’s patron may demand a public act of faith (e.g., burning a forbidden text, sponsoring a mission).
  \item \textbf{Excommunication:} When the clock fills, the character may be stripped of benefits, hunted, or simply shunned. This can be a great source of drama, not a dead end.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Final Advice for Players}

Faith in Fate’s Edge is not a background colour – it is a source of story. Whether you are a devout Everflame paladin, a cynical smuggler who lights a candle to the Traveler before every voyage, or a heretic who fled the Covenant, your beliefs will shape your choices. Embrace the costs, lean into the contradictions, and let the patrons surprise you.

\begin{quote}
\itshape
``I have lit candles for the Everflame, whispered to the Traveler at crossroads, and carved Mykkiel’s seal into a debtor’s door. Every faith asks for a piece of you. The question is not which piece – but whether you will miss it when it is gone.''
--- Dusana of the Raven Road
\end{quote}
