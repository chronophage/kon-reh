\section{No Coin, Only Consequence}
Ňāabel{sec:no‚ąícoin}

In Fate's Edge, there is no abstract currency. No gold pieces, no silver scales, no ledgers of mundane wealth. What you \emph{know}, whom you \emph{know}, and what you \emph{owe} matter more than what you carry.

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Incidental expenses} ‚ąí‚ąí a meal, a night at an inn, a riding horse, a week's trail rations ‚ąí‚ąí are \textbf{hand‚ÄĎwaved}. Assume your character has enough from past earnings, barter, or simple hospitality to cover ordinary life. If the fiction demands scarcity, the GM will introduce a Supply Timer or a Complication.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Significant acquisitions} ‚ąí‚ąí assets, favors, magical items, political influence, or the service of a powerful ally ‚ąí‚ąí require one of three forms of payment:
  \begin{enumerate}
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Experience Points (XP)} ‚ąí‚ąí Spend your hard‚ÄĎwon growth to secure lasting resources.
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Barter or Strings} ‚ąí‚ąí Trade goods, services, information, or narrative leverage (Strings) for equivalent value.
    ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Obligation} ‚ąí‚ąí Accept a Debt Timer or increase Obligation to a patron (terrestrial or supernatural) in exchange for immediate gain.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

This chapter explains how to spend XP on non‚ÄĎpersonal advancement, how to pool XP for party assets, how to use Strings and Obligation as currency, and how to form a player‚ÄĎowned terrestrial patron: a \textbf{Trust}.

\section{Abstracted Treasure: Finds That Change the Story}
Ňāabel{sec:abstracted‚ąítreasure}

You do not track copper or silver. Instead, treasure is \textbf{leverage} ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí each meaningful find has a one‚ÄĎtime narrative effect. A bag of gold is not treasure. A gold coin that was once paid as a traitor's blood‚ÄĎprice \emph{is}.

\subsection{Finding and Using Treasure}
Ňāabel{subsec:using‚ąítreasure}

When players loot a significant location (a dragon's lair, a fallen noble's vault, a sunken temple), the GM awards \textbf{1‚Äď3 treasure items} at appropriate tiers. No coin. No weight. No bookkeeping.

A character may carry at most \textbf{3 treasure items} total (any mix). Unused treasure fades after one session unless invested in an Asset (pay the treasure's XP equivalent to keep it as a permanent String).

Spend a treasure item to:

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Improve Position} on a relevant roll by one step (Minor).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Auto‚ÄĎsucceed} on a non‚ÄĎcombat roll where leverage applies (Standard).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Rewrite a scene's stakes} (Major) ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí e.g., ``I use the king's true name to make his guards stand down.''
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Treasure as XP (Optional)}
Ňāabel{subsec:treasure‚ąías‚ąíxp}

A player may convert a treasure item into XP instead of using its narrative effect:

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Minor $‚Üí$ 1 XP
  ‚ĄĻtem Standard $‚Üí$ 2 XP
  ‚ĄĻtem Major $‚Üí$ 4 XP
\end{itemize}

The GM should warn: \emph{``You can sell the dragon's scale for power, or you can use it to command the dragon's respect.''}

\subsection{Integration with Existing Systems}
Ňāabel{subsec:treasure‚ąíintegration}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Assets:} A treasure item can be spent to acquire a Minor Asset instantly (no downtime).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Debt Timers:} A treasure item can clear 1‚Äď3 segments of a Debt Timer (GM discretion).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Strings:} A treasure item can become a permanent String if invested during downtime.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Boons:} A treasure item can be cashed for 2 Boons instead of its narrative effect.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The Golden Rule of Treasure:}
\emph{If it doesn't change the story, it's not treasure.} Name it. Describe it. Then watch your players hoard it not for its value, but for its power to change what happens next.

\section{What Costs XP (and What Doesn't)}
Ňāabel{sec:xp‚ąícosts‚ąítable}

\subsection{Hand‚ÄĎWaved (Free)}
Ňāabel{subsec:handwaved}

Always assume you have enough for:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Ordinary meals, lodging, basic travel rations.
  ‚ĄĻtem A riding horse or pack mule (if fictionally plausible).
  ‚ĄĻtem Basic tools (rope, lantern, writing kit, lockpicks).
  ‚ĄĻtem Minor repairs, small bribes (a drink, a gift).
  ‚ĄĻtem Routine transportation (ferry, caravan passage) \emph{unless} the journey itself is the adventure.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{GM Guideline:} When a player asks ``Do I have enough for X?'' and X is not a major story resource, answer ``Yes'' unless scarcity is the point of the scene.

\section{Pooling XP for Party Assets}
Ňāabel{sec:party‚ąípool}

A party may create a \textbf{shared pool} of XP to acquire and maintain assets that benefit everyone. The pool is tracked separately from individual XP.

\subsection{Creating a Pool}
Ňāabel{subsec:creating‚ąípool}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Any player may contribute any amount of their unspent XP to the party pool.
  ‚ĄĻtem Contributions are voluntary and irreversible (unless the party agrees to dissolve the pool ‚ąí‚ąí see \ref{subsec:dissolving‚ąípool}).
  ‚ĄĻtem The pool has no owner; decisions about spending from it require \textbf{majority vote} (or table consensus).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Spending from the Pool}
Ňāabel{subsec:spending‚ąípool}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem The pool can only be used for \textbf{party assets} (shared safehouse, group ship, guild charter, spy network) or for \textbf{forming a Trust} (see \ref{sec:forming‚ąítrust}).
  ‚ĄĻtem It cannot be used for individual Attribute/Skill increases, personal Talents, or personal Followers.
  ‚ĄĻtem When an asset bought with the pool requires \textbf{upkeep} (see \ref{subsec:upkeep‚ąíoptions}), the upkeep can be paid from the pool (efficient method) or by assigning a party member to handle it (intensive method).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dissolving the Pool}
Ňāabel{subsec:dissolving‚ąípool}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem If the party permanently disbands or agrees to liquidate, the remaining XP in the pool is \textbf{lost} ‚ąí‚ąí it represents sunk investment in shared infrastructure.
  ‚ĄĻtem Instead, the party may \textbf{transfer} the asset to one character (who must pay its XP cost from their own XP to ``buy out'' the others). The other contributors are compensated with an equivalent String from that character (see \ref{sec:barter‚ąístrings‚ąíobligation}).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Example:} Alexander (9 XP banked), Lyra (4 XP), and Sera (6 XP) want a shared safehouse (Minor Asset, 4 XP). They contribute 2 XP each to the pool (total 6 XP). They spend 4 XP to acquire the safehouse, leaving 2 XP in the pool for future upkeep. The safehouse is now a party asset. Later they add Mira (3 XP), who contributes 1 XP to the pool.

\section{Barter, Strings, and Obligation}
Ňāabel{sec:barter‚ąístrings‚ąíobligation}

When you lack XP, you may pay with \textbf{barter} (trading goods or services), \textbf{Strings} (narrative leverage), or \textbf{Obligation} (debt to a patron). These are not coin ‚ąí‚ąí they are story fuel.

\subsection{Barter}
Ňāabel{subsec:barter}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Trade an item of comparable value (a magical sword for a spy network, a noble's secret for a ship).
  ‚ĄĻtem The GM adjudicates fairness. A barter exchange usually requires a Sway or Broker roll (DV 2‚ąí‚ąí4).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Service Barter:} Promise a future task in exchange for immediate gain. This creates a Debt Timer [4‚ąí‚ąí6] for the recipient.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Strings as Currency}
Ňāabel{subsec:strings‚ąícurrency}

Strings are defined in the core rules as narrative permissions ‚ąí‚ąí a favor owed, a secret known, a charter held. They can be \textbf{spent} as currency:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
‚ä§rule
\textbf{String Value} & \textbf{Can purchase} \\
‚ą£rule
Minor String (a small favor, a local contact) & One‚ÄĎuse discount of 1 XP on an asset, or a single use of an Asset's off‚ÄĎscreen effect \\
Standard String (a noble's oath, a guild seal) & A Minor Asset (4 XP value) or clear 2 segments of a Debt Timer \\
Major String (a life‚ÄĎdebt, a royal charter) & A Standard Asset (8 XP value) or a Follower (up to Cap 3) \\
‚ä•tomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

To convert a String into XP, the player must \textbf{use} the String in play ‚ąí‚ąí narrate how it secures the asset. The GM may require a roll (e.g., Presence + Sway to call in the favor).

\subsection{Obligation as Payment}
Ňāabel{subsec:obligation‚ąípayment}

Instead of XP, accept a \textbf{Debt Timer } or increase \textbf{Obligation} to a terrestrial patron (see \ref{sec:terrestrial‚ąípatrons}).

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
‚ä§rule
\textbf{Debt Timer Size} & \textbf{Equivalent Value} \\
‚ą£rule
[4] & Minor Asset or one‚ÄĎtime service \\
[6] & Standard Asset or Cap 3 Follower \\
[8] & Major Asset or Cap 5 Follower \\
‚ä•tomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

You may clear a Debt Timer segment by spending 2 XP (as if buying off the debt with hard work), or by performing an appropriate service (GM discretion).

\paragraph{Example:} The party wants a ship (Standard Asset, 8 XP). They have no XP, but a Sidhi pirate captain offers them the ship in exchange for a \textbf{Debt Timer [6]}. When it fills, they must raid an Oshiiran grain barge for her. They accept.

\section{Player‚ÄĎOwned Terrestrial Patrons: Trusts}
Ňāabel{sec:trusts}

A \textbf{Trust} is a collective entity ‚ąí‚ąí a guild, a covenant, a company, a secret society ‚ąí‚ąí founded and run by the player characters. It acts as a \textbf{terrestrial patron} (see \ref{sec:terrestrial‚ąípatrons}), but the players collectively decide its demands and benefits.

\subsection{Forming a Trust}
Ňāabel{sec:forming‚ąítrust}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Requires \textbf{10 XP from the party pool} (cannot be paid individually).
  ‚ĄĻtem The party names the Trust, defines its purpose (e.g., ``The Silkhand League ‚ąí‚ąí‚ąí spice merchants and smugglers''), and chooses an initial \textbf{Seat} (headquarters or symbol).
  ‚ĄĻtem The Trust starts with a \textbf{Debt Timer [4]} (the cost of founding) that the players must clear through roleplay or XP before it can grant benefits.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{How a Trust Works}
Ňāabel{subsec:trust‚ąímechanics}

The Trust is a \textbf{non‚ÄĎplayer character} entity with:

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Obligation Capacity} equal to the party's combined Spirit + Presence (or a fixed value of 6 for simplicity). Members incur Obligation to the Trust when they ask for its help.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Debt Timer } (size 4, 6, or 8) ‚ąí‚ąí tracks external obligations the Trust owes to other factions.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Asset Pool} ‚ąí‚ąí the Trust can own Assets (safehouses, ships, contact networks) bought with party XP (see \ref{sec:party‚ąípool}).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Follower Pool} ‚ąí‚ąí the Trust can employ Followers (e.g., guards, clerks, spies) paid from the party pool.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Benefits of Belonging to a Trust}
Ňāabel{subsec:trust‚ąíbenefits}

A character who is a member of the Trust (all founders are members; new members can be inducted) may:

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Call on the Trust} ‚ąí‚ąí once per session, gain +1 die on a roll directly related to the Trust's purpose (e.g., smuggling, trade, information). This costs no Obligation.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Borrow an Asset} ‚ąí‚ąí use one of the Trust's Assets for a scene. Mark +1 Obligation to the Trust per Asset used.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Request a Follower} ‚ąí‚ąí temporarily command a Trust follower (Cap 1‚ąí‚ąí2) for a mission. Mark +1 Obligation per follower.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Use the Trust's Strings} ‚ąí‚ąí the Trust may accumulate Strings (favors from others). A member may spend one such String as if it were their own (costs 1 Obligation).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Obligation to the Trust}
Ňāabel{subsec:trust‚ąíobligation}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem A member's Obligation to the Trust is tracked separately from other patrons. Capacity = Spirit + Presence (see \ref{sec:obligation‚ąícapacity}).
  ‚ĄĻtem When Obligation fills (exceeds capacity), the Trust \textbf{demands a task} ‚ąí‚ąí a mission that advances its goals. If refused, the member is \textbf{expelled} and loses all Trust benefits.
  ‚ĄĻtem Obligation can be cleared by:
  \begin{itemize}
    ‚ĄĻtem Performing a service for the Trust (GM discretion, usually clears 1‚ąí‚ąí2 segments).
    ‚ĄĻtem Contributing XP to the Trust pool (2 XP per segment).
    ‚ĄĻtem Donating a personal Asset or String of equivalent value.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Ňāabel{subsec:trust‚ąígrowth}

The party may invest additional XP from the party pool to expand the Trust:

\subsection{Trusts as Story Engines}
Ňāabel{subsec:trust‚ąístory}

The GM should use the Trust as a source of complications:

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Schism} ‚ąí‚ąí A faction within the Trust disagrees with leadership. Start a Trust Loyalty timer [6].
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Hostile Takeover} ‚ąí‚ąí Another group (rival guild, Ashaani agents) tries to seize the Trust's assets.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Debt Called In} ‚ąí‚ąí A faction to which the Trust owes a Debt Timer makes a demand that endangers the party.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Reputation} ‚ąí‚ąí The Trust gains a reputation (good or bad). High reputation grants +1 die on social rolls within its sphere; low reputation attracts enemies.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Example Trust ‚ąí‚ąí The Silkhand League:}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Founded}: 10 XP from party pool (Alexander 3, Lyra 2, Sera 3, Mira 2)
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Seat}: A warehouse in Silkstrand's dyeworks district
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Initial Assets}: None (spent XP on founding)
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Debt Timer }: 4 (they owe the local harbor master)
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Members}: The four PCs
\end{itemize}
After a few sessions, they contribute another 8 XP to the pool and buy a \textbf{Standard Asset}: a spy network among the port's Sidhi pilots. They also add a \textbf{Trust Follower} (Cap 2, a runner named Tema). Now when Lyra needs to know a ship's schedule, she marks 1 Obligation to the Trust and asks Tema. When the Trust's Obligation to her reaches capacity, the Trust will demand that she intercept a rival's cargo ‚ąí‚ąí a mission that becomes the next session's adventure.

\section{Summary for Players}
Ňāabel{sec:currency‚ąísummary}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{No coins.} Incidental expenses are free.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Treasure is leverage.} Minor, Standard, or Major finds that change the story.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Significant things cost XP, barter, Strings, or Obligation.}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Pool XP} for party assets.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Form a Trust} ‚ąí‚ąí a player‚ÄĎowned terrestrial patron ‚ąí‚ąí for shared power and shared trouble.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Use Strings as currency.} Collect favors, trade them, spend them.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Debt is story.} Accepting a Debt Timer is not failure; it's future adventure.
\end{itemize}
