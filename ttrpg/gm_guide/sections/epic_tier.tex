As characters reach Tier IV and V, the game changes. The scope expands from local concerns to regional, national, or world‚ąíshaking events. This chapter focuses on how to run these epic tiers effectively, with special attention to how the game \textbf{naturally scales} through Boon scarcity ‚ąí‚ąí not inflated Difficulty Values.

\section{What Changes at Tier IV‚ąí‚ąíV}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Scope:} Actions affect cities, nations, or the entire known world.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Consequences:} Failure can shift political borders, end bloodlines, or awaken ancient horrors.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Time:} Campaigns often span months or years, with downtime becoming a major phase.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Legacy:} Characters become legends ‚ąí‚ąí their deeds echo for generations.
\end{itemize}

The core mechanics remain the same. The difference is \textbf{weight} ‚ąí‚ąí every success and every failure reshapes the fiction in larger, more permanent ways.

\section{Natural Scaling: The Boon Economy Does the Work}

One of Fate's Edge's most elegant features is how it automatically scales difficulty without inflating numbers. At high tiers, characters have large dice pools (often 8‚ąí‚ąí10 dice). They succeed more often and generate fewer Misses and Partials ‚ąí‚ąí the primary sources of Boons.

\subsection*{Boon Generation Basics (Reminder)}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Miss (0 successes):} Gain 2 Boons.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Partial (0 < successes < DV):} Gain 1 Boon.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Clean Success (successes $‚Č•$ DV, SB = 0):} No Boons.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Success with SB (successes $‚Č•$ DV, SB > 0):} No Boons.
\end{itemize}
Boons do not come from winning. They come from \textit{struggling}.

\subsection*{Why This Works}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Low‚ąítier characters need the help.} Their small pools produce unreliable results; Boons give them a cushion.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{High‚ąítier characters must rely on skill, not luck.} With huge pools and reliable success, they no longer have a safety net.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{The scarcity creates strategic depth.} At high levels, spending a Boon on a re‚ąíroll means you cannot activate your spy network later. Every Boon is a meaningful choice.
\end{itemize}

‚ąądex{brittleness}
At high tiers, your power comes from skill, not luck. Fewer Boons mean every roll matters. This is intentional ‚ąí‚ąí embrace the tension.

\medskip
\noindent The simulation insights show that \textbf{chaos players thrived at high tier} because they understood this. They did not hoard Boons; they accepted scarcity and adapted by:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Relying on high Skills and clever positioning.
  ‚ĄĻtem Using Bonds to generate Boons when needed.
  ‚ĄĻtem Spending Boons only on critical moments.
\end{itemize}
A \textquotedbl brittle\textquotedbl\ character ‚ąí‚ąí one with few resources but immense competence ‚ąí‚ąí is not a flaw. It is the design. It makes every decision weighty and every success earned.

\medskip
\noindent \textit{Let the dice fall. Let the Boons be precious. Let the tension sing.}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Do Not Increase Boon Awards}
Resist the urge to give out extra Boons because players \textquotedbl look cool\textquotedbl\ or \textquotedbl rolled a 10.\textquotedbl\ The scarcity is intentional. It makes high‚ąítier play feel tense and consequential. If you inject Boons, you remove the resource pressure that defines epic play.

\subsection*{Example: Tier I vs. Tier V}
A Tier I rogue picks a lock (DV 3, 5 dice). She rolls a Miss $‚Üí$ gains 2 Boons, re‚ąírolls, succeeds.  
A Tier V master thief picks the same lock (DV 3, 10 dice). He rolls a Clean Success $‚Üí$ no Boons. Later, he fails a critical roll to disable a ward ‚ąí‚ąí he has only 1 Boon left. He must decide: re‚ąíroll this roll, or save it for the escape. The tension is real.

\section{Do Not Inflate Difficulty Values}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=red!5!white, colframe=red!75!black, title={Warning: DV Inflation Breaks High‚ąíTier Play}]
‚ąądex{DV!inflation warning}
A higher DV does not mean \textquotedbl more challenging\textquotedbl\ ‚ąí‚ąí it means \textquotedbl fewer Partials, fewer Boons, faster escalation.\textquotedbl\ At high tiers, players have large pools, but raising DV to 5 or 6 will starve them of Boons entirely and make failure feel arbitrary.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Scale Challenge With These Tools Instead:}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Timers:} Add a 4‚ąí‚ąí6 segment timer to the scene (\emph{Palace Coup [4]}, \emph{Ritual Completion [6]}). Players see it tick.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Position:} Worsen Position when the fiction demands danger. A Desperate roll already re‚ąírolls successes ‚ąí‚ąí that is plenty.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Consequences:} Use SB to add layered costs (see \ref{sec:outcome‚ąísb}).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Faction Timers:} Advance long‚ąíterm threats (\emph{War [8]}) regardless of player success in individual scenes (see \ref{sec:core‚ąíresolution‚ąícycle}).
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{When Is a Higher DV Appropriate?}
Only when the \emph{fiction} itself demands it: a god's impossible ward, a lock designed by a legendary artisan, a ritual that twists reality. Even then, keep it to DV 5‚ąí‚ąí6 and telegraph clearly: \textquotedbl This is a mythic challenge. Even with your power, it may fail.\textquotedbl

\section{Failure Must Matter}

At high tiers, a failed roll should never mean \textquotedbl nothing happens.\textquotedbl\ Instead:

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{A rival gains leverage.} \textquotedbl You cannot open the vault, but you see the Duke's sigil on the lock ‚ąí‚ąí he knows someone inside.\textquotedbl
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{A secret becomes public.} \textquotedbl Your investigation fails, but the guard captain mentions your name to a reporter.\textquotedbl
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{A faction timer advances.} \textquotedbl The ritual continues. \emph{Summoning [8]} ticks +1.\textquotedbl
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{An asset is compromised.} \textquotedbl Your safehouse is discovered ‚ąí‚ąí it is now \emph{Neglected}.\textquotedbl
\end{itemize}

If failure merely delays action without changing the situation, you are not using the system to its full potential.

\section{Using Bonds as the Primary Boon Engine}

At high tiers, Boons should mainly come from \textbf{Bond‚ąídriven actions} (see \ref{subsec:boons‚ąíearn}). Encourage players to:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Call on Bonds in difficult moments: \textquotedbl I owe you ‚ąí‚ąí I ask for your help now.\textquotedbl
  ‚ĄĻtem Expose allies or followers to danger to gain Boons.
  ‚ĄĻtem Accept new obligations or narrative fallout in exchange for Boons.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Example: Bonded Allies Transferring Boons}
The party faces a mythic guardian. Sariel, the rogue, has 0 Boons left. Kestra, the mage, has 2 Boons but is pinned at range. They share a Bond (\textquotedbl She saved me from the shadow cult.\textquotedbl)

\begin{quote}
  Kestra: \textquotedbl I spend my action to transfer 2 Boons to Sariel. As I channel, I shout: `Remember the Forge ‚ąí‚ąí you owe me!'\textquotedbl\\
  GM: \textquotedbl The Bond flares. Sariel receives 2 Boons. Spend them wisely.\textquotedbl
\end{quote}
Sariel uses 1 Boon to re‚ąíroll a failed stealth check and 1 Boon to improve her Position to Dominant. She slips past the guardian and disables the trap.

This turns a Bond from backstory into a tactical resource. At high tiers, such exchanges are the difference between victory and defeat.

\section{Designing High‚ąíTier Scenes}

When planning an epic scene, ask:
\begin{enumerate}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{What changes if the characters succeed?}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{What compromises emerge on a Partial success?}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{What new dangers arise on a Miss?}
\end{enumerate}

If the answer to \textquotedbl Miss\textquotedbl\ is \textquotedbl they try again,\textquotedbl\ increase the stakes, not the DV.

\subsection*{Example Scene: The Royal Vote}
The party tries to convince the council to declare war on a neighboring realm.
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Clean Success:} The vote passes; the army marches under their banner.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Success with SB:} The vote passes, but a rival is appointed general.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Partial:} The vote is delayed; new timer \emph{Council Deadlock [4]} appears.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Miss:} The vote fails. The rival declares war anyway ‚ąí‚ąí without the party's leadership.
\end{itemize}
No DV was inflated. The stakes did the work.

\section{Pacing and Scope}

\subsection*{Embrace Longer Timelines}
Time becomes a tool. Use downtime to:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Advance faction timers.
  ‚ĄĻtem Allow rumors to spread.
  ‚ĄĻtem Let players build legacies (fortresses, guilds, schools).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Abstract the Mundane}
At high tiers, do not roll for climbing walls or picking common locks. Assume competence. Save rolls for choices that matter.

\subsection*{Let Players Command Armies}
Treat armies as high‚ąíCap followers (see \ref{sec:followers‚ąíassets‚ąígm}). Use \textbf{Battle Timers} (4‚ąí‚ąí6 segments) for skirmishes. Do not resolve each soldier's attack ‚ąí‚ąí abstract with leadership rolls.

\section{Caveats and Warnings}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Do not design adventures like Tier I.} Linear dungeons and small‚ąíscale fetch quests feel beneath legendary heroes. Instead, give them systemic problems: a curse that erases memory, a political schism, a dying god.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Do not hoard Boons.} Spend SB and optional Threat Pool points (see) to create memorable moments. Players earned their power ‚ąí‚ąí challenge them with creative complications, not starvation.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Do not fall into \textquotedbl superhero\textquotedbl\ mode.} The world should still react believably. A legendary warrior can be assassinated in their sleep. A master mage can be outmaneuvered politically.
\end{itemize}

\section{Tools for Managing Boon Scarcity}

High‚ąítier play demands that GMs and players rethink how Boons are used. The following tools help make scarcity feel tense, not frustrating.

\subsection*{Boon‚ąíWorthy Moments (Player Guidance)}
At Tier IV‚ąí‚ąíV, a Boon should never be spent on a trivial re‚ąíroll. Instead, reserve Boons for:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Negating a catastrophic failure} (e.g., re‚ąírolling a Miss that would kill an ally).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Activating a key Asset} (e.g., calling in a spy network to reveal a weakness).
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Shifting Position from Desperate to Controlled} before a mythic roll.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Powering a Prestige or Epic talent} that changes the scene.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{GM tip:} Remind players of this at the start of each high‚ąítier session.

\subsection*{The \textquotedbl Substituting Fatigue for Boons\textquotedbl\ Rule (Revisited)}
Recall that a player may pay 1 Fatigue instead of 1 Boon (see Section \ref{subsec:boons‚ąíspend}). At high tiers, this becomes a lifeline. Encourage players to use it, but attach a narrative cost: \textquotedbl You push through the pain, but your hand trembles ‚ąí‚ąí your next roll is at Desperate Position.\textquotedbl

\subsection*{Follower Sacrifice as Boon Substitute}
A loyal follower (Cap 3+) can take the consequence of a failed roll in place of a Boon. The follower suffers Harm 1 (or a Condition) and the player avoids spending a Boon. This should be used sparingly (once per session maximum) and only when the fiction supports it.

\subsection*{Let the Roll Stand}
At high tiers, players may reflexively reach for Boons to re‚ąíroll one failure. Sometimes, the most dramatic outcome is to \textbf{let the failure stand}. As GM, you can say: \textquotedbl You have 0 Boons left. The die is cast. What does failure look like?\textquotedbl\ This forces players to engage with consequences rather than erase them.

\subsection*{Example: A High‚ąíTier Turn with 0 Boons}
The party's archmage, Jora (Tier V), attempts to counterspell a world‚ąíending ritual. She has 0 Boons. She rolls 8 dice (Wits 5 + Arcana 3) vs DV 4. Result: 3 successes ‚ąí‚ąí a Partial.
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Without Boons:} She cannot re‚ąíroll. The GM rules: \textquotedbl The ritual slows, but a backlash rips through the ley lines. A nearby village is consumed by wild magic. You save the world, but at a cost.\textquotedbl
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{If she had a Boon:} She could re‚ąíroll a failure and possibly achieve a Clean Success, avoiding the backlash.
\end{itemize}
The scarcity of Boons made the Partial outcome feel meaningful, not like a failure.

\subsection*{Physical Trackers for Boon Scarcity}
To make Boon scarcity visible and tactile:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Use a \textbf{small bowl or tray} in the center of the table with poker chips representing the \emph{party's} total Boons (players still track individual Boons, but a shared visual reinforces scarcity).
  ‚ĄĻtem When a player spends a Boon, they physically move a chip to a \textquotedbl spent\textquotedbl\ pile. When they earn a Boon, they add a chip.
  ‚ĄĻtem For high‚ąítier sessions, set a \textbf{table limit of 3 Boons maximum per player} (down from 5). This makes each chip precious.
\end{itemize}

\section{Sample High‚ąíTier Scenario: The Dying Sun}

\textbf{Premise:} The realm's patron sun‚ąíspirit is fading. Without it, crops fail, undead rise, and the land dies.

\textbf{Scope:} Continental.

\textbf{Timers:}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \emph{Sun's Fading [10]} ‚ąí‚ąí ticks each session, faster if neglected.
  ‚ĄĻtem \emph{Cult of Eternal Night [6]} ‚ąí‚ąí a faction that worships the darkness.
  ‚ĄĻtem \emph{Alliance of the Dawn [8]} ‚ąí‚ąí political support for a ritual to save the sun.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Success:} The party finds a lost relic, performs a ritual, and restores the sun ‚ąí‚ąí but must choose a cost (e.g., their own lifespans, a Patron's demand).

\textbf{Partial:} The sun is saved, but the cult is not destroyed ‚ąí‚ąí they will return.

\textbf{Miss:} The sun dies. The party must lead a desperate exodus or accept a dark bargain for a new light.

This scenario cannot be solved by a single roll. It requires investigation, diplomacy, combat, and sacrifice ‚ąí‚ąí perfect for epic tier play.

\section{Final Thought}

At Tier IV and V, your players are no longer adventurers ‚ąí‚ąí they are \textbf{legends in the making}. Honor their power by giving them problems that require wisdom, sacrifice, and creativity to solve.

The dice still matter. Consequences still flow. But now, every choice echoes across nations and generations.

Make it legendary.
