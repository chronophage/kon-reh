\textbf{Your Job:} Frame pressure, track consequences, let the fiction move forward.

\medskip
\textbf{Default Settings} \quad DV = 3 \quad \textperiodcentered \quad Position = Controlled \quad \textperiodcentered \quad One short Timer 

\medskip
\textbf{The Core Loop (every meaningful action)}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item Player declares intent \& approach (Attribute + Skill)
  \item You state Position \& set relevant Timers
  \item Player rolls d10s; count \textbf{Successes (6+)} \& \textbf{Story Beats (1s)}
  \item Compare to DV $\rightarrow$ use \textbf{Outcome Matrix}
  \item Apply consequences \& update the fiction
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\textbf{Outcome Matrix (what you do)}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Clean Success} (S $\geq$ DV, SB=0) $\rightarrow$ intent achieved, no complication
  \item \textbf{Success with SB} (S $\geq$ DV, SB>0) $\rightarrow$ intent achieved, spend SB for a twist
  \item \textbf{Partial} (0 < S < DV) $\rightarrow$ progress made, player gains 1 Boon
  \item \textbf{Miss} (S = 0) $\rightarrow$ no progress, player gains 2 Boons, spend SB to escalate
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Story Beats (SB) -- spend them to create drama}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \textbf{1 SB:} Minor -- noise, tick a timer, small setback
  \item \textbf{2 SB:} Moderate -- alarm raised, lose advantage, worsen Position
  \item \textbf{3+ SB:} Major -- reinforcements, scene shift, break an asset
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\textbf{Golden Rule}\par
\emph{When in doubt, make a ruling that keeps the story moving.} \\
Set DV=3, Position=Controlled, ask for the roll, and resolve with the Outcome Matrix.

\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{effectbox}
  \textbf{Position} (Dominant/Controlled/Desperate) = How risky is this action? -- determines severity of failure and re-rolls.\\
  \textbf{Effect} (Limited/Standard/Great) = How much do you achieve on a success? -- determines scope, scale, or quality.
  
  \vspace{4pt}
  \small
  \textit{Example:} Disarming a trap -- Limited Effect lets one person cross, Standard lets two, Great lets all four.
\end{effectbox}

  \item \textbf{DV 3, Position Controlled} -- your default settings.

% SELLING THE ACTION BOX
% =========================================================
\begin{tcolorbox}[
  colback=red!5!white,
  colframe=red!75!black,
  title=\textbf{Selling the Action -- When Players Give You Gold},
  fonttitle=\bfseries\large,
  sharp corners,
  drop shadow,
  before skip=10pt,
  after skip=10pt,
  breakable
]
\textit{``Don't just tell me you swing your sword. Show me the dust in your eyes, the tremble in your arm, the desperate prayer you whisper to the Traveler.''}\\
\textit{``And don't just paint a pretty picture -- show me what your character risks, fears, or loves in this moment.''}

\medskip
When a player \textbf{sells the action} -- not by piling on adjectives, but by revealing what their character cares about, what they stand to lose, or why this moment matters -- you have permission to give them a mechanical edge. This is a reward for \textbf{roleplay investment}, not vocabulary.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{How to Rule It}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Basic description:} ``I attack the goblin.'' -- No bonus.

  \item \textbf{Tactical description:} The player uses the environment, positioning,
        or a clever tactic.  
        \textit{Example:} ``I feint high, then drive my shoulder into the goblin's
        shield, shoving it aside to open its flank.''  
        $\rightarrow$ \textbf{Improve Position by one step} (e.g., Controlled $\rightarrow$ Dominant)
        for this roll.

  \item \textbf{Character-driven description:} The player states a stake, invokes
        a bond, reveals a fear, or makes a hard choice visible. \textit{One
        heartfelt sentence counts more than a paragraph of adjectives.}  
        \textit{Example:} ``I think of my mother, who died because I wasn't brave
        enough. I won't let that happen again. I plant my feet and dare the
        goblin to come through me.''  
        $\rightarrow$ \textbf{Improve Position by one step \textit{and} bank 1 Story Beat}
        (or upgrade Effect by one step, your choice).
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{What Counts (and What Doesn't)}

\textbf{Counts:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Stating a clear stake (``If I fail, my sister doesn't eat.'')
  \item Invoking a bond mid-action (``I owe Michael my life. I'm not letting him face this alone.'')
  \item Revealing a fear or hope tied to the roll (``I'm terrified of fire, but I grab the beam -- the children are behind me.'')
  \item Making a hard choice visible (``I could run, but I don't. Running is what got me exiled.'')
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Does not count:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Piling on adjectives (``gleaming,'' ``mighty,'' ``ancient'') without stakes.
  \item Passive sensory laundry lists (``the cold wind, the wet stones, the dim light'').
  \item Repeating the same florid description for every roll.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Guidelines for Fair Play}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No penalty for plain speech.} Never punish a player who is tired,
        shy, or simply prefers quick resolution. The bonus is a gift, not a tax.
  \item \textbf{Be consistent.} Reward genuine investment, not vocabulary. A
        quiet player who delivers one honest sentence deserves the bonus as much
        as an eloquent storyteller.
  \item \textbf{Let the fiction guide the bonus.} A desperate, reckless action
        described with real stakes might upgrade Effect but worsen Position --
        the mechanics should mirror the fiction.
  \item \textbf{Invite quiet players.} Ask leading questions: ``What's at stake
        for your character right now?'' or ``What are you afraid will happen if
        you fail?'' Gentle prompts help shy players find their moment.
  \item \textbf{Group ``selling.''} If the whole table contributes to describing a
        group action (a coordinated ambush, a joint ritual), grant the bonus to
        the primary roller or give each participant a small benefit (e.g., a
        one-time +1 die).
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Example}
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Basic:} ``I sneak past the sentry.'' (No bonus, roll as normal.)

\textbf{Tactical:} ``I wait for the wind to rattle the shutters, then pad across
the wet cobblestones -- heel-toe, heel-toe -- keeping the stack of barrels between
me and the sentry's gaze.''  
The GM nods: ``Great. Because you're using the wind and the barrels, roll at
\textbf{Dominant Position}.''

\textbf{Character-driven:} ``My little brother is in that camp. I've hidden from
every fight since we left home -- but not this time. I creep forward, not quiet
anymore, because he needs to know someone came for him.''  
The GM: ``That's the heart of it. Roll at \textbf{Dominant Position}, and I'll
bank a Story Beat to make his reaction matter.''
\end{quote}

\noindent
\textit{The dice still decide success or failure. But a well-told action makes
the story richer -- and earns the player an edge when the dice fall.}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{How to Think in Fate's Edge}
\label{sec:gm-mindset}
\index{GM mindset!core principles}
\index{pressure engine}

Running \textbf{Fate's Edge} is less about adjudicating difficulty and more about managing momentum.  
Your primary job is not to block success, protect outcomes, or simulate realism---it is to \textbf{frame pressure}, \textbf{track consequences}, and \textbf{let the fiction move forward}.

If you catch yourself asking ``How hard should this be?'' you are already one step behind. Instead ask:
\begin{itemize}
\item What is at stake right now?
\item What happens if this goes wrong?
\item What pressure is already in play?
\end{itemize}
The rules exist to answer those questions---not to replace them.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  colback=blue!3!white,
  colframe=blue!60!black,
  title={GM Mindset: The Pressure Engine},
  breakable
]
\index{pressure engine}
Think of each scene as a \textbf{pressure engine}:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c c}
\textbf{Dice} & create outcomes \\
\textbf{Timers} & track fallout \\
\textbf{Story Beats} & fuel escalation \\
\textbf{Boons} & reward risk
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Your job is to keep that engine turning---not to decide where it ends. 
Let the procedures do the work. Your attention belongs on the fiction.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{The Core Loop (In Practice)}
\label{subsec:gm-core-loop}
\index{core loop}
Every meaningful action follows the same simple loop:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Player declares intent and approach.}
\item \textbf{You state Position and set any relevant Timers.}
\item \textbf{The roll resolves the moment.}
\item \textbf{You apply consequences and update the fiction.}
\end{enumerate}
Do not search for the ``right roll.'' If the action matters, the procedure applies.

\subsection{Your Defaults}
\label{subsec:gm-defaults}
\index{default settings}
When in doubt, trust the fiction. The system will follow. Fall back on these defaults:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{DV}: 3
\item \textbf{Position}: Controlled
\item \textbf{Consequence}: Fatigue, time pressure, or exposure
\item \textbf{Timers}: One short timer tied to the scene
\end{itemize}
Escalate only when the fiction demands it.

\subsection{What You Are \emph{Not} Doing}
\index{GM pitfalls}
You are \emph{not}:
\begin{itemize}
\item calling for rolls to gate progress
\item inflating difficulty to ``challenge'' the players
\item protecting NPC plans from disruption
\item hiding information behind unnecessary checks
\end{itemize}
If the players act decisively, the world must respond decisively.

\subsection{What You \emph{Are} Doing}
You \emph{are}:
\begin{itemize}
\item stating Position clearly and confidently
\item letting partials move the story forward
\item spending Story Beats to make failure interesting
\item allowing success to create new problems
\end{itemize}
The game works best when nothing stalls.

\section{Narrative Time: Rounds, Turns, Scenes, and Arcs}
\label{sec:narrative-time}
\index{narrative time}\index{round}\index{turn}\index{scene}\index{session}\index{arc}

Fate's Edge measures time by narrative weight, not real seconds. Use these shared definitions to keep the table aligned.

\begin{description}
\item[Round] Spotlight turns for all participants in a conflict (including Actions, Moves, and Reactions). A round ends when everyone present has had an opportunity to act.
\item[Turn] Spotlight for an individual player: one Action and one Move (see \ref{sec:core-resolution-cycle} in the SRD). Turns are the basic unit of player agency.
\item[Significant Time] Three or more rounds of structured conflict, or any period outside of combat where extended effort is required (e.g., a ritual, a chase, or several hours of travel). Use Significant Time to trigger long-term timers and downtime actions.
\item[Scene] A unit of narrative play covering a specific conflict, location, or situation. Equivalent to a D\&D ``encounter'' (but avoid that comparison; a scene may be social, exploratory, or a montage). A scene ends when its central question is resolved.
\item[Session] One play session (typically 3--6 hours). A session may contain one major scene or several smaller scenes, and ends with Experience Point awards and timer updates.
\item[Arc] A complete story arc (3--6 sessions) that advances character goals, resolves a major front, and moves a campaign timer. At the end of an arc, the GM may call a Crown Spread or trigger a finale.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Significant Time in Play}
When the fiction calls for Significant Time (e.g., performing a ritual, climbing a cliff, or resting for an hour), the GM may:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Advance a relevant timer by 1 segment.
  \item Allow the party to clear 1--2 Fatigue (if conditions allow).
  \item Call for a single roll to represent the entire extended effort (DV set by the scope of the task).
\end{itemize}

\section{The Core Resolution Cycle}
\label{sec:core-resolution-cycle}
\index{resolution cycle}

When a player rolls, they engage the world through risk, consequence, and discovery.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Declare Action \& Approach:} Player states intent, Attribute + Skill.
\item \textbf{Set Difficulty Value (DV):} Based on narrative stakes.
\item \textbf{Establish Position:} Dominant, Controlled, or Desperate.
\item \textbf{Roll Pool of d10s.}
\item \textbf{Count Successes (6+) and Story Beats (1s).}
\item \textbf{Check Against DV} using Outcome Matrix.
\item \textbf{Apply Outcome:} Clean Success, Success with SB, Partial, or Miss.
\item \textbf{Spend SB} for complications and twists.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Clean Success.}
The action succeeds as intended. No Story Beats generated unless specified. Fiction advances cleanly.

\paragraph{Success with SB.}
Action succeeds but generates \textbf{Story Beats}. Success stands; cost is GM spending those SB, ideally applied to the inciting action or its immediate consequences.

\paragraph{Miss.}
Action fails and generates \textbf{Story Beats}. GM uses those SB to introduce complications, advance opposition, or shift the situation against the character.

\paragraph{Partial Success (GM Adjudication).}
A Partial indicates progress but not full resolution. How that manifests is determined by the GM, based on context, scale, and narrative stakes.

Partial results may:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Improve the character's \textbf{Position} on a follow-up attempt
  \item Reduce the effective \textbf{DV} of the next roll
  \item Achieve the goal at reduced scale or limited duration
  \item Succeed fully but generate a \textbf{Story Beat} as cost or complication
  \item Reveal information or create a new opportunity instead of immediate resolution
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Guiding Principle.}
Partials represent \emph{meaningful progress}. Convert progress into advantage, resolution, or complication, depending on what best serves the fiction.

\begin{tcolorbox}[title=Position Effects, colback=white!97!gray, colframe=black!80!gray, breakable]
\index{Position!mechanics}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{lX}
\toprule
\textbf{Position} & \textbf{Mechanical Effect} \\
\midrule
Dominant & May re-roll one failure (die < 6). \\
Controlled & Default state; no re-rolls. \\
Desperate & Must re-roll one success (die 6+), keeping second result. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\begin{tcolorbox}[title=Difficulty Ladder -- Beginner Focus, colback=white!97!gray, colframe=black!80!gray, breakable]
\index{DV!ladder}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{lX}
\toprule
\textbf{DV} & \textbf{When to Use} \\
\midrule
2 & Routine: clear path, no pressure, almost guaranteed. \\
3 & Default: mild challenge, some risk -- use most often. \\
4 & Hard: serious opposition, bad angle, or strong resistance. \\
5+ & Extreme: major boss encounters or dramatic gambles. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Position \& Difficulty Value}
\label{sec:position-dv}
\index{Position}\index{DV}

Position and DV are your primary dials for setting stakes and difficulty. They work together but answer different questions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{DV (Difficulty Value)} -- How \emph{hard} is the task? (2 = routine, 3 = pressured, 4 = hard, 5+ = extreme)
\item \textbf{Position} -- How \emph{risky} is the attempt? (Dominant = safe, Controlled = balanced, Desperate = dangerous)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{DV 3 as Default}
Unless the fiction clearly indicates otherwise, start at \textbf{DV 3}. This gives a roughly 50--60\% success rate for an average pool (4--6 dice) and leaves room for partials and misses. \textbf{Do not inflate DV to ``challenge'' players} -- use Position, Timers, and SB spends instead.

\subsection{When to Worsen Position}
Worsen Position when the fiction puts the character at a disadvantage:
\begin{itemize}
\item Exposed or surrounded $\rightarrow$ Desperate
\item Rushed or distracted $\rightarrow$ Controlled or Desperate
\item Surprised or ambushed $\rightarrow$ Desperate
\end{itemize}
\emph{Never} worsen Position just to make a roll ``harder.'' Position is about \textbf{narrative danger}, not arithmetic.

\subsection{The Golden Rule}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=yellow!5,colframe=yellow!75!black,title={Golden Rule}, breakable]
\index{Golden Rule}
\textbf{When in doubt, make a ruling that keeps the story moving.}

If you cannot remember a rule, set DV = 3, Position = Controlled, and ask the player to roll. Resolve the outcome with the Outcome Matrix. You can look up the rule after the session.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Example: Using SB to Escalate a Scene}
\label{subsec:bridge-sb-example}
\index{Story Beats (SB)!example}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!5,colframe=gray!75!black,title={Spending SB for Drama (The Bridge)}, breakable]
The party is negotiating a narrow mountain bridge. A rival mercenary captain demands a bribe.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Player rolls a Partial.} The GM gains 2 SB.
  \item \textbf{GM spends 1 SB:} ``The captain leans on his sword -- you notice his hand creeping toward the hilt. \emph{Tensions rise}.'' (Tick a new timer: \textbf{Tempers Flare [4]}.)
  \item \textbf{GM spends the second SB:} ``A loose stone falls from the bridge and splashes into the river far below. The captain's archers nock arrows. \textbf{Position worsens to Desperate}.''
\end{itemize}
The player still succeeded in negotiating a lower toll, but the GM used SB to introduce escalating pressure and a visible timer. The scene now crackles with danger.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Outcome Matrix}

\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{lX}

\textbf{Roll Result} & \textbf{What Happens} \\
\midrule

\subsection{Armor Conversion \& Fatigue Clearing}
\label{subsec:armor-conversion}
\index{armor!conversion}\index{fatigue!clearing}

\textbf{Your job as GM is simple:} When a player character takes a hit, you state the incoming Harm value. \textbf{The player applies their own armor conversion} using the Armor Conversion Table on their sheet. This keeps your hands free for narration and pacing.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{You say:} ``You're hit. Harm 3.''
  \item \textbf{The player:} Consults their armor type on the table, applies the conversion, and describes the result.
  \item \textbf{You:} Narrate the fiction based on what they mark.
\end{enumerate}

The table itself is player-facing (see Appendix A.5). Your only responsibility is to announce the Harm value and trust the player to handle the math.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=yellow!5!white, colframe=yellow!75!black, title={GM Tip: The Roller-Coaster Effect}, breakable]
\index{fatigue!roller-coaster}
\textbf{This creates dramatic narrative swings:} 
When Harm is applied and armor conversion reduces it, the shock of injury may clear existing Fatigue. This represents a ``second wind'' -- a character near collapse who takes a wound and suddenly finds clarity through adrenaline.

\emph{Let the player describe what this looks like.} A fighter who was gasping for breath (Fatigue 3) takes a glancing blow (Harm 1, converted to Fatigue 1, triggering overflow) and suddenly stands tall, bleeding but focused. The mechanics and the fiction reinforce each other.

\textbf{Your job:} Ask the player: ``What does that look like?'' Let them own the moment.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Learning Through Examples}
\label{sec:learning-examples}
\index{examples!core loop}

This section walks the same situation through multiple passes, adding one rule at a time. Use it as a mental template when you improvise.

\subsection*{Example 1: Core Loop Only}
\label{subsec:ex-core-only}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!3,colframe=gray!60,title=\textbf{Example 1 -- No SB, No Boons}, breakable]
Lyra tries to sneak past a guardian in a narrow hallway. (Agility + Stealth = 5d10).\\
GM: normal risk, DV 3, Controlled Position.\\
Roll: 3, 5, 6, 7, 9 $\rightarrow$ 3 successes (6,7,9).\\[0.3em]
DV 3 with 3 successes = \textbf{Success}.\\[0.3em]
GM: ``You slip past the guardian quietly. You're in position behind it if you want to act.''
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Example 2: Add Story Beats Only}
\label{subsec:ex-sb-only}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!3,colframe=gray!60,title=\textbf{Example 2 -- Adding Story Beats}, breakable]
Same situation. Lyra rolls 5d10: 1, 4, 6, 6, 8.\\
Successes: 3 (6,6,8). One 1 $\rightarrow$ 1 SB for GM.\\[0.3em]
DV 3 with 3 successes = \textbf{Success} with 1 SB.\\[0.3em]
GM spend (1 SB = Minor): ``You get past the guardian, but your cloak snags and tears. The sound echoes -- the next room has a -1 Position penalty for stealth checks.''
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Example 3: Add Boons Only}
\label{subsec:ex-boons-only}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!3,colframe=gray!60,title=\textbf{Example 3 -- Adding Boons}, breakable]
Later, Lyra scales a loose stone wall (Athletics + Agility = 4d10). DV 3.\\
Roll: 2,3,4,5 $\rightarrow$ 0 successes = \textbf{Miss}.\\[0.3em]
GM awards \textbf{2 Boons}. ``You slip and land hard, taking 1 Harm. Mark 2 Boons -- you can use them later to turn the tide.''\\
Next attempt, Lyra spends 1 Boon to re-roll a die, turning failure into success.
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection*{Example 4: Putting It Together}
\label{subsec:ex-full}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=gray!3,colframe=gray!60,title=\textbf{Example 4 -- SB + Boons + Position}, breakable]
The party flees as the tower collapses. A crumbling staircase blocks the exit.\\
GM: ``Desperate. DV 4 to get everyone across safely.''\\
Lyra leads, rolling 6d10 (Agility + Athletics + help): 1,2,6,7,7,9.\\
4 successes vs DV 4 = Success. 1 rolled 1 = 1 SB to GM. Desperate Position.\\
GM: ``You all make it across, but the last step collapses behind you. I spend 1 SB: your exit path is gone. You'll need a new way out.''\\
\textbf{Lesson:} Even on a success, SB let you bend the fiction toward drama without erasing the player's win.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Risk as the Engine of Drama}
\label{sec:gm-risk-drama}
\index{risk as drama}\index{Story Beats (SB)}

\textbf{Fate's Edge} is built on \textbf{meaningful risk}. Safety is boring. When characters have something to lose---reputation, allies, ideals, life---their actions become heroic or tragically memorable.

Your primary tool for managing risk is the \textbf{Story Beat (SB)} economy. When the dice show a \textbf{1}, the world reacts. The GM gains SB to introduce complications, escalate threats, or reveal hidden dangers. \textbf{Important:} SB are generated on \emph{any} roll that produces a 1, whether the overall roll succeeds or fails. They are not punishment; they are fuel for an unpredictable, responsive narrative.

Example: A successful sword swing defeats an opponent. A Story Beat spent could mean the blade is notched, or that the defeat draws a more powerful foe. The drama continues.

\subsection{What Are Story Beats, Really?}
\label{sub:what-are-story-beats}
\index{Story Beats (SB)!philosophy}

New GMs often ask: \textit{``What are Story Beats really? Are they just complications? Are they a resource I should hoard for the big moment?''}

The answer is simpler and more profound than you might expect. \textbf{Story Beats are the game's permission to make things interesting.}

Every time a player rolls a 1, they are inviting the world to push back. The dice have spoken: something has gone slightly wrong, or slightly awry, or slightly \emph{unexpected}. The SB is your license to honor that invitation. It is not a punishment. It is not a penalty. It is the universe saying, \textit{``Yes, and...''}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=yellow!5!white,colframe=yellow!75!black,title=The GM's Secret Weapon,breakable]
\index{Story Beats (SB)!as pacing tool}
\textbf{Story Beats are your primary pacing tool.}

A scene without SB is a scene where the players are in control. A scene with SB is a scene where the world pushes back. The two should alternate. If you spend SB early and often, you create pressure. If you hoard them, the scene feels flat.

\noindent\textit{``Do not save your Story Beats for the climax. The climax is made of the Story Beats you spent along the way.''}
— The Gray Wanderer
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{What SB Are Not}

SB are not:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Punishments.} They are not the GM's way of saying ``you failed.'' They are the world's way of saying ``something interesting happened.''
  \item \textbf{A finite resource.} You are not limited by SB. If the fiction demands a complication, introduce it—even with 0 SB. SB simply tell you: \textit{Players rolled 1s here; they want the world to push back.}
  \item \textbf{One-time effects.} A single SB can create a complication that ripples through the entire session. A guard who is alerted now will be a problem later.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{What SB Are}

SB are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Narrative fuel.} They give you permission to escalate, to twist, to surprise.
  \item \textbf{Player-generated pressure.} The players rolled 1s. They are telling you, \textit{``Make this interesting.''}
  \item \textbf{Tension creators.} Each SB spent is a moment where the players' plans go slightly wrong—or become more complicated.
  \item \textbf{Story seeds.} A minor complication now can grow into a major plot thread later.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{A Practical Example}

Imagine the party is sneaking past a guard. The rogue rolls Stealth and gets 3 successes against DV 3—a Clean Success. But one of the dice shows a 1. The GM gains 1 SB.

\textit{What does the SB mean?}

It does not mean the rogue fails. It means the world notices. The guard does not see the rogue, but a loose stone skitters across the floor. The guard looks up, peers into the darkness, and mutters, \textit{``Probably just a rat...''} before returning to his post. The guard is now \textit{slightly suspicious}. Later, when the party is deeper in the compound, that guard will be more alert. A later failure might trigger a full alarm.

\noindent\textbf{That is a Story Beat.} A small consequence that grows over time. A seed planted for future drama.

\subsection{The SB Spend Menu}
\label{sub:sb-spend-menu}
\index{Story Beats (SB)!spending}

Use this menu to guide your spending. The listed costs are guidelines, not rules. A 1 SB complication in one scene might be a 2 SB complication in another.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{|p{2cm}|X|}
\hline
\textbf{SB Cost} & \textbf{Effect} \\
\hline
1 SB & \textbf{Minor pressure:} Noise, distraction, tick a 4–6 segment timer +1, worsen Position by one step. \\
\hline
2 SB & \textbf{Moderate setback:} Alarm raised, lose cover/advantage, lesser foe appears, split the party briefly. \\
\hline
3+ SB & \textbf{Serious trouble:} Reinforcements arrive, key gear breaks, major twist shifts the scene, authority arrives, environment changes dramatically. \\
\hline
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

\noindent\textbf{Pro tip:} One strong beat (e.g., 3 SB for reinforcements) creates more drama than three weak beats (1+1+1 SB for noise). Be generous—unused SB is wasted momentum. One strong beat creates a memory. Three weak beats create a blur. The players will remember the reinforcements. They will not remember the three times a guard looked up.

\textit{Spend your SB. Spend them early. Spend them often. The big moment is made of the small moments you already spent. However, remember \textbf[have} big moments.}

\subsection{The SB Cycle}
\label{sub:sb-cycle}
\index{Story Beats (SB)!cycle}

The SB economy creates a virtuous cycle:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Players roll.} They succeed or fail, but the dice show 1s.
  \item \textbf{GM gains SB.} The world is given permission to push back.
  \item \textbf{GM spends SB.} A complication is introduced.
  \item \textbf{Players react.} They adapt, overcome, or fail again.
  \item \textbf{The cycle repeats.} Each roll generates new SB, new complications, new drama.
\end{enumerate}

The cycle never stops because the dice never stop rolling. A Miss generates 2 Boons and the GM spends SB. A Partial generates 1 Boon. Even a Clean Success with a 1 generates SB. The story always moves forward.

\noindent\textit{``The SB economy is the engine of drama. It turns every roll into a question: What happens next?''}
— The Gray Wanderer

\section{Your Toolkit}
\label{sec:gm-toolkit}
\index{GM tools!overview}

To help guide the story, \textbf{Fate's Edge} provides interconnected tools:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dice Pool:} Attribute + Skill d10s. 6+ = Success. 1 = Story Beat (SB) for you. Compare successes to Difficulty Value (DV) set by stakes.
    \item \textbf{Position \& Effect:} Position (Dominant/Controlled/Desperate) defines stakes of failure. Effect (Limited/Standard/Great) describes what a clean success achieves.
    \item \textbf{Timers:} Visual trackers for ongoing challenges (e.g., 4-segment lock, 8-segment faction rise).
    \item \textbf{Deck of Consequences (optional):} A standard deck to generate thematic complications when SB are spent. Suits suggest nature of trouble.
\end{itemize}

These tools are designed to be learned quickly and used intuitively, getting out of the way so you and your players focus on the story.

\subsection{Can I Roll Fewer Dice? No!}
\label{sub:why-no-fewer-dice}
\index{GM philosophy!dice pools}

A question that comes up often, especially from new GMs, is:

\textit{``The player is rolling 8 dice. That's a lot. Can I ask them to roll fewer dice to make it more challenging?''}

\textbf{No. Absolutely not. Here is why.}

The dice pool is the player's primary expression of their character's competence. A character with Body 4 and Melee 4 has invested significant XP—they have earned those 8 dice. Asking them to roll fewer dice is the same as saying, \textit{``I don't care about your character's skills; I want the outcome to be random.''}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=red!5!white,colframe=red!75!black,title=The Golden Rule of Dice Pools,breakable]
\index{GM philosophy!golden rule}
\textbf{Never reduce a player's dice pool.}

If you want to make a challenge more difficult, use:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Position:} Make it Desperate. The player must re-roll successes, which is a meaningful tactical choice.
  \item \textbf{DV:} Raise the Difficulty Value. DV 4 is hard; DV 5 is a gamble.
  \item \textbf{Timers:} Add a visible timer. The player knows they are racing the clock.
  \item \textbf{Story Beats:} Spend SB to introduce complications. The success is earned, but the world pushes back.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{These are the tools Fate's Edge gives you. Use them.}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsubsection{Why This Matters}

The game is balanced around the dice pool. If you reduce it, you break the math:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Boons become scarce.} Fewer successes mean fewer Partials and Misses, which means fewer Boons. The player's resource economy collapses.
  \item \textbf{SB become scarce.} Fewer dice mean fewer 1s, which means fewer SB for you. The GM's narrative fuel dries up.
  \item \textbf{The roller-coaster effect breaks.} Fatigue and Harm no longer interact in the intended way.
  \item \textbf{The player feels cheated.} They invested in those dice. You are telling them their investment was a mistake.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{What You Can Do Instead}

If a player is rolling many dice and you want to maintain tension, consider:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Use Position.} Make the action Desperate. The player succeeds often, but they also succeed dangerously.
  \item \textbf{Raise DV to 4 or 5.} The player has a high pool, so make the target harder. This is the purpose of DV.
  \item \textbf{Add a timer.} The player may succeed, but success takes time. The tower is collapsing.
  \item \textbf{Spend SB.} The player succeeds—but the success creates a new problem.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{A Practical Example}

The party's ranger has Wits 4 and Survival 4. She wants to track a fleeing assassin. She is rolling \textbf{8 dice}.

\noindent\textbf{Wrong:}
GM: ``Tracking is hard. Roll 4 dice instead of 8.''

\noindent\textbf{Right:}
GM: ``Tracking is hard, and the assassin has a head start. This is a DV 4 task, and the trail is growing cold—Position Desperate. You're pushing hard.''

Ranger: ``I spend 1 Boon to improve Position to Controlled.''

GM: ``Roll 8 dice. Successes and 1s as normal.''

\noindent\textbf{Outcome:} The player rolls 8 dice (their full pool), succeeds (DV 4), and generates a 1. The GM spends the SB to introduce a complication: the assassin's trail leads to a hidden door. The door is locked. The ranger succeeded—but the story continues.

\noindent\textit{``The dice are not a resource to be rationed. They are a declaration of competence. Let the players roll their full pool. Then use Position, DV, and SB to shape the outcome. That is the game.''}
— The Gray Wanderer
