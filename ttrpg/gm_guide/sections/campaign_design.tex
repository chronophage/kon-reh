In \textbf{Fate's Edge}, campaigns are not a sequence of disconnected adventures‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíthey are \textbf{pressure systems}.  
Every choice the players make pushes on something: a faction, a threat, a relationship, a belief. Over time, those pressures accumulate, shift, and eventually \emph{break}.

Your job as GM is not to hide this pressure‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíit is to \textbf{shape it, reveal it, and let the players decide where it lands}.

This chapter presents the core tools for doing that work: \textbf{Timers}, \textbf{Fronts}, \textbf{the Crown Spread}, and long‚ąíterm campaign structures. Used well, they create momentum without railroading and stakes without arbitrary escalation.

\section{Foundational Principles for Campaign Design}

Before diving into tools, internalize these rules of thumb. They will save you more prep time than any other advice.

\subsection{Set Situations, Not Plots}
‚ąądex{campaigns!situations vs plots}

A \textbf{situation} is a stable condition with inherent conflict. A \textbf{plot} is a sequence of events you expect the players to follow.

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Plot:} ``The players must retrieve the Sunstone from the Ruined Tower, then defeat the necromancer, then close the rift.''
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Situation:} ``The necromancer has claimed the Ruined Tower. The Sunstone inside could seal the rift. The village is starving because crops wither near the rift.''
\end{itemize}

Situations give players \textbf{agency}. They can negotiate with the necromancer, steal the Sunstone, collapse the tower, divert a river to flood the rift‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíany solution that fits the fiction.

\subsection{Design Timers, Not Every Element}
‚ąądex{timers!design principle}

You do not need to know exactly how a faction will act in session 12. Instead, give them a \textbf{timer} with a trigger condition.

\begin{quote}
\emph{``The Cult of the Eternal Night [6] advances when the players fail to protect a solar shrine or when the GM spends 2+ SB on occult activity.''}
\end{quote}

When the timer fills, the cult achieves its goal‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíand you improvise the specifics based on the current fiction.

\subsection{Read Your Player Sheets}
‚ąądex{player sheets!as campaign fuel}

Your players have already given you the best campaign hooks. Look for:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Bonds:} ``I owe my life to the Vault‚ąíQueen's wardens.'' $‚Üí$ The Vault‚ąíQueen calls in the debt.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Complications:} ``A sealed hold refuses you entry.'' $‚Üí$ That hold is now a key location.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Patrons:} Any Patron choice is a promise to involve that Patron's agenda.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Skills at 3+}: If a player invested in Lore 3, they want to discover ancient secrets.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Use Obligation as a Story Engine}
‚ąądex{Obligation!as story fuel}

Obligation (to Patrons, terrestrial patrons, or even the party's own Trust) is not a punishment‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíit is a \textbf{guarantee of future adventure}. When a player marks Obligation, note it. When their capacity is near full, introduce a demand.

\begin{quote}
\emph{``Your Patron, the Traveler, appears in a dream. The road to Highpass is washed out, and a pilgrim family is trapped. Guide them safely, and clear 2 Obligation. Refuse, and mark Fatigue 2‚ąí‚ąí‚ąíthe road will not open for you again until you do.''}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Embrace the Cascade}
‚ąądex{consequences!cascade}

A single failure should never end the campaign. Instead, let it \textbf{cascade} into new problems.

\begin{quote}
\textit{The party fails to stop the ritual. The demon is summoned. Now the campaign is not ``stop the ritual'' but ``contain the demon before it corrupts the capital.''}
\end{quote}

Each failure should change the situation, not end it.

\subsection{Let Timers Be Visible}
‚ąądex{timers!visibility}

Timers are not hidden GM tools. Show them to the players. Write \emph{Cult Influence [6]} on an index card and place it on the table. When you tick it, announce: ``The cult's ritual advances. You feel the ground tremble.''

Visible timers create \textbf{tension} and \textbf{informed choices}. Players can prioritize which timer to slow.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=blue!5!white, colframe=blue!75!black, title=Physical Trackers for Timers]

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Dry‚ąíerase boards or laminated sheets} with pre‚ąídrawn timer circles (4/6/8/10 segments). Mark ticks with a wet‚ąíerase marker.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Index cards with pie‚ąíchart wedges} drawn in pen; use a paperclip to mark current segment.

\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Ask Questions, Then Use the Answers}
‚ąądex{player input}

During Session Zero and between sessions, ask:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem ``What does your character fear will happen if you fail this mission?''
  ‚ĄĻtem ``Who in your past might try to stop you?''
  ‚ĄĻtem ``What secret are you hiding from the rest of the party?''
\end{itemize}
Use these answers to seed complications. The player who fears losing their family? Their family is now in danger.

\subsection{Design for Your Tier}
‚ąądex{tiers!campaign design}

\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Tier I‚ąí‚ąíII (0‚ąí‚ąí90 XP):} Local scope, 3‚ąí‚ąí5 session arcs. Threats affect a village, a guild, a noble house.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Tier III (91‚ąí‚ąí150 XP):} Regional scope, 6‚ąí‚ąí10 session arcs. Threats affect a city, a province, a trade route.
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Tiers IV‚ąí‚ąíV (151+ XP):} Continental scope, 15+ session arcs. Threats affect nations, the natural order, reality itself.
\end{itemize}
Do not give a Tier I party a world‚ąíending prophecy. Give them a rival who stole their map.

\section{Core GM Mechanics}

Understanding the core resolution loop is essential for effective campaign design. Every meaningful action follows this cycle:

\begin{enumerate}
  ‚ĄĻtem Declare intent and approach (Attribute+Skill)
  ‚ĄĻtem GM sets DV (2‚ąí‚ąí5+) and Position (Dominant/Controlled/Desperate)
  ‚ĄĻtem Roll dice pool (Attribute+Skill d10s)
  ‚ĄĻtem Count successes (6‚ąí‚ąí9=1 success; 10=2 successes)
  ‚ĄĻtem Apply outcome using the Outcome Matrix
  ‚ĄĻtem GM spends Story Beats (SB) to introduce complications
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Difficulty Values (DV)}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{DV 2:} Routine‚ąí‚ąíclear path, no pressure
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{DV 3:} Pressured‚ąí‚ąímild opposition (default)
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{DV 4:} Hard‚ąí‚ąíserious resistance
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{DV 5+:} Extreme‚ąí‚ąídramatic gamble
\end{itemize}
Default DV is 3. Don't inflate DV to ``challenge'' players‚ąí‚ąíuse Position instead.

\subsection{Position}
Position tells you how risky the action is and changes how dice behave:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Dominant:} You press advantage‚ąí‚ąíre‚ąíroll one failure
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Controlled:} Balanced normal risk‚ąí‚ąíno re‚ąírolls
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Desperate:} You act under duress‚ąí‚ąíre‚ąíroll one success
\end{itemize}
Spending 1 Boon improves Position by one step before rolling. The GM may spend SB to worsen Position.

\subsection{Outcome Matrix}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Successes $‚Č•$ DV, SB=0:} Clean Success‚ąí‚ąíIntent achieved, no complication
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Successes $‚Č•$ DV, SB>$>$0:} Success with SB‚ąí‚ąíIntent achieved; GM spends SB
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{0 $<$ Successes $<$ DV:} Partial‚ąí‚ąíProgress; gain 1 Boon
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Successes = 0:} Miss‚ąí‚ąíNo progress; gain 2 Boons; GM spends SB
\end{itemize}
A Partial is not failure‚ąí‚ąíit moves the story forward. A Miss is never ``nothing happens.'' The GM must introduce a complication.

\subsection{Resources: Boons and Story Beats}
‚ęēsection{Boons (Player Resource)}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Earned on Partial (1 Boon) or Miss (2 Boons)
  ‚ĄĻtem Maximum 5 Boons held; reduce to 2 at scene end (excess lost)
  ‚ĄĻtem Spent to: re‚ąíroll a die, activate an Asset, or improve Position by one step
  ‚ĄĻtem During downtime: convert 2 Boons $‚Üí$ 1 XP (max 2 XP/session)
\end{itemize}

‚ęēsection{Story Beats (GM Resource)}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Earned when players roll a 1 on any die
  ‚ĄĻtem Spent to introduce complications:
  \begin{itemize}
    ‚ĄĻtem 1 SB: Minor‚ąí‚ąínoise, distraction, tick a 4‚ąí‚ąí6 segment timer +1
    ‚ĄĻtem 2 SB: Moderate‚ąí‚ąíalarm raised, lose advantage, worsen Position
    ‚ĄĻtem 3+ SB: Major‚ąí‚ąíreinforcements, scene shifts, break an Asset
  \end{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Be aggressive with SB‚ąí‚ąíunused pressure is wasted momentum
\end{itemize}

\section{Complications and Hazards}
‚ąądex{complications}‚ąądex{hazards}

Complications are the narrative, mechanical, and environmental consequences that arise from failure, pressure, or the GM spending Story Beats. Hazards are ongoing complications that require action to avoid or mitigate.

\subsection{Using Story Beats for Complications}
When spending SB, follow this menu:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{1 SB:} Minor pressure‚ąí‚ąínoise, distraction, tick a relevant timer +1, worsen Position by one step
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{2 SB:} Moderate setback‚ąí‚ąíalarm raised, lose cover/advantage, lesser foe appears, split the party briefly
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{3 SB:} Serious trouble‚ąí‚ąíreinforcements arrive, key gear breaks (temporarily), major twist shifts the scene
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{4+ SB:} Scene‚ąíshaping turn‚ąí‚ąía trap springs, authority arrives, the environment changes dramatically, a Patron manifests an omen
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hazard Timers}
Represent active hazards (fire, flooding, toxic gas) with Threat Timers:
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem Tick the timer on missed rolls, GM SB spends, or at scene intervals
  ‚ĄĻtem When the timer fills, the hazard triggers (e.g., collapse, wave of fire)
  ‚ĄĻtem Characters may attempt to slow or disable the hazard (e.g., Tinker to seal a leak, Magic to freeze spreading flames)
  ‚ĄĻtem Treat as a Progress Timer when players work against it
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Social Hazards}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Rumors:} A timer that advances when the party fails social rolls; when full, a faction turns hostile
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Scrutiny:} Worsens Position for stealth or deception
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Reputation Damage:} Future social rolls start at worse Position
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Magical Hazards}
\begin{itemize}
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Backlash Zone:} An area where free casting automatically generates +1 SB per spell
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Ward/Seal:} Requires a roll to pass; failure triggers a consequence (harm, alarm, etc.)
  ‚ĄĻtem \textbf{Leaking Veil:} Spirits or fey occasionally manifest spontaneously (GM spends SB to introduce)
\end{itemize}

\section{Running Travel as a Medieval Trek}
Ňāabel{sec:gm‚ąítravel}
‚ąądex{travel!GM techniques}

Travel in Fate's Edge is not a line on a map. It is a \textbf{medieval journey} ‚ąí‚ąí uncertain, weather‚ąíbeaten, dependent on local guides, hospitality, and the kindness (or cruelty) of strangers. For full procedures (weather tables, road condition dice, regional decks), see the \textit{Travel Guide} and \textit{Player's Guide} Chapter 10. This section gives you the GM principles.

\subsection{Lead with Weather, Not Distance}
Open each leg with a weather description, then set Position based on that weather (e.g., Desperate for navigation in fog, Controlled for stealth in rain). The weather \emph{is} the terrain.

\subsection{Maps Are Lies; Guides Are Truth}
Require a \textbf{Guide} role for each leg (PC or hired NPC). The Guide's roll (Wits + Survival or Lore) determines if they find the safe route, the short route, or the one that leads to a cultist ambush.

\subsection{Hospitality as a Resource}
When the party seeks shelter, draw a \textbf{Heart} from the regional deck ‚ąí‚ąí that's the host. Their attitude affects what they offer. Spending a \textbf{String} (e.g., ``I helped this village last year'') can improve the outcome.
