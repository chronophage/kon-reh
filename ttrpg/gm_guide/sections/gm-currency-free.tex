\chapter{Running a Currency-Free Game}
\label{ch:gm-currency-free}
\index{currency!GM guidance}\index{XP!as currency}

\section{Why Fate's Edge Has No ``Money''}
In many role-playing games gold, silver, or credits are the primary levers for power: better gear, bribes, training time, and so on. Fate's Edge flips that model. All character growth, acquisition of assets, and even the purchase of services are measured in \textbf{experience points (XP)}. The only exception is a short-term ``scrip'' that can be used for flavor in mercantile-heavy campaigns, and even that is modeled as a timer rather than a true currency pool.

\subsection{Design Goals}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Keep the focus on narrative risk and reward, not on bookkeeping.
  \item Make every XP expenditure a meaningful story decision.
  \item Allow the GM to inject scarcity or abundance through timers and narrative pressure, not through price lists.
\end{itemize}

\section{XP as the Universal Resource}
Every time a character gains XP they are earning a narrative token that can be spent in three broad ways:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Character Advancement} -- increase attributes, learn new rites, or buy talents (see the Player's Guide).
  \item \textbf{Asset Acquisition} -- purchase Followers, Assets, or other long-term benefits (cost = XP, see Chapter \ref{ch:gm-assets-followers}).
  \item \textbf{Immediate Effect} -- spend XP in place of coin for a single, dramatic purchase (e.g., bribing an official, buying a rare component, hiring a guide). The GM decides the XP cost based on the fictional weight of the item or service.
\end{enumerate}

Because XP is earned through play (Boons, Milestones, Acts of Service, etc.), spending it always feels like a trade-off: ``Do I improve my sword arm now, or do I secure a safehouse for the next arc?''

\subsection{GM Guidance on XP Pricing}
When a player asks, ``Can I buy X with XP?'', use the following rubric:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Trivial (flavor, no mechanical benefit)} -- 0 XP (just narrate it).
  \item \textbf{Minor (small advantage, one-time use)} -- 1--2 XP (e.g., a night's lodging, a simple tool, a rumor).
  \item \textbf{Moderate (noticeable edge, reusable)} -- 3--5 XP (e.g., a decent weapon, a safehouse for a week, a minor title).
  \item \textbf{Major (significant power, long-term impact)} -- 6--10 XP (e.g., a fortress lease, a noble title, a spy ring).
  \item \textbf{Epic (campaign-shaping)} -- 11+ XP (e.g., a regional trade monopoly, a city-wide charter, a legendary artifact).
\end{itemize}

Adjust these numbers to match the power level of your campaign; the key is that the cost should feel like a meaningful sacrifice, not a routine expense.

\section{Temporary ``Scrip'' for Mercantile Campaigns}
Some groups enjoy the feel of buying and selling goods in a bustling market. Rather than tracking actual coin, introduce a \textbf{Scrip Timer} that represents the party's liquidity in the local economy.

\subsection{How the Scrip Timer Works}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The timer has a number of segments equal to the typical daily wage for a common laborer in the setting (e.g., 4 segments = 4 days of work).
  \item When the party earns money (selling loot, completing a job, finding a treasure), the GM advances the timer by the appropriate number of segments.
  \item When the party spends money on goods, services, or bribes, the GM retreats the timer by the same amount.
  \item If the timer reaches zero, the party is \textit{broke} and must find work, barter, or rely on patronage before they can make further purchases.
  \item If the timer fills completely, the party is \textit{flush} and can afford minor luxuries without XP cost (the GM may grant a small narrative benefit, such as a favorable rumor or a temporary asset).
\end{itemize}

Because the timer is a shared resource, it creates natural tension: a successful heist might fill the Scrip Timer, but a failed negotiation could drain it just as the party needs to buy supplies for the next leg of the journey.

\subsection{Example: Scrip Timer in a Port City}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The port's daily wage is 2 silver; we set the Scrip Timer to 4 segments (8 silver total).
  \item The players sell a smuggled crate for 3 segments (6 silver). The timer moves from 0/4 to 3/4.
  \item They need to hire a guide and buy provisions costing 2 segments. The timer retreats to 1/4.
  \item Later, a storm destroys their cargo; they lose 1 segment (representing lost investment) and are now at 0/4 -- broke. They must take on an odd job or call in a favor before they can purchase anything else.
\end{itemize}

Note that the Scrip Timer never interacts with XP directly; it is purely a narrative tool for market flavor. If the group prefers to avoid even this abstraction, simply treat all purchases as XP expenditures and skip the timer.

\section{Differences from Traditional Currency Systems}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No Price Lists} -- Instead of looking up a cost in a table, the GM judges the fictional weight of the item and assigns an XP value.
  \item \textbf{XP is Earned Through Play} -- Characters cannot ``farm'' gold by grinding encounters; XP comes from story milestones, Boons, Acts of Service, and meaningful failures.
  \item \textbf{Scarcity is Narrative, Not Mechanical} -- A shortage of money is felt through a low Scrip Timer or a high Obligation timer, not through a character's empty purse.
  \item \textbf{Encourages Creative Solutions} -- Players are more likely to barter, offer favors, or use assets when they lack XP, leading to richer role-playing.
\end{itemize}

\section{Caveats and Things to Consider}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Avoid XP Inflation} -- If you award XP too freely, players will be able to buy anything they want, removing tension. Keep XP gains tied to meaningful story beats.
  \item \textbf{Watch for ``XP as Coin'' Abuse} -- Some players may try to spend XP on every minor convenience. Remind them that the \textbf{XP-as-currency} rule is for \textit{major} expenses only; routine costs (food, lodging, cheap gear) should be handled narratively.
  \item \textbf{Balance Scrip Timer Size} -- A timer that is too large makes money feel meaningless; too small makes the party constantly broke. Adjust the segment count to match the average wealth level of the setting.
  \item \textbf{Communicate the Trade-Off} -- When a player spends XP on a purchase, explicitly state what they are giving up (e.g., ``Spending 4 XP means you won't be able to raise your Weapon skill this arc''). This reinforces the decision-making nature of the system.
  \item \textbf{Use Timers for Other Resources} -- Supply, Fatigue, Obligation, Mandate, Crisis, etc., all follow the same timer-based philosophy. Treat money the same way if you want a unified feel.
\end{itemize}

\section{Running a Currency-Free Game -- Practical Tips}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=blue!5!white, colframe=blue!75!black, title={GM Tips for a Currency-Free Table},breakable]
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Start with a Clear XP Economy} -- Explain at Session Zero that XP is the only measurable wealth and that spending it is a story decision.
  \item \textbf{Use the Scrip Timer Sparingly} -- Only introduce it when the market is a focal point of the story; otherwise, hand-wave purchases as ``you have enough from recent work.''
  \item \textbf{Reward Creative Barter} -- If a player offers a service, a favor, or an asset in lieu of XP, treat it as a valid transaction and adjust timers or obligations accordingly.
  \item \textbf{Keep Track of Major XP Spending} -- A simple note on the GM screen (e.g., ``Spent 6 XP on a safehouse -- reduces future Mandate gains by 1'') helps you see the long-term impact of purchases.
  \item \textbf{Let the World React} -- When the party spends a lot of XP on gear, have NPCs notice their newfound wealth (or lack thereof) and adjust their attitudes, leading to new plot hooks.
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Example Session: The Merchant's Dilemma}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The party completes a smuggling run and earns 8 XP. They decide to spend 5 XP to purchase a modest warehouse (Asset tier: Minor). Their XP pool drops from 8 to 3.
  \item The warehouse gives them a +1 bonus to any \textit{Trade} or \textit{Smuggle} roll made from that location.
  \item Later, they need to bribe a customs officer. The GM declares the bribe costs 2 XP (moderate expense). The party spends it, leaving them with 1 XP.
  \item With only 1 XP left, they cannot afford a rare component for a rite. Instead, they approach a local alchemist and offer to guard his shop for a night (a favor). The GM accepts, and the alchemist provides the component at no XP cost, but adds a small Obligation to the alchemist's faction.
  \item At the end of the session, the GM awards 2 XP for a successful Milestone (the smuggling run) and 1 XP for an Act of Service (guarding the shop). The party ends with 4 XP, ready for their next investment.
\end{enumerate}

This flow shows how XP drives every meaningful purchase, how the Scrip Timer can be used for market flavor without breaking the XP-centric core, and how narrative alternatives (favors, obligations) keep the game fluid and engaging.

\section{Final Thought}
Treating XP as the universal currency shifts the focus from ``how much gold do I have?'' to ``what story do I want to buy with my hard-earned experience?'' As GM, your job is to make those choices feel weighty, to keep the timers ticking, and to let the world react to the players' decisions---whether they are spending XP on a mighty fortress, a humble meal, or a favor that will echo later in the campaign. Embrace the freedom, watch the tension rise, and let the players' ambitions shape the world, one XP at a time.
