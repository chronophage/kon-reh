\chapter{Enhanced GM Play}
\index{enhanced GM play}

You have mastered the core rules. Now it is time to add **tactics and tricks** that make sessions tighter, more dynamic, and more fun for you as the GM. This chapter offers low‑overhead techniques to build tension, handle player indecision, adjust difficulty on the fly, and give you a few light “game” elements to play with.

\section{Your Core Resource: Story Beats}
\index{Story Beats!GM tactics}

Story Beats (SB) are already your primary resource. Use them more creatively:

\subsection*{Advanced SB Spends}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{1 SB:} Ask a leading question – “You notice something odd about the guard’s uniform. What is it?”
  \item \textbf{2 SB:} Introduce a minor environmental hazard – a loose floorboard, a dripping pipe, a sudden draft.
  \item \textbf{3 SB:} Force a player to make a quick decision – “The door is closing. Stay and fight, or dive through?”
  \item \textbf{4+ SB:} Reveal a hidden connection – “The bandit’s dagger has your family crest.”
\end{itemize}
These turns SB from mere setbacks into narrative tools that engage players directly.

\section{Optional: Threat Pool (A Light GM Resource)}
\index{Threat Pool}

If you want a small, fun resource to manage, use the **Threat Pool**. It gives you a few extra points per scene to “play” with, without adding complexity.

\subsection*{How It Works}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Start each scene with \textbf{3 Threat points}.
  \item Threat points do not carry over between scenes.
  \item You may spend Threat points on the following:
\end{itemize}

\begin{fatebox}[Threat Pool Spend Options]
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lX}
\toprule
\textbf{Cost} & \textbf{Effect} \\
\midrule
1 Threat & An enemy gains +1 die on its next action. \\
1 Threat & Introduce a very minor environmental detail (creaking floor, flickering light). \\
2 Threat & An enemy acts immediately (interrupt initiative order once). \\
2 Threat & Add a minor clock (e.g., \textit{Fire Spreading [4]}) to the scene. \\
3 Threat & Introduce a new, weaker enemy (Cap 1–2). \\
3 Threat & Force a player to re‑roll a single die (their choice). \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{fatebox}

Threat points are not a new economy – they are a simple pacing tool. Use them or ignore them; the game works fine either way.

\section{Pacing and Tension Tactics}

\subsection*{The Three‑Beat Scene Structure}
For any important scene, aim for three emotional beats:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Setup:} Establish stakes and environment.
  \item \textbf{Clash:} Roll dice, spend SB, introduce complications.
  \item \textbf{Aftermath:} Resolve or escalate – but never leave a scene hanging.
\end{enumerate}
If a scene drags, ask: “What is the main question this scene answers?” Answer it, then move on.

\subsection*{Using Clocks as Countdowns}
Instead of saying “you have three rounds”, show a clock: \textit{Reinforcements Arrive [4]}. Players can see it tick. This creates genuine urgency without fudging.

\subsection*{Ending a Scene Before It Drags}
When the main conflict is resolved, cut. Do not play out cleanup unless there is remaining tension. “You defeat the bandits – the rest flee. What do you do next?” Move on.

\section{Handling Player Indecision}

\subsection*{Offer Two Clear Choices}
Never give more than two obvious paths. “Do you sneak past the guard or try to bribe him?” A third option (“or wait”) is usually a disguised stall. If they cannot choose, the world decides for them – the guard notices them.

\subsection*{Ask Leading Questions}
Instead of “What do you do?” ask “Are you rushing in or staying back?” Framed choices keep momentum.

\subsection*{Use a Ticking Clock}
If players hesitate, tick a clock. “While you argue, the fire spreads – \textit{Flood [4]} advances 1.” They will act.

\section{Adjusting Difficulty on the Fly}

\subsection*{Lower DV Instead of Fudging Dice}
If a challenge is too hard, reduce its DV by 1. The players never need to know. This is cleaner than ignoring a bad roll.

\subsection*{Offer a Devil’s Bargain}
Instead of a flat failure, ask: “You can succeed, but something else goes wrong. What is it?” This turns failure into player‑driven complication.

\subsection*{Add a Clock Instead of Raising DV}
If a scene needs more tension, add a 4‑segment clock (\textit{Security Alert}) rather than increasing DV. Players see the clock and feel pressure, but the core roll remains fair.

\section{Spotlight Management}

\subsection*{Rotate by Environment}
In a quiet library, the scholar shines. On a battlefield, the fighter takes lead. Watch whose skills match the scene and give them first action.

\subsection*{Use Bonds to Pass the Spotlight}
Say: “You owe Marcus a debt. What do you do to protect him?” This directly engages a bond and moves focus.

\subsection*{Quick Check‑Ins}
If one player is dominating, ask another: “While that happens, what is your character doing?” A simple question brings them back in.

\section{Using Failure to Advance the Plot}

\subsection*{Fail Forward}
A failed lockpick does not mean “nothing happens”. It means “the lock clicks, but loudly – a guard stirs.” The story moves.

\subsection*{Failure Reveals Information}
“You cannot pick the lock, but you notice the brand of a noble house – someone here has expensive tastes.”

\subsection*{Offer a Hard Choice Instead of Flat Failure}
“You can force the lock, but your pick will break – you will not be able to open another door tonight.” Let the player choose.

\section{Foreshadowing and Secrets}

\subsection*{Show, Don’t Tell}
Instead of “this place is dangerous”, describe: a bloody handprint, a cold draft, a whisper that stops when you enter.

\subsection*{Use SB to Plant Clues}
Spend 1 SB to add a subtle detail: “You notice a torn piece of cloth on the window latch. It is a livery colour you recognise.”

\subsection*{Reveal Secrets Through Play, Not Exposition}
Do not recap lore; let players discover it via rolls, clues, and NPC dialogue. If they miss it, it stays hidden – or they can find it another way.

\section{Running Cliffhangers}

\subsection*{End a Session After a Miss or Partial}
The dice just gave you a perfect cliffhanger. End there: “The door swings open – and you see her.” No further description.

\subsection*{Ask a Compelling Question}
“The ritual completes. What happens to the city?” Write down the answer; start the next session with it.

\subsection*{Write Down the Cliffhanger}
Keep a note – “Last thing: the door opened to reveal the queen’s double.” Read it aloud at the start of the next session.

\section{Recovering from Bad Rulings}

\subsection*{Admit It Quickly}
“I ruled that incorrectly. From now on, we will do it this way.” Players appreciate honesty.

\subsection*{Offer a Retroactive Boon}
If a bad ruling hurt a player, give them a free Boon. No need to retcon the past.

\subsection*{Only Retcon if the Game Breaks}
If the ruling was minor, move on. If it invalidates a major plot point, say “Let’s rewind that scene – my mistake.” Then replay the last few minutes.

\section{Lightweight Faction Loyalty (Optional)}
\index{faction loyalty}

Track faction standing on a simple **–3 to +3 scale**. No dice, no complex tables.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{–3:} Enemy – actively works against the party.
  \item \textbf{0:} Neutral – indifferent.
  \item \textbf{+3:} Ally – will sacrifice for the party.
\end{itemize}

Shift loyalty by **1** after major interactions (saved the faction, betrayed them, etc.). Display the number openly. That is it.

\section{Complication Trading (Player Choice)}
\index{complication trading}

When a player fails or partially succeeds, offer them a **choice of complication**:
\begin{itemize}
  \item “You fail to pick the lock. Does the noise alert a guard, or do you break your tools?”
  \item “You miss the shot. Does the arrow hit an ally, or do you lose your balance and fall?”
\end{itemize}
This keeps players engaged even in failure. No point economy needed.

\section{Cross-Cultural Synergy (Creativity Tip)}
\index{cross-cultural synergy}

Encourage players to combine cultural elements from different regions. If they propose a clever mix (e.g., Aeler engineering + Kahfagian sailing = a faster ship), give them a **+1 die** or **reduce DV by 1** for that roll. A simple reward for creative world‑building.

\section{Final Thought: You Are Playing Too}
\index{GM fun}

Do not forget that you are a player at the table. Use SB and the optional Threat Pool to have fun, but keep the game moving. Your enjoyment is as important as theirs.

Now go run a memorable scene.
