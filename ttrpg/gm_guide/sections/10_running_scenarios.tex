\chapter{Example Play and GM Survival Guide}
\index{example play}\index{gm survival guide}

This chapter provides two practical resources:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A complete, annotated example session of **Fate’s Edge** (Tier I–II).
  \item A short **GM Survival Guide** – quick, actionable advice for common table situations.
\end{enumerate}
All other GM guidance (session zero, safety tools, scene framing, clocks, etc.) is covered in other chapters. This chapter focuses on **seeing the system in action** and **handling the unexpected**.

\section{Example Session: The Heist at Lord Silas’s Manor}
\index{example session}\index{heist}

This walkthrough follows three characters as they attempt to steal a sealed charter from a rival merchant prince. It demonstrates how scenes are framed, how pressure escalates, and how success, failure, Boons, and Story Beats work together.

\subsection*{The Table}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Valerius} — Ecktorian ex-legionary (Body 3, Warfare 2)
  \item \textbf{Elara} — Vhasian agent and negotiator (Wits 3, Sway 2)
  \item \textbf{Kael} — Dwarven geomancer (Lore 3, Geomancy 2)
\end{itemize}
The GM has prepared no fixed plot — only factions, motives, and clocks.

\subsection*{Campaign Context}
The group seeks a sealed charter proving their patron’s claim to a trade route. The charter is held by \textbf{Lord Silas}, a rival merchant prince.
\begin{itemize}
  \item Silas is politically insulated.
  \item His manor is guarded but discreet.
  \item Exposure is as dangerous as failure.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Scene 1: Information Gathering}
\textbf{GM frames:} “You are in Silkstrand’s lower markets at dusk. Silas’s manor rises above you. Guards are visible, but relaxed.”

\noindent\textbf{Stakes:} Success = identify an entry vector; Failure = guard suspicion escalates.
\noindent\textbf{Clock introduced:} \emph{Manor Alert} [4]

\paragraph{Action.} Elara scouts using \textbf{Wits + Subterfuge}. Position: Controlled.  
She rolls 2 Successes vs DV 3 → \textbf{Partial Success}.

\paragraph{Resolution.} GM rules:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Elara identifies a servant gate (progress achieved).
  \item GM advances \emph{Manor Alert} by 1 (cost).
  \item Elara gains \textbf{1 Boon}.
\end{itemize}
The fiction advances; pressure is visible.

\subsection*{Scene 2: Entry}
The group scales the outer wall at night. Position: Desperate.  
\textbf{Stakes:} Success = inside undetected; Failure = immediate pursuit.

\paragraph{Action.} Valerius rolls \textbf{Body + Athletics}.  
He rolls 4 Successes including a 10 → \textbf{Clean Success with flourish}.

\paragraph{Resolution.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The group enters cleanly.
  \item GM reduces future Position penalties inside.
  \item No Story Beat is spent.
\end{itemize}
Strong rolls relieve pressure without removing future danger.

\subsection*{Scene 3: Complication Inside}
Kael attunes to the stone to locate the vault. Position: Controlled.  
Kael rolls 0 Successes → \textbf{Miss}.

\paragraph{Resolution.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Kael gains \textbf{2 Boons}.
  \item GM spends 1 SB to introduce a servant unexpectedly entering.
  \item GM advances \emph{Manor Alert} by 1.
\end{itemize}
The failure creates momentum rather than stalling play.

\subsection*{Scene 4: Social Pivot}
Elara calms the servant. Position: Risky.  
New clock: \emph{Servant Panic} [4].  
She rolls a \textbf{Success with Cost}.

\paragraph{Resolution.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item The servant is convinced.
  \item GM spends SB to advance \emph{Servant Panic} by 2.
  \item The group now operates under time pressure.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Scene Cut}
The GM cuts immediately: “You have the vault location. You also hear boots on marble.”  
This prevents overplaying and preserves tension.

\subsection*{Scene 5: The Vault}
The vault is magically reinforced. Clock: \emph{Vault Integrity} [6].  
Kael attempts a geomantic bypass. He rolls Partial (2 Successes vs DV 4).

\paragraph{Resolution.} GM chooses proportional progress:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Vault Integrity} advances by 2.
  \item GM takes a Story Beat instead of increasing Alert.
  \item Kael gains 1 Boon.
\end{itemize}
Partials are \emph{GM-shaped outcomes}.

\subsection*{Scene 6: Extraction}
The vault opens as guards arrive. \emph{Manor Alert} at 3/4.  
Valerius creates a distraction while Elara escapes with the charter. The final roll succeeds — but the GM fills \emph{Manor Alert} as the cost.

\paragraph{Outcome.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Charter secured.
  \item Silas knows he was robbed.
  \item New faction clock begins: \emph{Silas’s Retaliation} [6].
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Session Close}
\noindent\textbf{End-of-session review:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Boons generated primarily from Partials and Misses.
  \item Story Beats spent to escalate, not negate success.
  \item New clocks seeded future conflict.
\end{itemize}
The session ends with a clear win, lasting consequences, and visible momentum.

\section{High-Tier Play in Brief (Tier IV–V)}
\index{high-tier play}\index{boon scarcity}

At Tier IV–V, the core mechanics remain the same, but the context shifts. Here are the key differences, illustrated by a brief scenario.

\subsection*{What Changes}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Larger dice pools} → more automatic successes, fewer Misses.
  \item \textbf{Fewer Boons earned} → Boons become precious, strategic currency.
  \item \textbf{Failure has wider consequences} – a missed roll can shift faction clocks or expose an asset.
  \item \textbf{Bonds and Assets} become the primary sources of Boons.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Example: House Morrenn Conflict}
A Tier IV party leads a regional faction against House Morrenn. Campaign clocks: \emph{Morrenn Ascendancy} [6] and \emph{Faction Stability} [6].

\paragraph{Scene: Council Confrontation.} Elara spends her only Boon on a Tier IV talent, succeeds, but the GM spends a Story Beat to mark her as a personal enemy – creating a new clock \emph{Targeted Retaliation} [4].

\paragraph{Scene: Asset Pressure.} Assassins strike the safehouse. No Boons remain, so Valerius accepts Harm and a worse Position to protect NPCs. \emph{Faction Stability} advances by 1 – people saw leadership under fire.

\paragraph{Scene: Risky Ritual.} Kael attempts a geomantic collapse to delay troops. He rolls a Miss, gains 2 Boons, but \emph{Morrenn Ascendancy} advances and long-term fallout (destabilised ley fault) appears.

\paragraph{Result.} The party ends with 1–2 Boons, strained assets, and multiple clocks near resolution. No DV inflation was needed – the stakes and resource scarcity did the work.

\subsection*{Key Takeaway}
At high tier, power comes from reach, not safety. Boons are for decisive moments, not routine re‑rolls. Failure is often chosen to regain leverage.

\section{GM Survival Guide}
\index{survival guide}\index{GM tips}

This section offers quick, actionable advice for the most common GM challenges. It assumes you have read the core rules and other chapters – this is the “cheat sheet” for the table.

\subsection*{When Players Go Off the Rails}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Do not panic. Ask: “What is your goal?” (not “How do you do that?”).
  \item Name the simplest obstacle in their way – then set Position and DV.
  \item If they ignore the obvious, let the world react. A missed roll means the guard they bypassed earlier shows up.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{When a Scene Stalls}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Ask a leading question: “Who are you trying to impress?” or “What are you afraid of here?”
  \item Tick a clock. “While you argue, the fire spreads – \emph{Collapse [4]} advances 1.”
  \item Cut to another player. Shift spotlight.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{When You Don’t Know a Rule}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Make a ruling that keeps the story moving. Say: “We’ll do it this way for now; look it up later.”
  \item Default to DV 3, Controlled Position, and a consequence that feels right.
  \item Trust your gut. The system is robust.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{How to Say “No” (Without Firing the Player)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item “No, but…” – offer a harder path, a cost, or a requirement.
  \item “Yes, but…” – let them succeed, then add a complication.
  \item “Let’s find out” – call for a roll with high DV and Desperate Position. The dice decide.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Using Clocks Effectively}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Name clocks clearly: \emph{Alarm Raised [4]}, not “Bad Stuff [6]”.
  \item Only keep 1–3 clocks active per scene. More than that is noise.
  \item Tick a clock on a Miss or a Partial – even if the player otherwise succeeds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Managing Player Spotlight}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Rotate by environment: scholar in the library, fighter on the wall.
  \item Use Bonds to pass focus: “You owe Rina – what do you do to protect her?”
  \item Quick check‑in: “While that happens, what is your character doing?”
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{When You Feel Burnt Out}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Call a break. Even five minutes resets the table.
  \item Ask players to recap – they will remind you of forgotten details.
  \item End the session early if needed. No game is better than a bad game.
  \item Use a published adventure or a random generator for one week. Give yourself a rest.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Final Survival Mantra}
\begin{quote}
\emph{Set the DV low. Make the world react honestly. Let the pressure do the work.} \\
— The Fate’s Edge GM Mantra
\end{quote}

If the table feels engaged, tense, and safe – you are running the game correctly.
